Ch. III.


On the sound understanding of this faith.

For the sound understanding of this faith, sacred doctrine teaches that in the divine there are two emanations, three hypostases, four relations, five notions, and from these only three personal properties.

Now the reason for understanding the aforesaid is this: since the first and highest principle, by the very fact that it is first, is most simple, and by the very fact that it is highest, is most perfect, it therefore communicates itself most perfectly, because it is most perfect, and preserves complete indivision, by the very fact that it is most simple. And therefore, with the unity of nature preserved, there are therein perfect modes of emanating. Now the perfect modes of emanating are only two, namely by way of nature and of will. The first is generation, the second is spiration or procession, and therefore these are therein.

And because by two substantial emanations two hypostases must necessarily emanate, it is also necessary to posit that the hypostasis that first produces does not emanate from another, lest there be an infinite regress: therefore there are therein three hypostases.

And because to each emanation there corresponds a twofold relative relation, therefore there are therein four relations, namely paternity, filiation, spiration, and procession.

Now through these relations the divine hypostases become known to us; and besides this, that hypostasis also in which there is the first principle of originating becomes known because it is not produced — for this is a mark of nobility in it — therefore there are five notions, namely the four aforesaid relations together with innascibility.

And because each of the persons has one property through which it is principally known, therefore there are only three personal properties, which are expressed properly and principally by these names, namely Father and Son and Holy Spirit.

For since it is proper to the Father to be innascible or unbegotten, to be a principle not from a principle, and to be the Father: innascibility makes him known by way of negation, although consequently by way of position, because innascibility in the Father posits a fontal fullness; principle not from a principle, by way of position with negation; to be the Father, by way of position and relation, properly, completely, and determinately.

Similarly, since the Son is image, word, and son: image names that person as an expressed likeness; word, as an expressive likeness; son, as a hypostatic likeness. Again, image names him as a conformed likeness; word, as an intellectual likeness; son, as a connatural likeness.

In this manner, since it is proper to the Holy Spirit to be a gift, to be the bond or charity of both, and also to be the Holy Spirit: gift names him as given voluntarily: charity or bond, as given voluntarily and preeminently; Holy Spirit, as given voluntarily, preeminently and hypostatically.

Hence it is that through these three names, which are Father and Son and Holy Spirit, the personal properties of the three persons are intimated.

These things, therefore, must be held for a sound understanding of faith in the Trinity.
