Ch. II.


On grace, insofar as it assists toward meritorious good.

Second, the grace of the Holy Spirit must be considered by us in comparison to free choice,

and this in a twofold manner. First, namely, insofar as it is an aid toward merit: second, insofar as it is a remedy against sin.

Concerning the grace of God, therefore, insofar as it is an aid toward meriting , these things must be held: that since grace is spoken of generally, specially and properly: generally it is called the divine aid bestowed freely and gratuitously upon a creature, indifferently toward any act whatsoever: and without this kind of aid of grace we can neither accomplish anything nor endure in being. - Specially grace is called the aid divinely given so that one may prepare oneself to receive the gift of the Holy Spirit, by which one may arrive at the state of merit: and such is called gratuitous grace, and without this no one sufficiently does what is in oneself to prepare oneself for salvation. - Properly grace is called the aid given divinely for meriting, which indeed is called the gift of sanctifying grace, without which no one can merit, nor advance in good, nor arrive at eternal salvation. For it, as the root of meriting, precedes all merits: on account of which it has been said that "it precedes the will, so that it may will: but it follows after, lest it will in vain." Hence no one can merit it by condign merit, but "it itself merits to be increased by God along the way, so that once increased it may merit to be perfected in the fatherland and in everlasting glory by God himself, whose it is to infuse, increase and perfect grace according to the cooperation of our will and according to the purpose or good pleasure of eternal predestination.

Now the reason for understanding the aforesaid is this: because, since the first principle by its omnipotent power and most benign generosity brought forth every creature from nothing into being, and through this the creature of itself has non-being, but has all its being from another, it was so made that, on account of its defectibility, it would always need its principle, and the first principle, on account of its benignity, would not cease to exert its influence. Since therefore the rational spirit, by the very fact that it is from nothing, is in itself defective; by the very fact that it is a limited and needy nature, is curved in upon itself, loving its own good; by the very fact that it is entirely from God, is totally subject to God; and because it is defective, of itself it tends toward non-being; because it is curved, of itself it does not rise to the rectitude of perfect justice; because it is totally subject to God, and God does not need its goods, it can do nothing of itself and by its own power by which it would make God its debtor, and especially of the eternal reward, which is God, except through divine condescension: hence it is that, in order to be preserved in being, since it is defective, it always needs the help of divine presence, sustenance, and influence, by which it may be sustained in being; which, although it is universal toward all creatures, is nevertheless named by the name of grace, because it does not proceed from what is owed, but from the liberality of divine goodness.

Hence it is also that, in order to prepare itself for the gift of supernatural grace, since it is curved in upon itself, it needs the gift of another grace freely given, especially after fallen nature, by which it may be rendered capable of moral goods, which are goods from circumstance, which in no way can be called good unless they proceed from a right intention, namely that they be done not for our own sake but for the sake of the highest good, to which our curved spirit does not rise unless it is anticipated by God through some grace freely given.

Hence it is no less the case that, in order to perform meritorious works of eternal reward, since it is totally subject to God and a debtor of its entire self, it needs the gift of sanctifying grace, by which God condescends to it, first accepting his image and will before the operation flowing from it: because, since "the cause is nobler than the effect," no one can make himself better nor do a work pleasing to God unless he himself first pleases, so that God first looks upon him before looking upon his gifts. And therefore every root of meriting is founded in sanctifying grace, whose function is to make a person worthy of God: on account of which no one can merit it by the merit of condignity, but only by the merit of congruity.

Once possessed, grace itself merits its own increase in the state of the wayfarer through its good use by a worthy merit. For since God alone is the fontal principle of infusing grace itself, He alone is the principle of augmenting it by way of infusing, and grace by way of merit and worthiness, and free choice by way of cooperating and meriting, inasmuch as free choice cooperates with grace and makes what belongs to grace its own.

And therefore free choice through grace merits not only the increase of grace in the state of the wayfarer by a worthy merit, but also its completion in the state of the fatherland by a condign merit, both on account of the sublimity of the gift of the Holy Spirit cooperating in the merit; and on account of the truthfulness of God who promises; and on account of the mutability of free choice that consents and finally perseveres; and on account of the difficulty of the state of meriting; and on account of the dignity of Christ our Head who intercedes, who ought to be glorified with His members; and on account of the liberality of God who repays, whom it does not befit to render small things in return for the service of one faithfully obeying Him; and on account of the nobility of the work, which proceeds from charity, which weighs as much in the sight of the Judge as the love from which it proceeds, which incomparably prefers God above all creatures, and therefore cannot be sufficiently and fittingly recompensed except in God and the supreme Good.

From all of which, as from seven reasons, the sevenfold grace causes eternal glory to be merited by a merit not only of congruity, but also of condignity.
