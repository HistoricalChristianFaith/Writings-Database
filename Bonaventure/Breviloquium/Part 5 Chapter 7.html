Ch. VII.


On the exercise of grace with respect to things to be believed.

In the fourth place, therefore, it remains to consider grace with regard to the exercises of merits. And concerning these, four things must be considered. First, the exercise of grace in things to be believed, such as the articles of faith; second, in things to be loved, such as those things which pertain to the order of loving; third, in things to be carried out, such as the precepts of the divine law; fourth, in things to be asked for, such as the petitions of the Lord's Prayer.

Concerning the articles of faith, the following must be held: that although through faith we are bound to believe very many things which are above reason, and generally all things which are contained and set forth in the canon of Sacred Scripture; yet specially and properly, those are called articles of faith which are contained in the series of the Apostles' Creed: which in one way are twelve, if regard is had to those who composed the Creed, and in another way fourteen, if we consider what things are radically to be believed as the foundations of all things to be believed.

Now the reason for understanding the aforesaid is this: since the first principle in itself is supremely true and good, and in its work is supremely just and merciful: and to the supremely true is owed firm assent, to the supremely good fervent love, to the supremely just universal subjection, to the supremely merciful trustful invocation: and grace is that which orders our mind to the due worship of the first principle: hence it is that grace itself directs and regulates toward due and meritorious exercises in things to be believed, things to be loved, things to be carried out, and things to be asked for, according as the supreme truth, goodness, justice, and mercy in the blessed Trinity requires.

Since therefore truth is to be believed, and a greater truth is to be believed more, and consequently the supreme truth is to be believed supremely; and the truth of the first principle is infinitely greater than every created truth and more luminous than every light of our intellect: hence it is that, in order that our intellect may be rightly ordered in things to be believed, it is necessary that it believe the supreme truth more than itself, and that it bring itself into the obedience of Christ; and through this, that it believe not only those things which are according to reason, but also those which are above reason and contrary to the experience of the senses: for if it refuses, it does not exhibit due reverence to the supreme truth, since it prefers the judgment of its own industry to the dictate of the eternal light; which cannot be without the swelling of pride and blameworthy elation.

Again, because truth that is above reason or beyond reason is truth neither seen nor apparent, but rather hidden and most difficult to believe: therefore, in order that it may be firmly believed, there is needed an illumination of truth elevating the soul, and there is also needed a testimony of authority strengthening the soul. The first is accomplished through infused faith, the second through authentic Scripture, both of which come from the supreme truth through Jesus Christ, who is the Splendor and the Word, and through the Holy Spirit, who shows and teaches the truth and nonetheless causes belief. Hence it is that authority provides the support of faith, and faith assents to authority. And because authority principally resides in sacred Scripture, which was composed entirely by the Holy Spirit for the direction of the catholic faith: hence it is that true faith does not disagree with Scripture, but assents to it with unfeigned assent.

Finally, because the truth to the belief of which we are bound by faith, and which sacred Scripture principally treats, is not just any truth, but divine truth, either as it is in its own nature or as it is in the assumed nature — for in the knowledge of this truth consists both the reward of the fatherland and the merit of the way —: hence it is that the articles of faith, which are the foundations of faith, pertain either to the Divinity or to the humanity. And since the Divinity is to be considered in three persons, namely the Father begetting, the Son begotten, and the Holy Spirit proceeding, and in a fourfold operation, namely creation in the being of nature, recreation in the being of grace, resurrection in the restoration of life, and glorification in the bestowal of glory: hence it is that the articles pertaining to the Divinity are seven.

Similarly, because Christ's humanity is to be considered as conceived of the Holy Spirit, as born of the Virgin, as suffering on the cross, as descending into hell, as rising from the dead, as ascending into heaven, as coming to the final judgment; hence it is that there are seven articles of the humanity, and thus fourteen in all, after the manner of the seven stars and the seven golden lampstands, in the midst of which the Son of Man walked.

Since therefore there is one Christ in divine and human nature; and there is only one supreme truth, which is the one, first, supreme, and sole ground of believing, which does not change through the ages: hence it is that concerning all the aforesaid articles there is only one faith about the same, unchanged in those of the present as in those of the past as in those of the future, although it is more clear and explicit in those who follow Christ than in those who preceded his coming; just as the New Testament is clearer than the Old, in which the aforesaid articles are contained.

And because the Holy Spirit brought together these articles of faith, contained in the depths of the Scriptures, into one through the twelve Apostles as through the most steadfast witnesses: hence it is that the aforesaid articles were collected into the one Creed of the Apostles. And therefore, according to the Apostles who composed it, they can be called twelve articles, because each Apostle, for the building up of the faith, set down one article as one living stone: which the Holy Spirit rightly prefigured in the twelve men who drew twelve stones from the bed of the Jordan for the construction of the altar of the Lord.
