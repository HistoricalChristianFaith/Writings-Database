Chapter XI.


On the production of man with respect to the whole composite.

Concerning the whole man placed in paradise, these things must be held: namely, that a twofold sense was given to him, namely interior and exterior, of the mind and of the flesh. - A twofold motion was given to him, namely commanding in the will and executive in the body. - A twofold good was given to him, one visible, the other invisible. - A twofold precept was given to him, namely of nature and of discipline; the precept of nature: Increase and multiply; the precept of discipline: Of the tree of knowledge of good and evil thou shalt not eat.

In accordance with which a fourfold help was given to him, namely of knowledge, conscience, synderesis, and grace, from which he sufficiently possessed the means to stand in the good and advance, and to guard against evil and turn away from it.

The reason for understanding the aforesaid is this: because the First Principle made this sensible world to manifest Himself, namely so that through it, as through a mirror and a vestige, man might be led back to God the Maker, to love and praise Him. And accordingly there is a twofold book: one written within, which is the eternal art and wisdom of God; and another written without, namely the sensible world. Since, therefore, there was one creature that had sense within for knowledge of the interior book, namely the Angel; and another that had all its sense without, namely any brute animal: for the perfection of the universe there had to be made a creature endowed with this twofold sense for knowledge of the book written within and without, that is, of Wisdom and of its work. And because in Christ the eternal Wisdom and its work come together simultaneously in one person, He is therefore called the book written within and without for the restoration of the world.

And because to every sense there corresponds a motion, therefore a twofold motion was given to man: one according to the impulse of reason in the mind, and another according to the impulse of sensuality in the flesh. It belongs to the first to command, and to the second to obey, according to right order: but when this is reversed, then the rectitude and governance of the soul is cast down from its state.

And because to every motion and sense there corresponds an appetite for some good, therefore a twofold good was prepared for man: "one visible, the other invisible; one temporal, the other eternal; one for the flesh, the other for the spirit. Of these goods, God gave one and promised the other, so that one might be possessed freely, and the other sought through merit."

And because a good is given in vain unless it is preserved, and is promised in vain unless one attains to it, therefore a twofold precept was given to man: one of nature for preserving the good given, the other of discipline for meriting the good promised, which could in no way be better merited than through pure obedience. Obedience however is pure when a precept obliges of itself alone, not from any other cause; and such is called a precept of discipline, because through it one learns how great is the virtue of obedience, which by its merit leads to heaven, but by its contrary casts one down into hell. Therefore that commandment was not given to man on account of any need that God had for human service, but to provide a way of meriting the crown through pure and voluntary obedience.

And since man, by reason of a defective nature formed from nothing and not confirmed through glory, was able to fall, the most benign God conferred upon him a fourfold aid: a twofold aid of nature and a twofold aid of grace. For He implanted a twofold rectitude in nature itself: one for judging rightly, and this is the rectitude of conscience ; the other for willing rightly, and this is synderesis , whose office it is to murmur against evil and to spur toward good.

He also superadded a twofold perfection of grace: one of gratuitously given grace, which was a knowledge illuminating the intellect for knowing himself, his God, and this world, which had been made for his sake; the other of sanctifying grace, which was a charity enabling the affections to love God above all things and neighbor as oneself . - And thus before the fall, man possessed perfect natural endowments, clothed nonetheless with divine grace. From which it is manifestly gathered that if he fell, this was solely through his own fault, because he scorned to obey.
