PART FIVE. On the Grace of the Holy Spirit.


Chapter I.


On grace, insofar as it is a gift divinely given.

After the treatise on the incarnation of the Word, which is the origin and font of every gratuitous gift, some things must be said concerning the grace of the Holy Spirit, which presents itself for our consideration in a fourfold manner. First, insofar as it is a gift divinely given.

Second, in comparison to free will.

Third, in comparison to the habits of the virtues.

Fourth, in comparison to the exercise of merits.

Concerning grace, therefore, insofar as it is a gift divinely given, the following must be held: that it is a gift which is immediately given and infused by God. For indeed, with it and in it the Holy Spirit is given, who is the uncreated gift, the best and perfect gift, which descends from the Father of lights through the incarnate Word, according to what John in Revelation saw a river, shining like crystal, proceeding from the throne of God and of the Lamb.

It is nonetheless a gift through which the soul is perfected and made the bride of Christ, the daughter of the eternal Father, and the temple of the Holy Spirit: which in no way comes about except from the dignifying condescension and condescending dignity of the eternal Majesty through the gift of His grace.

It is, finally, a gift which purges, illuminates, and perfects the soul; vivifies, reforms, and stabilizes it; elevates, assimilates, and joins it to God, and thereby renders it acceptable: on account of which a gift of this kind is rightly called and ought to be named grace making pleasing.

Now the reason for understanding the aforesaid is this: since the first productive principle, out of its supreme benevolence, made the rational spirit capable of eternal beatitude; and the reparative principle restored that capacity, weakened by sin, unto salvation; and eternal beatitude consists in possessing the supreme good; and this is God, a good surpassing beyond all proportion every dignity of human service: no one at all is worthy to attain that supreme good, since it is entirely above all the limits of nature, unless, by God condescending to him, he is elevated above himself. But God does not condescend through his unchangeable essence, but through an influence flowing from himself; nor is the spirit elevated above itself through local position, but through a deiform habit. It is therefore necessary for the rational spirit, in order to become worthy of eternal beatitude, that it become a partaker of the deiform influence. Now this deiform influence, because it is from God and according to God and for the sake of God, therefore renders the image of our mind conformed to the most blessed Trinity, not only according to the order of origin, but also according to the rectitude of choice and according to the repose of fruition. And since he who has this is immediately led back to God, just as he is immediately conformed to him, therefore that gift is immediately bestowed by God as by an inflowing principle: so that, just as the image of God immediately emanates from God, so the likeness of God immediately abides from him, which is the deiform perfection of the divine image, and therefore is called the image of re-creation.

Again, since he who enjoys God possesses God, therefore with grace, which by its deiformity disposes to the fruition of God, there is given an uncreated gift, which is the Holy Spirit, which he who has, has God also.

And since no one possesses God without being more specially possessed by him; no one possesses and is possessed by God without loving him preeminently and incomparably and being loved by him as a bride by a bridegroom; no one is so loved without being adopted for the eternal inheritance as a son: hence it is that grace which makes pleasing renders the soul a temple of God, a bride of Christ, and a daughter of the eternal Father. And because this cannot be except from the supreme condescension and graciousness of God, therefore it cannot be through any habit naturally implanted, but only through a gift divinely and gratuitously infused; which clearly appears if one considers how great a thing it is to be a temple of God, a son of God, and nonetheless to be indissolubly and, as it were, matrimonially joined to God through the bond of love and grace.

Finally, because our mind is not made conformable to the most blessed Trinity according to the rectitude of choice except through the vigor of virtue, the splendor of truth, and the fervor of charity: and the vigor of virtue purges, stabilizes, and elevates the soul: the splendor of truth illuminates, reforms, and assimilates the soul to God: the fervor of charity perfects, vivifies, and unites the soul to God, and from all these a man becomes pleasing and acceptable to God: hence it is that that deiform influence is said to have all the ten aforementioned acts, yet in such a way that it is denominated from the last as from the most complete.

For it is called grace making pleasing, because it makes the one who possesses it pleasing to God, since it is not only given freely by God, but is also according to God and for the sake of God: since it exists for this purpose, that through it the work flowing from God may return to God, in which, after the manner of an intelligible circle, consists the fulfillment of all rational spirits.
