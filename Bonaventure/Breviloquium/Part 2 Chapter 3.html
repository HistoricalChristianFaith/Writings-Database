Ch. III.


On corporeal nature with respect to its being.

Concerning corporeal nature with respect to being, these things are to be held: that the whole machine of the corporeal world consists in celestial and elemental nature, such that celestial nature is distinguished into three principal heavens, namely the empyrean, the crystalline, and the firmament. Within the firmament, which is the starry heaven, are contained seven orbs of seven planets, which are: Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, the Sun, Venus, Mercury, and the Moon.

Elemental nature, however, is distinguished into four spheres, namely of fire, air, water, and earth; and thus proceeding from the highest summit of heaven down to the center of the earth, ten celestial orbs and four elemental spheres are encountered, from which the whole machine of the sensible world is integrated and constituted in a distinct, perfect, and orderly manner.

The reason for understanding the aforesaid is this: since corporeal nature, for its own perfection and for the expression of the multiform wisdom of the First Principle, requires a multiformity of forms, as is apparent in minerals, plants, and animals, it was necessary to posit certain simple bodies that could be mixed in manifold ways for the introduction of multiform forms; and such is the nature subject to contrariety, and this is elemental nature. It was also necessary that a nature be made through which these contraries could be reconciled in a mixture; and such is a nature far removed from contrariety, of which kind is the nature of light and of the supercelestial body.

And since mixture cannot occur except through contraries that act and are acted upon, it was therefore necessary that a twofold contrariety be established in the elements: namely, with respect to active qualities, which are hot and cold, and with respect to passive qualities, which are moist and dry. And because each element both acts and is acted upon, it therefore has two qualities, one active and the other passive, yet in such a way that one is principal and proper to it; and through this it is necessary that there be only four elements according to the four aforesaid qualities, combined in fourfold manner.

Celestial nature, however, is either uniform and immobile, and this is the empyrean, because it is pure light; or mobile and multiform, and thus the firmament; or mobile and uniform, and thus the heaven midway between the empyrean and the starry, which is the crystalline heaven. The fourth member, however—namely, that it be multiform and immobile—cannot stand, because multiformity disposes toward variety of motion, not toward uniform rest.

There are therefore three heavens, of which the first is luminous throughout, namely the empyrean: the second is throughout transparent, namely the crystalline: the third is conjoined from both, namely the firmament. Since therefore there are three incorruptible heavens and four changeable elements, in order that there might be a fitting connection, concord, and correspondence, God disposed seven planetary spheres, which by their variety of motions and incorruptibility of forms would be, as it were, a certain bond and juncture of the lower elemental spheres and the upper celestial ones, for perfecting and adorning the universe: which is said to be ordered and connected according to numerical proportions by the ten celestial spheres and the four elements, rendering it proportionally as beautiful as it is perfect and ordered, so that in its own way it might represent its Principle.
