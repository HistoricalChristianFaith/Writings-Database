Ch. IV.


On the branching of grace into the habits of the virtues.

Third, it remains to treat of grace in comparison to the habits of the virtues, concerning which three things must be considered. The first is how one grace branches into the habits of the virtues; the second is how it branches into the habits of the gifts; the third is how it branches into the habits of the beatitudes.

Concerning the branching of grace into the habits of the virtues, therefore, these things must be held: that although there is one grace making the soul pleasing to God, nevertheless there are seven gratuitous virtues by which human life is governed: three indeed theological, namely faith, hope, and charity; and four cardinal, namely prudence, temperance, fortitude, and justice, which in one way is a common and general virtue, and in another way special and proper.

Now these seven virtues, although they are distinct and have their own excellences, are nevertheless connected and equal to one another in the same subject; and although they are gratuitous, informed by grace, they can nevertheless become unformed through fault, charity alone excepted, and again be informed through penance, when grace arrives, which is the origin, end, and form of the virtuous habits.

Now the reason for understanding the aforesaid is this: because, just as the productive principle, by its supreme perfection in giving life to nature, gives not only living with respect to first act, but also with respect to second act, which is operating, so it is necessary that the reparative principle bestow life on the spirit in gratuitous being, both with respect to being and with respect to operating. And since for one living being according to one primary life there are many vital operations for the perfect manifestation of that life — since acts are diversified by their objects, and the diversity of acts requires a distinction of habits — hence it is that although there is one vivifying grace, it must nevertheless necessarily branch into various habits on account of various operations. And since certain moral works are primary, such as believing; certain ones intermediate, such as understanding the things believed; and certain ones final, such as seeing the things understood; and in the first the soul is rectified, in the second it is made ready, in the third it is perfected: therefore grace making one pleasing to God branches into the habits of the virtues, whose role is to rectify the soul; into the habits of the gifts, whose role is to make ready the soul; and into the habits of the beatitudes, whose role is to perfect the soul.

Again, since the perfect rectitude of the soul requires that it be rectified according to its twofold face, namely the superior and the inferior, and with respect to the end, and with respect to those things which are directed toward the end: Therefore it is necessary that the soul, with respect to the superior face in which the image of the eternal Trinity consists, be rectified through the three theological virtues, so that, just as the image of creation consists in a trinity of powers with a unity of essence, so the image of re-creation consists in a trinity of habits with a unity of grace, through which the soul is rightly borne toward the supreme Trinity according to the three things appropriated to the three persons; so that faith directs toward the supremely true by believing and assenting, hope toward the supremely arduous by relying and expecting, charity toward the supremely good by desiring and loving.

It is also necessary that with respect to the inferior face the soul be rectified through the four cardinal virtues. For prudence rectifies the rational power, fortitude the irascible, temperance the concupiscible, while justice rectifies all these powers in relation to another.

And because that other can determinately be one's neighbor, the same person can also be compared to himself as to another, and it can also be God himself: hence it is that justice is said to encompass all the powers. It is also called not only a cardinal virtue, but also a general one, comprehending the rectitude of the whole soul, since it is called "rectitude of the will." Whence it comprehends not only the virtues ordering toward one's neighbor, such as equity and liberality: but also toward oneself, such as penitence and innocence; and also toward God, such as latria, piety, and obedience.

Finally, since all rectitude of the virtues according to their gratuitous being flows from grace as from its origin and root: and according to their meritorious being is related to charity as to its origin, form, and end: hence it is that the other gratuitous virtues with respect to their habits are connected and with respect to their meritorious acts are equal.

Hence it is also that the other habits of the virtues can be unformed, with the sole exception of charity, which is the form of the virtues. For when they are possessed without grace and charity, in which the life of the virtues consists, then they are unformed. But when grace is infused upon them, then they are formed and adorned and become acceptable to God: just as colors without light are invisible, but when light comes upon them, they become luminous, beautiful, and pleasing to sight. Whence just as from light and colors one thing comes to be in the character of a motive power, and one light suffices to illuminate many colors: so from grace and unformed habits, when they are formed, one thing comes to be according to the character of the meritorious and the gratuitous: and one grace nonetheless suffices for the formation and gratification of diverse habits.
