SCHOLION.


I. In composing this Commentary, St. Bonaventure, besides the interlinear and ordinary Gloss (which is for the most part derived from the Commentary of St. Jerome on Ecclesiastes), made use both of the aforementioned exposition of Jerome himself and of another by Hugh of St. Victor, which, at least in the editions, does not extend beyond chapter 4, verse 7 of Ecclesiastes. From Hugh he borrowed that principal division of threefold vanity, namely of mutability, iniquity, and penality. That he was also acquainted with the Mystical Exposition on Ecclesiastes of Salonius, bishop of Vienne (see Migne, Patrologia Latina vol. LIII, col. 993 ff.), who flourished around 453, a certain passage cited by us seems to suggest, unless perhaps he received that opinion as reported by other authors. Nor have we found any certain trace that he made use of the Commentaries of Alcuin and Rupert, abbot of Deutz, who in expounding Ecclesiastes closely follow Jerome.

Moreover, this exposition is a work entirely proper to the Seraphic Doctor and reflecting his native character. He himself, employing at times other versions, especially those of Jerome and the Septuagint, expounds the text of the Vulgate according to the literal sense, so that by means of very many parallel passages of Scripture, as if Scripture itself were explaining Scripture, he illuminates the meaning. After no small portion of the text has been expounded according to the literal sense, there is usually appended a brief exposition of the mystical or spiritual sense of the same portion. Furthermore, in their proper places, more than eighty questions composed in the scholastic manner are added, by which special difficulties arising concerning the doctrine or the more obscure meaning of passages are briefly and clearly resolved.

II. In drawing out, dividing, and arranging weighty thoughts from the words of Sacred Scripture, the Holy Commentator demonstrates the most ingenious subtlety. Indeed, very many more recent expositors of this book deny that a fixed order and logical progression can be found in it. Whatever may be thought about this, for understanding our author's true view concerning the order arranged by him, what he sets forth in the Collations on the Hexaemeron, collation 44, n. 5, with respect to all the books of Scripture concerning the, so to speak, organic order of Sacred Scripture, or concerning the diversity that exists between its order and the order of human books, can be of service—namely that in Scripture one thought is not placed after another in such a manner: "It is not so," he says, "because it is most perfectly ordered, and its order is similar to the order of nature in the germination of the earth," etc.

It is indeed difficult to retain in the mind the very great number of divisions and subdivisions. Lest therefore the reader be confused, we have already taken care as best we could in arranging the text, both with titles which we have placed above the major parts, and with marginal notes and typefaces which they call italics. We think it neither superfluous nor unwelcome to the reader that we set forth the multitude of divisions and principal statements which are found in the literal exposition, outlined in these four diagrams in a single overview.
