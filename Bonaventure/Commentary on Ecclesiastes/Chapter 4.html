Chapter IV.


Second, concerning the vanity of malice in subjects according to a threefold distinction.

I turned myself to other things, etc. Above, the vanity of malice in prelates was treated; here, in subjects. The vanity of malice is touched upon here with respect to a threefold distinction: first, oppression, in maliciously harming; second, envy, in maliciously grieving, at that passage: Again I contemplated; third, sloth, in withdrawing from good work; and this is touched upon at that passage: The fool folds his hands.

(Vers. 1. 2. 3.). He therefore describes the emptiness of oppression : and with regard to the consideration of oppression he says: I turned myself to other things, supply: to be considered; and I saw the oppressions that are carried on under the sun, that is, in this world: Ezekiel twenty-two: "The people of the land practiced oppression and seized violently, they afflicted the needy and the poor, and they oppressed the stranger with calumny without judgment." And oppression is called the violent extortion of goods, as if through justice, but through malice.

And he adds the aggravation of misery, on account of the calamity of the oppressed, because they are oppressed unjustly, inconsolably, irremediably.

Because unjustly, he says: And the tears of the innocent, namely on account of such oppressions: Job thirty-five: "Because of the multitude of oppressors they will cry out, and they will wail because of the violence of the arm of tyrants".

Because inconsolably: and no consoler, which indeed aggravates the cruelty: whence Lamentations 1: "There is none to comfort her among all her dear ones." On the contrary, Sirach 7: "Be not wanting in comforting those that weep, and walk with those that mourn";

Nor does he lack only consolation, but indeed a remedy: whence he also adds: Nor could they resist their violence, being deprived of the help of all, supply: I saw. Concerning this evil it is said in Proverbs twenty-two: "Do not do violence to the poor because he is poor, nor crush the needy at the gate." Therefore in Second Chronicles twenty: "In us there is not such strength that we can resist this multitude which rushes upon us."

But as for the detestation of guilt, he adds: And I praised the dead more than the living, namely because they do not see these evils and cruelties, which no good person can see without grieving: in 3 Kings 19, Elijah said: "It is enough for me, Lord; take my soul, for I am no better than my fathers."

And I judged happier than both, namely the dead and the living, who has not yet been born nor has seen the evils that are under the sun: as if to see these evils or to have seen them is a great misery: 1 Maccabees 2: "Woe to me, said Mattathias, why was I born to see the destruction of my people and the destruction of the holy city, and to sit there?" -- Thus therefore is noted here the consideration of oppression, the aggravation of misery, because they are oppressed, desolate, destitute of help, and then the detestation of fault.

(Vers. 4.). Again I contemplated etc. Here secondly is touched upon the vanity of envy in grieving maliciously over the good and prosperity of one's neighbor, which he also saw: whence he adds: Again I contemplated. This contemplation is a free and penetrating and fixed gaze. I contemplated, I say, all the labors and endeavors of men: labors, as regards the body; and the endeavors of men, as regards the soul.

Or: labors and industrious efforts, that is, virtues: whence another translation: Every virtue of work: above in chapter two: "I detested all my industry, with which I most diligently labored under the sun." And I observed, that is, I perceived in my mind: to lie open, that is, to be exposed: to the en-vy of one's neighbor, because the envious person, seeing good things, immediately grieves: Sirach fourteen: "Evil is the eye of the envious, turning away his face and despising his own soul." And in this therefore is vanity, namely of malice: Proverbs twenty-eight: "A man who hastens to become rich and envies others," etc. And superfluous care, namely of punishment, because when he ought to think about his own goods, he always thinks about those of others: whence Proverbs twenty-three: "Do not eat with an envious man, and do not desire his foods, because like a diviner he estimates what he does not know."

(Vers. 5. 6.). The fool folds etc. Here thirdly is touched upon the vanity of sloth, on account of which a person is rendered weary and lazy toward every good, and through this foolish. Therefore he says: The fool folds his hands together: therefore a fool, because lazy; Proverbs 12: "He who pursues idleness is most foolish"; Proverbs 6: "Go to the ant, O sluggard, and learn wisdom." This fool folds his hands, because he refuses to work: Proverbs 26: "The sluggard hides his hands under his armpit and it wearies him if he brings them back to his mouth." And from this, because he refuses to work, he becomes poor and thin on account of the want of poverty; whence he says: And eats his own flesh, saying: this is said hyperbolically, because he becomes thin, not having food: Proverbs 21: "Desires kill the sluggard, for his hands refuse to do anything."

But in this laziness of sloth, he seems to himself to have an excuse of wisdom; whence he adds, saying: Better is a handful with rest than both hands full with labor and affliction of spirit. This is the opinion of the lazy man, by which he excuses himself; whence it is said in Proverbs twenty-six: "The sluggard is wiser in his own eyes than seven men who give wise answers." - And thus there is suggested in the lazy man the torpor of sloth, because he folds his hands;the endurance of want, because he eats his own flesh;the excuse of ignorance, because he praises rest.

Questions.


I. Here the question is raised concerning what he says: I praised the dead more than the living.

Against this: 1. Below in the ninth chapter: A living dog is better than a dead lion.

2. Furthermore, this is seen by reason: because a habit is better than a privation, therefore life is better than death: therefore, from the conjugates, the living are better than the dead.

I respond: it must be said that to praise one thing more than another, or to prefer it, can be done in two ways: either sim-ply, or with respect to some condition. Simply speaking, the living is preferred to the dead, unless the condition of blessedness is added; but in some respect the dead is preferred to the living: first, because he does not have so many occasions of sinning; whence the Apostle said in Romans 7: Wretched man that I am, who will deliver me from the bondage of this body? Second, because he does not have so many occasions of sorrowing, because he does not see so many evils; Jonah, last chapter: It is better for me to die than to live. And with respect to this condition, the dead is preferred to the living, to emphasize present evils and miseries.

II. Likewise, it is asked about what is said: I judged happier than both him who has not yet been born.

On the contrary: 1. He has nothing good: therefore he is not happier.

2. Furthermore, everything that has in some way the character of happiness is desirable: but no one can desire nonexistence, as Augustine says: therefore he who has not been born is in no way happy.

III. Likewise it is asked whether anyone could, with a rightly ordered appetite, will this, namely to prefer non-existence to punishment.

That it is so: Matthew twenty-six: "It would have been better for him if he had not been born"; therefore, if every better thing is to be desired and preferred, so also is this.

That it does not, is evident, because it takes away all good: therefore nothing is worse than it.

I respond: it must be said that happiness exists in someone in two ways: truly and by estimation. Truly it exists in one alone, by estimation in many according to diverse opinions and considerations and its conditions found in diverse things. For since it is the most delightful good, Epicurus therefore places it in pleasures, and so with the others. Since therefore the happy person is troubled by no wickedness or misery, and this condition is found in the one who has not yet been born, by reason of this condition he says: I judged more fortunate, etc. I say also that by reason of that condition it is desired by many who are afflicted.

As to the question whether it ought to be desired or preferred: it must be said that punishment is twofold: temporal and eternal. Neither punishment takes away the whole, but nevertheless eternal punishment always takes away. Corruption into nonbeing, however, takes away the whole, but once. If therefore such corruption into nonbeing is compared to temporal punishment, it is simply worse. But if compared to eternal punishment, they are related as mutually exceeding and exceeded, because the one exceeds in deprivation, the other in continuation. And because the latter is infinite in duration, while the former is momentary, in this respect it exceeds and can more reasonably be fled and scorned.

IV. Likewise, the question is raised concerning what he says: I observed that all things are exposed to the envy of one's neighbor. I ask whether a man ought to cease from good work on account of his neighbor's envy.

That it is so, seems evident: because one's neighbor ought to be loved more than any present advantage: therefore if one's neighbor perishes through envy, that advantage ought to be set aside.

Against this: A good work provides a good example and stirs others to good; therefore, if what is good should be made known for praise, a man ought not to conceal it on account of a neighbor's envy.

I respond: that work is either of necessity, or of utility. If of necessity, it is by no means to be abandoned; if of utility, either an equally good result can come about in his absence as in his presence, or not: if so, a man ought to remove that work from his sight, to take away the occasion, when he reasonably conjectures that the other is envious; but if he cannot, he ought to do it, nor does he give the occasion, but the other takes it.

Art. 2. On the vanity of avarice, and first on its consideration.

I considered and found etc. Above he dealt with the vanity of malice; here he deals with the vanity of avarice: and first is set forth the consideration of avarice; second, because the miser does not want to have a companion, in detestation of this is set forth the commendation of the companionship of another, there: It is better therefore that two be together etc.

(Vers. 7. 8.). In the consideration, therefore, of the vanity of avarice, he says: Considering, I found another vanity under the sun, namely another, because the one spoken of above was of malice, but this one is of avarice. - Hence he adds: There is one alone, and he has not a second, neither son nor brother, and he ought to be content with little as one who is alone, because he does not even wish to have a companion: Sirach 11: "I have found rest for myself, and now I will eat of my goods alone"; and yet he entirely wastes away through avarice both in deed and desire and understanding. In deed: hence he says: And yet he does not cease to labor: Sirach 31: "The rich man has labored in gathering wealth." In desire: His eyes are not satisfied with riches: Sirach 14: "The insatiable eye of the covetous man shall not be satisfied with his portion of iniquity." In understanding: hence he adds: Nor does he reflect, saying: For whom do I labor and deprive my soul of good things? Psalm 38: "He stores up treasures and knows not for whom he shall gather them." From these things, therefore, he infers vanity: In this also there is vanity and a most grievous affliction: below in chapter 5: "Riches kept to the harm of their owner: for they perish in a most grievous affliction."

Second, on the commendation of the society of others.

(Vers. 9. 10. 11. 12.). It is better therefore that there be two, etc. Here is touched upon secondly the commendation of the companionship of another, and this in detestation of the anxiety of avarice: on account of which he says: It is better therefore that there be two, although the miser would choose the opposite: and he adds the reason: For they have the advantage of mutual companionship: and therefore, in Luke 10, the Lord "sent the disciples two by two before his face."

And he shows a threefold benefit: namely in lifting up, in preserving, in defending. Regarding lifting up he says: If one falls, he will be supported by the other: Deuteronomy twenty-two: "If you see your brother's donkey fallen on the road, you shall not despise it, but shall lift it up with him". The solitary man lacks this benefit: therefore he adds: Woe to him who is alone, because when he falls, he has no one to lift him up, therefore he is wretched: whence in the Psalm: "Look upon me and have mercy on me, for I am alone and poor".

As for the benefit in preserving, he adds: And if two sleep together, they shall warm one another, that is, they will be preserved from the cold; Ephesians 5: "No one ever hated his own flesh, but nourishes and cherishes it." The solitary person lacks this benefit; whence he adds: One, if he sleeps, how shall he be warmed? An example of this: in 3 Kings 1 it is said that David had grown old and could not get warm, and it is added that they sought Abishag the Shunammite, who slept in the king's bosom and warmed him; he could not be warmed by himself, since he was alone.

Regarding the advantage in defending, he adds: And if anyone prevails against one, two will resist him, and thus it avails for defense: 2 Kings 10: "If the Syrians prevail against me, you will be my help; but if the sons of Ammon prevail against you, I will help you." And indeed he makes this clear by an example: A threefold cord is not easily broken. Just as a cord joined to a cord is stronger, so also is a person: Proverbs 18: "A brother who is helped by a brother is like a strong city."

Spiritually, here is noted a threefold effect of charity, namely, mutual raising up, mutual consolation, and mutual defense. Regarding raising up, he says: If one falls: Galatians, last chapter: "If a man is overtaken in any fault, you who are spiritual, instruct such a one in the spirit of gentleness"; whence it is said in Sirach thirty-seven: "Establish within yourself a heart of good counsel."

On account of consolation he says: They will warm one another: First Thessalonians chapter five: " Comfort the fainthearted ": and in the same place, chapter two: " We became little ones in your midst, as if a nurse were cherishing her children ".

On account of defense he says: Two shall resist: Isaiah 50: "Let us stand together: who is my adversary?"

On account of this threefold effect of charity it is called a threefold cord: Hosea 11: "I will draw you with the cords of Adam, with the bonds of charity."

Questions.


I. The question is raised here concerning the solitary life, whether it should be preferred to companionship. That it should, is shown as follows:

1. Because it is said in the third chapter of Lamentations concerning the good youth: He will sit alone and be silent: therefore it is good to be alone.

2. Furthermore, Bernard says: "As often as I was among men, I returned less a man": therefore it is not good to be among men.

3. Likewise, he who is in society must necessarily be anxious about how to conform himself to society; but the Apostle says in 1 Corinthians 7 that it is good not to marry in order to avoid anxiety about pleasing a wife; therefore similarly it is good to flee all society.

Contra: 1. Genesis 2: It is not good for man to be alone: therefore solitude is blameworthy, and by contrast companionship is praiseworthy.

2. Likewise, Matthew 18: Where two or three are gathered in my name, there I am in the midst of them: he does not say: where one, but where two or three: therefore life in community is better than the solitary life.

3. Likewise, this is proved by this text, because companionship supports, nurtures, and defends, which are three goods of which the solitary are deprived.

I respond: it must be said that there is a threefold solitude: one through a lack of love, just as the miser does not wish to have a companion in riches, nor the envious person in good things, nor the proud person in great things: and this solitude is entirely evil. Another solitude is through a lack of consolation, as it is said in the Psalm: I am alone and poor, and this one is miserable. And there is another solitude through the quiet of contemplation , and this one is praiseworthy and honorable.

Similarly, there is a threefold kind of companionship: one, namely, that is disturbing and hindering, such as the companionship of the wicked; another needing support and consolation, such as the companionship of wives and the infirm; and another helping and advancing, such as the companionship of the perfect. The first is to be fled, the second to be tolerated, but the third to be sought: and this is found in religious life. When therefore Scripture commends companionship, it is understood of this kind; and this does not conflict with the solitude of contemplation, because Bernard says in his commentary on the Song of Songs: "A man can be entirely alone among many, if he flees curiosity."

And thus the objections are clear.

Art. 3. On the vanity of imprudence, which is first to be detested on account of present evil.

Better is a child, etc. He treated above the vanity of malice and avarice; here he treats the vanity of imprudence; and because the vanity of imprudence is blameworthy and detestable, therefore he inveighs against it in two ways: first, on account of the present evil; second, on account of the succeeding or consequent evil, there: I saw all the living.

(Vers. 13. 14.). The present evil that accompanies imprudence is that it subjects a rich and old king to a poor boy possessing prudence, and this indeed is a great vanity. And therefore he says: A poor and wise child is better than an old and foolish king, although the latter surpasses in power, surpasses in wealth, surpasses even in age; below in the tenth chapter: "More precious is wisdom and a little glory for a time than folly"; and Proverbs chapter twelve: "Better is a poor man who is sufficient for himself than a boastful man who lacks bread." And he explains what he said: foolish, because he has a deficiency of foresight regarding the future, which is one part of prudence; whence he says: Who does not know how to foresee for the future, namely the outcome of events; Deuteronomy chapter thirty-two: "Would that they were wise and understood and foresaw their last end."

That from prison and chains one sometimes comes forth to a kingdom: Wisdom 10: "The just man who was sold He did not abandon in his bonds, but delivered him from sinners, until He brought him the scepter of the kingdom." And another, born to a kingdom, is consumed by want, and this because of his pride: Sirach 10: "The Lord has destroyed the thrones of proud rulers, and has made the meek to sit in their place."

Second, it is to be detested on account of the evil that follows.

(Vers. 15. 16.). I saw all the living. Here is touched upon secondly the evil succeeding him on account of his imprudence, because he himself is cast down, and another is exalted: whence he says: I saw all the living, who walk under the sun, that is, many or innumerable, hyperbolically, with the second youth, that is, who follows him in the kingdom: whence he says: who shall rise up in his place. And so others follow the succeeding youth, despising the old man. The example is of David, who rises to the kingdom of Saul, whom the children of Israel followed, having abandoned the house of Saul, as is said in 2 Kings 5, that all came to David.

And the reason why they follow the youth, having abandoned the old man, is because the foolish old man benefited no one; and the following text indicates this: Infinite is the number of all the people who were before him, that is, before the coming of the youth they were under the foolish king and adhered to him. And those who shall come after, supply: those adhering to the old man, shall not rejoice in him, that is, in the foolish old man, because he benefited no one nor will he, because he does not know how; Sirach 20: "A fool will have no friend, nor will there be thanks for his good deeds; for he does not distribute with right judgment what ought to have been given." And from this he concludes vanity; whence he says: But this too is vanity and vexation of spirit, namely, another vanity from those previously mentioned, above in chapter one: "I saw all things that are done under the sun, and behold, all is vanity"; and the reason is added: "The perverse are corrected with difficulty, and the number of fools is infinite." - And thus the section on the vanity of fault is concluded.

Spiritually, this can also be explained concerning Christ. The poor and wise child is Christ, a child on account of his innocence: whence Isaiah forty-two according to another reading: "Behold, my child, my chosen one, my soul has been well pleased in him." Poor, because he was made needy for us: Second Corinthians eight: "You know, brothers, the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that for your sake he became poor, though he was rich." Wise, indeed Wisdom itself, First Corinthians one: "We proclaim Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God"; and below in chapter nine: "There was found in it a poor and wise man."

An old and foolish king is the devil, who is old; Job 40: "He is the beginning of the ways of God": whence "he has grown old in evil days." He is a king, because he reigns over the wicked: whence Job 41: "He is king over all the children of pride." Foolish, because he attempted what he could not accomplish: whence Isaiah 14 he said: "I will ascend into heaven, I will exalt my throne above the stars of God." Although he is most cunning at deceiving, he does not know how to foresee that someone might go forth from prison to a kingdom, so that Christ might go forth from the prison of the flesh and from the bonds of the passion to the kingdom of heavenly glory: Hebrews 1: "He sits at the right hand of the Majesty on high." Even the devil, born to a kingdom, that is, to receive a kingdom, is consumed by want, as Colossians 2 says: "Despoiling principalities and powers, he led them away boldly."

And what follows is an explanation of the preceding. And I saw all the living under the sun with the second youth, that is, Christ: John 12: "Behold, the whole world has gone after him." He rises up, because he cast the devil out of his kingdom: John 12: "Now is the judgment of the world, now the prince of this world shall be cast out." The infinite number of men who were before him, that is, before the coming of Christ: and the faithful, who shall come after, will not rejoice in him, that is, in the foolish king, that is, the devil, because they rejoiced only in Christ: whence the Apostle in the last chapter of Philippians: "Rejoice in the Lord always." One must rejoice in the Lord, not in the devil.

Part two, member II. On the threefold remedy against the threefold vanity of fault, that is, against the evil which follows upon malice.

Guard your foot. Above we treated the vanity of fault. Since that vanity is deadly and pestilential, therefore here the remedy against that vanity is treated. And because a threefold vanity preceded, namely of malice, avarice, and imprudence, therefore here he sets forth three remedies. And first he gives the remedy against malice; second against avarice, at: The covetous man shall not be satisfied; third against imprudence, at: What more does he have, etc. But since the disease of malice is in itself irremediable and can further corrupt others, therefore the remedy set forth here is not against malice, but against the corruption of malice, or the evil that follows in others from beholding the malice of another.

Art. 1. On the remedy against the vanity of malice, from which arises a threefold disorder.

Now from this arises a threefold disorder, namely in deed through the disobedience of transgression: in speech through the foolishness of words: in the heart through erroneous thought. The incautious observer of another's wickedness rushes headlong into all of these. For when someone considers the impunity of the wicked, he believes there is no providence: behold, erroneous thought: and through this he freely speaks foolish things, because he believes there is no one to reprove him: and further he transgresses the commandments, because he does not believe there is anyone to punish.

First, therefore, he gives a remedy against disobedience: second, against foolish speech, there: Do not rashly speak anything, etc.: third, against erroneous thought, there: If you see the oppressions of the needy, etc.

First, a remedy is given against disobedience.

(Vers. 17.). As a remedy against disobedience, an exhortation is introduced, and the reason for the exhortation is added. He exhorts to diligent guarding of one's feet, lest one transgress the divine commandments: therefore he says: Guard your foot, lest perhaps it turn aside from the right way: Hebrews 12: "Make straight steps with your feet, so that no one limping may go astray." Entering the house of God, that is, into the church, or into the service of God, into which a person ought to enter with fear: whence in the Psalm: "I will enter into your house, and I will worship toward your holy temple in your fear." Thus one ought to keep one's foot from disobedience and have it ready for obedience: for which reason he says: And draw near, that you may hear, that is, obey: draw near, because Deuteronomy 33: "Those who draw near to his feet will receive from his teaching"; hear, by obeying: Jeremiah 17: "Hear the word of the Lord, all you who enter through these gates. Thus says the Lord: guard your souls."

And the reason for the exhortation is added: For obedience is much better than the sacrifices of fools, who do not know what evil they do, who do not recognize their transgressions nor care to obey. Gregory: "Obedience is better than sacrifices, because in obedience one's own will is slain, in sacrifice the flesh of another ". The comparison is improper, because obedience is good, and therefore pleasing; whence Lamentations 3: "It is good for a man when he has borne the yoke from his youth"; but the sacrifices of fools displease. Proverbs 15: "The sacrifices of the wicked are abominable to the Lord"; whence Saul was rebuked, because out of love of offering he transgressed the divine command. 1 Kings 15: "Does the Lord desire holocausts or sacrifices, rather than that he be obeyed? For obedience is better than sacrifices, and to hearken is more than to offer the fat of rams".
