PROLOGUE OF THE COMMENTARY ON ECCLESIASTES


General Introduction.

1. Blessed is the man whose hope is the name of the Lord, and who has not looked upon vanities and false madnesses. Since, as blessed Dionysius holds, the good is the first object of desire, and the good is twofold, namely temporal and eternal, according to that passage of Second Corinthians chapter four: The things which are seen are temporal, but the things which are not seen are eternal, it generates in us a twofold love or desire, namely of charity and of lust, so that charity is the love returning to eternal things, and lust to earthly things. And because the agreement and difference of desires is followed by the agreement and disagreement of customs, and according to agreement in laws and customs the unity of a city consists, from this twofold love a twofold city arises, namely the city of the heavenly Jerusalem and the city of Babylon. The foundation of the first is ordered charity; of the second, perverted lust. The heavenly city therefore, because it is founded in charity, which raises up to eternal things, rests in eternal things and places its beatitude in them. But the city of Babylon, which through lust is pressed down to earthly things, places its beatitude in these earthly things.

And thus it follows from what has been said that just as there is a twofold good, so there is a twofold love, a twofold city, so also a twofold beatitude, true, namely, in eternal things, and false in temporal and perishable things: Psalm: They called the people blessed who have these things, etc.

And just as the love of charity and the love of lust so oppose each other that they cannot exist at the same time, so also these two beatitudes: Matthew 6: No one can serve two masters. Therefore, if anyone wishes to be blessed, it is necessary that he love future goods and despise present ones. Therefore the Holy Spirit through the Psalmist describes the blessed man by this twofold act: by the love of heavenly things, which make one blessed, when he says: Blessed is the man whose hope is the name of the Lord; and by the contempt of earthly things, which make one vain, when he says: And he has not looked upon vanities and false madnesses. Truly blessed is the man whose hope is the name of the Lord.

2. But the Lord alone makes blessed, nor is there any other name under heaven that makes blessed. For the Lord alone is blessed by essence: 1 Timothy 6: Keep the commandment without spot, blameless, until the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, whom in his own times the blessed and only mighty one, the King of kings and Lord of lords, will show. Therefore in him alone does blessedness and power exist by essence, but our blessedness is by participation: insofar as it is by participation, it has its origin from him who is so by essence: therefore we are blessed by participating in the fountain of blessedness, who is God. Therefore Revelation 19: Blessed are they who are called to the marriage supper of the Lamb. For first God through the betrothal communicates blessedness to the Lamb, and consequently to those who are called by the Lamb.

3. He alone also has affluence: because of Wisdom, which is God, it is said uniquely in Proverbs 3: She is more precious than all riches, and all things that are desired cannot be compared to her.

Length of days is in her right hand, and in her left

are riches and glory. But our blessedness is through the gathering of all good things: therefore it is in him alone, who has the abundance of all things. And so it is immediately inferred: She is a tree of life to those who lay hold of her, and whoever holds her fast is blessed: Wisdom 7: All good things came to me together with her etc.

4. He alone has the repose of immutability, because all things change, but He Himself does not change: James 1: With whom there is no change nor shadow of turning. But our blessedness consists in the tranquil possession of gathered goods, because without peace there can be no blessedness. Since

Therefore in nothing is there perfect tranquility except in God; we cannot therefore be at peace nor be made blessed except in God: whence Revelation fourteen: Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord. Henceforth now the Spirit says, that they may rest from their labors.

5. He alone also has the Immortality of eternity, as is said in the last chapter of First Timothy: Who alone has Immortality and dwells in inaccessible light. Our blessedness, however, consists in the unending duration of goods possessed. For a state that can come to an end is not blessed but is subject to misery. Since, therefore, no one can have endlessness except from the fountain of Immortality, in Him alone is one made blessed. Therefore it is said in the Psalm: Blessed are those who dwell in Your house, O Lord: they will praise You forever and ever, because their joy will be everlasting: Isaiah thirty-five: They shall come to Zion with praise, and everlasting joy shall be upon their heads: they shall obtain joy and gladness.

Blessed therefore is the man, whose hope is the name of the Lord.

6. But not blessed, Nay truly vain is he who hopes in the world: for he looks upon vanities and

false madnesses: Therefore he fades away, according to that passage in Jeremiah chapter two: Your fathers went far from me and walked after vanity, and became vain: after vanity, that is, after the world.

For the world with its things is vain, because it does not provide support to the one leaning on it: whence Isaiah 30: Egypt will help in vain and to no purpose, because it does not provide support to the one leaning on it. For it is a reed staff, upon which if a man leans, it will pierce both hands: because the world and all worldly things render all who trust in them weak and infirm and unfit for every good work: Therefore it is said to pierce the hands, therefore the Prophet, despairing of the help of worldly things, asks from the Lord: Give us help from tribulation, for vain is the salvation of man.

Vain also, because it does not provide fullness to the one who possesses it: whence it is said in Ecclesiastes five: The covetous man shall not be satisfied with money. And the reason for this is that all worldly things are vain, and vain things do not satisfy: whence it is said in Ecclesiasticus thirty-four: Vain hope and falsehood to the senseless man, like one who grasps at a shadow and pursues the wind: so is he who attends to lying visions: whence just as wind does not satisfy the stomach, nor does a shadow fill it, so neither do temporal things, which are a shadow of eternal things, for which the soul was created.

8. Vain also, because it does not give rest to the one who labors, and therefore whoever labors for worldly things labors in vain: Ecclesiastes 2: What does it profit a man from all his labor, with which he labors under the sun? All his days are full of toils and sorrows, nor does his mind rest at night: and is this not vanity? because he labors in vain, because he does not rest. This world cannot give rest, because it itself does not endure, but rather always revolves: therefore those who cling to it must necessarily go around in circles. Therefore it is said in the Psalm: In a circle the wicked walk.

Vain also, because it does not yield fruit to the one who loves it: whence it is said in Ecclesiastes 5: He who loves riches shall not reap fruit from them: and this therefore is vanity. He does not reap fruit, but rather loss, because by gaining the world he loses God and loses himself: and according to what is said in Matthew 16: What does it profit a man, if he gains the whole world, yet suffers the loss of his own soul?

10. Man, by gaining the world, suffers the loss of his soul.

For the world, by displaying loftiness raises one up into pride, and through this causes man to vanish, according to that passage in Job eleven: A vain man is raised up into pride, and thinks himself free like the foal of a wild donkey. For when he stretches himself toward vain things, he is raised up into vain pride, according to what is said in Job thirty: You lifted me up and, as it were, setting me upon the wind, you dashed me down violently.

The world also, by holding forth sweetness, inflames to lust, just as wine by its sweetness draws to gluttony and through this to lust: whence 3 Esdras 3: Wine, which prevails over all who drink it, seduces the mind. Likewise of king and orphan

makes the mind vain, and this by inflaming to lust, according to that passage in Ephesians 5: Do not be drunk with wine, in which is lust. To this vanity the world drew Solomon; Ecclesiastes 2: I said: I will go and abound in delights and enjoy good things;

and I saw that this also was vanity. The world also, by holding out sufficiency, draws one to covetousness, and through this makes the heart of man vain, according to that passage in Proverbs twenty-one: He who gathers treasures by a lying tongue is vain, he is senseless. Not only does avarice make a man vain, but indeed even without heart: for it is said in Ecclesiasticus ten concerning the miser, who cast away his inner parts: because, according to what the Truth says in Matthew six, where your treasure is, there is your heart also: but the treasure is outside.

therefore also the heart is outside.

The world also, by holding out wisdom, entices to curiosity, and through this makes one vain: whence the Apostle says in First Corinthians three, drawing from the Psalm: The Lord knows the thoughts of the wise, that they are vain; because, according to that passage of Jeremiah ten, every man is made foolish by his knowledge. For this is human wisdom, which does not know useful things, but changeable things: and therefore the Lord made it foolish, as is said in First Corinthians one: God has made foolish the wisdom of this world. Foolish wisdom is vain, because it is erroneous: whence it is said in Ecclesiasticus thirty-four: Divination of error and lying auguries and dreams of evildoers are vanity.

11. From this fourfold interior vanity of the heart

in four ways man vanishes outwardly.

For first, the vanity of pride renders unfruitful, According to that passage of Ecclesiasticus six: Do not exalt yourself in your thought like a bull, lest perhaps your strength be crushed, and you be left like a dry tree in the wilderness, and thus vain as regards the fruit of good work: concerning which Jeremiah ten says: Their works are vain and worthy of ridicule; in the day of their visitation they shall perish.

Vanity of avarice renders one busy, and through this subject to many labors: Ecclesiastes 4: Considering, I found yet another vanity under the sun: there is one alone, and he has not a second, and yet he does not cease to labor, nor are his eyes satisfied with riches. For riches are foreign gods, about which Jeremiah 16 says: You shall serve foreign gods, who will give you no rest day or night.

The vanity of luxury renders a person adorned, because the luxurious are accustomed to adorning themselves outwardly: whence in their person it is said in Wisdom chapter two: Let us crown ourselves with roses, before they wither: and this indeed is vanity, because it is said in the last chapter of Proverbs: Grace is deceitful, and beauty is vain: Therefore vain, because all the glory and beauty of the flesh is like the flower of grass.

The vanity of worldly wisdom renders one a vain talker. For while the heart wanders, the mouth pronounces vain things: 1 Timothy 1: The end of the commandment is charity from a pure heart and a good conscience and faith unfeigned, from which some having strayed have turned aside to vain talk, understanding neither what they say nor the things about which they make assertions. And because by worldly wisdom they are corrupted and deceived, they wish to lead others astray through vain words: whence Titus 1: There are many

disobedient, vain talkers and deceivers, especially those who are of the circumcision, who must be rebuked.

Thus therefore from the vanity of exterior things arises the vanity of hearts, and from this arises the vanity of exterior works.

12. According to this, therefore, it is concluded that there is a threefold vanity, namely of mutability, of iniquity and of punishment, and as Hugh of Saint Victor says, "the first is natural and fitting, the second is culpable, because perverse, the third is penal and miserable"; and one has its origin from another, as has been seen. Therefore whoever wishes to withdraw from the vanity of misery let him abandon the vanity of guilt, and whoever wishes to abandon this, let him not look to the vanity of mutable nature, but let him hope in and cling to the divine, and so he will be blessed. Blessed therefore is the man whose hope is the name of the Lord.

13. Since, therefore, it belongs to the wise man to teach the way of coming to beatitude, this was the chief and worthwhile endeavor for the wise Solomon: but because, as is clear from what has been said, for acquiring beatitude it is necessary that eternal things be loved and present things be despised, and furthermore to know how to conduct oneself properly in the midst of a crooked generation

Therefore he produced three books, namely Proverbs, in which he teaches his son to live wisely in this world: Ecclesiastes, in which he teaches to despise present things: and the Song of Songs, in which he teaches to love heavenly things, especially the Bridegroom Himself.

On the fourfold cause of this book.

14. Thus therefore it is clear what the purpose of the book of Ecclesiastes is. And since "the end imposes necessity on those things which are ordered to the end," since the end is contempt of present things, he treats of them not in the respect by which they attract, but in the respect by which they are despised: they attract under the appearance of good, but are despised under the appearance of vanity. Therefore it follows from this that the subject matter of this book is the vanities of present things, or the things themselves insofar as they are vain, to put it more accurately, because he proves vanity as a kind of property in things, first proposing, second proving, third concluding. Whence he proceeds by persuading and proving that threefold vanity which was spoken of above, namely of nature, fault, and misery.

And from this the method of treatment or form is clear. For it has a singular method of treatment among other books: for it proceeds as a preacher, setting forth the opinions of various persons, now of the wise man, now of the fool, so that from the opinions of many, one truth may shine forth in the minds of the hearers. - From the foregoing, it is concluded who the efficient cause must have been. For in this book, pleasures, riches, honors, and curiosities are taught to be despised as vanities. And because no one is believed concerning the contempt of such things unless he has experienced them

for a poor man who has not had riches is not believed concerning contempt for them, because he has not experienced them, and

Therefore he does not recognize

it was necessary that there be someone who had experience of all these things, namely one who was powerful, wealthy, given to pleasure, and inquisitive or wise. We have neither read of nor heard of any other who possessed these things in such great excellence as Solomon did:

Therefore he himself was, above all others, the more fitting author of such a book.

It is therefore clear what the material, formal, efficient, and final cause of this book is.
