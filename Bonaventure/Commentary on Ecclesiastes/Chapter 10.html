The right hand gives to understand eternal goods, the left hand temporal goods: whence

Proverbs 3: "Length of days is in her right hand, in her left hand are riches and glory." He therefore means to say that the wise think about and desire eternal goods, while the foolish desire temporal things: Song of Songs 2: "His left hand is under my head, and his right hand shall embrace me." The fool therefore thinks vain things and does not know how to consider things with discernment in any way, neither in himself nor in others, which is a greater absurdity. Therefore he adds: But also the fool walking on the way, namely on that way which seems good to him: Proverbs 12: "The way of a fool is right in his own eyes." Since he himself is foolish, that is, since he walks foolishly, because in darkness; above in chapter 2: "The fool walks in darkness." He considers everyone else fools, whence Proverbs 26: "The sluggard is wiser in his own eyes than seven men who give wise answers."

Question.


Here arises a question concerning what he says: He who sins in one thing, etc.

1. For if God always punishes less than what is deserved, then he who sins in one thing ought not to be punished except in one thing: therefore he ought not to lose many things, but one.

2. Likewise, from this it seems that there is a connection among the vices, just as among the virtues; because if vice is the privation of good, then if by one sin many goods are taken away, then by one sin a person incurs many vices: but this is only through connection: therefore there is a connection among the vices.

3. Likewise, that there is a connection in vices with respect to all things is seen; it is said in James 2: Whoever has sinned in one point is made guilty of all: therefore it seems that whoever commits one vice is as guilty as if he committed all of them.

But to the contrary: 1. Because some vices are contrary to each other: therefore if there were a connection among the vices, then two contraries would exist simultaneously in the same subject, which is impious to say.

2. Likewise, vices are not from God, but acquired through our own action; but it is certain that temperance and justice, when they are habitual, are not acquired in us by one and the same act: therefore similarly also with vices.

I respond: it must be said that one can speak of sin in terms of deformity and in terms of obligation to punishment. If in terms of deformity, it indeed regards three things, namely the privation of gratuitous good, the privation of natural good or natural aptitude, and furthermore the inclination to disordered act, which is called a certain disposition. In terms of the first good, there is indeed a connection among the vices, because whoever sins in one sin loses grace and all the virtues, insofar as they are gratuitous. But in terms of the privation of aptitude there is no connection, but rather a distinction among them, because each vice deprives its own proper aptitude. In terms of the third, namely the inclination to act, there is neither complete distinction nor complete connection, but in some cases one inclines to another, and in some cases it inclines occasionally, as the sin of gluttony to the sin of lust, and similarly in spiritual matters, pride to envy.

There is also a way to speak of sins with regard to the obligation of punishment, and thus they bind to a twofold penalty, namely the deprivation of vision and the severity of fire. With regard to the first there is a connection, because whoever sins in one sin loses the glory owed to all the virtues. But with regard to the second there is not a connection, but a distinction, because to each corresponds its own proper severity.

This saying therefore of Ecclesiastes and of blessed James is understood with regard to the loss of grace and glory.

1. To the objection, therefore, that one is punished beyond what is deserved: I say that this is false, because whoever sins in one act sins against the grace of all the virtues, and therefore deserves to lose everything.

If the spirit of him who has power. Above was set forth the contempt of the wise man and conversely his urgent commendation: here is subjoined thirdly the promotion of fools. And because the authority of fools is burdensome, therefore the armor of patience is set forth first, and the disordered authority of fools is subjoined, there: There is an evil which I have seen, etc.

(Vers. 4.). Therefore the armor of patience is set forth first, for the sake of obtaining which he says: If the spirit of him who has power, that is, the fury of a superior: for spirit is called fury in Isaiah 2: "Cease from man, whose breath is in his nostrils"; 3 Kings 19: "A great and strong wind, overthrowing mountains and crushing," etc. Shall ascend upon you, to oppress, namely: Jeremiah 4: "Behold, he shall come up as clouds, and his chariots as a tempest: woe to us, for we are laid waste." Do not leave your place, that is, the place of patience and constancy, in which the soul finds rest, according to that passage in Luke 21: "In your patience you shall possess your souls"; 1 Peter 2: "Servants, be subject in all fear to your masters, not only to the good, but also to the froward: for this is grace, if for conscience toward God a man endures," etc. And the reason of its usefulness is given: Because healing will make the greatest sins to cease. Here healing is called the affliction of scourges, by which a man is healed: Wisdom 6: "The care of discipline is love, and love is the keeping of the laws." The causal predication is on both sides, because if through many scourges one arrives at love, and through love the law is kept, therefore it makes sins to cease.

Another explanation: If the spirit of one having power, that is, if the spirit of ambition, that is, the malice of the ambitious, ascends over you, that is, if it comes into your mind that you wish to be a prelate: do not leave your place, namely the place of subjection and service, concerning which Luke fourteen says: "When you are invited to a wedding, sit down in the lowest place," and Sirach eleven: "Trust in God, and remain in your place." And the reason is given: Because healing will make the greatest sins cease. The translation of Symmachus has: Because modesty: whence First Corinthians nine: "I chastise my body and bring it into subjection, lest perhaps, having preached to others, I myself should be found reprobate."

Otherwise it is explained concerning the devil: If the spirit of one having power, that is, of the devil: Job, the second to last chapter: "There is no power upon earth that can be compared to him"; ascends over you, and this by tempting: Luke 11: "When an unclean spirit has gone out of a man, he walks through waterless places, seeking rest, and does not find it." Do not abandon your place, the place namely of penance: Ephesians 4: "Do not give place to the devil." Because healing will make the greatest sins cease: Origen's text has: salvation, which indeed is through the medicine of penance: Sirach 11: "The evil of one hour brings forgetfulness of the greatest pleasure," that is, a brief penance for a great sin.

(Vers. 5.). There is an evil which I have seen etc. Here the second point is touched upon, namely the disordered promotion of fools, both on account of a defect of knowledge and on account of a defect of life, because the foolish and wicked are placed in charge, while the wise and those rich in good conduct are cast down. On account of which he says: There is an evil which I have seen under the sun, that is, in the present state of mankind, as it were an error proceeding, not an error according to truth, but apparently an error, yet nevertheless according to just judgment, from the face of the prince, namely of him from whom is all power, who by just judgment sometimes gives wicked prelates: Hosea 13: "I will give you a king in my fury, and will take him away in my indignation."

(Vers. 6.). And he expresses what that evil is with regard to the defect of knowledge: The fool set in high dignity: set, he says well, because there is nothing there but a false placement: Psalm: "You set them up, you cast them down, when they were lifted up." Or: set like a useless stone: whence in Zechariah 11 it is said to the foolish prelate: "O shepherd and idol! who abandons the flock, the sword upon his arm and upon his right eye: his arm shall be utterly withered, and his right eye shall be utterly darkened": and therefore Proverbs 26: "As he who casts a stone into the heap of Mercury, so is he who gives honor to a fool." On the contrary: The rich sitting below: rich, namely in good morals and knowledge and grace, of whom 1 Corinthians 1: "In all things you have been made rich in him, so that nothing is lacking to you in any grace"; and Isaiah 33: "The riches of salvation are wisdom and knowledge."

(Vers. 7.). As for the defect of life, he says: I have seen servants on horses: by servants he means sinners, about whom John 8 says: "He who commits sin is a servant of sin"; on horses, he says in the plural to express pomp: Deuteronomy 17: "When he has been established, he shall not multiply horses for himself." On the contrary: Princes walking like servants, namely those princes who rule themselves through a good life: Seneca says: "If you govern yourself well, you will be a prince": he saw these as servants, but in the future it will be the opposite: Luke 14: "Everyone who humbles himself will be exalted, and he who exalts himself will be humbled."

QUESTION.


It is asked here whether it is just that the wicked should rule over the good. That this is so, it seems:

1. Because it is said in Romans 13, There is no power except from God: therefore evil princes do not have power except from God.

2. Likewise, John 19: You would have Christ said to Pilate, no power against me unless it had been given to you from above. And Augustine explains from above: that is, from God: therefore if Pilate was evil and used power badly, then that the wicked are in authority is from God.

3. Likewise, 1 Peter 2: Be subject not only to the good and gentle, but also to the harsh, and he gives the reason: for the sake of conscience; therefore if it is just to be subject to them, since everything just is from God, this also will be from him.

4. Likewise, Job thirty-four: Who makes a hypocrite to reign because of the sins of the people, etc.: therefore etc. On the contrary: 1. Hosea eight: They have reigned, but not from me; princes have arisen, and I did not know: therefore it seems that the authority of the wicked is not from God. 2. Likewise, servitude is a punishment inflicted for sin, therefore it is rightly and justly inflicted on no one except a sinner: therefore by opposites, neither is dominion justly given except to the just. Therefore if dominion is given to a sinner, and servitude to the just, there is a defect of order and justice there: therefore it is not from God.

3. Furthermore, if the power of the wicked is just and from God, because nothing from God is anything but just, and since no one ought to take away another's right: therefore no one ought to labor for the deposition of a wicked prelate; therefore whoever labors against this sins.

I respond: it must be said that an evil or unjust prelate falls under a threefold distinction. One kind is unjust in himself, yet enters office justly and exercises it justly: and such a one can justly hold authority, nor does he sin in this, and it is just to be subject to such a one. There is also another, who enters and exercises office unjustly: and such a one cannot justly hold authority, nor ought a person be subject to such a one. There is also another, who is unjust, but nevertheless enters office justly, yet exercises it unjustly, because he afflicts the good and promotes the wicked: it is just to be subject to such a one in those matters that pertain to justice; but in those matters that are against God, a person ought not be subject. And "because he deserves to lose his privilege who abuses the power granted to him," even if he entered office justly, nevertheless it is just that he be removed.

As for the objection raised regarding the first part, whether it is just for the wicked to rule: it must be said that for the wicked who govern well to rule is not an evil for the commonwealth; but for those who govern badly, it is just that they rule, not by their own justice, but by divine justice, because he makes him to reign on account of the sins of the people. - And thus the response to the first objections is clear, that the power of wicked men is from God.

I. To the first objection raised to the contrary, I say that he means with regard to those who enter illegitimately and by their own authority, not by divine authority.

2. As to what he objects concerning order, I say that the punishment of sin remains even without sin for the sake of discipline: so also I say concerning servitude among the good.

3. To the objection: if from God, then it is just for that man to have such power: it must be said that in comparison to his own merits it is not just, but rather unjust; in comparison to the merits of the wicked subjects it is just. And because for different reasons it is lawful for us to will contrary things where the divine will is not clear, and we do not know which of these things will be more pleasing to God in the future, a man can justly labor to have an unjust prelate removed.

Moreover, he had such power from God, but the execution from himself: he had power justly, but he exercises it unjustly: and therefore as long as he has power from God, he is to be obeyed: but because he exercises it unjustly, one must work so that he does not have it. Thus this point is clear.
Second, a remedy is given against the vanity of security.

He who digs a pit etc. He set forth above the vanity of security: here secondly he intends to give a remedy against that vanity: and because the security arose from a defect of just retribution and of the governance of providence, which appeared to exist: therefore there are here two parts. First he gives a remedy against the defect of the order of providence: second, against the defect of just retribution, below in chapter eleven: Cast your bread etc.

First, a remedy is given against the security arising from a defect in the order of providence.

The disorder of governance comes from a threefold cause: either because an evil man is placed in charge, or because a foolish man,

or because he is useless and negligent. First, therefore, he gives a remedy against a bad prelate; second, against a foolish prelate, there: The words of a wise man's mouth are grace; third, against a carnal and negligent prelate, there: Woe to you, O land, whose king is a child.

First, as regards the failing of governance, when an evil prelate is placed in charge.

Therefore, as regards the first point, he proceeds as follows. First he says that a good prelate should not be removed: second, that an evil one should not be tolerated or promoted: third, he says that the one promoted should be corrected: fourth, that one should not secretly detract from him.

(Verse 8.). First, therefore, he says that a good prelate should not be removed, neither by fraud nor by violence. Not by fraud; whence he threatens: He who digs a pit, namely by preparing a snare for a good prelate, shall fall into it: Psalm: "He opened a pit and dug it out, and he fell into the pit which he made." Nor should he be removed by force; whence he threatens the one doing the contrary: He who breaks down the hedge, that is, the good prelate, who guards his subjects like a hedge: Sirach thirty-six: "Where there is no hedge, the possession shall be plundered." A serpent shall bite him, that is, the devil with his demons, who are biting serpents: Jeremiah eight: "Behold, I will send among you the worst serpents, against which there is no charm, and they shall bite you."

(Verse 9). He who moves stones etc. Here he shows secondly that a harsh and useless prelate should not be promoted, and he threatens those who promote a harsh one; whence he says: He who moves stones, that is, promotes the harsh and wicked to greater dignity; will be afflicted by them, that is, through them, or on account of them: Proverbs 26: "As he who casts a stone into the heap of Mercury, so is he who gives honor to a fool"; and later: "He who rolls a stone, it will return upon him"; and Sirach 27: "He who throws a stone on high, it will fall upon his head." Nor should the vain and useless be promoted; therefore he says: He who splits wood, another translation: He who hews: he hews who wishes to place a useless prelate as support for the building. A useless prelate is compared to wood, because just as unfruitful wood when cut is given over to the fire, so also the prelate: Matthew 7: "Every tree that does not bear good fruit will be cut down and cast into the fire." He will be wounded by them, because namely the conscience is wounded: Sirach 27: "The deceitful stroke of the deceitful will open wounds." Concerning these wounds, Isaiah 1: "Wound and bruise and swelling sore — it is not bound up nor soothed with oil."

(Verse 10). If the iron be blunted etc. Here he adds thirdly that the prelate who has in some way turned aside to evil is to be corrected, because he can still be of worth, although much labor is required. Therefore he says: If the iron be blunted, that is, if the sharpness of goodness and wisdom has been changed through fault in the prelate, and this not as before, supply "sharp," but has been dulled, through fault, with much labor, namely of correction, it shall be sharpened, that is, it shall be restored to its former uprightness: Proverbs 27: "Iron is sharpened by iron, and a man sharpens the face of his friend." And through this comparison of bodily things he draws a conclusion in spiritual matters: And after diligence, that is, the labor of diligence, wisdom shall follow, just as after the sharpening of iron there follows the straightness and fineness of the edge for cutting: Proverbs 15: "The ear that hears the reproofs of life shall abide in the midst of the wise"; and Proverbs 2: "If you seek her as money and dig for her as for a treasure, then you shall understand the fear of the Lord and find knowledge."

(Verse 11.). If it bites etc. Here he shows fourthly that such a prelate should not be secretly detracted from, and he shows the magnitude of the sin of detraction through a comparison in bodily things. Therefore he says: If a serpent bites in silence, which is proper to the serpent, because it secretly infuses venom, he who secretly detracts has nothing less than it, because, just as that is fraudulent, so also is this, because this is to curse the deaf: Leviticus 19: "You shall not curse the deaf, nor place a stumbling block before the blind." Just as that is deadly, so also is this: Galatians 5: "If you bite and devour one another, take heed lest you be consumed by one another": Proverbs 26: "The words of a whisperer are as though simple, and they reach even to the innermost parts of the belly." Just as of old they were delivered by looking upon Christ, according to what is said in Wisdom 16: "He who turned was not healed by what he saw, but by you, Lord, the Savior of all": so also now. Whence he himself said, John 15: "If they have kept my word, they will keep yours also."

QUESTION.


It is asked whether secretly speaking evil of someone is detraction and sin, when the speaker knows it to be true. That it is not, it seems:

1. Because, just as a good person is worthy of praise, so a bad person is worthy of blame: therefore just as it is just to praise the good, so it is just to censure the bad: therefore just as one who praises the good does not sin, neither does one who speaks ill of the bad.

2. Likewise, just as one ought to rejoice in the good, so one ought to sympathize with the bad: therefore, just as someone can recount to another the good of someone else while rejoicing together without sin, so also the bad while sympathizing: therefore, one who speaks ill of another in secret, if he sympathizes, does not sin.

3. Furthermore, the Lord says in Matthew eighteen that when a neighbor sins, he is to be corrected before one or before two; and if he does not listen, it is to be told to the Church: therefore at least concerning the incorrigible person, it seems that a man may speak evil of him to anyone.

On the contrary: 1. What you do not wish to be done to you, do not do to another; therefore what you do not wish to be said about you, you ought not to say about another; but you ought not to wish that someone would speak evil about you in your absence, because it would profit you nothing; therefore, etc.

2. Likewise, he who speaks evil of another takes away his good name: therefore since a good name is better than many treasures, therefore since a man cannot take away another's treasures without sin, similarly etc.

3. Likewise, it is said in Leviticus 19: You shall not curse the deaf; but he who speaks ill behind someone's back curses the deaf; therefore he acts against that precept.

4. Furthermore, sin committed in public causes scandal: therefore whoever publicizes some sin which is not public causes scandal: but he who speaks ill of another makes known a sin which is hidden: therefore he scandalizes the one with whom he speaks.

I respond: it must be said that whoever speaks evil of another either speaks compelled by necessity, or led by usefulness, or driven by the desire to detract. One is compelled by necessity when one is bound by a precept either human or divine: human, when one speaks to him who has the power to command and commands him to speak; divine, when conscience dictates to a person that he is bound to accuse his neighbor so that he may be corrected, according to that precept of the Lord concerning fraternal correction.

Then he is motivated by usefulness, when he relates the evil of someone to a person who can help and not harm, and to whom it is beneficial to hear that evil and not harmful, as when someone relates it to a person who loves and who sympathizes and has the will to correct that person.

But when necessity is not considered, nor is utility considered, even if it be true, one is not excused from the lust of detraction, indeed one sins with the sin of detraction, even if one speaks the truth.

The arguments, therefore, which prove that it is a sin to speak evil of another in their absence, are to be conceded, unless necessity or usefulness intervenes: for then it is clear that they do not hold.

1, As to what is objected to the contrary, that it is just to criticize, etc., it must be said that although they are worthy of criticism, man nevertheless ought not to criticize. Nor is the case similar with praise, because a good reputation stirs one to good, but a bad one to evil, and therefore evil should not be made public, as good should be.

Furthermore, it is not mine to judge.

2. To the objection concerning compassion, it must be said that true compassion is that in which the one showing compassion is wounded, not that in which the wretched person is harmed. And because this person, in this denunciation, harms the sinner in his reputation, therefore he ought not to accuse.

3. To what is objected concerning the Lord's commandment, it must be said that the Lord means that those who can and wish to be of help should be called; and by the Church he does not mean the multitude, but means the one who can be of help and ought to correct, and to such a person it should be told.

Second, as to the defect of governance, when a foolish prelate is placed in charge.

The words of a wise man's mouth etc. He gave above a remedy against the malice of a prelate: here he gives a remedy against the foolishness of a prelate, showing that a foolish prelate ought not to be promoted, but rather is worthy of ruin: first, because of a defect of discreet speech; second, because of a defect of prudent consideration; third, because of a defect of fruitful action.

(Vers. 12.13. 14.). Because of the lack of discreet speech, the fool casts himself down, just as by antithesis the wise man is exalted. Therefore he says: The words of the mouth of the wise man are grace, because through them, on account of discretion, he acquires grace: Sirach 20: "The wise man makes himself lovable by his words": but on the contrary: The lips of the fool will cast him down, that is, the words which proceed from the lips of the fool: Proverbs 18: "The mouth of the fool is his destruction, and his lips are the ruin of his soul".

And the reason for the rashness is lack of discernment, because he indiscreetly inclines: hence he says: The beginning of his words is foolishness. And the reason for this is that he does not consider, whence Proverbs twenty-nine: "Have you seen a man swift to speak? Foolishness is more to be hoped for than his correction." He also indiscreetly concludes: hence he says: And the end of his mouth is the worst error, because foolishness in the end leads to an erroneous judgment; Proverbs fourteen: "The imprudence of fools errs"; because, as was said above in the second chapter, "the eyes of the wise man are in his head, the fool walks in darkness." Furthermore, he perseveres in foolishness: therefore he adds: The fool multiplies words, thus by multiplying foolish words he becomes obstinate in malice: against which in the Psalm: "Do not incline my heart to words of malice," etc.; and this is useless and vain, whence it is said in Proverbs nineteen: "But he who pursues words will have nothing"; and Proverbs fourteen: "Where words are most plentiful, there is frequently want."

(Vers. 14.). Man knows not etc. Here he shows secondly the defect of provident consideration: because, since he is ignorant of the past, he cannot foresee the future. Therefore he says: Man knows not what was before him, as one foolish and thoughtless: James 1: "He beheld himself and went away and immediately forgot what manner of man he was." And what shall be after him, who can tell him? As if to say: no one: above in chapter eight: "He who is ignorant of the past can know the future by no messenger," and this indeed is very harmful: whence Deuteronomy 32: "O that they were wise and understood and would foresee their last end."

(Verse 15.). The labor of fools etc. Here he shows thirdly that in the fool there is a deficiency of fruitful work, because in his labor he is greatly afflicted and profits little. Therefore he says: The labor of fools will afflict them, because they do not labor in good things: Jeremiah 9: "They have labored to act wickedly": whence Wisdom 5: "We have wearied ourselves in the way of iniquity and destruction, and we have walked through difficult paths; but the way of the Lord we have not known." And he gives the reason for this: Who do not know how to go to the city, that is, they do not know the right way to go to that city, of which Isaiah 26: "Sion, the city of our strength; the Savior shall be set therein as a wall and a bulwark." Fools do not know how to go to this city: whence above in chapter 6: "What has the wise man more than the fool? And what has the poor man, except to go there where life is?"

Spiritually, here by the wise man we understand Christ, by the foolish man the Antichrist. The words of the mouth

of the wise man are grace, because the words of Christ are gracious: the Psalm: "Grace is poured forth in thy lips, therefore God hath blessed thee forever"; and Luke chapter two: "All who heard him were astonished at his understanding and his answers"; and others said in John chapter seven: "Never did a man speak like this." On the contrary, the fool is the antichrist, of whom it is said in the Psalm: "The fool hath said in his heart: There is no God." His lips will cast him down, because on account of the wickedness of his teaching he will be plunged into the depths, Job chapter eighteen: "The steps of his strength shall be straitened, and his own counsel shall cast him down." For his words will be words of blasphemy and pride: therefore foolish and erroneous. Whence it is said in Daniel chapter eleven: "He shall speak great things against the God of gods, and shall prosper, until the wrath be accomplished"; and Second Thessalonians chapter two: "The man of sin shall be revealed, the son of perdition, who opposeth and is exalted above all that is called God," etc.

This fool will not only be indiscreet in speaking, but also improvident, because he will not consider the ruin that is about to follow him, according to what the Apostle says in 2 Thessalonians 2: "Whom the Lord Jesus will slay with the breath of his mouth." Fools will follow this foolish one and will be afflicted: Who do not know how to go to the city, through true faith and love: 2 Thessalonians 2: "God will send them the working of error, who did not believe the truth, but consented to iniquity"; because they did not receive the love of the truth: therefore, not knowing, they will be deceived: whence in the Psalm: "They did not know nor understand, therefore they walk in darkness."

Third, regarding the defect of governance, when a useless and lax prelate is promoted.

Woe to you, O land, etc. He gave a remedy against the disorder that arises from a bad and foolish ruler; here thirdly he gives a remedy against the disorder that arises from a useless and negligent ruler. He shows, therefore, that such a lazy and carnal person is not to be placed in authority nor even to be tolerated. And this part has two sections: because first he denounces him, showing him unworthy of authority; second he says that one should not secretly detract from him, at the words: In your thought, etc.

He shows, therefore, that the carnal and lazy man is unworthy of prelacy: first, on account of the disorder of gluttony; second, on account of the weakness or idleness of sloth; third, on account of the unfitness of merriment; fourth, on account of the subversion of justice, through love of money. (Verses 16–17.) On account of the disorder of gluttony, the carnal and lax prelate is unworthy of prelacy, and woe to those who set him over themselves. Therefore he says: Woe to you, O land, whose king is a child, that is, a follower of passions; for children are followers of passions; Isaiah, second to last chapter: "The child of a hundred years shall die." Therefore woe, because it is an occasion of eternal damnation and a sign of divine indignation; Isaiah 3: "I will give children to be their princes, and the effeminate shall rule over them." And because princes follow the leader, therefore he says: And whose princes eat in the morning, quickly seeking pleasurable things; and therefore they are always foolish, because it is said in Proverbs 20: "Wine is a luxurious thing, and drunkenness is riotous; whoever is delighted by these things will not be wise."

And because "opposites placed next to each other shine forth more clearly," he commends the contrary, saying in detestation of it: Blessed is the land whose king is noble, namely with nobility of character, because nobility alone is what adorns the soul with virtuous conduct; concerning which, the last chapter of Proverbs says: "Her husband is noble in the gates, when he sits among the senators of the land." And because princes follow their leader, he therefore adds: And whose princes eat at their proper time, that is, at the due hour after business has been completed, and this for refreshment, not for luxury. The Interlinear Gloss: "They eat only to live; they do not live to eat," like those of whom 2 Peter 2 says: "Stains and blemishes, abounding in delights, reveling in their feasts." (Verse 18.) Through slothfulness it shall be brought low, etc. Here secondly it is shown that the lax prelate is unworthy on account of the idleness of sloth, which results in the destruction of the house that was built. For which reason he says: Through slothfulness the framework shall be brought low. Framework refers to the joining of timbers to cover a house. This is brought low through slothfulness, because, while its repair is neglected, it collapses; so also the spiritual building is destroyed through sloth. Proverbs 18: "He who is slack and negligent in his work is brother to him who squanders his own works." And because where there is no fear of the Lord that stings, sloth reigns, therefore it is said in Sirach 27: "If you do not hold yourself steadfastly in the fear of the Lord, your house will quickly be overthrown." And through the weakness of hands the house shall leak. He says the same thing, but explains it more fully:

the house leaks, when it is rainy and does not protect from the showers, in which it is not good nor safe to be: whence Proverbs twenty-seven: " A dripping roof on a cold day and a quarrelsome woman are compared." This house leaks when the hands are weak and slack: therefore Hebrews twelve: " Lift up your slack hands and loosened knees, and make straight steps with your feet."

(Vers. 19.). In laughter they make bread etc. Here thirdly he shows the unworthiness of the lazy and carnal man on account of the unfitness of joy, in which the carnal always wish to remain, and on account of which they do everything that they do. Therefore he says: In laughter they make bread and wine Gloss: Prelates in laughter, that is, in foolish joy; Luke 12: "My soul, you have many good things for many years; eat, drink, be merry." And therefore he adds: That they may feast while drinking: for they do it so that they may eat, not so that they may break bread for the hungry: James 5: "You have feasted upon the earth and have nourished your hearts in luxuries." And for this they are rebuked in Isaiah 5: "The harp and the lyre and wine are in your banquets, and you do not regard the work of God."

(Vers. 19.). And all things obey money, etc. Here the fourth point is touched upon, that they are unworthy because of the subversion of justice, which they subvert out of love of money. Therefore he says: And all things obey money, because all love money above all things: Isaiah 1: "They all love gifts, they pursue rewards," and from this follows the subversion of justice: "They do not judge for the orphan, and the cause of the widow does not come before them": and Jeremiah 6: "From the least to the greatest, all are devoted to avarice.

In your thought etc. Above he showed the carnal and slothful man to be unworthy of prelacy; here he restrains subjects from secret detraction, if such a one happens to be placed in charge; and first he sets forth the exhortation, then gives the reason.

(Vers. 20.). He exhorts, therefore, that one should neither speak evil by diminishing another's reputation, nor curse by wishing misery upon him. Therefore he says: Do not detract from the king in your thought, that is, in secret: Exodus twenty-two: "You shall not detract from the gods, and you shall not curse the ruler of your people." And in the secret of your chamber, that is, of your conscience, do not curse the rich man, and this by wishing evil: Romans twelve: "Bless those who persecute you, bless and do not curse";

and 1 Peter 3: "Not curse for curse".

Because the birds of heaven etc. Here the exhortation's reason is given, because what you said in secret will be made public by the Angels, because the birds of heaven will carry your voice. He calls demons the birds of heaven because of their swiftness, for they are swift, or because of their rapacity; whence Genesis 15: "The birds of heaven descended upon the carcasses, and Abraham drove them away"; and Matthew 13: "Another fell by the wayside, and the birds came and ate it." And here heaven is called the murky air, in which demons dwell. These carry the voice, because they frequently make known what is hidden: Matthew 10: "Nothing is hidden that will not be known." And he who has wings will report the matter, that is, the devil will report your evil speech, because he himself is the accuser; but he will be cast out at last, whence Revelation 12: "The accuser of our brethren has been cast out, who accused them day and night."

QUESTIONS.

I. But a doubt arises here, because he says that the birds of the air, that is demons, report the detraction of thought: therefore it seems that our will and thought are known to the devil.

But against this: 1. Because it is said of God alone that he is the one searching hearts and minds: therefore he alone knows thoughts and affections.

2. Likewise Augustine says that if the devil had known the patience of Job, he never would have tempted him, because he does not tempt anyone except to conquer, not to be conquered.

I respond: it must be said that neither demons nor even good Angels, except in the Word, can know our thoughts and hidden affections; and I call hidden those which are not manifested through signs; but those which are manifested through signs, demons detect and know them; and because there are few of which some signs do not appear either in deed, or in the face, or in speech, or in some other way, therefore he says that they hear.

II. Likewise, there is a question concerning what he says, that the birds of heaven will carry or announce.

I ask: how will they carry it? If it is said that to him about whom it is spoken: this is false, because then anyone would know all the hidden evils that are said about him. He understands, therefore, that they will carry it to God.

But against this: 1. God is more immediate and more present to us than demons, since he is not far from each one of us. If therefore to carry or to announce is done to one who is distant, it is clear that it is not the same.

2. Likewise, that carrying—through whom does it happen? Either through good Angels, or through evil ones: not through good ones, because the good ones procure our salvation, not therefore our damnation: therefore they would not offer evil things. If through demons: on the contrary: Demons do not see God, nor do they speak to God any more than evil men do: therefore if evil men do not carry things to God, then neither do demons.

I respond: it must be said that birds here are called evil spirits or demons: these are said to carry evil and hidden merits to God, while good Angels carry good merits. But to carry does not mean to report to God by voice, or to notify Him as though He were ignorant, but to know our wickedness and by knowing it to remember it and to be a witness. The good, however, add intercession beyond knowledge, memory, and testimony, while the evil add the desire to accuse.

1. 2. And thus the response is clear, because they do not report as to a distant one, but as to a judge. And to the second, because the wicked by their office observe evil things and procure damnation: therefore it is fitting for them.
