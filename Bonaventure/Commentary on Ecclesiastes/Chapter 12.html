Chapter XII.


Part three, section II. On punishment, which is pure affliction, treated both generally and specifically.

Remember your Creator etc. He treated above the vanity of punishment, which is both a penalty and an occasion of sin: here he treats of that which is pure affliction, which calls back from evil and urges toward good. And this part has two sections: because first he touches on the punishments and miseries in general: second, in particular, at the passage: When they shall be moved etc.

Art. 1. The punishment, which is pure affliction, is described generally.

Poenalitatem igitur in communi describit quantum ad tria: primo, quantum ad praesentiam nocivi: secundo, quantum ad absentiam iucundi: tertio, quantum ad defectum remedii: ex quorum consideratione hortatur meminisse Dei.
He therefore describes the penalty in general with respect to three things: first, with respect to the presence of what is harmful; second, with respect to the absence of what is pleasant; third, with respect to the lack of remedy; from the consideration of which he exhorts us to remember God.

(Vers. 1.). Therefore, as regards the presence of harm, he says: Remember your Creator, giving him praise and confession, in the days of your youth, that is, as long as you are young and healthy: Sirach 17: "You shall confess while living; living and in health you shall confess and praise the Lord, and glory in his mercies." And the reason for this is that one should not wait for the time of old age, in which there is the presence of harm; on account of which he says: Before the time of affliction comes: Sirach 18: "Before sickness apply the remedy, and before judgment examine yourself." And the years draw near of which you will say: They do not please me. These are the years in which human nature is disturbed by many troubles; and these occur in old age and draw near, because in the Psalm: "Our years shall be considered as a spider's web," etc.

(Vers. 2.). As to the absence of what is pleasant he says: Before the sun grows dark, in whose sight there is great delight: above in the eleventh chapter: "Sweet is the light, and it is delightful for the eyes to see the sun"; whence Tobit 5: "What manner of joy shall be to me, who sit in darkness and do not see the light of heaven"? And to signify the complete absence, he excludes all heavenly light: whence he says: Before the sun grows dark, namely so that it is not seen in its circuit, and the light, so that its influence is not seen, and this regarding the day; and the moon and the stars, regarding the night: Isaiah 13: "The stars of heaven and their brightness shall not spread forth their light, the sun is darkened in its rising, and the moon shall not shine with its light".

As for the lack of remedy, he says: And the clouds return after the rain. After rain, the sky usually clears: when therefore after rain the disturbance of clouds returns, it is a sign that there is no return to joy and gladness: Tobit 3: "After a storm you bring calm, and after weeping and tears you pour in rejoicing." But those days will be turned into darkness and will not return to light: Job 3: "Let it look for light and not see it, nor the rising of the dawning dawn."

Spiritually he exhorts here each person to remember the benefits of God and the benefactor himself, before man is handed over to a reprobate mind and falls into the blindness of error. Therefore they ought to remember God, because he is the creator. First indeed he gave being: Isaiah forty-five : " I made the earth and created man upon it", therefore God must be remembered: against which Deuteronomy thirty-two: " You have forgotten God, your Creator". He gave power;, Deuteronomy eight: " You shall remember the Lord your God, because he himself gave you strength, that he might fulfill his covenant ". He gave good work: Philippians two : " God works in us both to will and to accomplish i : and Isaiah twenty-six: " All our works you have wrought in us, O Lord ": and therefore he must be remembered ; the Psalm: " I was mindful of the works of the Lord, for I will be mindful from the beginning of your wonders ".

Because he was incarnated for us: Song of Songs chapter one: "We will exult and rejoice in you, remembering your breasts more than wine."

Because the redeemer, who suffered for us: Lamentations 3: "Remember my poverty and my transgression," etc.; who was given to us as food from heaven: Psalm: "The merciful and compassionate Lord has made a memorial of his wonders; he has given food to those who fear him."

Because he is a rewarder according to merit, the Psalm says: "You will render to each one according to his works"; on account of this it is said elsewhere in the Psalm: "I have been mindful of your judgments" etc. As to the desire, Isaiah twenty-six: "Your name and your memorial are in the desire of the soul" etc.; and therefore it is added: "My soul has desired you in the night." Rewarding beyond desire: Isaiah sixty-three: "I will remember the mercies of the Lord, above all things that the Lord has rendered to us" etc.

Therefore we ought to remember this Creator both by giving thanks and by turning to him: before the sun grows dark, namely of justice, Christ, when man falls from faith: Amos 8: "The sun shall go down for them at midday"; and the light, of grace, when man falls from charity, for then he loses grace: Wisdom 5: "We have erred from the way of truth, and the light of justice has not shone upon us"; and the moon, the understanding of sacred Scripture, which namely receives its light from Christ: Revelation 12: "A woman clothed with the sun, and the moon under her feet." The woman clothed with the sun is the Church of the Gentiles, to whom the full understanding of sacred Scripture has been given. And the stars, the examples of the perfect: Baruch 3: "The stars gave their light in their watches," namely at night, "and they said: We are here." And let the clouds return, etc., that is, let the teaching of preachers cease: Ezekiel 3: "Son of man, I will make your tongue cleave to the roof of your mouth."

Art. 2. On purely afflictive punishments in particular, according to their threefold distinction.

When they shall be moved etc. Above he treated the suffering that comes upon and follows youth in general; here he treats it in particular. And first he treats the miseries preceding death; second, those accompanying, at: Because man shall go: third, those following, at: Before the silver cord is broken.

On the miseries preceding death.

Prima pars habet duas: quia primo agitur de poenalitate, quae attenditur in membrorum debilitate: secundo vero, de signo debilitatis, ibi: Excelsa quoque timebunt.
The first part has two sections: because first it deals with the punishment which is observed in the weakness of the members; and second, with the sign of weakness, at the verse: They shall also be afraid of that which is high.

(Vers. 3.). First, therefore, he shows the weakening to come in old age with respect to six kinds of members: first, namely, with respect to the shaking of the bones, which are designed to protect the body, when he says: When the keepers of the house shall tremble. The house is the framework of the body: this is fragile, and therefore needs a guardian: and so the Philosopher says that nature made the bones hard to guard the soft members, as the ribs for the belly, and the skull for the head. These are shaken, when their joints become loose.

Likewise, as regards the weakening of the legs for sustaining the weight of the body: whence he says: And the strong men shall stagger: the strong men are the legs, because, just as strong men bear the weight of war, so the legs bear the weight of the body. These strong men stagger when the legs tremble and totter and do not securely support.

Third, as regards the diminishing of teeth for chewing food: whence he says: And the grinders shall be idle, their number being diminished: the grinders are the two jaws, which after the manner of two millstones grind food. These, when the number of teeth is diminished, are idle, because they cannot grind food for eating.

Fourth, as regards the darkening of the eyes for seeing: therefore he says: And those who look through the windows shall be darkened: those who look through the windows are the eyes, which are like two openings for seeing. These are darkened when the elderly cannot see the light, as Eli: 1 Kings 3, who "could not see. The lamp of God, before it was extinguished," etc. As also Isaac, of whom Genesis 27: "Isaac grew old, and his eyes grew dim," etc.

(Verse 4). Fifth, regarding the immobilization of the lips for speaking, when he says: And they shall shut the doors in the street. The doors are the lips, by which the gate of the mouth is closed. These doors are shut in the street when a person on account of old age cannot speak loudly enough to be heard by many; therefore he says: In the low voice of the grinders, that is, of those speaking softly. He says grinding for speaking, because just as a person moves the jaws to crush food, so one moves the jaws and lips to pronounce formed words.

Sixth, regarding the deafness of the ears for hearing, he says: They shall rise up at the voice of the bird, that is, the rooster, because the elderly cannot hear loud sounds, since they are easily harmed and most easily startled. Nor can they hear resonant and harmonious sounds: hence he says: And all the daughters of song shall grow deaf. The daughters of song, which hear harmonious sounds, are the ears: these grow deaf in the elderly, because they cannot hear resonant voices; in 2 Kings 19, Barzillai said to David: "I am eighty years old; are my senses still vigorous enough to discern sweet from bitter, or can I still hear the voices of singing men and singing women?"

These therefore are the six defects which follow or accompany old age: because the elderly cannot repel harmful things, they cannot support themselves, they cannot eat, they cannot see, they cannot speak, they cannot hear.

They shall also be afraid of that which is high etc. Above he set forth the weakening of the body in the old man; here he adds signs of weakening, which are four: trembling of the body, grayness of the head, swelling of the belly, and cooling of desire.

(Verse 5). The first sign of infirmity, therefore, in

. old age is trembling of the body; on account of which he says: They shall also be afraid of what is high, and terrors shall be in the way, that is, all the members of the body will become tremulous: and that is a sign of the dissolution and weakening of the retentive power of the members, which cannot hold the members steady: and therefore it is compelled to shake continuously; whence the wretched old man can say that verse of the Psalm: "Fear and trembling have come upon me," etc.

The second sign is the whitening of the head, regarding which he says: The almond tree shall flourish, that is, the head and beard shall grow white: for the almond tree, when it blooms, becomes white, and this is a sign of the weakening of strength: for since in the old person the retentive power over the humors is weakened, the humors therefore putrefy and also grow cold, as a sign of which white superfluities proceed from the body of the old person: and therefore they grow white.

The third sign of senile infirmity is the swelling of the belly or of other members of the body; with regard to which he says: The locust shall be made fat. The fattening of the locust even around its old age is rather a swelling than true fatness, in the same way that there is fattening in the elderly, and this is a sign of the weakness of the digestive heat. For because the digestive heat fails, the phlegmatic, watery, and undigested humor abounds, which fills the body of the old person and seems to fatten it.

The fourth sign is the diminishing of lust: on account of which he says: The caper shall be scattered. The caper is a certain herb which "is beneficial for the kidneys," as the Gloss says, and strengthens the act of the generative power; and according to Jerome, in Hebrew it has a common name for both the herb and for lustful desire. In whatever meaning therefore it is taken, the sense is correct: the caper shall be scattered, that is, the fervor of lust shall be diminished; and this is a sign of weakness in the old person. For in the act of generation, heat moves, and digested moisture supplies the material. Because therefore old age is cold and dry, heat and moisture fail, and therefore the fervor of lust is diminished.

On the miseries accompanying death in the dying person and in those who grieve with them,

(Vers. 5.). Because man shall go into the house etc. The miseries preceding death have been described. Here is described the very misery of death both in the dying person and in the mourners. In the dying person, when he says: Because man shall go into the house of his eternity, that is, he will die. For the house after death is eternal, because above in the eleventh chapter, "if the tree fall toward the south, there it shall be; if toward the north, there it shall be"; and thus the house after death is rightly called the house of eternity. As for the misery in the mourners, he says: And the mourners shall go about in the street: they go about, because for grief they know not how to rest: Psalm: "The mischief of their going about, the labor of their lips shall cover them." And they shall mourn over the dead as though unwilling to be consoled, but they ought not do so: Sirach twenty-two: "The mourning for the dead is seven days."

On the miseries that follow death.

Before the silver cord is snapped etc. Above he touched upon the miseries preceding and accompanying death: here he touches upon those that follow, which are the dissolution of the body and the migration of the spirit to God: which are described in this order.

(Vers. 6. 7.). First is noted the dissolution of the sinews, when he says: Before the silver cord is broken, and the words must be repeated: "Remember your Creator" etc. The intertwining of the sinews is rightly called a cord, because it girds and binds the whole body, and silver, on account of their whiteness and transparency. The silver cord, therefore, is broken when the binding of the sinews is dissolved.

Second, the dissolution of the veins or arteries, when he says: And the golden band runs back,— the wrapping of the veins is rightly called a band, because, just as a band is wound around, and by it the whole binding is preserved, so by veins and arteries the whole framework of the body is encircled and preserved. This wrapping of the veins and arteries is rightly called golden, because gold is red, and veins of this kind are red on account of the redness of the blood contained within them. Therefore the golden band runs back when the wrapping of the veins and arteries does not endure.

Third is noted the breaking of the vessel of urine, when he says: And the pitcher be broken at the fountain. The pitcher is the receptacle of water drawn from a fountain, and the bladder is the receptacle of urine proceeding from the liver as from a fountain. For urine is the filtrate of blood and other humors, which proceed from the liver into the whole body as from a fountain. Therefore the pitcher is broken at the fountain when the bladder ruptures and cannot receive the filtrate of humors which proceeds from the liver.

Fourth is noted the destruction of the channel of impure waste, when he says: And let the wheel be broken over the cistern. The wheel is the instrument by which the drawing of foul and muddy water from the cistern takes place: the cistern contains for the most part muddy water; and such is our belly. Therefore, for the wheel to be broken over the cistern is for the channel of impure waste issuing from the belly to be dissolved in death.

Fifth is noted the incineration of the whole body, when it is said: And let the dust return to its own earth, whence it was, that is, let the body be incinerated, according to that passage in Genesis 3: "You are dust, and to dust you shall return." To its own earth, because it is from it: Sirach 40: "All things that are made of earth shall return to earth." Sixth is touched upon the spirit or the soul's return to God: whence he says: And let the spirit return to God, who gave it. The Lord himself gives the spirit, whence it is said in the Psalm: "He who fashioned their hearts one by one," etc.; and to him every spirit returns as to the rewarder; Romans 14: "We shall all stand before the judgment seat of Christ," etc.

Spiritually, from that place: When they shall be moved, etc., the final state of the Church is described here: first, regarding what precedes the judgment; second, regarding what accompanies it, at: Because man shall go into the house; third, regarding what follows, at: Before the silver cord is broken.

The state before the judgment, which will be at the coming of the Antichrist, is described with respect to three things: first, with respect to the diminution of faith; second, with respect to the spread of error, at the words: And they shall rise up at the voice of the bird, etc.; third, with respect to the manner of spreading, at the words: The almond tree shall flourish, etc.

First, therefore, he foretells that there will be a diminution of faith with respect to four classes of persons: first, with respect to prelates; second, with respect to the perfect; third, with respect to the simple; fourth, with respect to the wise. First, therefore, he foretells the shaking of the prelates, when he says: When the keepers of the house shall be moved. This house is the Church, of which it is said in the Psalm: "Holiness becometh thy house," etc. The keepers are the prelates, to whom the care of the Lord's flock has been entrusted: Isaiah sixty-two: "Upon thy walls, O Jerusalem, I have set watchmen." The keepers, therefore, shall be moved when the prelates shall be shaken in their faith; and this indeed will be at the coming of the Antichrist; whence the last chapter of Judith: "The mountains shall be moved from their foundations."

Secondly he predicts the hesitation of the perfect, when he says: And the mighty men shall totter: The mighty men are those perfect in charity and hope, according to that passage in Isaiah 40: "They that hope in the Lord shall renew their strength."

These will waver: Matthew twenty-four: "False prophets will arise and will give signs, so that, if it were possible, even the elect may be led into error"; and in the same place: "And unless those days had been shortened, no flesh would be saved: but for the sake of the elect they will be shortened." Third, he foretells the fall of the simple, when he adds: And the grinding women will be idle, diminished in number, that is, the simple will cease from good work, afflicted with weariness on account of the great deception practiced upon them: whence he says: diminished in number, because they will be greatly diminished: Matthew twenty-four: "Two at the mill, one will be taken, and the other left."

Fourth, he foretells the error of the wise, when he says: And those who look through the holes will grow dark, that is, the wise, who see subtly, will be enveloped in the darkness of error: Job 12: "They shall grope as in the darkness and not in the light, and he shall make them stagger like drunkards." And because those who fall from faith fall from faithful confession, therefore he says: And they will shut the doors in the street, that is, in public: in the low sound of the grinding, that is, murmuring. But true confession is in public; whence Matthew 10: "What you hear in the ear, preach upon the housetops."

And they shall rise up at the voice etc. Here secondly is noted the spreading of error after the diminishing of faith, with respect to three things. First he says that falsehood will be preached and heard; second, that truth will be silenced; third, that the power of the antichrist will be exalted and feared.

First indeed, falsehood will be preached by the antichrist, when he says: They will rise up at the voice of the bird. The voice of the bird singing in the night is the preaching of the antichrist preaching in the night of darkness and unbelief. At the voice, therefore, of him will rise up those who believe his preaching. For this reason it is said in Matthew twenty-four, that they should not give credence: "If therefore they say to you: Behold, he is in the desert, do not believe it." Nevertheless, many will hear and believe; in John five it is said to the Jews: "If another comes in his own name, him you will receive."

Second, he predicts that truth will be silenced, because there will be no one to hear it; therefore he says: All the daughters of song shall grow deaf. Then the daughters of song grow deaf when they cannot hear the sound of truth, which is like a certain harmony. This will be in the time of the Antichrist, as was foretold: in Isaiah chapter thirty it is foretold concerning that people who will cling to the Antichrist: "It is a people provoking to wrath, lying children, children unwilling to hear the law of God."

Third, he foretells that the power of the Antichrist will be feared, when he says: They shall also be afraid of that which is high, and fears shall be in the way, etc., that is, all, both small and great, will dread the power of the Antichrist and out of fear will worship him: whence Revelation thirteen: "All the earth wondered after the beast, saying: Who is like unto this beast, and who shall be able to fight against it?" And after: "And power was given him over every tribe and people and tongue, and all that dwell upon the earth shall worship him, whose names are not written in the book of life."

The almond tree shall flourish. Above was described the weakening of faith and the spread of error: here thirdly is described the manner of multiplying error. And this manner will be by a threefold way, namely through miracles, through gifts, through torments.

First, therefore, the method of drawing into error through miracles is foretold, when he says: The almond tree shall flourish, so that just as Aaron's rod miraculously blossomed and produced almonds, according to what is said in Numbers 17, so in imitation the antichrist will make the rod bloom and wither and perform many other miracles, through which the foolish will be converted to him: Revelation 13: "And the beast performed great signs and deceived those dwelling on the earth because of the signs which were given to it to perform in the sight of men."

Second, he foretells the manner of drawing people through gifts, when he adds: And the locust shall be fattened. The locust is fattened when a greedy man is filled with money and through it is turned away from God: Deuteronomy 32: "The beloved grew fat and kicked back; grown fat, fattened, enlarged," etc. In this way the antichrist will turn many away from the faith, which was signified in 2 Maccabees 7: "Antiochus, when the youngest still remained, urged him not only with words, but also promised with an oath that he would make him rich and blessed, and that, having departed from the laws, he would hold him dear."

Third, he predicts that some will be drawn away through torments, when he says: And the caper shall be scattered. The caper, as was said, is an herb that provokes lust. By this are understood the carnal, who care for the flesh in its desires. These are scattered when they fall away from the faith through fear of torments; for they are among those of whom it is said in Luke 8: "They believe for a time, and in the time of trial they fall away." The time of trial will be at the coming of the Antichrist; whence in Matthew 24: "There will be such tribulation as has not been from the beginning of the world until now."

Because man shall go into the house etc. He treated above the things preceding judgment: here he treats secondly of the judgment itself, in which the good will be rewarded, and the evil will be punished.

As to the rewarding of the good, he says: Man shall go into the house of his eternity, namely when it will be said to them: "Come, blessed of my Father, receive the kingdom which has been prepared for you from the foundation of the world," Matthew 25. And then they will go to the home of eternal joy: Isaiah 35: "They shall come to Zion with praise, and everlasting joy shall be upon their heads: they shall obtain joy and gladness."

As for the wicked, he says: And the mourners will go about in the street, namely the wicked: whence Wisdom 5: "They will say within themselves, repenting and groaning for anguish of spirit": and Matthew 24: "They will see the sign of the Son of Man in heaven: and then all the tribes of the earth will mourn, and they will see the Son of Man," etc.

Before the silver cord is broken, etc. He treated above of the things preceding judgment and accompanying it; here he treats of the things following. He describes six consequences after the state of judgment. First is noted the cessation of eloquence, when it is said: Before the silver cord is broken. Silver is sonorous and signifies eloquence; the silver cord is the beautiful joining and binding together of words, which, like a cord, binds a person while it persuades, according to what is said in Proverbs 7: "She ensnared him with many words, and with the flattery of her lips she drew him in." Then therefore the silver cord will be broken, when after the day of judgment worldly eloquence will be silenced; Matthew 22: "Friend, how did you come in here not having a wedding garment? But he was speechless."

Second, the abolition of prelacy or power is noted, when he says: Let the golden fillet return. By the fillet, which was placed on the head of the priest, is understood the order of power and prelacy, by which the Church is bound together. This fillet returns to its origin, namely when Christ alone will reign, and all other power will be taken away; and this will be after the judgment: 1 Corinthians 15: "Then the end, when he shall have delivered the kingdom to God and the Father, when he shall have abolished all principality and dominion and power."

Third, the emptying of worldly wisdom is noted, when he says: And let the pitcher be broken at the fountain. This pitcher is the curiosity of the heart, which is brought to draw up wisdom; concerning which figuratively in John chapter four: "A woman of Samaria came with a pitcher to draw water." The woman of Samaria is the gentile world, which comes with a pitcher, that is, with curiosity, to draw up wisdom; whence it is said in Acts chapter seventeen: "The Athenians spent their time in nothing else but either to tell or to hear some new thing." The fountain, moreover, is worldly wisdom; whence in the Psalm: "You broke open fountains and torrents," etc. The pitcher therefore is broken at the fountain when no place remains for one to curiously devote oneself to worldly wisdom; Isaiah chapter thirty: "And it shall be broken like the vessel of a potter with a most powerful shattering," etc.

Fourth, the removal of worldly wealth is noted, when he adds: And let the wheel be broken at the cistern. By the cistern, which receives waters, is understood the accumulation of worldly wealth, according to that passage of Jeremiah 2: "They have forsaken me, the fountain of living water, and have dug for themselves broken cisterns," etc. The wheel signifies the fickleness and changeability in possessing; whence these are called goods of fortune. Then therefore the wheel is broken at the cistern, when all hope of having temporal goods is taken away: which was signified in Exodus 14: "The Lord looking upon the camp of the Egyptians overturned the wheels of the chariots," because, in Sirach 40, "the substance of the ungodly shall dry up like a river."

Fifth, it is noted how for the wicked there will be the infliction of punishment, when he says: And the dust returns to the earth. The impious person and sinner is rightly called dust, according to that verse of the Psalm: "Not so the wicked, not so, but like the dust which the wind casts from the face of the earth." Earth is the depth of darkness, according to that verse of Proverbs twenty-five: "Heaven above, and earth below." Therefore the dust returns to the earth when the sinner is shut away in the lowest depth of infernal darkness; Revelation twenty: "Whoever was not found written in the book of life was cast into the lake of fire." Sixth, it is noted how for the just and spiritual there will be the bestowal of glory, when he says: The spirit returns to God, who gave it. The spirit is the spiritual and good person, who is joined to God through grace: First Corinthians six: "He who clings to the Lord is one spirit." This spirit returns to God, not because it was previously with him and afterwards departed when it came to the body and in death returns again, as Origen held; but it is said to return because it is from him and through him and is finally ordered toward him. And this indeed will be in glory after the judgment, according to what is said in Colossians three: "When Christ, your life, shall appear, then you also will appear with him in glory."

The conclusion of the threefold proof.

(Vers. 8.). Vanity of vanities etc. As was said from the beginning of the book, this entire book has its order directed toward proving one proposition, which he proposed at the beginning of the book, namely this: Vanity of vanities etc. He proved this throughout the entire book and now at the end concludes by saying: Vanity of vanities, said Ecclesiastes. Above he likewise said, that is, proposed: but now: Ecclesiastes said, that is, concluded. He said, I say: Vanity of vanities. This phrase is taken materially, and the act of speaking passes over it, so that the meaning is: Ecclesiastes spoke this phrase: Vanity of vanities: and in this phrase that genitive designates origin, so that the meaning is: Vanity of vanities, that is, from vanities arises vanity. Ecclesiastes also said this, namely: All is vanity, and the substantive verb is understood from common usage, so that the meaning is: All things are vanity.

From this twofold statement the following argument is drawn: If vanity arises from vanities, and all things are vanity, therefore all created things do not make their lover blessed, but make him vain, according to that passage in Jeremiah 2: "They walked after vanity, and became vain." If therefore all ought to desire blessedness and flee vanity, then all ought to despise earthly goods. And this is the conclusion of this book.

Epilogue.

And since he was most wise etc. It was said above that this book has three principal parts, namely the prologue, the treatise, and the epilogue. With the first two parts completed, here begins the third part. In this epilogue, he proceeds in the following order. First, the diligence of the writer is noted; second, the authority of his words; third, the curiosity of the hearers is restrained; fourth, the sum of what has been said is revealed.

(Vers. 9. 10.). Therefore, first the diligence of the writer is suggested: because, since he was wise, he did not hide his wisdom, but expounded it to others. Therefore he says: And since Ecclesiastes was most wise: 3 Kings 4: "The wisdom of Solomon surpassed the wisdom of all the Orientals; and he was wiser than all men." Since he was so wise, he was not negligent, but diligent. Hence he taught the people: Sirach 24: "I will yet pour out doctrine like prophecy, and will leave it to those who seek wisdom"; and afterwards: "Look, for I have not labored for myself alone, but for all who seek truth." He explains his diligence, namely through the multitude of writings and teachings: and here the three books which he composed are touched upon. First Ecclesiastes, when he says: And he expounded the things he had done, supply: in this book of Ecclesiastes: above in the first and second chapters he narrated spiritual and carnal works, so that in evil things he might give an example of what to detest, and in good things an example of what to imitate.

Second, the book of Proverbs is touched upon, when he says: And searching he composed parables, which are useful words, that is, in Proverbs: at the beginning of Proverbs: "The Parables of Solomon, son of David, king of Israel, for knowing wisdom and discipline, for understanding words of prudence." Third, the book of the Song of Songs is touched upon, when he says: And he wrote discourses most upright and full of truth. He says this because those words which are spoken in the Song of Songs seem to be words of lust and desire; therefore he says: Discourses most upright, that is, just and good: Proverbs eight: "All my discourses are just, there is nothing wicked or perverse in them." The words of the Song of Songs also seem to be false and jesting, when it says: "Your nose is like a tower"; therefore he says: And full of truth: for that is full which contains something within and holds it; so also the words of the Song of Songs inwardly conceal and encompass truth; and this is necessary for all teaching: Sirach thirty-seven: "Before all works let a truthful word precede you."

(Vers. 11.). The words of the wise etc. Here secondly is noted the authority of his words or teachings, because they were not lightly spoken, but rather spoken with deliberation and inspired by God, and therefore they are firm and stable. And for this reason he says: The words of the wise are like goads, that is, penetrating to the interior; and like nails driven deep, that is, into the depths, which are immovable, and therefore are not easily driven from the heart, but remain and lead to good. Hence the word of God is compared to a sword in Ephesians 6: "And the sword of the Spirit, which is the word of God." And therefore it pierces the heart, according to what is said in Acts 2: "When they heard these things, they were pierced in their heart." Therefore the words of the wise are to be pondered deeply, because they were not lightly spoken; thus he says: Which through the counsel of masters, that is, through deliberate examination and judgment, were given by one shepherd, that is, by Christ, who is the shepherd, according to what is said in John 10:

"I am the good shepherd." By this shepherd the words of the wise were given, because, as Augustine says, "he who teaches within has his chair in heaven"; hence Matthew twenty-three: "One is your master, Christ." Yet they were given through the counsel of masters, that is, of the ministers of this supreme master, whom he himself sent and taught: hence it is said in First Peter five: "Elders, feed the flock of God which is among you, so that when the prince of shepherds shall appear, you may receive the unfading crown of glory." He himself sent these: Matthew, last chapter: "Go therefore and teach all nations." (Vers. 12.). Beyond these etc. Here thirdly the curiosity of the hearers is restrained, whom he addresses under the person of a son, saying: Beyond these, my son, do not seek, so as always to wish to hear new things: for it suffices to know what is necessary: Sirach three: "The things that God has commanded you, think on them always, and in many of his works be not curious."

And a twofold reason is given for this admonition, because the pursuit of this curiosity is endless and laborious. It is endless ; therefore he says: Of making many books there is no end: because the curious never have so many that they do not wish to hear more, because they never wish to hear old things, but always new ones: whence Acts 17: "The Athenians spent their time in nothing else but either telling or hearing something new." Therefore it is said in Daniel 12: "Very many times shall pass, and knowledge shall be manifold," because knowledge is always in some way renewed. Not only is curious inquiry endless, but also laborious : therefore he adds: And much study is an affliction of the flesh: Sirach 31: "The vigil of honor will waste the flesh." It is, I say, a great affliction, and on the contrary, of little profit, according to what was said above in chapter 8: "There is a man who day and night takes no sleep with his eyes. And I understood that man can find no reason for the works of God, of those things which are done under the sun."

(Vers. 13.). The end of speaking together etc. Here fourthly is touched upon the meaning of what has been said through a certain distribution, to hearing and remembering which he arouses the attention of the disciples when he says: The end of speaking, that is, the conclusion and consummation of the discourse, let us all hear together, as being useful and necessary for all.

And the meaning of all that has been said consists in this, namely in the turning away from evil, or in the doing of good: to which all the teaching and speech of Solomon is ordered, indeed all of Scripture. Therefore first, as regards the turning away from evil, he says: Fear God: Proverbs 16: "By the fear of the Lord everyone turns away from evil." As regards the doing of good, he says: And keep his commandments: Deuteronomy 6: "Hear, O Israel, and observe, that you may do what the Lord has commanded." This is the whole man, that is, the complete and perfect man. For if he does good and does not turn away from evil, he is not a whole man, but half; again, if he turns away from evil and does not do good, he is only half; but if he turns away from evil through fear and does good through the observance of the commandments, he is now the whole man, that is, the completely just man. The Lord requires nothing more from man: Deuteronomy 10: "And now, Israel, what does the Lord require of you, except that you fear the Lord your God and walk in his ways, and serve the Lord with your whole heart and with your whole soul, and keep the commandments of the Lord your God?"

(Verse 14). And the reason why God is to be feared and his commandments are to be observed is this: because he will demand an account of all things, whether of good or of evil. Hence he adds: And God will bring every work into judgment, for every error, because nothing will remain unexamined, neither what was done through malice nor what was done through error: Matthew 12: "For every idle word that men shall speak, they shall give account." And not only will evil deeds be examined, but also good ones; therefore he adds: Whether it be good or whether it be evil, it will be brought into judgment: whence in the Psalm: "When I shall take the appointed time, I will judge justices"; and again, Second Corinthians 5: "We must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ": therefore he will judge all things, because he regards all things. And this is what ought greatly to urge us to fear and the observance of the commandments; and therefore he concludes with this, just as Boethius also does at the end of the Consolation: "A great necessity of uprightness is imposed upon us, since we do all things in the sight of a judge who discerns all things."

Here ends Ecclesiastes.
