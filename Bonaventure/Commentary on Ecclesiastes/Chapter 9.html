Chapter IX.


(Verse 1). There are just and wise men etc. Here the second point is touched upon, namely the uncertainty of discernment. For a man does not know about himself whether he is a good man or a bad one, even the good man himself. Therefore he says: There are just and wise men, and thus good; and their works are in the hand of God, that is, in the power of knowing, accepting, and rewarding. In his hand are merits and rewards. There are the merits of good thoughts: Proverbs 21: "As the divisions of waters, so the heart of the king is in the hand of the Lord." There are the merits of good affections: Psalm: "In his hand are all the ends of the earth." There are also those of good speech: Wisdom 7: "In his hand are both we and our words." There are also those of works, as in the text at hand. There are also rewards in his hand: Wisdom 3: "The souls of the just are in the hand of God"; and since they are in the hand of God, they ought to be known. And yet man does not know whether he is worthy of love or hatred, and thus he does not know how to discern; 1 Corinthians 4: "I am conscious of nothing against myself, yet I am not justified by this" etc.

(Verse 2). But all things are kept for the future. The third point is noted here, namely the reason for uncertainty,

which arises from this, that in the present recompense is not given according to merits, but is reserved for the future. And therefore he says thus: Man does not know... But all things are kept uncertain for the future, so that they may be made certain — 1 Corinthians 4: "Do not judge before the time, until the Lord comes, who will both bring to light the hidden things of darkness and make manifest the counsels of hearts"; which indeed he does not do now, but reserves, according to that passage in Job 14: "You have sealed up my offenses as in a bag"; and thus in the present all things are in secret. And it is added: Because all things equally befall the just and the impious etc.: all things, good and evil, equally befall the just and the sinner, whom he describes under six distinctions. The just and the impious, with regard to the belief of faith, which namely justifies the impious: Habakkuk 2: "The just man shall live by his faith." The good and the evil, with regard to the purity of love, because holy love makes one good: Matthew 7: "A good tree cannot bear bad fruit." The clean and the unclean, with regard to the uprightness of conduct: for that is observed in outward matters, Tobit 4. To him who offers hosts and victims and to him who despises sacrifices, with regard to

Equal things befall all these both with regard to the condition of nature, according to that passage of Wisdom 7: "There is therefore one entrance into life for all, and a similar departure"; and with regard to the condition of guilt: Romans 3: "All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God"; and with regard to the condition of punishment: Ecclesiasticus 8: "Remember that we are all in corruption."

(Vers. 3.). This is the worst etc. Here the fourth point is touched upon, namely the origin of vain security, because great security arises from this lack of distinction, which is the occasion of many sins. Therefore he says: And this is the worst among all things that happen under the sun, because the same things happen to all. Therefore the worst, because it is the occasion of many sins; for God does not seem to care about us. Whence arises that statement in Job twenty-two: "The clouds are his hiding place, and he does not consider our affairs." And he shows that evils arise from this, because security arises, through which man falls into malice and contempt. Therefore he says: Whence also the hearts of the children of men, that is, of the wicked and carnal, are filled with malice and contempt. By filling the multitude of vices is designated, so that malice resides in the concupiscible appetite, and contempt in the irascible appetite, which are the two roots of sins; Genesis six: "The earth was corrupted before God and filled with iniquity"; and this in their life, that is, as long as they have the opportunity to merit: First Thessalonians two: "That they may fill up their sins always." And because such people are cast headlong into punishment, therefore he adds: And after these things they shall be brought down to hell: Psalm: "But you, O God, shall bring them down into the pit of destruction"; and Job twenty-one: "They spend their days in prosperity, and in a moment they go down to hell."

Questions.


I. Here arises a question concerning what he said: I understood that man can find no reason for the works of God.

Therefore, if we must know the reason for what we understand, and we can find the reason for nothing, then we understand nothing.

2. Likewise, rational nature exists for understanding and perceiving reason: therefore if we cannot discover reason, we possess the rational faculty in vain.

I respond: it must be said that "words must be taken according to their subject matter"; but he does not speak of the works of God in general, but of the divine judgments, which are works of God, according to that verse of the Psalm: Great are the works of the Lord, etc. "These judgments, as Gregory says, are not to be examined, but to be venerated with fearful silence"; because, even if they have a reason, they nevertheless have a hidden reason, and one which we cannot investigate by ourselves, because it is above us; and if we understand, this is through the illumination of the Holy Spirit, who searches the deep things of God, not through human discovery. Of other works, such as creatures, many reasons are found, and much knowledge is had: but he does not speak of those works here.

II. Likewise, there is a question about what he says: Man does not know whether he is worthy of love or hatred.

This seems false: 1. Because in Romans 8: Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? I am certain, etc.: therefore he has an instance there.

You will say that he knew by revelation; it is objected that man can know by himself, because the dignity of divine love places in us sanctifying grace; but Augustine says that those things which are essentially in the soul are better known than those which are known according to species: therefore if we are certain of those things which we know according to species, as when something sweet is placed upon the tongue, much more so of grace.

2. Likewise, Augustine says that he who has faith is certain that he has faith. Therefore he who has love or charity is certain that he has it: therefore he who has grace is certain of it: therefore he is certain whether he is pleasing to God.

3. Likewise, concerning hatred someone can be certain: he who has sinned and wishes to sin is certain that he is worthy of hatred.

Contra: I. First Corinthians four: I am not conscious of anything against myself, yet I am not justified in this etc.

2. Likewise, Job 9: If he comes to me, I will not see him: and if he departs, I will not understand: and in the same place: If I am simple, my soul itself will not know it.

3. Likewise, Sirach five: Concerning forgiven sin, do not be without fear; therefore no one is certain.

To this some respond that it is understood of the hatred of predestination and reprobation, of which no one is certain; and thus it admits of no objection, because predestination does not place in us any effect by which it could be known. But another response must be given, because this response does not satisfy the text; for the text says: He does not know whether he is worthy of love; but no one is worthy of nor merits being predestined; therefore it is understood according to present justice.

For this reason others respond that knowledge is twofold, namely necessary and infallible: and this we cannot have, because no one can know with certainty whether he has grace, except through revelation, as the Apostle did. There is another kind that is probable and through signs: and this indeed can be had and is had: because many through probable signs know that they are in grace.

i. 2. As to the objection that the soul sees essentially: I say that habits in the soul are not known except through the soul seeing itself disposed toward some act; and because the acts of unformed and formed faith, and of gratuitous and natural love, are so similar that a person can scarcely or never discern the difference, therefore, even if one knows the habit, one still does not know whether it is gratuitous.

3. As for the objection concerning hatred, I say that he is speaking about the just, because they do not know whether they are worthy of love or of hatred; not about the openly wicked.

Whatever the case may be regarding charity and faith, whether a man knows that he has them or not; nevertheless he does not know whether he is worthy of God's love or hatred: for we can believe, but how do we know that the Lord hates the wicked and loves the good, when he bestows so many and such great goods upon the wicked just as upon the good? Hence Ecclesiastes did not say that they do not know themselves to be just, but that they do not know themselves to be loved by God.

III. It can also be doubted regarding what he says: This is the worst thing under the sun, that the same things happen to all.

AGAINST THIS: 1. It is worse when good things happen to the wicked and bad things to the good than when the same things happen to both, because, as was said above, that is undeserved and unworthy.

2. Likewise, it was said above: I judge this most vain about this, that the wicked are secure, and evils befall the just; but "what is said by way of superabundance applies to one alone": therefore etc.

I respond: it must be said that intensification and diminution in vanity or wickedness can be taken properly: and so it is called intensively, in the way that it is said: a pearl is whiter than a horse: and so intensively the vanity is greater when the wicked are exalted and the good are brought low. Furthermore, more and less can be taken extensively, because it is the cause or occasion in more cases or of more people: and in this way the lack of distinction between the good and the wicked is a greater vanity, because it is the origin of many evils. For if bad things happened to the good, and good things to the wicked, the good could be distinguished from the wicked: but when the same things happen to both, they cannot be distinguished.

Second, it is shown how pleasure arises from this.

There is no one who lives forever. He has already shown how from lack of distinction arises the vanity of security: here secondly he shows how from security arises pleasure: and he does this indeed in this order. First is noted the certainty of death ,- second, through death the worsening of one's state: third, the commendation of pleasure; fourth, the confirmation of the commendation.

(Verse 4). First, therefore, the certainty of death is noted by this, that no one escapes its judgment. Therefore he says: There is no one who lives forever: therefore in the Psalm: "Who is the man who shall live and not see death?" And into this condemnation Adam fell with his posterity, when it was said to him in Genesis 3: "You are dust, and to dust you shall return." And who would have confidence in this matter, supply: there is no one; in this matter, namely the hope of living forever: Job 7: "My days have passed more swiftly than a web is cut by the weaver, and they are consumed without any hope, for my life is a wind, and it shall not return," etc.

Better is a living dog etc. Here secondly it is shown that through death there is a deterioration of condition: and he proposes this under a metaphor: Better is a living dog than a dead lion. Although the lion surpasses the dog, nevertheless death renders it inferior, because a living dog can do something, a dead lion can do nothing, and yet while living it is more powerful than a dog: Proverbs 30: "The lion, the mightiest of animals, will not tremble at the approach of any." Thus metaphorically he means to say that any living person, however lowly, is better than a dead one, however good.

(Vers. 5. 6.). And he proves first with regard to the act of knowledge. For the living know that they shall die: at least of this they are certain: 2 Kings 14: "We all die, and like waters that flow down into the earth, which do not return." Or: 'that they shall die,' that is, given over to the necessity of death; Romans 8: "The body is dead because of sin;" For the dead know nothing more, because knowledge presupposes life — and thus the living surpass the dead in the act of knowledge: for they have neither motion nor sensation. With regard to the act of remembrance he says: Nor do they have any further reward, namely of fame in the memory of others: because their memory is delivered to oblivion: above in chapter 2: "There shall be no remembrance of the wise man, nor of the fool, forever and in times to come," etc. Or: because their memory does not have the act of remembrance: Job 7: "He who goes down to hell shall not come up, nor shall he return any more to his own house, nor shall his place know him any more."

As for the act of affection he says: Love and hatred and envy have perished together, because they are not moved as before in life. As for the act of outward operation he says: Nor do they have a part in this world, and he explains with respect to what: and in the work that is done under the sun, because they cannot work, according to that passage in John 9: "The night comes, when no one can work."

(Vers. 7. 8. 9.). Go therefore and eat etc. Here the third point is touched upon, namely the commendation of present delight, to the embracing of which he exhorts first according to taste; whence he says: Go therefore and eat your bread with joy: by bread is understood everything that pertains to food. And drink your wine with a glad heart: by wine, everything that relates to drink. Because God is pleased with your works: as if to say: you are not certain whether they displease; therefore presume that they please, and so eat and drink securely, according to that passage in Isaiah twenty-two: "Behold, joy and gladness, to eat flesh and to drink wine: let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we shall die."

Then according to sight he says: At all times let your garments be white, so that you may appear attractive in clothing: and let oil not be lacking from your head, so that you may appear attractive in appearance. In this way those adorn themselves who wish to please in the sight of men. The example is of Judith, who when she wished to please the eyes of Holofernes, adorned herself, according to what is said in Judith 10: but she did this for a good purpose, because otherwise it is reprehensible: Sirach 11: "Never glory in clothing."

Then regarding touch he says: Enjoy life with the wife whom you love. This enjoyment is described in Proverbs seven: "Come, let us be intoxicated with breasts and enjoy the desired embraces, until the day dawns." And this not only once, but all the days of the life of your instability, which are given to you under the sun. Truly our life is unstable, according to that passage in Job fourteen: "He never remains in the same state." And for greater expression he says: All the time of your vanity. Our life is a time of vanity: whence in the Psalm: "Man is made like unto vanity: his days pass away like a shadow."

(Vers. 9. 10.). For this is the portion etc. Here the fourth point is touched upon, namely the confirmation of the commendation, and the confirmation consists in this, that man expects no other reward nor has any other fruit, and therefore he ought to accept what he can: thus he says: For this is your portion in life and in the labor with which you toil under the sun, namely, to take delight in the present: Wisdom 2: "Let us everywhere leave signs of our joy, for this is our portion, and this is our lot."—And because no other is to be expected, man ought to take from this as much as he can: therefore he says: Whatever your hand is able to do, work at it earnestly, that is, while you have time, do not wait for the future: and therefore it is added: For neither work, in things to be done; nor reasoning, in things to be deliberated; nor knowledge, in things to be known; nor wisdom, in things to be loved, will be in the netherworld, to which you hasten: these things are not there, because, as is said in Job 10, "that land is dark and covered with the shadow of death, where there is no order, but everlasting horror dwells." Thither sinners hasten, and all before the coming of Christ, as regards limbo: Job 17: "All that is mine shall descend into the deepest hell." Thither sinners hasten, drawing nearer day by day: Deuteronomy 32: "The day of perdition is at hand."

But this can be explained spiritually, whereby first it exhorts to the delight of contemplation: and secondly, to the urgency of action. For contemplative men, what is necessary is meditation on Sacred Scripture, purity of conscience, and the embrace of interior devotion.

As for the meditation on sacred Scripture he says: Eat bread and drink wine: bread as regards difficult things, and wine as regards easy things: Proverbs 9, Wisdom says: "Come, eat my bread and drink the wine which I have mixed for you." As for the purity of conscience he says: Let your garments be white at all times, that is, a clean conscience: Revelation 3: "They have not defiled their garments, they shall walk with me in white." On the contrary it is said in Isaiah 9: "A garment mixed with blood shall be for burning and fuel for the fire." And because internal joy follows from cleanness of conscience, therefore he says: And let oil not be lacking from your head: whence in the Psalm: "That he may make the face cheerful with oil"; Matthew 6: "But you, when you fast, anoint your head," etc. — As for the embrace of internal devotion he says: Enjoy life with the wife whom you love. This wife is religious life, in which one must devote oneself to contemplation: Proverbs 5: "Rejoice with the wife of your youth, a most dear hind," etc. This beloved wife was prefigured by Rachel, for whom "Jacob served seven years," Genesis 29.

Then as for the active, he says: Whatever your hand is able to do, work earnestly. To work earnestly is to work swiftly, against negligence: Ecclesiasticus 31: "In all your works be swift, and no infirmity will come upon you"; fervently, Proverbs 18: "He who is slack and negligent in his work is the brother of him who destroys his own works"; continuously, so that one does not grow lukewarm: Ecclesiasticus 27: "If you do not hold yourself steadfastly in the fear of the Lord, your house will quickly be overturned"; perseveringly: 2 Timothy 2: "He who strives in the contest will not be crowned unless he has striven lawfully," that is, perseveringly. And the reason is added why one must earnestly do good works in the present: because the time of merit is short; whence: In the netherworld there is no work, for exercising oneself in piety: John 9: "I must work while it is day." And therefore it is said in Galatians, the last chapter: "Let us do good while we have time, to all, but especially to those of the household of faith." There is no reason, for inquiring and discerning what is good and what is evil: Job 12: "They will grope as in darkness and not in light, and he will make them stagger like drunkards"; Isaiah 19: "And the Lord has mingled in its midst a spirit of dizziness." There is no wisdom and knowledge: knowledge, namely for contemplating God in his works, nor wisdom, for contemplating him in himself: on these, Isaiah 33: "The riches of salvation are wisdom and knowledge; the fear of the Lord is its treasure."

QUESTIONS.

I. It can be asked concerning what he says: The dead know nothing more.

This seems to be false: because the soul separated from the body is freer; but the freer a substance is from the body, the more suited it is for knowledge; therefore the dead are more apt for knowing; either, then, the soul perishes, or it knows.

II. Likewise, there is a difficulty regarding the statement that they have no more reward. This is manifestly heretical, because it takes away divine justice.

III. Likewise concerning what he says, that love and hatred have perished together. Against this: An evil will always remains; therefore if an evil will exists through love and hatred, therefore etc.

IV. Likewise concerning that exhortation: Go therefore and eat your bread and drink your wine, etc.

If we wish to respond to these things by saying that this is spoken in the person of Epicurus, then the answer is clear: but if we wish to maintain that it is spoken in his own person, it must be said that when he says that the dead know nothing, this is understood with regard to those things which are in this world: because they do not know these worldly things, nor are they in the memory of worldly people, nor do they have affection for worldly things. And this is clear from the text, because he says: Nor do they have a part in this world.

As for the objection raised about that exhortation, I say that that exhortation is not stated absolutely, but is a conclusion drawn from the aforementioned vanity. Hence he says: Go therefore, that is to say: if a man cannot discern whether in the present he ought to please God and in the future be rewarded — if this, I say, he cannot know, nor can he live forever — therefore he can rejoice without care. But that does not hold except on the supposition that the Lord does not reward differently in the future than in the present, which is the aforementioned vanity, which he will refute below in chapter eleven.

Secondly, security arises from the appearance of a defect in the governance of providence; which appearance is shown in two ways.

I turned myself to another thing, etc. As was said above, security exists among the wicked, first, because the retribution of justice seems to be lacking; second, because the governance of providence seems to be lacking. The deficiency that appears to exist in the retribution of justice has been treated; here secondly is treated the deficiency that appears to exist regarding governance.

of providence, for these things do not seem to be governed in an orderly way, but by chance. And this part has two sections, because first the defect of order is shown in general, second in particular, there: This wisdom also I have seen etc., concerning the disorder of authority.

First, the defect of order is shown in general.

Regarding the first point, he proceeds as follows. First he shows the disorder and chance that seems to pertain to the conduct of life: second, that which seems to pertain to the termination of death, there: Man does not know his end.

(Verse 11) Therefore, as regards the conduct of life, he shows the disorder, because things are not suited to those purposes for which they have fitness. On account of which he says: I turned myself to another thing, because above he considered retribution, here he turns himself to consider or-der. And I saw under the sun, and he enumerates five gratuitously given gifts which are defrauded of their proper acts: the race is not to the swift, though they are fit for running: Amos 2: "Flight shall perish from the swift." Nor the battle to the strong, though they are fit for fighting: 2 Kings 1: "The mighty have fallen, and the weapons of war have perished." Nor bread to the wise, though they are fit for distributing food; Isaiah 3: "I am not a healer, and in my house there is no bread." Nor riches to the learned, though they are fit for possessing all gifts; below in the same chapter: "There was found in it a poor man." Nor favor to the skilled, though they are fit for doing pleasing works: Isaiah 20: "All who worked well were confounded." But time and chance in all things, as if all things come by chance, and temporal things are subject to change: above in chapter 3: "All things have their time and pass away in their due seasons." And the aforesaid gifts are distinguished, because the first two regard the body: agility, for attaining what is desired, and strength for resisting evil inflicted; the following three regard the soul, namely wisdom in the heart, learning in the mouth, and skill in work.

(Verse 12.). Man does not know his end etc. Here the second is touched upon, namely the defect of governance regarding the termination of death, because it is determined for no one, neither when nor how he must die. And it seems that such and such a death comes about by chance. And therefore he says: Man does not know his end, that is, the day of death: Matthew twenty-four: "Watch therefore, for you do not know at what hour your Lord will come." And he gives an example of how there is disorder and uncertainty, saying: But as fish are caught by the hook, namely unknowingly, and as birds are caught by the snare, namely by deceit; Sirach eleven: "As a partridge is lured into a cage, and as a roe into a snare: so also is the heart of the proud." So are men caught in an evil time: First Thessalonians five: "When they shall say: Peace and security, sudden destruction shall come upon them." When it shall come upon them suddenly, that is, unexpectedly: Isaiah thirty: "Suddenly, while it is not expected, its destruction shall come, and it shall be broken as a potter's vessel."

Spiritually here is suggested the heavenly election of the good, and afterward the hidden damnation of the wicked. Therefore, in order to attribute the entire operation of good things to God, he says first: The race is not of the swift: because it is not from themselves that they run, but from God: Romans 9: "It is not of him who wills nor of him who runs, but of God who has mercy". Nor the battle of the strong, because in the Psalm: "The king is not saved by great strength". Nor bread of the wise, from themselves, but from God: John 6: "My Father gives you the true bread from heaven". Nor riches of the learned, namely of wisdom and knowledge, from themselves, but from God: 1 Corinthians 1: "In all things you have been made rich in him", namely in Christ. Nor favor of the skillful, from themselves, but from God: Psalm: "God loves mercy and truth; the Lord will give grace and glory".

But as to the hidden damnation of the wicked, he says: Man does not know his end: because " the way of the wicked is dark " etc. Like fish by a hook, that is, the pleasure-seekers, who are caught by the hook of pleasure: Habakkuk 1: "They lift up everything with a hook ". And like birds by a snare, that is, the ambitious by the snare of honor: 1 Timothy 3: " Not a neophyte, lest, puffed up with pride, he fall into the judgment of the devil ". So are men caught, that is, the greedy, in an evil time: Job 27: " When the rich man falls asleep, he will take nothing with him ".

QUESTIONS.


I. It can be doubted concerning what he says, that he saw time and chance in all things.

Everything therefore seems, according to this, to happen by chance.

But on the contrary. It seems that not all things happen by chance:

Because things which happen according to purpose do not happen by chance; but many things happen from purpose; therefore not all things happen by chance.

2. Furthermore, it seems that there is chance in nothing, because as Plato says, "there is nothing apart from the first cause whose origin a legitimate cause has not preceded": therefore according to this, nothing happens by chance, but all things proceed from a determinate cause.

3. Likewise, in every operation God is the principal agent; but whatever God does as principal agent, he does both knowingly and with purpose, and nothing of this kind happens by chance: therefore nothing at all happens by chance.

I respond: it must be said that chance and fortune are causes by accident, not because they are other causes from causes by themselves, but because when nature and intellect do something to which they are directly ordered, they do this by themselves, and there is no chance there; but when from their operation something happens to which they are not directly ordered, on account of the concurrence of another cause, there is chance: as is clear from the creditor found in the marketplace and the stone falling on someone's head. I say therefore that if the outcomes of things are compared to the first cause, which is God: since God foresees all things, nothing is by chance according to the ordering of divine foreknowledge. But if we speak by comparison to created causes, either concerning immutable ones, such as the motion of the higher bodies, or concerning mutable ones, such as the lower causes which are under the sun: in the disposition of these mutable causes, it is hardly the case that some unintended effect does not occur, indeed beyond intention: and therefore chance is found in all things, not because all things happen by chance, but because around every mutable creature something happens by chance.

3. What is objected regarding divine foreknowledge is now clear. For chance is not spoken of in relation to divine foreknowledge.

2. To the second objection, that all things have a cause: it must be said that it is true that all things have a legitimate cause, namely nature, or intellect , but nevertheless by this it is not excluded that something can act or does act outside the order, God excepted.

II. The question is raised concerning what he says: As fish are caught by the hook: whether the catching of fish is by chance or not. That it is so, is shown as follows:

I. Because the fact that this fish comes more than another is not by nature nor by reason, therefore it is by chance.

2. Likewise, that one man obtains what another does not — this is not by reason of nature or of skill: therefore it is by chance.

But against this: If someone intending to find gold were to dig, it would not be chance: therefore since one who fishes intends to catch, there is no chance involved.

I respond: it must be said that one can speak of capture in comparison to the ones catching, and thus it is not by chance; one can also speak in comparison to the fish, which go to the hook: and because they go on account of food, and something else befalls them, with respect to them there is chance.

Second, the defect of order in the governance of providence is shown in particular, and this in three ways.

This wisdom also I have seen under the sun. He determined above the deficiency of the governance of providence in general: here he intends to show in particular, namely concerning prelacy, because the wise are despised and fools are promoted. There are therefore in this section three parts. In the first he shows the abasement of the wise man: in the second he adds the commendation of the abased, at: And I said that better etc. ; in the third he introduces the promotion of the fool, at: If the spirit of him who has power etc.

The downfall of the wise man is therefore described in this order. First, the praise of wisdom is set forth; second, the fall is expressed, in which there is a failure of power; third, the aid of wisdom is noted; and fourth, the contempt of the wise man.

(Vers. 13.). Therefore the praise of wisdom is set forth first, when he says: I have seen this wisdom under the sun, that is, I have known it by its effect in worldly things: and I proved it to be very great, that is, I approved and praised it as exceedingly great. For wisdom is great in conquering an inferior enemy; greater, in conquering an equal; greatest, in conquering the most powerful. Hence it is said in Job 26 concerning the supremely wise one: "His wisdom struck the proud one."

A small city etc. Here is noted secondly the defect of power, and this with respect to resisting an attacker: therefore he says: A small city, and few men in it

and this word must be supplied: "was," or it must be construed with the following verb. The siege was complete. And because it was small and had few men, it was therefore weak in defending itself; whence Jehoshaphat said in 2 Chronicles 20: "In us there is not such great strength that we can resist this great multitude," because, namely, they were few. Especially then they cannot resist, when the attacker is strong; on account of which he says: A great king came against it, namely, through his power; and he surrounded it with ramparts, through great malice; and he built siege works all around, for battle; and the siege was complete, through the inability to resist. An example of this is found in Judith 7 concerning Bethulia, which was besieged by Holofernes. The Lord also foretold this to Jerusalem in Luke 19: "Your enemies will surround you with a rampart and hem you in on every side."

(Vers. 15.). And there was found in it etc. Here the third thing is noted, namely the succor of wisdom, where there was a lack of power. Therefore he says: And there was found in it a poor man, and thus powerless, and wise, and he delivered the city by his wisdom. An example of this, how a wise person delivered, is found concerning Judith in her book, how she delivered Bethulia from Holofernes by her wisdom, though she was weak. Another example is found in 2 Kings 20, where it is said that a wise person in Abel delivered the city from the assault of Joab. Against this it is said in Baruch 3: "And because they did not have wisdom, they perished on account of their foolishness"; this is said of the mighty.

And no one afterwards etc. Here the fourth point is touched upon, namely contempt for the wise deliverer, the sign of which is forgetfulness. Therefore he says: No one afterwards remembered that poor man. Behold, contempt and ingratitude: against which it is said in Sirach twenty-nine: "Do not forget the kindness of your guarantor, for he gave his life for you " etc.

Spiritually four things are to be considered here: what the city is, who the great king is, who the poor wise man is, and how no one remembers him.

The city is the Church: the Psalm: Glorious things are said of you, O city of God. This city is small through humility, because in these alone the Lord dwells: Isaiah, last chapter: "To whom shall I look, if not to the poor and contrite in spirit and trembling at my words?" Few men in it, in comparison with the wicked: Matthew 20: "Many are called, but few are chosen." The great king is the devil, of whom it is said in the second to last chapter of Job: "He is king over all the sons of pride"; he is also great, according to what is said in the same place: "There is no power on earth that can be compared to him." This king comes against the city through temptations; surrounds it, by offering manifold pleasures; he built fortifications, by procuring occasions of sin; and perfectly besieges it, when he entangles it in transgressions of the commandments. On these things, Luke 19: "Your enemies will surround you with a rampart and hem you in and cast you to the ground, and they will not leave a stone upon a stone."

A poor and wise man, who is Christ, who is a man from the strength of virtue: Jeremiah 31: "A woman shall compass a man." He is also poor: 2 Corinthians 8: "Though he was rich in all things, for your sake he became poor"; wise, indeed Wisdom itself: 1 Corinthians 1: "We call Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God." He was found by those who seek: Matthew 7: "For everyone who asks receives, and he who seeks finds." He delivered the people, Isaiah 19: "They shall cry to the Lord because of the oppressor, and he shall send them a savior and a defender, who shall deliver them"; he delivered by his wisdom, though he could have done so by power: Job 26: "By his wisdom he struck the proud one." - And no one afterwards remembered him, because poverty is despised: Proverbs 19: "The brothers of a poor man hate him; moreover his friends have withdrawn far from him." No one remembered him by having compassion, as it is said in Lamentations 3: "Remember my poverty and my transgression, the wormwood and the gall"; but on the contrary, Amos 6: "They suffered nothing over the affliction of Joseph." By giving thanks: Luke 17: "And there was not found any who returned to give glory to God except this stranger." By imitating in deed: Isaiah 17: "You shall be desolate, because you have forgotten the God of your salvation, and have not remembered your strong helper." By confessing with the mouth: Genesis 40: "When prosperity followed, the butler forgot his interpreter."

And I said etc. The contempt and forgetfulness of the wise man has been shown; here is shown secondly the commendation of the despised one. He commends wisdom with a threefold preeminence: first, by setting it above the strength of power; second, above the defense of armament; third, above the height of worldly glory.

(Vers. 16.17.). He therefore first sets wisdom before strength in his judgment. Therefore he says: And I said that wisdom is better than strength; Wisdom 6: "Better is wisdom than strength, and a prudent man better than a strong one." And because it is better, therefore it should not be despised: and yet nonetheless it is despised. Therefore he says: How then is the wisdom of the poor man despised? as though it should not be despised: but on account of poverty it is held in contempt: whence in Sirach 13 it is said of the poor man: "He spoke sensibly, and no place was given to him: the rich man spoke, and all were silent, and they carry his word up to the clouds." And a sign of the contempt of wisdom is added: And his words were not heard: and yet they are very much to be heard: Proverbs 8: "Now therefore, children, hear me: Blessed are those who keep my ways." And that the words of the wise are to be heard, he adds: The words of the wise are heard in silence, that is, they are worthy to be heard in silence: Sirach 32: "Listen in silence, and for your reverence good grace will come to you"; whence it is also added: "And do not presume to speak in the midst of great men." More than the cry of a ruler among fools, and his speech is more clamorous, because however much a man may cry out, the fool does not pay attention: Sirach 22: "He who tells wisdom to a fool speaks with one who is sleeping, and at the end of the narration he says: 'Who is this?'" That wisdom is therefore despised, this is because of the foolishness of the hearers: therefore Matthew 7: "Do not give what is holy to dogs." And the reason for this is given by the Apostle in 1 Corinthians 2: "The natural man does not perceive the things that are of the Spirit of God."

(Vers. 18.). Better is wisdom etc. Here secondly he sets wisdom before the defense of armor, saying: Better is wisdom than weapons of war, because weapons of war are for protection; similarly wisdom protects: Proverbs 2: "Counsel shall keep you, and prudence shall preserve you, that you may be delivered from the evil way and from the man who speaks perverse things." And that it protects better is clear, because he who loses wisdom through fault cannot defend himself by armor without losing many good things; whence he says: And he who shall sin in one thing, by departing from wisdom, according to what was said above in chapter 8: "Do not hasten to depart from his face, and do not persist in an evil work." Shall lose many good things: for by sinning in one thing, he loses wisdom, and by losing it, he loses many good things: Wisdom 7: "All good things came to me together with her"; and therefore, when she is lost, many good things are lost. Or: he who shall sin in one thing, against charity, shall lose many good things, according to that saying in James 2: "Whoever sins in one thing is made guilty of all." — And he makes this clear through a metaphor, because the awareness of sin is compared to a dying fly, because it produces and causes abomination in the affections and conscience.
