Chapter XI.


Secondly, a remedy is given against the vanity of complacency arising from the lack of the retribution of justice.

Cast your bread etc. He set forth above a remedy against the fact that the governance of providence seemed to fail due to the disorder of authority; here secondly he sets forth a remedy against the fact that the retribution of justice seemed to fail, on account of which man felt secure in evil and was sluggish toward good. This remedy consists in the consideration of future retribution; and since God bestows good things on those who do good and repays evil to those who do evil, therefore first, from the consideration of reward, he invites to good; secondly, from the consideration of punishment, he calls back from evil, there: If a man lives many years.

First, he invites to the good by consideration of the reward.

But the good to which he invites is the good of piety, which "is profitable for all things," and on account of which the just are especially saved, as is clear in Matthew twenty-five, where only works of mercy are enumerated. Therefore he exhorts here to works of mercy and almsgiving on account of retribution.

And first he exhorts that they be done willingly; second, that they be done abundantly, there: Give portions to seven; third, that they be done unceasingly, there: He who observes the wind never etc.

Therefore, since he exhorts that they be done willingly and gladly, he first sets forth the exhortation and then adds the reason.

(Vers. 1.). He exhorts therefore to willingly giving alms: therefore he says: Cast your bread, that is, give willingly and generously, not by extortion and as if compelled: Second Corinthians 9: "Each one, as he has determined in his heart, not from sadness or from necessity: for God loves a cheerful giver." Your bread, not another's; Sirach 34: "He who offers sacrifice from the substance of the poor is like one who slays a son in the sight of his father." Upon the passing waters. Waters many are many peoples, Revelation 17: passing waters are those who pass from nation to nation and from kingdom to another people: such are pilgrims and strangers: to these alms should be given; Isaiah 58: "Break your bread for the hungry, and bring the needy and wandering into your house." Or: waters are present tribulations, according to that of the Psalm: "The waters have come in even unto my soul": passing waters are the poor and afflicted, because through tribulations they pass to rest: to such alms should be given: Luke 14: (When you give alms, invite the poor and the weak, the blind, etc. And the reason is given: Because after a long time you shall find it, that is, the recompense for it: therefore the recompense does not fail, but is deferred: Sirach 12: "Do good to the just, and you shall find great recompense: and if not from him, certainly from the Lord": likewise Luke 16: "Make for yourselves friends of the mammon of iniquity," etc.

(Vers. 2. 8.). Give a portion to seven etc. Here he secondly exhorts to give abundantly: and he sets forth the ex-hortation and adds the reason. He exhorts therefore to abundance saying: Give a portion to seven, and also to eight, that is, according to the capacity of your strength give alms, so that whether you are poor or rich, you give a seventh and eighth of your goods, that is, you make the portion of the poor generous: 2 Corinthians 8: "That your abundance may supply their want"; and Luke 11: "Give alms from what you have left over". Therefore he says: Give a portion to seven: for by the number seven we understand what suffices to supply one's own necessity; whatever is beyond that is called seven and eight: and this is to be distributed to the poor, not to be gathered into earthly treasures, but heavenly ones: whence Matthew 6: "Store up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where neither rust nor moth destroys"; and Tobit 4: "If you have much, give abundantly: if you have little, strive to share even that willingly". And the reason for this generosity is given, when it is added: Because you do not know what evil etc. And a manifold reason is given, namely on account of caution against evil; whence he says: You do not know what evil will be upon the earth. Because therefore you are not certain, you need to provide for yourself, lest you fall into danger: and the best remedy is abundant almsgiving: Sirach 29: "Store up almsgiving in the bosom of the poor, and it will pray for you and deliver you from all evil".

On account of the amplification of merit he says: If the clouds have been filled, supply "by God," they will pour out rain upon the earth, on account of which rain the earth bears fruit: Hosea 6: "He will come to us like the rain, the early and the latter rain to the earth"; and this is for bearing fruit and multiplying the goods of the earth by the merit of almsgiving. For just as full clouds drench the earth with rain, so multiplied alms drench the soul with grace: Second Corinthians 9: "He who supplies seed to the sower will both provide bread for eating and will multiply your seed and increase the harvest of the fruits of your righteousness." On account of the stability of the reward, which cannot be changed or lost: on account of which he says: If a tree falls to the south: the tree is man: concerning which Job 14: "A tree has hope; if it has been cut down, it will grow green again." And the south wind gives to understand the warmth of charity and the state of glory: Song of Songs 4:

"Arise, north wind, and come, south wind: blow through my garden," etc. Therefore, for the tree to fall to the south is for a person to be brought into glory at death; and then there it will be, that is, it will never be able to be moved; Revelation 3: "He who overcomes, I will make him a pillar in the temple of my God, and he shall go out no more." If it falls to the north, there it will be, that is, if to infernal punishment; Jeremiah 1: "From the north shall all evil be spread"; whoever falls into hell, there it will be, because from there he will never be freed. For to the just one it is said in the last chapter of Isaiah: "Their fire shall not be extinguished, and their worm shall not die." In whatever place it falls, there it will be, whether for good or for ill; Galatians 6: "Whatever a person sows, that also shall he reap."

He who observes the wind etc. Here he exhorts to give unceasingly: and first he teaches that times are not to be observed: second, that the divine works are not to be scrutinized: third, that works of mercy are to be continued.

(Vers. 4.). As to the first point he says: He who observes the wind will not sow, because frequently a wind comes that one does not want, and the wind that would please does not come. And he who considers the clouds will never reap: and therefore it is necessary that when it is time to reap, one should reap, and when it is time to sow, one should sow, and not wait. So also in the matter at hand, while one has time, let him do good: Sirach 4: "Do not afflict the heart of the needy, and do not delay a gift to one in distress"; and Proverbs 3: "Do not say to your friend: Go and come back, tomorrow I will give to you, when you can give at once": and thus one ought not to delay.

(Vers. 5.). As you do not know what the way is etc. Above he said that times are not to be observed; here secondly he teaches that divine works are not to be examined or scrutinized, because we cannot. Therefore he says: As you do not know what the way of the spirit is. The spirit, which is God, because we are ignorant of this; whence John 3: "The Spirit breathes where he wills, and you hear his voice, but you do not know" etc.; and Job 9: "If he comes to me, I will not see him; if he departs, I will not understand." The spirit, which is the soul or spiritual creature, whose way, that is, origin, is not known, nor even its departure; above in chapter 3: "Who knows if the spirit of the sons of Adam ascends upward?" The spirit, which is the wind, whose way is not known, that is, its origin; whence in the Psalm: "Who brings forth the winds from his storehouses"; Exodus 15: "Your spirit blew, and the sea covered them." He says therefore: As you do not know what the way of the spirit is, and

by what reason the bones are joined together in the womb of a pregnant woman, that is, how they are fitted together and differentiated: because even Avicenna himself says that the formative power of the members is a divine power: 2 Maccabees 7: "I know not how you appeared in my womb: for I neither gave you spirit and soul, nor did I fashion the members of each of you": but he fashioned them, to whom Job 10 says: "You have knit me together with bones and sinews." Just as you are ignorant of these things, which are carnal: so you are ignorant of the works of the Lord, who is the maker of all things: works, that is, hidden judgments, of which it says in the Psalm: "Great are the works of the Lord, sought out according to all his purposes." These works we do not know: Romans 11: "O the depth of the riches of the wisdom and knowledge of God! How incomprehensible are his judgments, and how unsearchable his ways!"

In the morning sow your seed etc. Here he thirdly exhorts to doing good unceasingly: and first the exhortation: is set forth; second, the reason for the exhortation, is subjoined; and third, the recompense.

(Vers. 6.). He exhorts therefore to doing works of mercy unceasingly, for he says: In the morning sow your seed, that is, in the time of youth, because, Lamentations 3, "it is good for a man when he has borne the yoke from his youth." And in the evening let not your hand cease, that is, in old age, as if to say that at every time and age good works are to be done without ceasing: Galatians last chapter: "And let us not grow weary in doing good, for in due season we shall reap, if we do not falter." Therefore one must sow continually, because, as is said in Second Corinthians 9, "he who sows sparingly will also reap sparingly, and he who sows in blessings will also reap from blessings."

Because you do not know etc. Here the second point is noted, namely the reason for the exhortation: because man does not know when and which works God accepts, just as one who sows does not know which seed bears fruit. Therefore he says: Because you do not know which will rise up more: this, that is, the evening sowing, or that, that is, the morning sowing: above in the ninth chapter: "Man does not know whether he is worthy of love or hatred." And if both together, it will be better, because "more good things are to be preferred over fewer"; Isaiah thirty-two: "Blessed are you who sow beside all waters."

(Vers. 7.). Sweet is the light etc. Here the third thing is noted, namely recompense: and this is the sight of the light and of the eternal sun, in which there is the highest sweetness, and thus the recompense is great. And therefore he says: Sweet is the light, to behold. Concerning this sweetness, in the Psalm: "How great is the multitude of your sweetness, O Lord" etc. And it is delightful for the eyes to see the sun, that sun, of which Malachi 4 says: "The sun of justice shall rise for you who fear God." This sun it is delightful to see: therefore 1 Peter 1: "Into whom the Angels desire to look." And because only those who will be at the right hand will see that light: therefore in the Psalm: "Delights at your right hand forever."

These things can also be briefly explained concerning spiritual almsgiving, which consists in the instruction of one's neighbor: to which he similarly exhorts, to give willingly or liberally, abundantly and unceasingly:

willingly or generously, when he says: Cast your bread etc., that is, give freely; Matthew 10: "Freely you have received, freely give"; bread, namely of doctrine, of which is said in Lamentations 4: "The little ones asked for bread, and there was no one to break it for them." Upon the passing waters, that is, those who despise temporal goods, to whom alone it should be given: Matthew 7: "Do not give what is holy to dogs." Or: upon those of whom it is said in the Psalm: "We passed through fire and water, and you brought us out" etc.

Abundantly also to be distributed: therefore he says: Give

. seven parts: seven parts we understand as the doctrine of Christ in the Old Testament, because there the seventh day was commanded to be observed. By eight we understand the doctrine of the New Testament, because the eighth day is commanded to be observed. And because a man cannot abundantly communicate doctrine unless he is filled from above, he says: If the clouds are filled, they will pour out rain upon the earth. Clouds are good preachers, who fly to the heights through contemplation: Isaiah 60: "Who are these who fly like clouds and like doves to their windows?" These are filled by the Lord with knowledge: Isaiah 11: "The earth is filled with your knowledge, as the water of the covering sea": and this through the Holy Spirit: Wisdom 1: "The Spirit of the Lord has filled the whole world." These clouds, once filled, will pour out rain, that is, the doctrine of the divine word; Sirach 39: "He himself as.

showers, will send forth the words of his wisdom"; Isaiah fifty-five: "As the rain and snow come down from heaven and inebriate the earth and soak it: so shall my word be," upon the earth of the heart for bearing fruit; Hebrews six: "The earth drinking in the rain that comes often upon it, and bringing forth herbs meet" etc.

Unceasingly it must also be dispensed: therefore he says: He who observes the wind does not sow. To sow is to preach the word of God: Matthew 13: "The sower went out to sow his seed": and afterward he explains: "The seed is the word of God." This wind is the wind of detraction, of which Job 1 says: "A wind came from the region of the desert and struck the four corners of the house." He who watches this wind of detraction never sows, because he who fears the words of detracting men does not preach unto fruit, but rather is tossed about like a reed: Matthew 11: "What did you go out to see? A reed shaken by the wind"? And he who observes the clouds will never reap. The clouds, as the Gloss says, are the blandishment of flattery, which he who desires to have never reaps eternal life, because "he has received his reward," Matthew 6: and he sows in the flesh, and therefore will reap corruption, Galatians last chapter: "He who sows in the flesh" etc. And therefore these things must be despised, but one must preach morning and evening; 2 Timothy 4: "Preach the word, be urgent in season, out of season"; Acts 20: "For three years I did not cease day and night, admonishing each one of you with tears."

Second, he calls back from evil by consideration of future punishment.

If a man live many years etc. Above he exhorted to good by consideration of reward; here he intends to call back from evil by consideration of future punishment: and indeed he does this in this order. First he says that memory of the future time must be held: second he reproves present gladness; third he exhorts to cast away all wickedness and the abundance of malice.

(Vers. 8.). He exhorts and admonishes us to keep the memory of the future time, however much a person may abound in present prosperity. For this reason he says: If a person has lived many years, because truly, even if they seem many, they nevertheless cannot be many, according to that saying in Sirach 18: "The number of a man's days, at most a hundred years." And in all these has rejoiced, according to what is said of those in Job 21: "They hold the timbrel and rejoice at the sound of the organ: they spend their days in good things," etc. He ought to remember the dark time, that is, the day of judgment, which will be a dark time; Zephaniah 1: "That day, a day of wrath, a day of darkness and gloom"; and of the many days. These are the days or years of eternity: in the Psalm: "One day in your courts is better than a thousand." He ought to remember the day of judgment, so that he may fear; he ought to remember glory, so that he may hope and rejoice, and this according to the alternation of works: hence Sirach 11: "In the day of good things, be not unmindful of evil things, and in the day of evil things, be not unmindful of good things." Of the days, I say, many, which when they have come, the things past will be convicted of vanity, that is, they will be manifestly shown to have been vain: hence Jerome: When the kingdom of Christ has come, the other kingdoms will vanish. The things past will be convicted, namely of the vanity of mutability: Wisdom 5: "What has pride profited us? Or what has the boasting of riches brought us? All these things have passed away like a shadow." They will be convicted of the vanity of iniquity: Sirach 17: "What is more wicked than what flesh and blood has devised?" And these things will be convicted: Ephesians 5: "For the things that are reproved by the light are made manifest."

(Verse 9). Rejoice therefore, O young man, etc. Here secondly he reproves present joy, asserting that man must be judged for it; whence speaking ironically he says: Rejoice therefore, O young man, in your youth, because that age is suited to joy; whence Wisdom 2: "Come, let us enjoy the creature as in youth swiftly"; as if to say: do not rejoice in youth, lest you grieve in old age; whence Sirach 5: "Do not follow the desire of your heart in your strength." And he explains the joy so that it is complete both in the mind and in deed and in outward sign. As for the joy of delight in the mind, he says: And let your heart be in what is good, as if to say: do not rejoice in heart, because above in chapter 7: "The heart of the fool is where joy is." As for delight in deed: And walk in the ways of your heart, that is, outwardly fulfill in deed what you desire in heart, as if to say: do not walk thus; Sirach 18: "My son, do not go after your lusts, and turn away from your own will." As for joy in outward sign, he says: And in the sight of your eyes, so that the eye may gaze upon what the heart desires and your hand performs, according to what is said of those of whom 2 Peter 2 says: "Having eyes full of adultery and unceasing sin," as if to say: do not walk thus; Isaiah 3: "Because the daughters of Zion are haughty and walk with outstretched necks and wanton eyes," etc. And that this is said ironically is clear, because he threatens evil to those who rejoice thus; whence he adds: And know that for all these things God will bring you into judgment. And therefore all these things are to be fled; Job 19: "Flee from the face of the sword, for the sword is the avenger of iniquity, and know that there is judgment."

(Vers. 10.). Remove anger etc. Here thirdly he exhorts to cast away all wickedness, namely malice of heart and impurity of flesh. As for malice of heart he says: Remove anger from your heart, so that you may not be angry, because anger makes a man wicked: Matthew 5: "But I say to you, that everyone who is angry with his brother shall be guilty of judgment"; and if he is angry, he ought not to persist: whence Ephesians 4: "Let not the sun go down upon your anger." As for impurity of flesh he says: And remove evil from your flesh, and thus he excludes all impurity of the flesh: James 1: "Casting away all uncleanness and abundance of wickedness, receive with meekness the engrafted word." He removes evil from the flesh who mortifies it: Colossians 3: "Mortify your members which are upon the earth." And the reason for this is given: For youth and pleasure are vain, because they quickly pass away: 1 John 2: "The world passes away and its concupiscence," and therefore Wisdom 5: "What has pride profited us? Or what has the boasting of riches brought us? All these things have passed away like a shadow" etc.
