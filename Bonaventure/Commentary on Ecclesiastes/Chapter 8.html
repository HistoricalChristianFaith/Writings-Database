Chapter VIII.


Second, against the concupiscence that has been discovered, the remedy of wisdom is given from a fourfold consideration.

Who is like the wise man? Above he described the vanity of concupiscence. Here against that vanity he intends to give the remedy of wisdom: for no one is rescued from concupiscence except he who walks with wisdom. He therefore exhorts in this part to cling to wisdom from a fourfold consideration: first from the consideration of its nobility; second, of its power; third, of its usefulness; fourth, of the opportuneness of time. He therefore exhorts to cling to wisdom by consideration of its nobility, which is weighed in this, that it renders a man noble and glorious both with regard to appearance and with regard to speech. And this he first sets forth by asking, then confirms by answering.

(Ch. 7, v. 30.). Who is such, as the wise man? that is, who is so remarkable; Wisdom 8: "Because of her I shall have glory among the multitudes, and honor among the elders and the young," he speaks of wisdom. Who is so remarkable in word? whence he says: And who knows the explanation of the word? supply: except the wise man; Wisdom 8: "He knows the subtleties of speeches," etc.; and therefore in doubtful matters the wise man is to be reverently heard; thus Wisdom 8: "The faces of princes shall wonder at me; when I am silent they shall wait for me, and when I speak they shall look upon me, and when I speak at greater length, they shall lay their hand upon their mouth.

(Vers. 1.). He proposed by inquiring, now he confirms by responding: therefore he says: The wisdom of a man shines in his countenance: Proverbs 17: "In the face of the prudent, wisdom shines," that is, it appears in his works: Ecclesiasticus 19: "By his appearance a man is known, and by the meeting of his face the sensible man is recognized." For just as a good man is known by his countenance, so a wise man by his works. Nor is it surprising if he is remarkable in appearance, because God does this: whence he says: And the Most Powerful will change his face. This Most Powerful one is God; Job 36: "God does not cast away the mighty, since he himself is mighty"; and further: "Behold, God is exalted in his strength, and none is like him among lawgivers." He changes the face, the outward one, by first changing the inward one, that is, by turning it toward himself. For the face of the soul is the higher portion of reason, by which it is born to see God. This face God alone changes; Proverbs 21: "As the divisions of waters, so is the heart of the king in the hand of the Lord; wherever he wills, he turns it."

(Verse 2.). The wise man is worthy of regard not only with respect to appearance, but also with respect to his word through wisdom; in whose person he says: I observe the mouth of the king, says wisdom. This King is God, of whom 1 Timothy last chapter says: "Blessed and alone mighty, King of kings and Lord of lords." The mouth of this King is the Son of God, through whom he speaks to us; Hebrews 1: "In these last days he has spoken to us in the Son." Therefore to observe the mouth of the king is to attend diligently to the word of the Son of God; Luke 11: "Blessed are those who hear the word of God and keep it." Wisdom therefore says: I observe, and I cause others to observe, according to that passage of Deuteronomy 6: "Observe, that you may do what the Lord has commanded you, and it may be well with you." And he explains in what things he observes, namely in the commandments; therefore he says: And the precepts of the oath of God, which namely proceed from his mouth. And I observe, namely, the precepts of the oath of God: they are called precepts of the oath because they are firm and inviolable; Matthew 24: "Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away." Or: they are called precepts of the oath because they were given with a covenant and an oath; Exodus 24: "Moses took blood and sprinkled it upon the people, saying: This is the blood of the covenant of the Lord concerning all these words."

(Vers. 3. 4.). Do not hasten etc. Here secondly he exhorts to cling to wisdom by consideration of his power, when he says: Do not hasten to depart from his face, that is, do not turn aside from his will: and this on account of sin of heart, as though you would hide yourself from him. Concerning this departure, Deuteronomy thirty-two:

"He forsook God who made him, and departed from God his salvation." Lest therefore you depart from his face, wishing to hide in evil: Sirach sixteen: "Do not say: I shall be hidden from God, and from on high who will remember me"? As Cain, who said to the Lord: "Behold, you cast me out today from your face, and from your face I shall be hidden." And if it happens that you depart, do not remain in evil: therefore he adds: Neither remain in an evil work: Sirach five: "Do not delay to be converted to the Lord, and do not defer from day to day." And the reason for this is the consideration of divine power, because he acts omnipotently: Because whatever he wills, he will do: Psalm: "All things whatsoever he willed, he did"; and Isaiah forty-six: "My counsel shall stand, and all my will shall be done." - He also acts easily; therefore he says: And his word is full of power, Psalm: "The voice of the Lord in power, the voice of the Lord breaking the cedars": and Jeremiah thirty-two: "No word shall be difficult for you, who show mercy unto thousands." He also acts irreproachably: Nor can anyone say to him: Why do you act thus? Romans nine: "Who are you, that you answer back to God"?

(Vers. 8.). He who keeps the commandment etc. Here thirdly he exhorts us to cling to wisdom by consideration of our benefit. For there is great benefit in clinging to it and keeping its commands. On account of which he says: He who keeps the commandment shall experience nothing evil: indeed he shall have many good things: Matthew 19: "If you wish to enter into life, keep the commandments"; and John 8: "If you abide in my word, you shall truly be my disciples, and you shall know the truth, and the truth shall set you free."

(Vers. 5. 6. 7. 8.). A time and a response etc. Here fourthly he exhorts to keeping the commandments of divine wisdom by consideration of the opportuneness of time, which a wise man considers in his works. On account of which he says: The heart of the wise man understands a time and a response: he understands the time in which one ought to act: Ephesians 5: "See, brothers, how you walk carefully, not as unwise, but as wise, redeeming the time, because the days are evil." Not only the time, but also the response in time: Colossians 4: "Walk in wisdom toward those who are outside, redeeming the time. Let your speech always be seasoned with the salt of grace, so that you may know how you ought to respond to each person."

And the wise man does well who considers the opportune time; which he proves when he adds: For every matter, that is, in every matter, there is a time and opportunity, and much affliction of man, supply: in diligently considering these things; above in chapter three: "All things have their time, and all things pass away in their own seasons under the sun." For there is an opportune time for studying: Sirach thirty-eight: "Write wisdom in a time of leisure." There is also an opportune time for praying: Psalm: "At midnight I rose to give thanks to you." There is also an opportune time for doing good: Galatians six: "While we have time, let us do good to all."

And this opportunity is in the present time: and he shows this to be in the present time, showing that the future time is dangerous, because it cannot be foreseen: whence he said: He who is ignorant of the past can know future things by no messenger, because future things can be known much less than past things: above in chapter seven: "Who can tell him what shall be after him?"

Nor can it be prevented, whence he says: Nor is it in man's power, that is, authority, to prevent the spirit, namely from departing, because no one can prevent the time of death from coming. And he explains this by adding: Nor does he have power in the day of death; Job 14: "You have appointed his limits, which cannot be passed." It cannot be avoided: whence he says: Nor is he allowed to rest, when war presses upon him, namely on the day of judgment; Wisdom 5: "The world shall fight for him against the senseless." Which war the wicked shall not be able to avoid; whence in Revelation 6 they will say to the mountains: "Fall upon us," etc., because then "flight shall perish." Nor can it be concealed, therefore he says: Wickedness shall not save the wicked, as though he could pretend to be good: Job 36: "He does not save the wicked, and he grants judgment to the poor"; and Proverbs 11: "Treasures shall not profit in the day of vengeance: justice shall deliver from death": therefore Job 10: "If I am wicked, woe is me."

Questions.


1. There is a question concerning what he says: I observe the precepts of the oath of God: therefore God swears.

To the contrary: I. Matthew five: Let your speech be: yes, yes: no, no: therefore what is more than this is from evil. Therefore if it is not fitting for the perfect to swear, and nothing is fitting for God except what is of the highest perfection, therefore to swear does not befit God.

2. Furthermore, it is shown that it is superfluous, because God cannot lie; therefore the simple word of God is equally as true as an oath.

3. Likewise, it seems that he cannot swear: because to swear is to bring someone forward as witness and confirmation: but God does not have another by whom he could confirm: therefore neither can he swear.

I respond: it must be said that an oath is the end of all dispute, and it is a pure and firm assertion, than which none can be firmer, and this for the purpose of producing certain belief. Thus, when we wish to produce certain belief in the hearer, we swear; so God, condescending to our weakness, indeed swears, in order to render our faith solid.

1. As for the objection, therefore, that it is from evil: it is explained that it is from the evil of punishment, not of the one swearing, but of the one to whom the oath is made, that is, on account of his doubt.

Nevertheless, it must be said otherwise that the Lord does not there prohibit the oath as something evil; but because men are prone to speaking falsehood, He prohibits the readiness to swear indiscriminately, lest at some point they fall simultaneously into lying and swearing to the contempt of God. And because God cannot lie, this reasoning has no place with respect to God.

2. To the objection that he cannot lie: I say that even though an oath in God does not add anything beyond a simple assertion as regards the reality itself, it nevertheless does add something according to our estimation, because faith is made firmer for us, and a wondrous condescension toward us is shown.

3. To the objection that He cannot bring forward another witness: I say that just as He is His own witness, because He is truth itself, so He swears by Himself; and this is the best testimony, because light manifests both itself and other things.

II. Concerning this there is also a doubt, because he says: He who keeps the commandment shall experience nothing evil. Against this: below in the same book: There are just men to whom evils happen, as though they had done the works of the wicked.

I respond: there is experiencing an evil that remains and an evil that passes away: and he speaks here of the experience of an evil that always remains, not of one that passes away

III. There is also a question about what he says: He who keeps the commandment, etc.

1. Since there are many precepts to be observed, why does he say "precept" in the singular?

2. Furthermore, it is possible for someone to keep the divine commandments out of fear, as the Jews kept them; and yet such people are not saved.

I respond: it must be said that although the precepts are many through the diversity of works, nevertheless they have unity both in comparison to the one commanding and in comparison to the one observing and in comparison to the end. In relation to the one commanding, because he who commands is one; and therefore whoever transgresses one commandment cannot be obedient to the one commanding, because he who commands is one. — In comparison to the one observing there is unity, because he observes all things by one principle and governing rule, namely charity, which conquers all things. Whence, just as charity either loves all or none, so it observes all commandments or none. In comparison to the end there is unity, because one reward is owed, and they are to be observed for one reward. Because therefore this threefold unity converges in the due observance of the commandments, he rightly says: He who keeps the commandment, etc.

Art. 2. On the second occasion of sin, which is the vanity of false security, treated in two ways.

I considered all these things. He treated above of the vanity of inclination; here he treats of the vanity of false security: and since this vanity is dangerous, because it drives man to sin, or holds him fast once driven: therefore first is set forth the vanity: second, the remedy of vanity, there: He who digs a pit will fall into it, below in chapter ten .

First, the vanity of security is shown according to its twofold origin.

Security arises in two ways: first, namely from the appearance of a lack of just retribution; second, from the appearance of a lack of providential governance, and this second is touched upon there: I turned myself to other things and saw that the race is not to the swift, etc., below in chapter nine.

First, security arises from the appearance of a defect in just retribution, which appearance is set forth for a threefold reason.

The retribution of justice, however, appears to fail because of the exaltation of the wicked, because of the oppression of the good, because of the indistinction of both: there are therefore three parts here. First it is shown how the vanity of security arises from the promotion of the wicked: second, how from the oppression of the good, at: There is also another vanity which occurs upon the earth, etc.; third, how it arises from the indistinction of these and those, at: And I applied my heart to know wisdom, etc.

First, concerning the exaltation of the wicked.

The origin, therefore, of security from the exaltation of the wicked is described in this order. First, the elevation of the wicked is introduced; second, how from this there arises for the impious the vanity of security; third, how from this is drawn the benefit of the good.

(Vers. 9. 10.). First, therefore, the exaltation of the wicked is indicated, which Ecclesiastes considered with his diligent consideration. For which reason he says: I considered all these things, supply: which I stated before. And I gave my heart to all the works that are done under the sun, so that I might consider all things: above in chapter one, "I gave my heart to know wisdom and learning, and errors and folly." And in this consideration he himself saw the exaltation of the wicked both in power and in reputation. As for the exaltation in power, he says: Sometimes a man has dominion over another man to his own harm, namely when a wicked man is in charge, because a good man rules to his own good when he rules well: whence 1 Timothy 3: "If anyone desires the office of bishop, he desires a good work"; but a wicked man to his own harm: whence Sirach 7: "Do not bind upon yourself double sins, for even in one you will not be guiltless." A wicked prelate binds upon himself double sins.

As for exaltation in fame, he says: I saw the wicked buried, namely by the depth of despair: Proverbs 18: "When the wicked man comes into the depth of sins, he despises." Or: buried, by a heap of flattery: Matthew 8: "Let the dead bury their dead"; Gregory: "The dead man buries the dead, when he overwhelms with a heap of flattery." Or: buried, by the continuation of transgression unto death, as if to say: those who were buried in wickedness and died in wickedness, according to what is frequently said; 3 Kings 16: "He died in the sins of Jeroboam, who made Israel to sin"; and according to what the Lord says to the Jews, John: "You shall die in your sin." Such wicked men he saw exalted in fame; therefore he says: Who also, while they were still living, supply: in their wickedness, were in the holy place, that is, in a position of dignity as if holy, just as also the antichrist, of whom Matthew 24: "When you shall see the abomination of desolation standing in the holy place," etc. And they were praised in the city as if for just works. And thus they were great in fame: Psalm: "For the sinner is praised in the desires of his soul"; and Proverbs 28: "Those who forsake the law praise the wicked; but those who keep [it]," etc.

(Vers. 10. 11.). But this also is vanity. Here the second point is touched upon, namely the vanity of false security: because from the fact that the wicked are not punished, others are rendered complacent: therefore he says: But this also is vanity, that is, a cause of vanity.

And he shows this: For because sentence against the evil is not pronounced quickly, but they are kindly waited for, according to what is said in Second Peter three: "The Lord does not delay his promise, but acts patiently on your account, not willing that any should perish, but that all should return to repentance." And without any fear the children of men perpetrate evils, and so they are secure, because they think nothing of nor fear future things: Proverbs twenty-eight: "Evil men do not consider judgment," because, namely, they are not punished now: Isaiah twenty-six: "Let us have mercy on the wicked, and he will not learn justice," etc.

(Vers. 12. 13.). Yet the sinner etc. Here the third point is touched upon, how from this is drawn the benefit of the good, because from this the good recognize divine mercy and are exercised in justice. Therefore he says: Yet the sinner, and the text should be construed thus: Yet from the fact that the sinner a hundred times does evil, that is, many times, according to that passage in Jeremiah 2: "How exceedingly vile you have become, repeating your ways." And is sustained through patience, namely of God, according to that passage in Romans 2: "Do you not know that the kindness of God leads you to repentance?" Hence Lamentations 3: "It is the mercy of the Lord that we are not consumed." I have known that it shall be well with those who fear God, that is, those who turn away from evil: Proverbs 16: "By the fear of the Lord one turns away from evil." Who revere his face, and out of reverence for him do not dare to sin, according to what Job 31 says of himself: "I always feared God as waves swelling over me, and I could not bear his weight." But this benefit of divine mercy and patience does not extend to the wicked; and therefore it is more profitable for them to die than to live. — Therefore he says: Let there not be good for the impious: he says this not by wishing but by foretelling, just as that passage of the Psalm: "Let sinners be turned into hell." Let there not be good, namely of glory: Isaiah 26: "Let the impious be taken away, lest he see the glory of God." Let there not be even the good of the present life: therefore he adds: Nor let his days be prolonged, that is, they will not be prolonged: Proverbs 10: "The years of the impious shall be shortened": and in the Psalm: "Men of blood and deceit shall not live out half their days." But let those who do not fear the face of the Lord pass away like a shadow. And indeed they pass away like a shadow: hence in the Psalm: "I saw the impious highly exalted," and afterward: "I passed by, and behold, he was no more"; hence in the person of the impious, Wisdom 5: "All these things have passed away like a shadow and like a messenger running ahead and like a ship that passes through the surging water."

Questions.


I. The question is raised here concerning what he says: Sometimes man rules over man: whether man can justly rule over man. That he can, is shown as follows:

1. Because in Romans thirteen: "There is no power except from God: and the Apostle says that one must serve masters, not only on account of fear, but also on account of conscience: therefore if everything that is from God is just, then for a man to have power over another man is just.

2. Furthermore, if conscience dictates to a man nothing but what is just, when it is right: and it dictates this, as the Apostle says: therefore it is just for a man to be subject to another man.

3. Likewise, the Apostle says in Ephesians 6: Servants, obey your carnal masters: but the Apostle neither exhorts nor commands anything except what is just: therefore, etc.

On the contrary: 1. Men are equal by nature, therefore that one should rule over another is against nature: but everything that is against nature is sin: therefore etc.

2 Likewise, all men are free by nature, therefore when a man is made a slave, this is by usurpation; but everything usurped is sin: therefore it is a sin for man to exercise dominion over another.

I respond: it must be said that a man can justly have dominion over another man. This is clear, because Noah by divine authority made his son Canaan a servant of his brothers; similarly Isaac did so regarding Esau and Jacob.

1. 2. Therefore, to the objection about what is against nature, and that every man is free: it must be understood that there is a twofold state, namely of nature destitute and of nature established. As regards the state of nature established, although all would have been free and equal, and it would not have been said to men that they should rule over anything except brute animals alone: nevertheless, as regards the state

. of corrupt nature it is otherwise.

Because indeed some are worse than others and evil, therefore by the just judgment of God they become servants, both in themselves and in their posterity.

There is also another reason for ruling, namely for the restraint of the wicked; for if there were no temporal power, everyone would do what seemed right to himself, and thus there would be a division of iniquity, and everyone would sin at will. And so there can justly be dominion and servitude among men.

Whether, however, the wicked can justly rule over the good will be inquired into below.

II. The question is raised concerning what he says: The impious man sins a hundred times and is sustained: whether this pertains to kindness or severity. That it pertains to kindness is shown:
Lamentations three: The mercy of the Lord, that we are not consumed.

Moreover, the Apostle in Romans 2: Do you not know that the kindness of God leads you to repentance?

But on the contrary: The impious man, the longer he lives, the more evils he commits, and the more evils he commits, the more severely he will be tormented: therefore if he were to die sooner, it would be better for him: therefore that his days are prolonged is rather for his harm than his good: therefore it is of severity.

I respond: it must be said that there are certain wicked people whom God foresaw would return to him: for such people, the prolongation of life is the effect of great mercy, both open and hidden. There are others whom he foresaw would multiply their sins and die in them: and for such people, to prolong [life] is of justice and of mercy: of mercy, because cruel punishment is deferred for them: but of hidden justice, because it is permitted to him who is in filth, that he become still more filthy: for he has therefore merited the prolongation of life, in this respect, of hidden severity.

Second, concerning the adversity of the good.

There is also another vanity, etc. Above he showed how security arises from the sight of the exaltation of the wicked: here secondly he shows how it arises from the sight of the adversity of the good. And indeed he does this in this order. First the adversity of the good is set forth: second, from this the security of the wicked: third, vanity is concluded: fourth, pleasure is commended.

(Vers. 14). Therefore the adversity of the good is set forth, from which vanity arises; on account of which he says: There is also another vanity that occurs on the earth, another, that is, from the one previously mentioned, because it has a different cause: for the previously mentioned one came from the exaltation of the wicked, but this one from the adversity of the good. Therefore he says: There are just men to whom evils befall, that is, adversities, as though they had done the works of the impious, according to what is found in Hebrews 11: "The saints experienced mockeries and scourgings, and moreover chains and imprisonments: they were stoned, they were sawn asunder, they were tempted." An example is Tobit, who was made blind, in Tobit 2; an example is Job, who was so afflicted, in Job 1. And the reason for this is given in Judith 8: "All who were pleasing to God passed through many tribulations as faithful ones."

And there are wicked men. Here the second point is noted, namely the security of the wicked. For since the wicked see that evils befall the good and good things befall themselves, they are rendered secure. Therefore he says: And there are wicked men, who are made so secure as if they had the deeds of the just: and this, because they are free from tribulation. An example is that rich glutton, Luke 16, to whom it was said: "Remember that you received good things in your life, and Lazarus likewise evil things." And therefore the wicked are made secure, because they prosper: whence Jeremiah 12: "Why does the way of the wicked prosper, it is well with all who transgress," etc.

But this too is most vain. Here thirdly vanity is concluded from this security of the wicked. Therefore he says: But this too I judge most vain. For it is vain when good things come to the good and the wicked alike, because it is unworthy on account of the wicked; more vain when evil things come to the good and the wicked alike, because it is unjust on account of the good; but most vain when evil things come to the good and good things to the wicked, because it is equally unjust and unworthy. Whence he bears it so indignantly that he seems from this to want to accuse God: Habakkuk 1: "Why do you look upon those who act wickedly and remain silent, while the wicked devours one more just than himself?"

(Vers. 13.). I praised therefore gladness. Here fourthly, from this pleasure is commended in detestation of so great a vanity, since good things come to the wicked and evil things to the good; and this is the praise and word of a troubled man. Therefore he says: I praised therefore gladness, namely of the flesh, as if this alone were good; therefore he says: That there would be no good for man under the sun, except that he should eat and drink, according to that passage of Isaiah twenty-two: "Behold, joy and gladness, slaying rams, eating flesh and drinking wine," as if there were no other life nor any other reward. Therefore he adds: And this alone he carries away with him from his labor, as if nothing else were to be expected, but one should rejoice in the days of his life, which God has given him under the sun: Wisdom two: "Let us everywhere leave signs of our gladness, for this is our portion."

Questions.


One can ask here which state is better and more useful: namely, whether the state of prosperity or of adversity. That adversity is better, it seems:

1. Because it is said in Revelation 3: Those whom I love, I rebuke and chastise: therefore tribulation is a sign of divine love.

2. Likewise it is said in Hebrews 12: He scourges every son whom he receives: therefore to be scourged is a sign of divine sonship?.

3. Likewise, Tobit 12: Because you were acceptable to God, it was necessary that temptation should prove you; therefore etc.

A. Likewise, Judith chapter eight: All who were pleasing to God passed through many tribulations as faithful ones; therefore that state is better.

Contra: 1. The Church seeks peace: "Give peace, O Lord, in our days": therefore if the Church seeks what is better, the state of prosperity is better.

2. Likewise, the Apostle, in 1 Timothy 2, has us pray for those who are placed in high positions, that we may lead a quiet and tranquil life: therefore the state of tranquility is better.

3. Likewise, if there were no sin, there would be no adversity, but complete tranquility and prosperity: therefore the state of prosperity is more consonant with the state of innocence and glory; but "that which is more similar to what is better is itself better": therefore etc.

I respond: it must be said that the state of prosperity and adversity can be compared in relation to the good or to the wicked. If in relation to the wicked, who are lifted up in prosperity and cast down in adversity, neither state is useful unless divine grace assists interiorly, so that when God scourges exteriorly, he enlightens interiorly: thus, when inspiration accompanies the scourge, which often happens, the state of adversity is more useful for the wicked. - But if we speak by comparison to the good: it must be said that some good people are perfect, of whom is said that passage from Proverbs 12: "Whatever befalls the just man will not grieve him": thus for the perfect the state of adversity is better and more useful, because in it they are tested, as gold is proved in fire. But the state of prosperity is not useful for them, either because they do not advance, or because they grow lukewarm, or because their spirits fall, just as David, strong in battles, fell at the sight of a woman.

There are also others who are imperfect, who love God and are drawn by temporal benefits: and since they do not have perfect charity, they fall easily: for such people prosperity is more useful. And because the Church has many who are tender and more who are weak than strong, she is compelled to pray for peace. Therefore that evil things befall the good in the present is a gift of God, according to Philippians 1: It has been granted to you not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him.

Third, concerning the indistinction of the good and the wicked, which is treated in two ways.

And I applied my heart etc. It has been shown how the vanity of complacency arises from the exaltation of the wicked and also from the oppression of the good. Here, thirdly, it is shown how it arises from the indistinction between the one group and the other. For because the wicked cannot be distinguished from the good, they are therefore complacent; and since this complacency has its origin from this kind of indistinction, and it is also the source of pleasure, therefore this section has two parts. First it is shown how complacency arises from indistinction; second, how pleasure arises from complacency, at the passage: There is no one who lives forever, etc.

First it is shown how security arises from this.

The first, then, is described in this order: first is noted the vain anxiety of discerning; second, the uncertainty of discernment; third, the reason for the uncertainty; fourth is noted from this the origin of false security.

(Vers. 16. 17.). Therefore there is first suggested the vain anxiety of discerning, which anxiety was diligent; whence he says: And I applied my heart, that is, I directed my understanding; to know wisdom, with regard to invisible things, and to understand the distinction of those things which take place on earth, with regard to visible things, because in Romans 1: "The invisible things of God are clearly seen from the creation of the world, being understood through the things that have been made." And this anxiety is vain, because however much a man labors, he cannot find out; therefore he adds: There is a man who day and night takes no sleep with his eyes, but rather labors continually in searching, above in chapter three: "I saw the affliction which God has given to the sons of men, that they might be occupied in it"; and he adds: "He made all things good and delivered the world to their disputation"; and although he labors so much, he profits nothing.

Therefore he adds: And I understood that man can find no reason for all the works of God, of those, namely works, which are done under the sun: Sirach forty-three: "Do not labor, for you will not comprehend." And the more he labors to seek, the less he will find: whence in the Psalm: "Man shall approach a deep heart, and God shall be exalted," that is, made distant; Augustine: "God is lofty; you lift yourself up, and he flees from you; you humble yourself, and he comes to you." Even if the wise man says he knows, he will not be able to discover it, that is, if he believes himself to know, he understands less, because it is said in Romans one: "Claiming to be wise, they became fools"; above in chapter seven: "I said: I will become wise, and it withdrew further from me." And he repeats the concern, adding that it was curious: for to apply concern where there is no usefulness is curiosity. Therefore he says: All these things I pondered in my heart, so that I might understand with curiosity: and this is reproved in Sirach three: "Do not search too much into superfluous things, and do not be curious about his many works." And thus the concern of discerning is clear, which was diligent and futile, and therefore curious.
