Chapter VII.


(Vers. 1.). What need is there? Here he calls back a third time from scrutinizing lofty things and inquiring into them, and this, since he has enough to occupy himself with in recognizing his own evils, or common ones. Therefore he says: What need is there for a man to seek things greater than himself? as if to say, there is no need: Sirach chapter three: "Seek not the things that are too high for you, and search not into things above your strength"; and Romans chapter eleven: "Be not high-minded, but fear." And he adds the reason why one ought not to seek greater things: Since he does not know what is profitable for him in his life, that is, what is useful for him: Wisdom chapter nine: "We can hardly judge the things that are on earth, and with labor do we find the things that are before us. But the things that are in heaven, who shall search out?" He is indeed ignorant of what is useful in this fleeting life; whence he adds: In the number of the days of his pilgrimage; Genesis chapter forty-seven: "The days of the pilgrimage of my life are few and evil"; Second Corinthians chapter five: "While we are in this body, we are pilgrims away from the Lord." And the time which passes by like a shadow;

1 Chronicles, last chapter: "We are strangers before you and sojourners, as were all our fathers. Our days are like a shadow upon the earth, and there is no delay." And so by himself he cannot inquire, nor even learn from another; therefore he adds: Or who shall be able to tell him what shall come after him under the sun? For man does not know the future, unless the Lord reveals it through grace. Therefore man ought not to extend his thought to this. Isaiah 48: "Before the day you did not hear them, lest perhaps you should say: Behold, I knew them. You have neither heard nor known, nor from that time was your ear opened"; below in chapter 8: "Man is ignorant of the past, and the future he can know by no messenger." Hence God alone knows and reveals the future.

Questions.


I. But a question is raised about what he says: It is better to see what you desire than to desire what you do not know. Therefore it is not contradictory that someone can desire what he does not know.
On the contrary: 4. Augustine says that "we can love unseen things, but unknown things in no way"; and so, in order that something be loved and desired, it is necessary that it be known.

2. Furthermore, desire follows the intellect: therefore where the intellect does not precede, desire does not extend itself; but the intellect does not extend itself to those things which it does not know: therefore neither does desire long for those things.

I respond: it must be said that there is a twofold knowledge, namely of certainty and of estimation; and so correspondingly there is a twofold ignorance: one through the privation of any certainty, and another through the privation of any estimation. I say therefore that for something to be desired, it is not necessary that knowledge of certainty: precede, for desire follows estimation alone; but it is necessary that some knowledge of estimation precede. Here, however, he speaks of ignorance through the privation of certainty.

II. There is a question about what he says: What need is there for a man to seek things greater than himself? From this it seems that a man ought not to scrutinize anything nor come to know by labor what is above him.

This same thing is proved by that passage in Sirach 3: Do not seek things higher than yourself. This same thing is in Romans 11: Do not be high-minded, but fear.

But on the contrary: 1. Man stands in the middle between visible creatures and God; but Augustine says that the soul of man is darkened when it fixes its eye on these earthly and lowly things; but it is illuminated when it turns toward eternal goods: therefore it ought always to seek those things. But those things are higher: therefore etc.

2. Furthermore, the soul is never perfected with complete perfection except in those things which are higher than itself; but everything perfectible ought to tend toward and seek that in which it is perfected: therefore it ought to seek higher things.

If you say that it does not mean that one ought not to seek nor consider higher things according to their substance, but that one ought not to consider those things which exceed the possibility of one's consideration: it is objected then: because all things which are of faith are above our intellect: therefore according to this, our intellect ought not to raise itself to believing those things which are of faith.

3. Likewise, then those who wish to examine the articles of faith will be most blameworthy; therefore all who dispute about the faith are blameworthy.

I respond: it must be said that emphasis should be placed on what he says, things greater than oneself, and on what he says, to seek. Things greater than oneself he calls those things which exceed our consideration, insofar as they exceed it. To seek he calls to inquire into and examine, as if to say that it is foolish to want to investigate those things which our inquiry cannot reach, with respect to those matters for which our consideration does not suffice. For there are some things about God that are manifest to us, and these are not greater than us; hence he does not discourage us from considering God. 2. 3. To the objection about faith, it must be said that he does not discourage believing, but seeking. - Furthermore, even though the things of faith are above our intellect by itself in its bare state, they are nevertheless not above the intellect illuminated by the grace of faith; hence to seek those things and to believe and to investigate up to that point to which the light of faith extends is not to extend oneself to things greater than oneself; and thus is every sober disputation concerning the articles of faith themselves. But if one exceeds that, unless it be from God, then the excess is blameworthy.

III. There is also a question about what he says: Who will be able to tell him what shall come after him under the sun?

On the contrary: In our soul there is a power which is to become all things and to make all things; but the possible intellect can receive in itself all present things, all past things, and all future things: therefore if the agent intellect can reduce it to act, then man can by himself know future things.

I respond: it must be said that even if the agent can form or abstract cognition in the possible intellect, nevertheless its operation depends both on the certainty of the thing outside and internally on sense; because "when the thing is lacking, knowledge perishes"; and "when sense is lacking, it is necessary that one knowledge fail according to that sense." Because therefore future contingents do not generate a likeness of themselves in sense, neither through themselves nor through a determinate antecedent cause: and they do not have in themselves the stability that is required for cognition: therefore our intellect, which receives from things, cannot of itself come to knowledge of them, except through him whose cognition does not depend on things.

Second, the teachings of wisdom are set forth, through which a remedy is applied against the vanity of foolishness.

A good name is better. Above he set forth the method of learning; here secondly he adds teachings of wisdom, through which a remedy is applied against the vanity of folly. And since, as is said in Wisdom 8, "wisdom teaches sobriety, prudence, justice, and virtue" or fortitude: therefore this part has four parts. In the first he sets forth teachings for having modesty: in the second, for having constancy, there: Oppression etc.: in the third, for having prudence, there: Do not say, what is the reason that etc; in the fourth, for having justice, there: Do not be exceedingly reckless etc.

First, five instructions are set forth for maintaining moderation.

Moreover, for attaining moderation, he gives five instructions. In the first, he teaches to prefer the honorable to the pleasant; in the second, the future life to the present; in the third, affliction to pleasure; in the fourth, sorrow to merriment; in the fifth, severity to softness or flattery.

(Vers. 2.). He therefore sets forth an instruction in which he teaches to prefer the honorable, such as a good name, to the pleasant, such as ointment. Therefore he says: A good name is better, that is, a good reputation displayed by good people, concerning which Sirach 41 says: "Take care of a good name, for this will remain with you more than a thousand precious and great treasures." Than precious ointments, which indeed are very fragrant, according to what is said in John 12: "Mary took a pound of precious ointment of spikenard and anointed the feet of Jesus, and the house was filled with the odor of the ointment"; and although ointments are fragrant, nevertheless a good name is more so, and therefore better: 2 Corinthians 2: "We are the good odor of Christ to God in every place," namely with regard to good reputation.

And the day of death. Here the second lesson is noted, in which he prefers the future life to the present, and thus the entrance into another life to the entrance into this one. And therefore he says: And the day of death, supply: is better than the day of birth, because one dies to rest; Revelation 14: "Blessed are the dead, who die in

the Lord: henceforth, says the Spirit, that they may rest from their labors." But here one is born to labor: Job 5: "Man is born to labor, and a bird to flight"; and therefore it is better: whence Sirach 30: "Better is death than a bitter life, and eternal rest than persevering languor".

(Vers. 3.). It is better to go to the house of mourning, etc. Here the third lesson is noted, in which he prefers affliction to pleasure, that is, mourning to feasting. On account of which he says: It is better to go to the house of mourning, where sins are mourned, because Matthew 5: "Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted"; than to the house of feasting, where delights are found: Proverbs 23: "Do not be among the feasts of drunkards," etc. And the reason for this is: because at feasts a man forgets himself, in the house of mourning he remembers his judgment; and therefore he adds: For in that place, namely in the house of mourning, the end of all men is brought to mind: Sirach 38: "Remember my judgment: for so also shall yours be. Mine is today, yours will be tomorrow." And the living person considers what will come to pass. And this is most useful: whence Sirach 7: "Remember your last things, and you will never sin"; and therefore it is said in Sirach 38: "Remember the last things and do not forget," because, as Jerome says, "he easily despises all things who always considers that he is going to die."

(Vers. 4. 5.). Better is anger than laughter. Here the fourth lesson is noted, in which he prefers sadness to merriment: because the former, namely sadness, corrects, and merriment deceives. And therefore he says: Better is anger than laughter, anger, namely, by which one is angered at the sins of others: Hebrews 12: "He scourges every son whom he receives"; and Revelation 3: "Those whom I love, I rebuke and chastise." This anger is better than the laughter of dissimulation: Sirach 30: "Play with your son, and he will bring you sorrow," etc.: and therefore: Better is anger. And the reason is given: Because by the sadness of the countenance the mind of the offender is corrected: Proverbs 25: "The north wind drives away rain, and a sad face a backbiting tongue."

And he confirms this teaching by the judgment of the wise man; whence he says: The heart of the wise is where sadness is; but on the contrary: The heart of fools is where joy is; the former seek sorrowful things in the present, the latter pleasant things. John 16: "You shall weep and lament, but the world shall rejoice." And that the former act wisely and the latter foolishly is clear from the outcome, because it is said to the sorrowful in Luke 6: "Blessed are you who weep now, for you shall laugh"; and on the contrary to those who rejoice: "Woe to you who laugh, for you shall weep."

(Vers. 6. 7.). It is better to be rebuked by a wise man. Here is noted the fifth lesson, in which severity is preferred to softness, as for example harsh correction to flattery. On account of which he says: It is better to be rebuked by a wise man than to be deceived by the flattery of fools: Proverbs twenty-seven: "Open rebuke is better than hidden love," because correction directs, but flattery deceives: Proverbs sixteen: "A wicked man entices his friend and leads him along a way that is not good": and Isaiah three: "O my people, those who call you blessed, they themselves deceive you," etc.

And he gives the reason through a simile: because the flattery of a fool lasts little and makes much noise, like thorns burning under a pot; therefore he says: Because as the sound of thorns under a pot sounds loudly, so is the laughter of the fool. Literally, the fool laughs loudly: Sirach twenty-one: "A fool raises his voice in laughter, but a wise man will scarcely laugh quietly." Or: loud laughter, when flattery is clamorous: Proverbs twenty-seven: "He who blesses his neighbor with a loud voice, rising early in the morning, shall be similar to one who curses." But this also is vanity, namely such flattery: in the Psalm: "Vain are the sons of men, deceitful are the sons of men."

Questions.


I. The question is raised about what is said: The day of death is better than the day of birth.

On the contrary: 1. Death is a privation, life is a possession: but a possession always has more of being and goodness than a privation: therefore life is better than death: therefore the day of birth is better.

2. Furthermore, it is a rule that "that whose corruption is good, its generation is bad"; but death is the corruption of life: therefore that whose death is good, its life is bad. But no one's life is bad except a sinner's: therefore no one's death is better than life except a sinner's: but this is false, because a sinner by dying tends toward hell.

I respond: it must be said that something is preferred to another sometimes by reason of itself, sometimes by reason of what follows upon it. That is to be preferred to another by reason of itself which of itself bespeaks more of goodness, just as a man is better than a horse. In another way, by reason of what follows, and thus death is preferred to life, and that dissolution, namely death, upon which follows rest and being with Christ. And he speaks here of this death, because in life man is in danger, in labor and in sorrow; after death one is immediately in joy, security, and tranquility.

I. And the response to the first objection is clear, because death is not preferred for its own sake.

a. What he objects secondly can be resolved similarly. For death is called evil in two ways: either because it takes away a great good, namely a good life, as a true and useful good; or because it leads to an evil end. In the first way, the death of the just is evil, that is, harmful, but in the second way it is very good, and in this respect it is to be preferred.

II. Likewise, it is asked about what he says: Anger is better than laughter.

To the contrary: 1. In Matthew 5: Whoever is angry with his brother shall be liable to judgment; but whoever laughs is not liable to any punishment; therefore it is worse to be angry than to laugh.

2. Likewise, to laugh can be without fault; but to be angry, since it implies a disturbance of reason, is scarcely without venial fault, and is never without penalty: therefore it is much worse than laughter.

I respond: it must be said that there is being angry at vice and being angry by nature: the first is of virtue and good, the second of vice and evil. Similarly, there is smiling at the good and laughing together at sin: the first is good; the second, evil. And he speaks of anger and laughter insofar as evil is regarded.

But then he could similarly give another rule with respect to the good: laughter is better than anger.

And it must be said that in the present life there are more evils than goods: therefore more people must be sorrowful than joyful, and therefore he prefers anger to laughter.
Second, four instructions for having constancy.

Calumny disturbs the wise man. Above he set forth instructions for having moderation; here follows the second part, in which he sets forth instructions for having constancy: and since constancy is lost through calumny and through anger, and is preserved through patience and through perseverance, therefore four instructions are set forth here. In the first is shown that calumny is to be avoided: in the second, that perseverance is to be embraced: in the third, that patience is to be maintained: in the fourth, that anger is to be shunned.

(Vers. 8.). In the first instruction, then, he shows that calumny is to be avoided, because by disturbing a man it renders him inconstant. Therefore he says: Calumny disturbs the wise man. Calumny is a litigious accusation, and therefore one must withdraw from it; Isaiah fifty-four: "Withdraw far from calumny, for you shall not fear." And it will destroy the strength of his heart, that is, the fortitude of charity: Matthew twenty-four: "Because iniquity will abound, the charity of many will grow cold."

(Vers. 9.). Better is the end etc. Here the second lesson is noted, in which perseverance is preferred to the beginning of justice. On account of this he says: Better is the end of prayer than the beginning: it is better to persevere in good than merely to begin: Matthew 10: "He who perseveres to the end, he shall be saved"; whence Bernard: "Perseverance is the consummation of all virtues."

Better is the patient one etc. Here the third lesson is noted, in which patience is commended, because without it there can be no steadfastness; therefore he says: Better is the patient one than the arrogant: the comparison is improper, because the patient one is good, but the arrogant one is evil: Proverbs 16: "Better is the patient man than the mighty one" etc. (Vers. 10.). Do not be quick etc. Here the fourth lesson is touched upon, in which it is taught that anger must be avoided, because it does not stand well with steadfastness; therefore he says: Do not be quick to anger: James 1: "Let every man be quick to hear, slow to speak, and slow to anger." And the reason is added: Because anger rests in the bosom of a fool; Job 5: "Anger kills the foolish man, and envy slays the little one." He well says rests, on account of its long duration: for the wise man, even if he grows angry, ought not to retain it long: Ephesians 4: "Let not the sun go down upon your wrath": but not so the fool: Proverbs 27: "Heavy is the sand" in the bosom of a fool.

Questions.


I. But there is a question about what he says: Calumny disturbs the wise man.

Against: Whatever befalls the just man will not grieve him, is found in Proverbs 12; therefore, if the wise man is just, he is not disturbed by calumny.

To this it is answered in one way, that there is the disturbance of impatience, and by this the just man is not disturbed; and of compassion, and by this he is disturbed, as the Apostle said in 2 Corinthians 11: Who is scandalized, and I am not on fire? as if to say: no one. — But the following text removes this exposition and adds: And it will destroy the strength of his heart.

Therefore it must be resolved, as is said in the Gloss, that there is a wise and just man who is perfect, and of him that passage in Proverbs is understood: and one who is imperfect, and of him the present passage is understood.

II. Likewise, what is it that he says here: Be not swift to anger 1

Against this: In the Psalm: Be angry, and do not sin.

I respond: it must be said that anger is manifold.

Third, four instructions for having prudence.

Do not say: What, do you think, is the reason etc. Having set forth instructions for modesty and constancy, he here sets forth instructions for having prudence. And because it belongs to the prudent person to discern where one may err, therefore he sets forth four instructions for having discernment. The first is for the discernment of useful things: the second, for the discernment of divine works: the third, for the discernment of times: the fourth, for the discernment of the merits of men.

(Vers. 11. 12. 13.). The first lesson, therefore, was given for the discernment of useful things: for in different times there are different advantages from different causes. Therefore he says: Do not say: What do you think is the reason that former times, that is, of the ancients, were better? because namely in them there was scarcity: than they are now, when namely there is already an abundance of things: because this question comes from thoughtlessness: whence he adds: For such a question is foolish. And the reason for this is: because he does not consider the usefulness of wealth, therefore he teaches how to discern it, saying: Wisdom is more useful with riches, because Proverbs 13: "The ransom of a man's life is his riches." And it profits more those who see the sun, that is, those who have right intention: because Sirach 13: "Good is substance to him who has no sin on his conscience, and most wicked is poverty in the mouth of the ungodly." And that money is useful, he shows by its effect: For as wisdom protects, namely from fault: Proverbs 2: "If wisdom enters your heart, and knowledge pleases your soul, counsel will guard you, and prudence will preserve you"; so also does money protect, namely when well spent: Sirach 29: "A man's almsgiving is like a purse with him, and it will preserve a man's grace like the apple of the eye." And although both are useful, nevertheless wisdom is more useful: therefore he adds: But wisdom and learning have this advantage, that they give life to their possessor: whence Proverbs 4: "Hear, my son, and receive my words, that the years of life may be multiplied to you": and Proverbs 3: "Wisdom is a tree of life to those who lay hold of her." But money without wisdom bestows death: James 5: "Come now, you rich, weep and howl in your miseries." And therefore wisdom is to be preferred to money: Proverbs 8: "Receive wisdom and not money; choose learning rather than gold."

(Vers. 14.). Consider the works of God etc. This is the second instruction, in which he teaches the discerning consideration of divine works or judgments, because the one whom he himself despises by the sign of reprobation, no one can bring back. Therefore he says: Consider the works of God, that is, the divine judgments, that no one can correct the one whom he has despised, that is, the one whom he himself has willed to visit with his judgment. Whence it is said in 1 Kings 2 concerning the sons of Eli: "And they did not listen to the voice of their father, because the Lord willed to kill them." These works and judgments must be considered with fear: Habakkuk 3: "I considered your works and was afraid."

(Vers. 15.). In the good day etc. This is the third instruction, in which he teaches that a discernment of times must be made: because God did not make all times uniformly, but in varied ways, and man ought to dispose himself according to the different times. Therefore he says: In the good day, enjoy good things, that is, do good with joy: Sirach 14: "Do not defraud yourself of a good day, and let not a portion of a good day pass you by," because in this day there is place and time for working. Whence John 9: "I must work while it is day"; and likewise we, whence the Apostle in Galatians, last chapter: "Let us do good to all, while we have time." And guard against the evil day, namely that one, of which Zephaniah 1 says: "A day of wrath, that day, a day of tribulation and distress, a day of calamity and misery." He who wishes to guard against this day must needs reflect on that passage from Sirach 11: "In the day of good things, be not unmindful of evil things." These days the Lord made and ordained in a just manner: therefore he says: For as this one, that is the good, so also that one, namely the evil, God made: and this by just judgment: So that man may not find just complaints against him. Therefore he makes good things for the good, evil things against the evil, because in the Psalm: "The Lord is just in all his ways."

(Vers. 16.). These things also I saw in the day of my birth. Here the fourth lesson is noted, by which he teaches that a diligent discernment of merits must be made, because they are greatly hidden. Therefore he says: This also I saw in the day of my birth, that is, after I was born, I saw this happen: The just man perishes in his justice, because he is regarded as though he were wicked: Isaiah 57: "The just man perishes, and no one considers it." And the wicked man lives a long time in his wickedness, as though he were just: at which Jeremiah was indignant, chapter 12: "Why does the way of the wicked prosper? It is well with all who transgress and act wickedly." And below in chapter 8: "There are just men to whom evil things happen, as if they had done the works of the wicked; and there are wicked men who are as secure as if they had the deeds of the just."

QUESTIONS.


I. The question is raised about what he says: Wisdom is more useful with riches.

1. Therefore it is better to be a wise rich man than a wise poor man: therefore to be poor is not a more perfect state.

2. Furthermore, "art and virtue concern difficult things"; but it is more difficult for a rich person to be saved than for a poor person: therefore it is of greater virtue: therefore it is a more praiseworthy state.

Against this is: that the Lord preferred the state of the poor both by deed and by word, Matthew nineteen.

I respond: it must be said that poverty is threefold: one from delight, another from endurance, another with impatience. The first is good and perfect, the second is imperfect, the third is blameworthy: Sirach 13: "Most wicked is poverty in the mouth of the ungodly." When it is said that wisdom is better with riches than without, this is understood concerning the poverty that is with endurance, or impatience.

2. As to the objection regarding difficulty, it must be said that there is a difficulty by reason of the kind of work, and this contributes to greater virtue; and there is a difficulty by reason of the disorder of the will, just as a miser gives a gift with more difficulty than a generous person, and this latter contributes nothing to virtue; and so the rich person is saved with more difficulty. - It is solved another way: because it is understood concerning superfluity and excessive poverty.

II. It is asked concerning what he says: That no one can correct him whom God has despised.

1. But God despises every sinner, because the Most High hates sinners, Sirach twelve; and: Likewise the ungodly man and his ungodliness are abominable to God.

2. Likewise, if he cannot be corrected, then neither can he be saved; therefore it ought not be imputed to him.

I respond: it must be said that just as there is a twofold love, namely eternal and according to present justice, so there is a twofold rejection of men: and he speaks here not of just any man, but of the reprobate, nor of just any rejection, but of rejection according to eternal reprobation, the sign of which is hardening in the present; and thus it does not apply to every sinner, but to the reprobate.

2. To the objection that it should not be imputed to him: it must be said that on the contrary it should, because God despises no one without cause. Hence also, although he cannot rise unless God extends his hand, he is nevertheless not excused, because it is his own fault that the hand is not extended to him. Hence Augustine says in the book On Correction and Grace that no one is corrected unless he is willing.

III. Likewise, a question is raised about what he says: The just man perishes in his justice, and the wicked man lives a long time in his wickedness.

I. But against this: In the Psalm: Bloody and deceitful men shall not live out half their days.

2. Likewise, concerning goods, Exodus 20: Honor your father, that you may be long-lived upon the earth, and live a long time: therefore life is prolonged for the one who observes the commandments.

I respond: it must be said that just as it is stated in Wisdom chapter four concerning the just man: Being made perfect in a short time, he fulfilled a long time, and few are counted as many; so conversely among the wicked, so also regarding the number of the saved and the damned: and the days of the good are longer, because they are better, not because they live longer.

It should be noted, however, that the years of men are sometimes shortened, lest they become evil: Wisdom 4: He was taken away, lest wickedness should alter his understanding, etc. Sometimes, lest they become worse: in the Psalm: Men of blood and deceit shall not live out half their days.

They are prolonged moreover for the good unto purification: Sirach two: For gold is tested in the fire: for the wicked, unto their correction: Romans two: Do you not know that the kindness of God leads you to repentance?

Fourth, four instructions for obtaining justice.

Be not over just. Above he set forth teachings for having modesty, constancy, and prudence; here in the fourth place he sets forth teachings for having justice: and because that justice which goes to excess in rigor and cruelty is not true justice unless it be corrected by piety, he therefore sets forth here four teachings. In the first he forbids the rigor of severity or the excess of justice: in the second, the headlong fall into impiety: in the third he commends the practice of mercy: in the fourth, the overlooking of one's own injury.

(Vers. 17.). In the first instruction, therefore, he discourages excess of justice in punishing, when he says: Do not be overly just, that is, do not exceed in the rigor of justice, because it is a fault to prosecute every fault to the full: hence James 2: "Judgment without mercy will be rendered to him who does not show mercy." You should also not exceed in scrutinizing fault: therefore he says: Nor be wiser than is necessary, because it is said in Proverbs 30: "He who blows his nose violently draws blood," therefore Romans 12: "Not to be wiser than one ought to be wise, but to be wise unto sobriety."

(Vers. 17. 18.). Do not be stupefied etc. Here is noted the second instruction in which he forbids the headlong fall into impiety: for impiety renders a man stupefied in feeling and hardened. Therefore he says: Do not be stupefied: Jeremiah thirty-one: "Everyone who eats a sour grape, his teeth will be set on edge." It renders cruel in action: therefore he says: Do not act impiously overmuch, that is, cruelly: Psalm: "I said to the wicked: Do not act wickedly": and Sirach seven: "Do not sow evils in the furrows of injustice, and you will not reap them sevenfold." It also renders blind in understanding: therefore he says: Do not be foolish: Proverbs twenty-four: "He who plans to do evil will be called a fool. The thought of the fool is sin." And because sin and foolishness hasten death: therefore the reason follows: Do not die before your time, that is, lest your death be hastened, because in the Psalm: "Men of blood and deceit shall not live out half their days": and again Luke twelve: "Fool, this night they will demand your soul from you." The example of Dathan and Abiron, who descended alive into hell.

(Vers. 19. 20. 21.). It is good for you to sustain the just one. Here the third instruction is noted, in which he admonishes to the showing of mercy: and he first sets forth the exhortation and then adds the reason.

He exhorts, when he says: It is good for you to support the just man, namely in justice: Proverbs 24: "Do not lie in wait and seek wickedness in the house of the just, and do not lay waste to his rest"; because it is said in Proverbs 17: "It is not good to bring harm upon the just, nor to strike the prince who judges rightly." Not only is the just man to be supported in a just cause, but a hand is also to be extended to him through almsgiving: whence he adds: But also from him do not withdraw your hand: Sirach 12: "Do good to the just, and you will find great recompense: and if not from him, certainly from God." Having thus set forth the exhortation, he adds a threefold reason: and the first is the divine will, on account of which he says: He who fears God neglects nothing, namely of those things which God wills to be done; Sirach 7: "Fear God with your whole soul, and purge yourself of your negligence with few."

The second reason is the dignity of the just person, on account of which he says: Wisdom strengthened the wise man more than ten princes of the city; and so, Wisdom 6, "wisdom is better than strength, and a prudent man is better than a strong one".

The third reason is human frailty, on account of which he says: There is not a just man upon earth, who does good and does not sin: and therefore one must have mercy on the sinner: Proverbs 24: "A just man falls seven times a day and rises again"; indeed, however perfect one may be, one sometimes sins: 1 John 1: "If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves, and the truth is not in us."

(Vers. 22. 23.). But also to all the words etc. Here the fourth instruction is touched upon, in which he exhorts

to the overlooking of personal injury: whence one ought not to inquire nor believe every person who reports things. Therefore he says: But also to all the words that are spoken, do not apply your heart, that is, do not give credence, because little faith should be given to words, since many people say many things: because, Sirach nineteen, "he who believes quickly is light of heart"; and because it is difficult to hear and not believe: Sirach twenty-eight: "Hedge your ears with thorns, and do not listen to a wicked tongue"; whence one ought not be anxious to hear what is said about oneself. And the reason is given: Lest perhaps you hear your servant cursing you: as Shimei cursed David, 2 Kings sixteen: whence Abishai says: "Why does this most wicked dog curse my lord the king? I will go and cut off his head." - And this indeed often happens, as a person can know from himself: For your conscience knows that you have frequently cursed others, and thus you ought not diligently pay attention to it nor punish: Sirach thirty-one: "Know the things of your neighbor from yourself." And again, forgive: for as it is said in Matthew eighteen: "Was it not necessary then that you also should have had mercy on your fellow servant, as I also had mercy on you?"

QUESTIONS.

I. It is asked here first concerning what he says: Do not be overly just.

On the contrary: 1. This is self-evidently true: the just man is good; therefore the more just, the more good; therefore the most just, the most good: therefore in justice there is no excess.

2. Likewise, whatever is said of God is said in the highest degree; but just is said of God; therefore God is supremely just. And it is evident that this is not evil; therefore if the highest degree in justice is not evil, then there is no excess therein.

II. Likewise, it is asked concerning what is said: Nor be wise more than will be necessary.

i. For he who loves more understands more; but no one can love too much; therefore neither can one be too wise, insofar as love is directed toward God.

2. Furthermore, no one is too wise, therefore neither can anyone be: therefore no one can know more than is necessary.

But on the contrary: Virtue consists in the mean and is corrupted by excess, just as by deficiency: therefore just as there can be sin in being too lenient, so also in punishing too much.

I respond: it must be said to this that the just person in one way is the same as one having such a virtue; and thus the excess of justice is measured by approach to the mean,

and thus there cannot be excess in that case, because no one can be too virtuous nor approach the mean too closely. In another way, just is said insofar as it names the act of that virtue which is to punish: and thus its excess is not measured by approach to the mean, but rather to the superfluous; then he is called more just who punishes more, and to punish to the greatest degree is not good. And this is how it is understood in the text.

2. To the second objection, that whatever belongs to God is said in the highest degree: this is true of what is said absolutely and what names by way of habit; but what expresses an effect is not said in the highest degree, just as God does not punish in the highest degree nor spare in the highest degree, but insofar as it is fitting.

II. As to what is asked concerning wisdom, it must be said that the habit of wisdom can in no way be excessive; but regarding the act, it should be noted that it is threefold, namely to taste, to know, and to search out: to taste and to know no one can do too much, but to scrutinize one can indeed do too much. And the reason for this is that excessive scrutiny leads to foolishness, but love toward God cannot be excessive, because He is to be loved supremely.

III. There is a question concerning what he says: Be not foolish, lest you die before your time.

On the contrary: Job 14: You have appointed his limits, which cannot be passed beyond: therefore it seems that on account of goodness they cannot be increased; therefore neither can they be diminished on account of wickedness.

If this is true, how does God promise long years in Scripture?

Likewise, concerning Hezekiah, in 4 Kings 20, how did God add fifteen years to his life?

Likewise, it also shortens; in the Psalm: Men of blood and deceit shall not live out half their days.

I respond: it must be said that to die in one's time is threefold: in one way, in the time of one's salvation, to which each one is ordained: thus all the good die in their time, and no evil persons do, because they never attain this, I speak of those who are evil in the end.

In the second way, to die in its time, that is, by a natural death, which indeed occurs through the consumption of the radical moisture, and thus the aged and decrepit die in their time.

Thirdly, to die in time

his own is to die at the time predetermined for him by God, according to divine foreknowledge, which is never mistaken. No one anticipates that time, no one passes beyond it. In the first way, the wicked die at a time not their own; in the second way, both the good and the wicked, who die by accidental death; in the third way, no one.

Ecclesiastes and Scripture speak here regarding the first mode; but Job understands regarding the third. Similarly, concerning Hezekiah it is said that the Lord changed his counsel.

IV. Likewise, there is a question about what he says: He who fears God neglects nothing.

But he who sins neglects something: therefore no one who sins fears God. But there is no just man who does not sin: therefore there is no just man who fears God.

V. Likewise it is asked, whether any just person can be without sin.

That it does not, is seen through what is said here and in 1 John 1: If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves.

But that he can, is shown:

1. By that passage in Job 17: I have not sinned, and my eye dwells in bitterness.

2. Likewise, if he cannot: no one sins in that which he cannot avoid; therefore, etc.

I respond: it must be said that negligence in one way implies contempt, and thus it is a mortal sin, and so it is understood here; in another way, it implies any omission of a circumstance, and thus it is venial, and no one is without it.

Similarly it must be said concerning sin, that without mortal sin many have lived, without venial sin no one using free will except Christ, and this we believe of the most blessed Virgin.

1. As to what is objected concerning Job, I respond: he does not deny that he sinned, but denies that he sinned so as to be punished in this way. Similarly, the heart reproves when sin has given pleasure; but when one did what one did not want, even if one sinned, the heart does not reprove 13 .

2. As to what is said about the impossibility of avoiding sin, it must be said that man has been driven to this through vice, and therefore he is not excused from sinning in some way. Furthermore, even if he cannot avoid it universally, he can nevertheless avoid any particular instance; therefore, etc.

Part III. The vanity of twofold punishment is proven, both that which is an occasion of sin and that which is pure affliction.

I have tried all things, etc. Above he treated the vanity of mutability and iniquity; in this part he intends to treat the vanity of punishment. And because punishment is twofold—one kind, which is the occasion of sin, such as concupiscence; another kind, which is pure affliction, such as death and bodily infirmity—therefore he first treats the first kind of punishment, which is the occasion of sin; second, that which is pure affliction, below in chapter twelve: Remember your Creator, etc.

Part three, section I. The twofold punishment that is the occasion of sin is shown. And since the punishment that is the occasion of sin is twofold—one, namely, from a deficiency of love, and this is proneness; the other from a deficiency of fear, and this is false security: the first renders one prone to evil, and the second renders one secure, because he does not fear since he does not believe he will be punished: therefore this part has two sections: in the first he treats of the vanity of proneness, in the second, of the vanity of false security: and the second part begins below at chapter eight: All these things I considered and gave my heart etc.

Abt. 1. Concerning the first occasion of sin, which is the proneness to sin through concupiscence, it is treated in two ways.

And because the vanity of the proneness of concupiscence is dangerous, nor is anyone freed except by the help of divine wisdom, therefore first is noted the discovery of concupiscence. Second is added the remedy of wisdom, at: Who is such as the wise man is, etc.

First is noted the discovery of concupiscence, and first is set forth a diligent inquiry into it, which is described according to three aspects.

And since, where there is the discovery of something hidden, a diligent inquiry precedes it: therefore first is described the diligent inquiry itself; second, the discovery of concupiscence, at the words: And I find more bitter than death, etc.

His diligent inquiry, therefore, which was so diligent that it degenerated into curiosity, is described in this order: first, with regard to the curiosity of his presumption, with regard to the sublimity of his inquiry, with regard to the studiousness of his consideration.

(Vers. 24. 23. 26.). Therefore, as to his presumptuous curiosity, he says: I tried all things in wisdom, that is, in inquiry—behold, the inquiry of curiosity: Sirach 3: "do not be curious about his many works." I said: I will become wise, behold, the pride of presumption: Isaiah 5: "Woe to you who are wise in your own eyes," etc.: and 1 Corinthians 3: "If anyone among you seems to be wise in this age, let him become a fool, that he may be wise."

As for the sublimity of the inquiry, he says: And it itself receded farther from me than it was before, because the question seemed more difficult to him the more he inquired, and this through the severity of divine judgment: Romans 1: " Claiming to be wise, they became fools",- and through the depth of the question: therefore he says: And deep is the profundity, who shall find it? Deep is the profundity of the inquiry into wisdom: Sirach 24: " I dwelt in the highest places, and my throne was in a pillar of cloud ". And therefore who can find it? Few sufficiently, but none perfectly: Sirach 43: "Do not labor, for you will not comprehend ": and Romans 11: O the depth of the riches of the wisdom and knowledge of God " etc.

As for the vigor of consideration, he adds: I surveyed all things in my mind, that is, I went around and diligently considered from every side: Sirach 6: "As one who plows and one who sows, draw near to her." And the motivating reason is touched upon: That I might know, in habit: and might consider, in act: wisdom, in the appetites: and reason, in operations. And conversely: That I might know the wickedness of the fool, in things to be done: and the error of prudence, in things to be believed: above in chapter one: "I gave my heart to know prudence and doctrine, and errors and foolishness."

Secondly, the discovery of concupiscence itself is described, along with its threefold condition.

And I find etc. The diligent inquiry has been described; here is described the discovery of carnal concupiscence, and this with respect to a threefold condition. First is noted the proneness of concupiscence itself, second its generality, third its causality.

(Vers. 27.). First, therefore, the vehement proneness of concupiscence itself is touched upon, because it leads to the greatest bitterness under the appearance of sweetness. Therefore he says: I found woman more bitter than death, and this after the perpetration of the wicked deed to which concupiscence drags one; hence Proverbs 5: "The lips of a harlot are a dripping honeycomb, and her throat is smoother than oil." To this that concupiscence drags one,

which had its origin from woman and has its nourishment in woman, which by her beauty ensnares through love: therefore he says: It is the snare of hunters, that is, of demons, who hunt the souls of men. She ensnares by words and glances: Proverbs 7: "She entangled him with many words and drew him forth with the flatteries of her lips." She ensnares by the beauty of her face: Sirach 9: "On account of the beauty of a woman many perish." She also ensnares by words: whence in the same place: "Her conversation burns like fire." Not only does she ensnare in love, but she also swallows up in wicked work: whence he says: And her heart is a net, when according to her desire one consents to wicked work. This is the net of the devil: Habakkuk 1: "He lifts up the whole with a hook; he drew it in his net." But afterward she binds through affection: and therefore he says: And her hands are bonds: Proverbs 7: "He follows her and does not know that the fool is dragged to bonds." From this proneness no one is rescued unless he is helped by the Lord, so great is it; therefore he says: He who pleases God will escape her, according to that passage in 1 Corinthians 6: "Flee fornication"; Wisdom 8: "I knew that I could not otherwise be continent unless God grants it." But he who is a sinner will be captured by her: Proverbs 5: "His own iniquities capture the wicked man, and he is bound by the cords of his own sins."

(Vers. 28. 29.). Behold, this I have found etc. Here the second point is noted, namely the universality of concupiscence, the reason for which was difficult to see; therefore he says: Behold, this I have found, said Ecclesiastes, one thing, namely the proneness of concupiscence itself, and another, namely its universality.

And I labored, supply, to find the reason, which my soul still seeks; this is the reason for the universality of concupiscence, why it is in all. And I did not find it, namely by my own ingenuity; because the philosophers were unable to know this: but nevertheless by faith he knew the reason and by experience he knew the effect, namely the universality. Therefore he says: One man out of a thousand I have found, and a woman out of all I have not found: it is a hyperbole, and he means to say that he found very few men in whom concupiscence did not reign: but he himself found no women: nor is it surprising, because Sirach twenty-five: "All malice is slight compared to the malice of a woman".

But we could explain this concerning Christ, who alone escaped from the corruption of original concupiscence, who alone was "free among the dead."

(Vers. 30.). Only this I have found. Here the third thing is noted, namely the causality of concupiscence, which indeed was not from God, but from the first sin. Therefore he says: Only this I have found, that God made man upright: Sirach seventeen: "God created man from the earth, and made him according to his own image." And the sign of this is the uprightness of stature, which still remains in the body. And he himself, namely by his own freedom, entangled himself in endless questions: because by departing from the One he became inclined to many and infinite things, because in them his concupiscence is neither ended nor satisfied; Proverbs thirty: "Three things are insatiable, and the fourth, which never says, enough: hell and the mouth of the womb and the earth," etc.

QUESTIONS.


I. It can be questioned regarding what he says here: I have tried all things in wisdom: whether in this he was reprehensible. That he was, it seems:

i. Through the text itself: I said: I will become wise, and it withdrew further from me.

2. Likewise, by what is said in Sirach 3: Be not curious about his many works.

Contra: 1. The spiritual man judges all things, and he himself is judged by no one : therefore if Solomon had the spirit of wisdom, then it was his role to judge concerning all things.

2. Furthermore, at the end of First Thessalonians the Apostle says: Test all things: hold fast that which is good: therefore it is good to try all things.

I respond: it must be said that to test something is twofold: in one way, to know through experience, just as one knows and tests the wine that one tastes; in another way, through examination and approval and disapproval. In the first way, only useful things are to be tested; but if someone wishes to test greater things, it is curiosity. But insofar as to test is the same as to examine, so Paul takes to prove, and thus all things are to be examined, not only good and useful things, but also useless things. So it is said that the spiritual man judges all things; so also it is said: test all things, that is: examine all things, whether they are good or evil in our works, about which we can have doubt; but in the works of God, about which we ought to assume that they have been well made, it is curiosity to test or examine. Since therefore Solomon extended his examination beyond those things which pertain to salvation, therefore it was curiosity.

And that must be conceded.

Those two authorities are understood concerning those things which pertain to salvation and are relevant to oneself, but Solomon went further.

II. Likewise, there is a question about what he says: I have found one man among a thousand. According to this, he seems to confirm that heresy that no woman is saved; and heretics strive to prove this:

1. Because, if only that which Christ assumed was saved,

and he assumed only the male sex: therefore that alone will be saved.

2. And they attempt to prove this by what is said in the last chapter of Mark: He who believes and is baptized: he does not say: she who is baptized. And the same thing is said in John 3.

The contrary of this is shown: I. By that which is said in Matthew twenty-one: The harlots shall go before you into the kingdom of God.

2. Likewise, this is shown because the Lord received both sexes, healed both bodily, and called both through the Apostles. Solomon himself also praised both, and concerning the valiant woman in the last chapter of Proverbs: She shall laugh in the last day. What then does he mean when he says: A woman among all these I have not found, etc.

I respond: it must be said that he speaks of the universality of concupiscence, and about this one can speak in two ways: in one way with regard to carnal propagation, because it is transmitted; in another way with regard to the pursuit through actual sin. If in the first way, the statement is true and proper, because Christ alone was conceived without sin and did not contract original sin. If in the second way, the statement is hyperbolic: and this is clear, because there is not only one good man who is to be saved, but rather many; and that is said with regard to the rarity of those who at that time were found free from the act of concupiscence.

As for the objection of the heretics, that he assumed the male sex: I respond that although he assumed the male sex, one must consider that he assumed it from a woman, which he would not have done if it did not pertain to salvation. Moreover, man and woman are of one nature and one body.

2. As for what they object from the Gospel, that it is in the masculine: it must be said that that masculine is used in a general sense and stands there for nature, not for sex; and frequently the masculine gender includes the feminine according to the custom of Scripture.
