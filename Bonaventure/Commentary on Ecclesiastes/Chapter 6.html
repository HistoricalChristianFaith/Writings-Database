Chapter VI.


Thirdly, he detests avarice, because riches are frequently transferred to strangers.

There is also another evil, etc. Above he showed that riches are to be despised, both because they do not enrich their possessors, and because they are frequently not transmitted to posterity. Here he shows thirdly that they are to be despised, because they frequently pass to strangers: this indeed he does in this order. First indeed is introduced the transfer of hoarded riches: second, the detestation of miserliness, at: If a man beget etc.; third is added the reason for the detestation, at: Therefore he comes in vain, etc.

(Vers. 1. 2.). First, therefore, Ecclesiastes considered the vanity that follows the rich, and he proposes it, assumes it, and concludes. He proposes, saying: There is also another evil, which I have seen under the sun: and he aggravates it from the circumstance: And it is frequent among men, that is, among the weak lovers of the world and the rich, of whom the Psalm says: "Sons of men, how long will you be heavy of heart? Why do you love vanity and seek falsehood?" etc.

And he takes up vanity, which is observed in this, that one preserves riches, and another devours them: therefore he says: A man to whom God has given riches, as regards movable goods; and wealth, as regards immovable goods; and honors, as regards sublime things. For all things are from God: riches are from God: Proverbs 3: "Length of days is in her right hand, and in her left hand riches and glory." And wealth is from God, especially that of which Sirach 13 speaks: "Good is the wealth where there is no sin in the conscience." Likewise honor is from God: whence in Daniel 5, Daniel said to Belshazzar: "O king! The most high God gave kingdom and magnificence and honor to your father." And to complete the perfect abundance of goods of fortune, he adds: And nothing is lacking to his soul of all that he desires, because he has sufficiently. He calls soul here the carnal life, of which John 12 speaks: "He who loves his soul shall lose it." Thus he has abundance for possessing, and yet he has avarice for retaining, which takes from him the power of enjoying: therefore he adds: Nor does God grant him the power to eat of it, because he continually preserves it, and in vain: whence Sirach 14: "To a covetous and tenacious man, wealth is without reason; and to an envious man, what good is gold?" Neither does he himself use it, nor his descendants, but strangers: whence he adds: But a stranger shall devour it: whence Sirach 14: "He who heaps up unjustly from his own soul gathers for others, and another shall revel in his goods"; and Proverbs 13: "The wealth of the sinner is kept for the just." And from this he infers vanity: And this is vanity and great misery: vanity, because he himself does not rejoice; misery, because, when another takes it away, he is saddened: Isaiah 1: "Strangers devour your land before your face," etc.

(Vers. 3.). If anyone should beget etc. Here is noted the detestation of miserliness by reason of the aforementioned transfer, and he reproves that very miserliness, however much successive prosperity one may have. Therefore he adds: If anyone should beget a hundred children, this is said hyperbolically, that is, many children: because the wicked frequently beget wicked children, in whom there is no joy, even if they are many: Sirach 16: "Do not delight in ungodly children; if they multiply, do not take pleasure in them." To abound in children is temporal prosperity; and another temporal prosperity follows: And should live many years, that is, for many years; and should have more days of life, namely, more than other men; and this indeed is temporal prosperity. The impious frequently have this prosperity: whence below in chapter seven: "The impious man lives a long time in his impiety," if he should abound in these prosperities. And his soul does not use the goods of his substance, that is, he has been miserly and avaricious in life: and he lacks burial in death, according to what is said of Jehoiakim in Jeremiah 22: "Jehoiakim shall be buried with the burial of an ass, rotting and cast out beyond the gates of Jerusalem."

Having set forth these conditions of prosperity and tenacity, he adds a detestation: Concerning this I pronounce, as a judge of the earth, that the stillborn is better off than he, because of the misery and great vanity which the stillborn lacks; whence it is said in Job chapter three: "Why did I not die in the womb? Why, having come forth from the womb, did I not immediately perish? Why was I received upon the knees? Why was I nursed at the breasts?" And again in chapter ten: "Why did you bring me forth from the womb?"

(Vers. 4. 5. 6. 7.). For he comes in vain etc. Here is subjoined thirdly the reason for detestation, namely that in this way the stillborn is better than the miser, because, just as the stillborn comes in vain and passes into darkness, since he dies immediately, so also does this one: therefore it is said: For he comes in vain and passes into darkness, namely the miser himself: Job 3: "To the man whose way is hidden, and God has surrounded him with darkness." And that such a greedy and miserly man comes in vain into this world, he shows on account of the erasure of his name in death and on account of his inexperience of good in life and on account of the incompleteness of desire in both states. On account of the erasure of his name he says: And his name shall be blotted out by oblivion, that is, he will not endure either in reality or in name, according to what the Lord threatens against Babylon in Isaiah 14: "I will destroy the name of Babylon and its remnants and offspring and posterity, says the Lord"; and Wisdom 2 in the person of the wicked: "Our name will be forgotten in time, and no one will remember our works."

On account of the inexperience of good, he adds: he does not see the sun, in which, namely, there is delight and experience of good, according to what is said below in chapter eleven: "The light is sweet, and it is delightful for the eyes to see the sun." The greedy man has not seen this, because he wanders astray: Wisdom 5: "We have erred from the way of truth, and the light of justice has not shone upon us, and the sun of understanding has not risen upon us," etc. And so he has not seen the sun, that is, he has not had the light of knowledge; nor has he known the difference between good and evil, because, namely, he has not experienced it.

Therefore he says: Even if he has lived two thousand years, because we do not have knowledge through length of time, but through experience: Sirach 34: "He who is not experienced knows few things," according to what the Philosopher says: "Experience produced art, inexperience produced chance." On account of the incompleteness of desire he adds: Do not all things hasten to one place? as if to say: on the part of the body they will pass into the earth; Sirach 41: "All things that were made from the earth shall be turned back into the earth"; and above in chapter three: "All things are subject to vanity, and all things go to one place." Therefore on the part of the body there is reduction to ashes, but on the part of the soul there is the incompleteness of its desire.

Therefore he says: All the labor of man is in his mouth: the mouth is called the beginning of desire: for he who desires, as it were, opens his mouth: labor therefore is in the mouth, that is, to labor toward the fulfillment of desire: whence Proverbs 16: "The soul of him who labors, labors for himself, because his mouth compels him." And although he does this to fulfill his desire, it is nevertheless not fulfilled: therefore he says: But his soul, namely of the miserly rich man, will not be filled with good things, as was said above in chapter five: "The miser will not be satisfied with money"; whence Habakkuk 2: "He has enlarged his soul like hell, and he himself, like death, will not be filled." Just as this is true of the greedy concerning money, so also concerning knowledge: 2 Timothy 3: "Always learning, and never arriving at the knowledge of the truth."

Questions.


I. It is asked concerning what he says: God did not give him the power to enjoy his goods.

On the contrary: This is free will; but he gave free will; therefore also the power.

I respond: it must be said that he has the capacity for enjoying, namely by which he is able to enjoy; but by the just judgment of God he is impeded by some punishment, because he incurs excessive anxiety and fear and sorrow; and because power denotes an unimpeded capacity, and this has been impeded by just judgment, therefore, etc.

II. Likewise it is asked concerning what he says, that nothing is lacking to his soul from all the things that it desires.

Against this: Above in chapter five: The covetous man shall not be satisfied with money: and again Jerome: "The miser lacks both what he has and what he does not have": therefore he speaks wrongly when he says: And nothing is lacking.

I respond: it must be said that to lack something can be in two ways: either with respect to sufficiency according to reality and nature, or according to opinion. I say therefore that a greedy man can indeed be so rich that nothing is lacking to him according to reality and nature; but yet never so rich that something is not lacking to him according to his own estimation, because he always desires to have more — and thus he is in want.

III. Likewise, it is asked concerning this point, that he prefers the stillborn to him. It seems that he does so wrongly:

1. Because the stillborn had knowledge of nothing, but this man did; likewise, the miser, when he sins mortally, enjoys what is only to be used.

2. Likewise, how does he say that he did not know the difference between good and evil, unless he had fully enjoyed good things? Therefore it would seem that the stillborn is better than all who do not delight in present goods and who are not carnal: but this is heretical.

I respond: it must be said that this follows according to that line of reasoning; but if this is said in the person of the carnal man, the answer is easy: maintaining nevertheless that he says this according to truth, I say that he places the stillborn child above such a miserly rich man, because the former, although he has not experienced good things, nevertheless has not endured evils: but the latter has endured evils and has not tasted good things; therefore the former is better off, and this is what he means with respect to this point.

As for the objection raised on the grounds that he has not fully enjoyed goods, it must be said that to enjoy in one sense means the love of something with rest in it; in another sense, rest and delight. I say therefore that whoever loves God possesses him, as Augustine says in the book of Eighty-Three Questions, and therefore whoever loves God takes delight in him. But money or a temporal thing, when it is loved and not possessed according to one's desire, begets sorrow. Therefore there can be enjoyment of these things, that is, supreme love, and yet little delight and great sorrow. Therefore it does not follow that, although such a miser sins, he rests in delight, but nevertheless he rests in love, because he loves it for its own sake.

2. To the objection that he did not know the difference: it must be said that by *simple* knowledge, he who always has good things knows good and evil; likewise also he who always has evil things; but not by knowledge *of experience*, unless he has both good and evil. And because such a one always has evil things, never good; therefore, etc.

As to the objection concerning the spiritual man, I say that he is not without spiritual delight, and therefore he sees the sun and knows the difference. But the greedy miser, because he is a sinner, does not have spiritual delight; because he is miserly, he does not have carnal delight either; and therefore he does not know the difference between good and evil.

Art. 3. On the remedy against the vanity of imprudence, which is the acquisition of wisdom, which is explained in two parts.

What more does he have etc. He set forth above the remedy against the vanity of malice and against the vanity of avarice: here thirdly he sets forth the remedy against the vanity of imprudence. Against this, however, the supreme remedy is the acquisition of wisdom; and because no one can acquire wisdom unless he has the proper method of learning, therefore this part has two sections. In the first he gives the method, in the second he unfolds the teachings of wisdom, there: A good name is better etc.

First, the manner of acquiring wisdom is explained by a threefold reason.

The method of considering or learning for one who wishes to be wise is handed down in this way. First, that one should learn useful things; second, that one should leave aside hidden things; third, that one should not inquire intolofty things.

(Vers. 8.). First, therefore, he teaches us to seek and consider useful things, and this is how one can arrive at the life of glory. Therefore he asks: What does the wise man have more than the fool, and what does the poor man have? He rightly joins these two together: poor and wise. Poor is said through contempt of present things; whence Matthew 5: "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven"; wise, through the savor of eternal things and right judgment, concerning which Proverbs 3: "The wise shall possess glory; the exaltation of fools is disgrace." But the fool is called the worldly man, who on the contrary, despising eternal things, gapes after earthly things, concerning whom Job 5: "I saw the fool with a firm root, and I cursed his beauty at once." What more does the wise and poor man have than the fool in this life? Supply: little. Except that he may go there where life is, namely eternal life, to which man ought to aspire, not to temporal things, which cannot be held; on account of which he has little here in mortal life, because "the learned and the unlearned alike die," above in chapter 2; but in eternal life he has much more, because he will obtain delightful, abundant, and secure being — all things — in the open vision and enjoyment of the supreme good, concerning which life John 17: "This is eternal life, that they may know you, the only true God," etc. To this life the wise proceed, because they direct their eyes toward it, as is said above in chapter 2: "The eyes of the wise man are in his head"; and therefore he follows Christ, the light, who, as is said in John 14, is "the way, the truth, and the life"; but the fool does not proceed; whence below in chapter 10: "The labor of fools shall afflict them, because they do not know how to go to the city."

(Vers. 9.). To this life one cannot rightly arrive without knowledge: therefore he adds: Better is it to see

what you desire, that is, to know the life which you desire: than to desire what you do not know, that is, to wish to arrive there in ignorance: and therefore it is necessary to place one's consideration upon it: Proverbs 4: "Let your eyes look straight ahead, and let your eyelids go before your steps." And indeed it is good to know this, but to desire what one does not know is foolish. Therefore he adds: But this also is vanity and presumption of spirit, namely, to desire what man does not know, just as James and John desired and asked to sit at the right hand, and they sought presumptuously: therefore it is said to them in Matthew 20: "You do not know what you are asking."

It can also be explained as a commendation of the knowledge of useful things, so that the meaning is that it is better to know desirable and useful things than to desire to know hidden things, which he forbids us to consider, because this is presumption of spirit, namely to wish to know what God has disposed in his providence.

(Vers. 10. 11.). And indeed he forbids scrutinizing this, consequently saying: Who is to be etc. Here he calls back from scrutinizing hidden things, such as the divine disposition: on account of which he says: Who is to be, his name has already been called, with regard to eternal election; Romans 4: "Who calls those things which are not, as though they were." And it is known, that

man is, as regards the brevity of the duration of life: Genesis 3: "You are dust, and to dust you shall return." And he cannot contend in judgment against one stronger than himself, as regards the condition of justice: for no one is so just that he can justify himself in judgment with God: Job 9: "I know that man is not justified when compared to God. If he wished to contend with God, he could not answer him one thing out of a thousand"; therefore Job said: "Who then am I, that I should answer him"; and therefore it is added: because he is stronger: whence Job 9: "If strength is sought, he is the mightiest; if equity of judgment, no one dares to give testimony on my behalf." Therefore this hidden disposition is said not to be scrutinized by us; because in our words little truth is found, especially when we wish to discuss those things which are hidden from us.

Therefore he adds: Words are most numerous, that is, few things are known: below in chapter ten: "The fool multiplies words." And having much vanity in disputing, and little truth, above in chapter three: "He has delivered the world to their disputation, so that man cannot find out the work that God has done from the beginning to the end"; and for this reason those who wish to scrutinize these things become vain, according to that word of the Apostle in First Corinthians chapter three: "God knows the thoughts of the wise, that they are vain."
