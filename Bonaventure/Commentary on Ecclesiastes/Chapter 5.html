Chapter V.


Second, a remedy is given against foolish speech, which occurs in a threefold manner.

Do not rashly speak anything. Here he gives a remedy against foolish or disordered speech. Now speech is disordered either because it is thoughtless, or because it is false, and he prohibits this where he says: If you have vowed anything, etc.; or because it is erroneous, and he prohibits this where he says: Do not give your mouth to cause you to sin, etc.

Regarding speech, inconsiderate, he forbids rashness and adds the reason and provides confirmation by example.

(Vers. l.)- He therefore forbids rashness; hence he says: Do not rashly speak anything, that is, inconsiderately; Sirach 9: "He who is rash in his word will be hateful"; and again Sirach 28: "Make a balance for your words and bridles for your mouth." And because a hasty man cannot deliberate, he therefore adds: Neither let your heart be hasty to utter a word, rather one ought to deliberate at length; hence Proverbs 29: "Have you seen a man hasty in speaking? Foolishness is to be expected rather than his correction." Hence there ought to be a distance between the mouth and the heart; against which Sirach 21: "The heart of fools is in their mouth." Hence it is said in James 1: "Let every man be swift to hear and slow to speak"; and Seneca: "I want you to be slow of speech."

And the reason is added, namely the divine judgment, which sees all things: therefore he says: For God is in heaven, supply: seeing all things: whence in the Psalm: "The Lord looked from heaven upon the earth"; and you upon the earth, placed openly before him, so you do not escape his notice: Sirach twenty-three: "The eyes of the Lord are far brighter than the sun, looking around upon all the ways of men." Therefore let your words be few, because namely you speak before God, because he himself will demand an account of all things: Matthew twelve: "For every idle word that men shall have spoken, they shall give account for it on the day of judgment."

And it can hardly be that one who speaks much does not displease God. Hence he makes this clear by example and simile, saying: Many cares are followed by dreams, and therefore it is foolish to pay attention to them: Sirach thirty-four: "Dreams have led many astray." And in many words folly is found: Sirach twenty: "He who uses many words injures his own soul": and Proverbs ten: "In much speaking, sin will not be lacking"; but on the contrary, below in chapter ten: "The fool multiplies words."

(Vers. 3. 4.). If you have vowed anything etc. Here he secondly prohibits false speech, by which, namely, what is promised is not fulfilled.

He therefore urges to fulfill what has been promised: whence he says: If you have vowed anything to God, that is, you have promised by a vow: do not delay to fulfill it: whence in the Psalm: "Vow and pay to the Lord your God, all you who are round about him, bring gifts"; and the Gloss: "To vow is voluntary, but to fulfill is necessary," because otherwise a man is false in his promise; whence he adds: For an unfaithful and foolish promise displeases God: unfaithful, in which there is no truth: foolish, in which there is no judgment, because a foolish promise should not be made: whence Isidore: "In evil promises, break your word; in a shameful vow, change your decree." Therefore he adds that truth must be kept in a promise, when he says: But whatever you have vowed, fulfill it, without wavering; Leviticus twenty-seven: "A clean animal that can be offered in sacrifice, if anyone has vowed it, shall be holy and cannot be changed, neither for better nor for worse."

Otherwise it would have been better not to vow; therefore he says: And it is much better not to vow, that is, it is less evil, because it is without fault, than not to fulfill what was promised after a vow: 2 Peter 2: "It would have been better for them not to have known the way of truth, than after knowing it to turn back"; because Luke 9: "No one who puts his hand to the plow and looks back is fit for the kingdom of God."

(Vers. 6.). Do not give your mouth etc. Here thirdly he forbids erroneous speech: and he discourages the error of speech, and then adds the reason, and afterward reveals the root of the error.

He discourages the error of speech, saying: Do not give your mouth to cause your flesh to sin, that is, do not speak an erroneous utterance by which you would be cast headlong into sins. And he specifies what kind of speech he means, adding: Neither say before the Angel: There is no providence, that is, do not say it, neither in secret nor in hidden places, because in hidden places the Angels hear, who are appointed for guardianship; whence below in the tenth chapter: "Do not detract from the king in your thought, and do not curse the rich man in the secret of your bedchamber, for the birds of heaven will carry your voice." And the reason for the exhortation is added: Lest perhaps the Lord, being angry, destroy your works, that is, render them useless: angry, not through a disturbance of mind, but through a rigid punishment of fault. Thus He was angry over the words

of Rabshakeh and all the works of the kings of the Assyrians he destroyed through an Angel by night: Isaiah thirty-seven: "If somehow the Lord your God may hear the words of Rabshakeh, whom the king of the Assyrians sent to blaspheme the living God"; Proverbs twenty: "A wise king scatters the wicked," etc.

And the origin: of this speech is added, because it does not arise from right reason, but from the illusion of dreams; on account of which he says: Where there are many dreams, there are very many vanities, because a dream is vanity: Isaiah twenty-nine : (The hungry one dreams and eats, and afterward, when he has awakened, his soul is empty ". And innumerable words, there follow upon dreams, because nothing certain can be known from them: because Sirach thirty-four: " Divination of error and lying auguries and dreams of evildoers are vanity ". And therefore one should not concern oneself with such things, but with God: whence he adds: But you, fear God, because he alone is the one who can destroy us and save us, not dreams, not auguries: Luke twelve: " I will show you whom you should fear: fear him who, after he has killed, has the power to cast into hell ".

Thirdly, a remedy is given against erroneous thinking.

(Vers. 7. 8.) If you see the oppressions etc. Here thirdly he draws back from erroneous thinking, namely that from seeing the wickedness of men one should not believe that providence is lacking; whence he says: If you see the oppressions of the poor: above in chapter four: "I saw the oppressions that are done under the sun, and the tears of the innocent." And violent judgments, which he saw, above in chapter three: "I saw under the sun in the place of judgment, wickedness." And justice overthrown in the province: which similarly he had seen above in chapter three: "I saw in the place of justice, iniquity." From this consideration, I say, one ought not to wonder, as though there were no one who governs nor who corrects; whence he says: Do not wonder at this matter, as though there were no one who governs, because there is one who orders all things; whence he adds: Because there is one higher than the high, and above these there are yet others more eminent: whence in Matthew chapter eight the centurion said: "I am a man placed under authority, having soldiers under me, and I say to this one: Go, and he goes."

And moreover the king of all the earth commands the one who serves: therefore, although they do not serve one another and are not ordered in themselves, they are nevertheless ordered by God, who knows how to make good use of evil things: in Genesis forty-one it is signified: "I am Pharaoh; without your command no one shall move hand or foot in all the land of Egypt." Hence it is said in Revelation nineteen: "He had written on his garment and on his thigh: King of kings and Lord of lords." And he himself perverts nothing: hence it is said in Deuteronomy ten: "The Lord our God, he is the God of gods and the Lord of lords, the great Lord, mighty, terrible, who does not regard persons nor accept bribes: he executes judgment for the orphan and the widow"; and therefore, if he commands, justice is not perverted.

QUESTIONS.


I. Here a question arises concerning what he says: Whatever you have vowed, pay it: whether one who has vowed indiscreetly is bound, supposing that he intends to obligate himself: because, if not:

Then he gains the advantage from his lack of discernment

2. Likewise, he is bound to act in truth: but he who does not do what he promised does not act in truth, but rather lies: therefore such a person is bound to do what he vowed.

3. Likewise, Jephthah made an indiscreet vow and fulfilled it, and he is commended by the Apostle in Hebrews 11: therefore we ought to carry out and fulfill indiscreet vows.

On the contrary: 1. What is indiscreet to say is indiscreet to do; if therefore he who vows in this way is indiscreet by the very fact that he says it, then if he does it, he is much more indiscreet and sins more greatly; therefore by omitting it he does well.

2. Likewise, a preceding evil does not excuse a subsequent one: therefore if someone vowed something foolish and does it, he is no less blameworthy than if he did it without a vow; nor is the vow less blameworthy than if he did not do it: therefore etc.

3. Likewise, Isidore: "In wicked promises, break your pledge; in a shameful vow, change your decision": therefore etc.

I answer: it must be said that he is not bound; indeed, if he does so, he adds sin upon sin. Hence Jephthah, by fulfilling his vow, sinned more than if he had not fulfilled it, because in vowing he was foolish and in fulfilling it he was impious, as Augustine says.

3. To what is objected, that it is commended: it must be said that it is not the vow nor the fulfillment of the vow that is commended, because the Apostle commends there faith, and he commends the faith of Jephthah, that he himself believed he would triumph over his enemies by the Lord's help.

1. To the objection that it brings benefit: I say that this is false, because immediately in the vow itself he sins and is worthy of punishment.

2. To what is objected concerning truth, it must be said that truth lays judgment as its foundation: because therefore he vowed without judgment, he cannot fulfill it with truth: and for this reason there cannot be truth in such a vow, and therefore there is no obligation.

II. It is asked whether he can change.

That it cannot, is seen through that passage in Leviticus last chapter: It cannot be changed, neither for worse nor for better.

That it is so, is seen from this: because it is permitted to make the Lord's portion better; therefore, even if it is not permitted to change for the worse, it is nevertheless permitted to change for the better.

I respond: it must be said that there is changing a vow for the altogether worse, and there is changing for the better, which includes, the former, as when one who vowed to enter a less perfect religious order enters a more perfect one, which encloses the former within itself. There is also changing for the better that does not include, as changing a journey to Rome into an overseas journey.

The first is not permitted at all, but is prohibited; the second is permitted in itself; the third is permitted, yet it is not permitted without the authority of a superior. For since I am bound to the Lord, I cannot change except according to His judgment; and since He does not speak to me except through His minister, who holds His place, I cannot change without the authority of a superior, unless the Lord Himself most certainly inspires from within. And thus that point is clear.

III. There is also a question about what he says: Do not say before the Angel: There is no providence.

On the contrary: it seems that there is no providence:

I. First, because providence gives due order to things; but this order is not found in the world, below in chapter ten: I saw servants on horses, and princes walking on the ground like servants.

2. Likewise second: because providence gives due recompense, but this is not in the world, below in chapter nine: The same things happen to all.

3. Likewise thirdly: because it gives due duration; but this simply does not exist in the world, below in chapter seven: The wicked man lives a long time in his wickedness.

But that it is necessary to posit this, is seen:

I. Because it belongs to the wise man to provide for his household: therefore much more so for the supremely wise.

2. Likewise, it belongs to a wise craftsman not only to make, but also to preserve; thus God preserves and governs: but he is wise, and a wise one governs everything that he governs through providence: therefore etc.

3. Likewise, because God is supremely wise, He cannot fail to see our evils: therefore, since He is supremely just, He cannot fail to punish evils and reward goods: therefore there is recompense for all things, and therefore providence as well.

IV. Likewise it is asked who sins more gravely: the one who denies providence, or the one who criticizes divine providence.

It seems that he who denies is more so, because he who believes that providence exists, by the very fact that he believes, does well; therefore he has something of good. But he who simply denies has nothing; therefore he is more blameworthy.

Against this: Jerome says that he sins more who affirms providence and accuses God, than he who simply denies providence.

I respond to the first, that there is providence.

1. To the objection concerning due order, the response is that order is twofold, namely particular and universal: something can be deprived of particular order, but not of universal order, as Boethius says. Universal order embraces all things: hence whoever falls from the order of nature falls into the order of punishment: just as Augustine's example concerns a cloud, in which there is a whirlwind and a disordered swirling in itself, yet ordered toward the breaking of the earth — so the wicked rule over the good for the testing of the good and the punishment of the wicked.

2. Similarly, concerning recompense it must be said that in the present life good things come to the wicked in a just manner, so that they may be rewarded here and eternally tormented in the future; and evil things come to the good, so that they may be purified here and eternally rewarded.

3. As to the objection concerning life, it must be said that the wicked live long for the stated reason, but the good die; because it is said of Enoch that he was taken away, lest wickedness should change his understanding. Since therefore the divine judgments in the present are hidden for the testing of men, and since the simple and unlearned do not perceive hidden things, it seems that all things are disordered; and therefore Ecclesiastes teaches us to look to him under whom all things are ordered.

As to the question of who sins more gravely, I respond that it must be said that to deny providence can happen in two ways: in one way, because we believe God to be ignorant, or because we believe that he does not care about the deeds of mortals. Likewise, to accuse is also twofold: either because he does not care, or because he perverts justice. In both ways, to accuse providence is worse, because one not only errs but indeed reviles and blasphemes God; and so one sins more gravely in an absolute sense, and knowledge there does not excuse but rather aggravates.

Art. 2. On the remedy against the vanity of avarice, since riches are to be despised for a threefold reason.

The greedy man will not be satisfied with money. Above he set forth the remedy against the vanity of malice; here he adds secondly the remedy against the vanity of avarice. Now the remedy against avarice is contempt for earthly goods: therefore here he urges contempt for riches. And he does this indeed by a threefold argument: for first he shows that riches are not to be desired, because they do not enrich their possessors: second, because they are not frequently transmitted to posterity, at the passage: There is another most grievous infirmity: third, because they frequently pass to strangers, at the passage: There is also another evil that I have seen under the sun, etc.

First, riches are to be despised, because they do not enrich their possessors, and this for a threefold reason.

First, therefore, he denounces avarice, because the riches that are desired neither help nor enrich their possessors, because they do not suffice: second, because they do not profit: third, because they afflict.

(Vers. 9.) Riches are therefore to be detested and despised, because they do not suffice for the greedy man, and this is what the greedy man first desires, namely sufficiency. Therefore he says: The covetous man shall not be satisfied with money: Sirach fourteen: "The eye of the covetous is insatiable." Hence Jerome: "The greedy man lacks both what he has and what he does not have"; and Seneca: "If you wish to make yourself rich, you must not add to your money, but subtract from your desire."

(Vers. 9. 10.). And he who loves riches etc. Here he secondly dissuades from the love of riches, because they do not profit. Therefore he says: And he who loves riches will not reap fruit from them, and thus they do not profit: Sirach 10: "Nothing is more wicked than to love money. For such a one has his soul for sale"; and thus riches profit the greedy man nothing, because he gives his soul for money: whence Matthew 16: "What does it profit a man, if he gains the whole world, yet suffers the loss of his soul"? as if to say: nothing. And from this he infers vanity in loving them: And this therefore is vanity, both of guilt and of punishment: concerning the first, the Psalm: "Sons of men, how long will you be heavy of heart" etc.; concerning the second, the Psalm: "Their days were consumed in vanity" etc. The Psalm: "Behold the man who did not make God his helper, but trusted in the abundance of his riches and prevailed in his vanity." And he shows that riches profit the possessor nothing: because they are consumed, and the master has nothing more than the servant: therefore he says: Where there are great riches, many also are those who consume them: thus all things are consumed. Whence Seneca: "Wolves follow corpses, flies follow honey: that crowd follows the prey, not the man"; Job 39: "Its young ones lick up blood, and where the carcass is, it is immediately present"; because, according to what is said in Proverbs 19, "many honor the person of the mighty and are friends of the one who gives gifts." And what does it profit

to the owner, except that he beholds riches with his eyes? as if to say: nothing more for himself than for others, because any one of the others eats just as much as he alone; and so he has no advantage over others, but rather a loss, because he has more sin; whence Sirach 11: "If you are rich, you will not be free from sin. If you pursue, you will not overtake," etc.

(Vers. 11.). Sweet is sleep. Here he shows thirdly that riches are to be spurned, because they afflict and do not permit a person to rest: whence they take away the sleep of quiet. Therefore he says: Sweet is the sleep of the worker, whether he eats little or much. And so the one who works and labors sometimes rests: Sirach thirty-one: "Healthy sleep is in a temperate person: he will sleep until morning, and his soul will delight in him." But on the contrary, concerning the rich person, he does not rest: whence he adds: But the fullness of the rich person does not allow him to sleep:

Or, because he has many riches, he has many cares, which take away sleep: Job 15: "The sound of terror is always in his ears, and when there is peace, he suspects ambushes"; whence above in the second chapter: "All his days are full of labors and sorrows, nor does his mind rest at night." Or also, because he eats and drinks much and does not labor, therefore he does not rest: whence Sirach 31: "Wakefulness and bile and torment for the senseless man," and thus on account of excess he does not rest.

QUESTIONS.

But it is asked concerning what he says, that riches do not profit the possessor.

1. For below in the seventh chapter: Wisdom is more useful with riches and profits more those who see the sun.

2. Likewise, Proverbs 13: The redemption of a man's soul is his riches; therefore they avail for redeeming the soul.

Contra: 1. Everything which it is simply better to let go than to hold is simply useless, because if the absence of something is useful, its presence is harmful: therefore if letting go of riches is good, holding them is useless.

2. Likewise, all usefulness is measured according to its ordering toward the ultimate end; therefore, that which presents an impediment to reaching the end is not useful, but useless. But riches are of this kind, because it is impossible for one who loves riches to enter into heaven, and it is difficult to have them and not love them; therefore the possession of riches is an impediment; therefore they do not benefit, but rather harm.

I respond to this that, as Bernard says, "these temporal things are in themselves neither good nor evil; the use of them is good, the abuse evil, the anxiety over them worse, the pursuit of gain from them most base." If therefore it is asked whether riches are useful or useless, I say that to the one who uses them well they are useful, but to the one who hoards them they are useless, and to the one who uses them badly they are harmful; whence it is said below in the same chapter: Riches kept to the harm of their owner; but to the one who uses them well they are of value, namely when he distributes them in works of mercy.

For thus they avail for the redemption of guilt: Daniel 4: Redeem your sins with almsgiving. They avail for the increase of grace: 1 Timothy 4: Exercise yourself unto godliness, etc. They avail for the attainment of glory: Luke 16: Make for yourselves friends from the mammon of iniquity, so that when you fail, they may receive you into eternal tabernacles.

1. To the objection that they are useless, because it is good to give them away: it must be said that to give away is to give to God, and this is to use riches well; hence they are useful for giving away, for giving to the poor, for purchasing the kingdom.

2. As to the objection that it is difficult to have and not to love: I respond that from this it does not follow that they hinder, because every virtue concerns what is difficult, and yet virtue does not hinder but helps. Nevertheless, the more useful and safer practice is to give up everything at once rather than to give little by little, in order to avoid the dangers that arise.

Secondly, riches are to be despised, because they are frequently not passed down to posterity.

There is also another infirmity, etc. Above he showed that riches do not enrich their possessors; here he adds secondly that they are to be despised, because frequently they are not passed on to descendants; and indeed he does this in this order. First is noted the consideration of future poverty in one's offspring. Second, from this he denounces the laborious anxiety of acquiring money. Third, in denouncing this, he commends the enjoyment of present gladness, adding that this is a gift of divine generosity.

(Vers. 12. 13. 14. 15.). He considers therefore first the future poverty in the offspring of the rich man, because, when they are preserved for a long time by the father, they do not remain to the son. Therefore he says: There is also another very grievous affliction, which I have seen under the sun, another, namely, from the aforementioned, which, I say, is in the possession of riches: whence he says: Riches preserved to the harm of their owner, because they cannot be gathered without the evil both of punishment and of guilt: Sirach 31: "Blessed is the man who is found without blemish, and who has not gone after gold nor placed his hope in treasures of money. Who is this? and we will praise him: for he has done wonderful things in his life." Or: to harm, for they cause a man to come to the harm of punishment; James 5: "Come now, you rich, weep and howl in the miseries that will come upon you." Of their owner, but in reality their slave, because avarice makes the avaricious man a slave: whence Ephesians 5: "Covetousness, which is the service of idols." — These, I say, perish in the worst affliction, because it is necessary that he leave them behind with sorrow: Job 27: "When the rich man falls asleep, he will take nothing with him." Nor are they only left behind by the master, but also lost by the son, whence he adds: He has begotten a son who will be in the utmost poverty. There is an utmost poverty, and this is of all goods, concerning which Proverbs 6: "Poverty will come like a traveler," etc.

And thus riches that are hoarded are not left to an heir, nor can they be carried away by the possessor; whence he adds: As he came forth naked from his mother's womb, so shall he return, not into the womb, but into the earth. And he shall take nothing with him of his labor: Job 1: "Naked I came forth from my mother's womb, and naked shall I return thither." - And this is greatly to be lamented; whence he adds: A miserable infirmity indeed: as he came, so shall he return: whence 1 Timothy, last chapter: "We brought nothing into this world, and without doubt neither can we carry anything out."

(Vers. 15. 16.). What then does it profit? Here secondly he reproves the anxiety of increasing wealth by consideration of approaching poverty: whence he infers as if from what was said before, that it is vain to afflict oneself with such great labors in the acquisition of things. Therefore he says: What then does it profit him, namely the greedy rich man, that he labored into the wind? that is, in vain, if it is so that "just as he came forth naked from the womb, naked he shall return," both he himself and his son, as if to say: nothing. Therefore in the Psalm: "Sons of men, how long will you be heavy of heart? Why do you love vanity and seek after falsehood"? Riches are compared to the wind on account of their vanity: whence Sirach thirty-four: "As one who grasps at a shadow and pursues the wind, so also is he who attends to lying visions"; he labored therefore into the wind, when he labored for the sake of acquiring riches.

And he adds the greatness of the toil: All the days of his life he eats in darkness, on account of loneliness; and in many cares, on account of anxiety; and in hardship, on account of the meagerness of expenditures; and sadness, on account of the necessity of expenditures. For he is saddened because he must eat something: Sirach 14: "An evil and envious eye will not be satisfied with bread, and he will be needy and sorrowful at his own table." Riches cause a man to be in cares and hardships, because Matthew 13: "The care of this world and the deceitfulness of riches chokes the word."

(Vers. 17. 18. 19.). This therefore seemed good to me. Here thirdly he commends the enjoyment of gladness in detestation of the affliction of avarice. For since the miser afflicts himself, and nothing profits him, therefore it seemed good to him to eat; and for this reason he says: This therefore seemed good to me, that one should eat and drink, as regards exterior enjoyment; and enjoy gladness from his labor, with which he labored under the sun: gladness, as regards interior enjoyment: and this, while he can; whence he also adds: The number of the days of his life, which God gave him, as if he should not wait for another time. And this is his portion. Concerning all these things, Isaiah twenty-two: "Behold joy and gladness, slaying rams, eating flesh and drinking wine: let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we shall die," as if that were their portion:

And he approves this consideration, as though it pleases God; hence he adds: And to every man, to whom God has given substance and riches: for these things are from God; hence Sirach 11: "Good things and evil, life and death, poverty and honor are from God." And has given him power to eat of them, by delighting outwardly: and enjoy his portion, by resting in them: and rejoice in his labor, by joyfully spending what he has acquired. This is the gift of God, for this power is from God: because John 15: "Without me you can do nothing." And he shows that it is a great gift, because the torment of cares and anxieties, which are great punishments, are tempered by delights. Therefore he adds: I say rightly that it is the gift of God, insofar as it frees from the torments of avarice.

For he will not much remember the days of his life, that is, he will not be much afflicted by cares that pertain to life: because God occupies his heart with delights, not spiritual ones, about which in Song of Songs 7: "How beautiful you are and how graceful, dearest, in delights!" but carnal ones, about which it was said above in chapter 2: "I made for myself singers, both men and women, and the delights of the sons of men".

QUESTIONS.


I. But here a question arises first concerning what he says: This has seemed good to me, that one should eat.

Contra: 1. Below in chapter ten: Woe to you, O land, whose king is a child, and whose princes eat in the morning: he therefore contradicts himself.

II. Furthermore he says: And let him rejoice.

Contra: 1. Below in chapter seven: It is better to go to the house of mourning than to the house of feasting, and again: The heart of the wise is where sadness is.

2. Likewise, the Truth himself says in Luke 6: Woe to you who laugh now, for you shall weep: therefore it is evil to rejoice, and all of Scripture condemns the joy of the flesh: what then does it mean that he commends it here?

To this the answer is given through the singular manner of proceeding in this book among all the books of Sacred Scripture. For he speaks as a preacher weaving together his disputation, in which diverse opinions are brought forward according to diverse persons, in whose character he speaks: whence he speaks one way in the person of the carnal man, as here, and another way in the person of the wise man, as below: It is better to go etc. But afterward he speaks in his own person, where he says: Let us all hear together the end of the discourse, where he as it were passes judgment on those whose case he had previously examined: Fear God, and that God will judge all things. Whence whatever is read in the book that is contrary to that judgment is annulled by that judgment: and such are all the things that are said in commendation of present pleasure: and thus by one solution the answer is given to all objections.

It can be said otherwise that Ecclesiastes himself, whatever he says, speaks in his own person; but nevertheless, to understand the things he says, two things must be attended to, namely the reason for speaking and the manner. The manner of speaking varies in two ways: for some things he says truly, and some ironically: truly, as in that passage: Cast your bread upon the passing waters, below in chapter eleven; ironically, as in that passage: Rejoice therefore, young man, in your youth. And this is clear from what follows: And know that for all these things God will bring you into judgment, below in chapter eleven.

Another variation is observed in the manner, because some things he says by approving: Do not rashly speak anything, etc.; some things by recounting, that he himself did thus, as he said above in the second chapter: I did not forbid my heart from enjoying every pleasure, etc.: this he does not approve, but recounts that he did thus, just as the Apostle also says of himself that he was a blasphemer. Similarly, some things he says by recounting what he thought, as when he said above in the second chapter: Is it not better to eat and drink? etc. And he uses this kind of speech frequently in this little book, as if narrating his temptations: hence this book was like a certain meditation of Solomon. And just as a man falls from one meditation into another according to various considerations, as when someone thinks this to be good, and afterward falls into another estimation: so Solomon narrates also in this book.

As for the reason for speaking, it should be noted that some things he says simply for their own approval, but some things he says not for approval, but for comparison with and condemnation of other things, just as one who wished to condemn avarice would praise prodigality: so too in this book he praised the dead in comparison with the living, and pleasure in contrast to avarice, because in avarice there is both punishment and guilt. He did this, however, to call people back from the latter, not to attract them to the former.

Furthermore, regarding the reason for speaking, it should be noted that some things he says to all, and some things to certain ones who hold certain assumptions, and from their assumptions he argues against them, as he said above, that there is no remembrance of the wise man, as of the fool. He said this to those wise men who placed their entire reward in the praises of men.

This rule, therefore, is to be generally observed in his words: that what he says truly and approvingly he says commending in itself, he says to all, that is, truly and assertively, universally and absolutely: these indeed are to be adduced as authoritative. But what he says ironically and what he says recitatively he says for the denunciation of others, or from some supposition, or to the contrary. That word, therefore, was spoken not assertively, but recitatively: for he recites his own trial, which he intends to narrate.

Or this was said not absolutely, but for the detestation of avarice

III. Likewise, there is a question concerning what he says, that this is the gift of God, that one may enjoy riches: but this is a sin: therefore etc.

IV. Likewise he says that God occupies the heart with delights in life: but this again is sin.

I respond: it must be said that the goods of fortune, of nature, and also of grace are all gifts of God: but those of fortune are the least, those of nature are intermediate, and those of grace are the best. Among the gifts of nature are counted health and strength; among the goods of fortune, prosperity and tranquility. For God gives riches to some, to whom he does not give health or tranquility, and these do not enjoy their goods: to some he adds in addition tranquility and health, and such people have the power of enjoying them: and because those are gifts of God, so also is the power: but furthermore, the evil misuse of power — this is not from God.

III. As to what is asked, that God is said to occupy his heart with delights: it must be answered that God did some things mercifully, and some things by abandoning through just judgment, just as He is said to hand over the wise of this world to a reprobate mind: so too He is said to occupy the heart with delights by permitting through just judgment that it be occupied and forget eternal things.
