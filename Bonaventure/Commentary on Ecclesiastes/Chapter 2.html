Chapter II.


(Vers. 1.). I said in my heart etc. Here the third point is touched upon, namely, the turning to pleasure on account of weariness from contemplation. For since in contemplating there was labor and affliction, he therefore thought to abandon this and turn himself to delights, in which there is pleasure. On account of which he says: I said in my heart, that is, I came to this consideration because of the spiritual affliction in contemplating. Hence this is a word and consideration not of wisdom, but of sloth, which, not finding rest within, seeks it without. I said therefore: I will go and abound in delights and enjoy good things. I will go, that is, withdrawing from the true good: the Psalm: Man became "a spirit that goes and does not return." I will abound in delights, as regards the multitude of pleasures, namely carnal ones as opposed to spiritual: Song of Songs 7: "How beautiful you are, dearest, in delights." From these he turned to carnal ones: Isaiah 22: "The Lord called to weeping and mourning" etc., and afterward: "Behold joy and gladness, slaughtering rams" etc. And enjoy good things, as regards rest: for one enjoys that in which the appetite finds rest: Wisdom 2: "Come, let us enjoy the good things that are, and let us use creation as in youth swiftly"; and Proverbs 7: "Come, let us enjoy the desired embraces." But because this consideration was blameworthy and reprehensible, he therefore rebukes himself, because present pleasure is not true pleasure, but deception: therefore he adds: And I saw that this also was vanity: because it neither endures nor refreshes, but fails and deceives.

(Vers. 2.). Therefore he says: I counted laughter an error, because it leads man astray: and to joy I said: why are you vainly deceived? because, namely, the one rejoicing in it is deceived. Laughter is outward, and joy is inward. Laughter deceives, because it offers good and in the end evil; Proverbs 14: "Laughter shall be mingled with sorrow, and mourning takes hold of the ends of joy": and James 4: "Let your laughter be turned into mourning," because Luke 6: "Woe to you who laugh, for you shall weep".

This laughter is the laughter of madmen, because it is erroneous and momentary. Likewise also joy inwardly deceives: Job twenty-one: (They rejoice at the sound of the organ: they spend their days in good things, and in a moment they descend to hell"; and Job twenty: "The joy of the hypocrite is but for a moment".

(Vers. 3.) I thought in my heart, etc. Here the fourth point is touched upon, namely the return to the consideration of useful things, and this by returning from pleasure to sobriety. On account of which he says: I thought in my heart to abstain my flesh from wine, because it inclines in a remarkable way toward pleasure; whence Ephesians 5: "Do not be drunk with wine, in which is excess"; and Proverbs 20: "Wine is a luxurious thing, and drunkenness is tumultuous: whoever delights in these will not be wise." Therefore he thought to leave behind the incitement of pleasure, that I might transfer my mind to wisdom, that is, to the consideration of wise things, and leave behind the consideration of foolish things, such as was the preceding consideration; whence he says: And that I might avoid foolishness; and this as an exhortation to wisdom; Proverbs 1: "How long, little ones, will you love childishness, and fools desire things that are harmful to them?" And this not only for his own benefit, but for the common benefit; therefore he says: That I might see what is useful for the children of men; and this in the present life, whence he also adds: What deed must be done, that is, what is opportune to do, under the sun in the number of the days of their life. This he saw and declared at the end of the book: "Fear God, and keep his commandments: this is the whole of man."

This is useful: to keep the commandments of God, because it is said below in the eighth chapter: "He who keeps the commandment shall experience nothing of evil"; and Proverbs the nineteenth: "He who keeps the commandment keeps his soul; but he who neglects his way shall die".

The putting away of iniquity: 2 Timothy 2: "If anyone cleanses himself from these things, he will be a sanctified vessel, useful to the Lord, ready for every good work." - The instruction of truth: 2 Timothy 3: "All teaching divinely inspired is useful for instructing in righteousness," so that a person may be righteous.

The exercise of piety: 1 Timothy 4: "Exercise yourself unto piety: for bodily exercise is profitable for a little, but piety is profitable for all things."

Useless things: of God's law proud transgression: 2 Chronicles 24: "Why do you transgress the commandment of the Lord, which will not profit you?" Wisdom without teaching: Sirach 20: "Hidden wisdom and an unseen treasure, what use is there in either?" Teaching without life: Luke 14: "Salt is good; but if the salt loses its flavor, with what shall it be seasoned?" etc. Contentious disputation: 2 Timothy 2: "Do not contend with words: for it is useful for nothing."

QUESTIONS.


It is asked concerning what he says: He who adds knowledge adds also sorrow.

Contra: 1. "All men by nature desire to know"; but desire, if it is fulfilled, delights the soul: therefore etc.

2. Likewise, either he speaks of true wisdom, and this is false, because Wisdom 8: "Her companionship has no bitterness." If of curious, this is false, because there is great delight for them when they can know something.

I respond: it must be said that it can be understood of both. Of curious knowledge it is true, because, just as with riches avarice and the appetite for having grows, so in the curious person the appetite for learning grows. But if of the knowledge of truth: insofar as it implies savor, it delights; insofar as it implies knowledge, it occasionally causes sorrow, either on account of the uncleanness of one's own conscience, or on account of the sight of others' perversity.

Secondly, he reveals his curiosity in the works of opulence, and first describes it with regard to artificial things, which are ordered toward riches, and this indeed for a fourfold reason.

I made my works great etc. He treated above of curiosity in the consideration of wisdom: here he intends to treat of curiosity in mechanical works, which regard those things that are of the body, while the former regard those of the spirit. The first are liberal, the second, artificial,

And this part has two [sections]: because first it treats of artificial things, which are ordered to riches: second, of those which are ordered to delights, there: I made for myself singers.

He therefore describes his preoccupation with those things that pertain to the possession of riches in this order: first, regarding the acquisition of stable possessions; second, regarding the multiplication of servants; third, regarding the increase of herds and animals; fourth, regarding the accumulation of minerals; in which four things nearly all the delights of worldly people consist.

(Vers. 4.5.6.). First, therefore, he expresses his pursuit regarding the possession of fixed properties, namely houses for dwelling, vineyards and gardens for eating, and pools for irrigating. Therefore he says: I made my works great, that is, I did great things: 3 Kings 10: "King Solomon was made greater than all the kings of the whole earth." And he expresses each one individually: I built myself houses, because he built many: whence 3 Kings 9: "After Solomon built two houses: the house of the Lord and the house of the king," he built a house for the daughter of Pharaoh; and this was a matter of curiosity and blameworthy: whence Isaiah 5: "Woe to you who join house to house." I planted vineyards, namely for drink: Genesis 9: "Noah planted a vineyard and drinking wine became intoxicated."

I made gardens and orchards and planted them with trees of every kind, for food: Song of Songs chapter six: "I went down into my garden to see the fruits of the valleys and to observe whether the vineyard had flourished and the pomegranates had budded."

And because these things needed to be irrigated, he therefore adds: And I made myself pools of water, that is, places in which there is a collection of waters: and this, so that I might irrigate the forest of sprouting trees, namely through irrigation, because it is said in Job twelve: "If he holds back the waters, all things will dry up." Such pools were necessary in the land of promise on account of the scarcity of water: Deuteronomy eleven: "The land which you shall enter to possess is mountainous and flat, awaiting rains from heaven."

(Verse 7). I possessed servants. Here is touched upon secondly the curiosity regarding the multitude of those serving, because of both sexes: whence he says: I possessed male servants and female servants: and also of every age: whence: And I had a great household: 3 Kings 10: "When the Queen of Sheba saw the wisdom of Solomon and the food of his table, the dwellings of his servants and the order of his ministers, she had no more spirit in her."

(Vers. 7.). Also herds. Here is touched upon thirdly the curiosity regarding the gathering of cattle and animals, both great and small, when he says: Also herds and great flocks of sheep, supply: I had: beyond all who were before me in Jerusalem. And that he had many is clear, because he consumed many: whence 3 Kings 4: "The food of Solomon for each day was ten fattened oxen and twenty pasture-fed oxen and a hundred rams, besides the hunting of deer, roebucks," etc.

(Vers. 8.). I heaped up for myself. Here is touched upon fourthly the curiosity regarding the accumulation of minerals, when he says: I heaped up for myself gold and silver, as regards metals, and the wealth of kings and provinces, as regards precious stones and gems: 3 Kings 10: "Each one brought gifts to Solomon: vessels of silver and gold, garments and weapons of war," and afterwards: "And he made it so that there was as great an abundance of silver in Jerusalem as of stones."

Spiritually, this Solomon, who made his works great, is Christ the Lord, who is the peaceful king, whose works are great: whence in the Psalm: "How great are your works, O Lord: you have made all things in wisdom," etc. But Christ magnifies his spiritual works, when he makes servants great and many, both with regard to the active life and with regard to the contemplative life.

For contemplatives, in order that they may be great in the state below, have many things bestowed upon them by Christ the Lord: houses, namely quiet consciences: Proverbs twenty-four: "Do not lie in wait and seek wickedness in the house of the just, nor lay waste to his rest." In this house one must rest, not in another's: since on the contrary, Sirach twenty-one: "The foot of the fool is quick into the house of his neighbor.

Vineyards, that is, the affections of devotion, from which wine is drawn forth: Judges nine: "Can I forsake my wine?" - Gardens, that is, outer enclosures: Song of Songs four: "A garden enclosed, a fountain sealed, my sister, my spouse."

Forests, that is, various virtues: Numbers 24: "How beautiful are your tents, O Jacob, like wooded valleys."

Orchards, that is, honorable and renowned works: Job 9: "My days have passed by like ships carrying fruit," which, namely, leave behind a good fragrance: 2 Corinthians 2: "We are the good fragrance of Christ to God."

Pools, that is, many tears: Song of Songs 7: "Your eyes are like the pools in Heshbon"; Jeremiah 9: "Who will give water to my head and a fountain of tears to my eyes"?

Just as Christ magnified his works in the contemplative life, so also in the active life, for which many things are necessary: servants and handmaids, through humility; Luke 17: "When you have done all the things that are commanded you, say: We are unprofitable servants."

Herds of oxen, through the vigor of works, because it is not for oxen to rest, but to plow: Job 1: "The oxen were plowing": Sirach 7: "Do not hate laborious works."

Sheep, through simplicity of intention: Matthew 10: "Behold, I send you as sheep in the midst of wolves"; 1 Peter 2: "As newborn babes, desire milk." Gold, through charity; Daniel 2 says of that statue that "its head was of the finest gold," because charity is the head of the virtues. - Silver, through sweetness of speech, because it has a sweet sound; whence in the Psalm: "The words of the Lord are pure words, silver tried in the fire"; and Sirach 6: "A sweet word multiplies friends and appeases enemies." - The riches of kings, through bountiful generosity: Sirach 13: "Good is the substance for him who has no sin on his conscience."

Secondly, he describes the pursuit of wealth with regard to artificial things, which are directed toward pleasures, and this in four ways.

I made myself singers. He dealt with his pursuit regarding those things which are ordered to riches; here he deals with those things which pertain to delights: and he does this indeed in the following manner. For since such things are not accomplished by just anyone, but by one who wills, is able, and knows how, therefore he first shows his diligence in preparing such delightful things; second, his power to bring them to completion; third, his wisdom in ordering them; and fourth, his pleasure in fully enjoying them.

(Vers. 8.). First he shows his excessive diligence in preparing pleasurable things of this kind, both with respect to hearing and with respect to sight. With respect to hearing he says: I made me singers, men and women, that is, of both sexes, and this for the sake of delights; hence he says: And the delights of the sons of men, and this with respect to hearing. With respect to sight he also made beautiful vessels; hence he says: cups and pitchers, for pouring wines, that is, I made, and this for the sake of delights. Hence he was very diligent, and this is blameworthy; hence Amos 6: "Woe to you who are wealthy in Sion"; and after: "You who sing to the sound of the psaltery, drinking wine in bowls".

(Vers. 9.). And I surpassed in riches etc. Here the second point is touched upon, namely the power to bring things to completion, because a poor man, even if he could begin such artful delights, could not however bring them to completion; but he himself could, as a rich man. Therefore he says: And I surpassed in riches all who were before me in Jerusalem. For he exceeded all: 3 Kings 3: "I have given you riches and glory, so that no one among all the kings in former days has been like you."

(Vers. 9.). Wisdom also etc. The third point is touched upon here, namely prudence for ordering, when he says: Wisdom also persevered with me, by which, namely, he ordered the things he had made; 3 Kings 10: "When the queen of Sheba saw Solomon's wisdom and the orders of those serving, their garments also and the cupbearers, etc., she had no more spirit in her"; and 3 Kings 4: "Solomon's wisdom surpassed the wisdom of all the Orientals and the Egyptians, and he was wiser than all men."

(Vers. 10.). And all things that they desired etc.

Here the fourth point is touched upon, namely his pleasure in fully enjoying those delightful things which he had prepared; and because there is a certain pleasure that has its origin from without, and a certain pleasure from an interior conception, he notes both in himself. As to the first he says: And all things that my eyes desired, namely seeing outwardly, I did not deny them, indeed he indulged to his own guilt; whence Lamentations 3: "My eye has plundered my soul"; and Jeremiah 9: "Death has come up through my windows".

As for interior pleasure he says: Nor did I forbid my heart, that is, the desire of the heart, from enjoying every pleasure and delighting itself in those things which I had prepared; against that passage in Sirach eighteen: "My son, do not go after your desires, and turn away from your will." And because desire often perverts the judgment of reason, a perverted judgment follows; whence he adds: And this I considered my portion, that is, I judged it to be better for me if I should enjoy my labor, namely in the present. And this is the judgment of the Epicureans, who do not expect any other good nor any other portion; on the contrary, the Saints do not have their portion in the present, but in the future; whence in the Psalm: "My portion, O Lord, let it be in the land of the living"; and again: "The Lord is the portion of my inheritance and of my cup," etc.

Questions.


I. It is asked, since it is a sin to take such delight in these songs, to what kind of sin is it reduced?

If you say that regarding lust: it is asked: since hearing is one sense, just as taste and touch, it seems that there ought to be a capital sin in hearing, just as in touch; or if not, I ask the reason.

2. Likewise I ask, whether to take delight in songs of this kind is a sin? It seems that it is not, because the Saints instituted singing for the glory of God.

That it is indeed a sin: Augustine in the Confessions: "As often as the singing has pleased me more than the words, I confess that I have sinned grievously."

I respond: it must be said that to take delight in songs of this kind is either on account of interior devotion, and this seeks nothing but the song of uprightness and its words; or on account of interior pleasure and wantonness; or on account of a certain curiosity. In the first way it is not a sin, but a merit; in the second way it is a sin connected to lust proximately; in the third way it is a sin of curiosity connected to lust remotely.

As for what he asks, why there is no capital sin concerning this sense, as there is concerning touch and taste: I respond that it is because there is not so great a corruption nor delight.

II. There is a question about what he says: And I surpassed in wealth all who were before me in Jerusalem. What does this mean? What he says seems to be no great distinction, because before him no one reigned except his father alone, since he himself went up and captured it and drove out the Jebusite.

To this some say that he speaks not only with regard to the kings of Israel, namely David and Saul, but also with regard to the judges

But this was not a great thing. — Because of this it is said that before him there were kings in Jerusalem from Canaan, and they indeed were rich and great, and with regard to them he says

Or the text can be read so that this specification in Jerusalem refers to the riches which he possessed in Jerusalem, not to the kings, so that the meaning is: I surpassed in riches in Jerusalem all who were before me, because none before him was like him.

III. Likewise, it is asked about what he says: Wisdom also persevered with me.

Against this: "The wisdom of doctrine is according to his name," Sirach 6; therefore no one is wise except him to whom things savor as they ought; but to Solomon they did not savor rightly, because he despised eternal goods and loved temporal ones; therefore he was not wise, but foolish.

I respond: it must be said that wisdom in one way implies knowledge alone, and in this sense it is said "wisdom is the knowledge of things divine and human"; in another way it implies taste and the savor of the affections and order, and in this sense wisdom is said to come from savor. In the first way, to be wise belongs to both good and evil people, who have an intellect illuminated to see many truths about God and creatures; in the second way, it belongs only to the good. In the first way, he persevered, because he did not lose his understanding; in the second way, he lost it, when he sinned and became a fool.

IV. A question is raised here about what he says: And this I considered my portion, if I should enjoy my labor.

1. Therefore it seems that Solomon was of the opinion of the Epicureans, who hold that there is no other joy except in the present; and this is heretical.

3. Likewise, if he himself was wise and understanding and stated this, then it must be presumed that this was true.

I respond: it must be said that there is a twofold judgment in us: one of deliberating and comparing reason, another of estimation. I say that Solomon by the judgment of deliberating and comparing reason never held this view nor was of this opinion, but only by estimation, just as every sinner estimates that it is good for him to sin; whence there is not an error of unbelief, but rather an error of deviation from the good. The Epicureans, however, not only estimate this, but rather assert with certainty that there is no other good; and therefore they are heretics and unbelievers.

2. To the objection that the wise man, etc.: it must be said that, even though he was wise, nevertheless in this he acted foolishly, and therefore he is not to be imitated; just as David was good, yet he did not act well when he had Uriah killed, nor does he say this for imitation, but for reproof.

Secondly, he reproves his own curiosity on account of the vanity he discovered, which consists in mutability, and first he reproves it in general.

And when I had turned myself. He dealt above with his curiosity: here he reproves it on account of the vanity found, which consists in mutability. And this part has two sections: because first he reproves his curious works generally, second specifically, there: I passed on to contemplate.

(Vers. 11.). He therefore found vanity and affliction in the works which he had done out of curiosity, on account of diligent consideration; for which reason he says: And when I turned to all the works which my hands had made, that is, had diligently considered, according to that word of the Apostle in Galatians 6 : "Let each one prove his own work." And to the labors in which I had toiled in vain, therefore in vain, because he had not done them for God, and therefore had labored to no purpose; Wisdom 3: "Their hope is empty, and their labor without fruit." I saw in all things vanity and affliction of spirit, and that nothing endures

under the sun: therefore they were vain and afflicting, because unstable. For inasmuch as they do not endure, they are vain; inasmuch as they are lost and do not endure, they are afflicting: Sirach 40: "The riches of the ungodly shall be dried up like a river, and shall resound like a great thunder in rain"; whence 1 John 2: "The world passes away and its concupiscence": and therefore: "Deceitful is its grace, and vain is beauty."

Second, he reproves his own curiosity specifically, and this in two ways: first, regarding study ordered toward wisdom, for a fourfold reason.

I turned to contemplate. Here in particular he shows vanity in his works: and it is divided: because first he shows vanity in works or in study ordered to wisdom; second, in study ordered to wealth, according to the twofold works of curiosity predetermined above, and this is found there: Again I detested all industry.

First, therefore, he rebukes his own curiosity regarding the pursuit of wisdom in this order: first, he indicates the diligence of his consideration; second, he suggests the discovered preeminence of the wise man over the fool; third, the equality further discovered; fourth, from this he concludes vanity and reproof according to his own consideration.

(Verse 12.) First, therefore, he indicates the diligence of his consideration in thoroughly examining those things in which he had applied himself. For this reason he says: I turned to contemplate wisdom, with respect to things to be chosen: and errors and folly, with respect to things to be avoided. I turned, I say, from pleasure: whence Sirach twenty-four: "Come over to me, all you who desire me," etc.; but although there was diligence in him, there was nevertheless not sufficiency: whence he adds: What is man, I said, that he can follow the King, his maker? As if to say: he is insufficient to consider and see the things which the Lord foresees. What is man? As if to say: something small and insufficient: Psalm: "What is man, that you are mindful of him"? That he can follow the King, his maker, that is, God the creator: Isaiah sixty-four: "And now, O Lord, you are our father, and we indeed are clay; and you are our maker, and we are all the work of your hands." Him no one can follow; Job eleven: "Can you perhaps comprehend the footsteps of God, and find the Almighty unto perfection"?

(Vers. 13.14.). And I saw that so much etc. Here the second point is noted, namely the discovered preeminence of wisdom over foolishness, because it surpasses it almost immeasurably. On account of which he says: And I saw that wisdom so much excelled foolishness, that is, was nobler, as light differs from darkness. And this comparison is apt, since wisdom illuminates, and foolishness blinds or darkens. Therefore he adds: The eyes of the wise man are in his head, namely for directing; the fool walks in darkness, as if he did not have eyes in his head. That wisdom illuminates, Wisdom 6: "Love the light of wisdom, all you who lead peoples"; whence also Christ, the wisdom of God, calls himself the light; John 8: "I am the light of the world." On the contrary, foolishness is darkness: Isaiah 5: "Woe to you who call good evil and evil good, who put light for darkness and darkness for light"; whence John 1: "The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness did not comprehend it"; therefore, because (they neither knew nor understood, therefore they walk in darkness"; John 3: "For they loved darkness more than light."

(Vers. 14. 15. 16.). And I learned that one was etc. Here the third point is touched upon: the equality of the wise man to the fool both in death and after death. As for the equality in dying, he says: And I learned that the end of both was one: one, that is, similar, because no one escapes the sentence of death on account of his wisdom: whence 2 Kings 14: "We all die, and like waters that flow down to the earth, which do not return." Just as the fool takes nothing away, so neither does the wise man: Job 1: "Naked I came forth from my mother's womb, and naked I shall return."

And on account of this equality he despised wisdom; therefore he says: And I said in my heart: if the end of both the fool and myself will be one and the same, what does it profit me that I have devoted greater effort to wisdom? as if to say: nothing as far as death is concerned: Sirach 40: (The day of union, that is, of death, from the one who sits" etc.

Not only equality in death, but also after death: whence he also adds: And I spoke with my mind, that is, I considered inwardly, and I perceived that this too was vanity, namely, to devote greater effort to wisdom than the fool, because there is no more memory of him than of the fool. - Therefore he says: For there will not be memory of the wise man any more than of the fool forever; and the reason is added: And future times will cover all things equally with oblivion. Whence above in the first chapter it was said: " There is no memory of former things, nor indeed of those which shall come after": Wisdom 2: And our name will receive oblivion through time, and no one will have memory of our works "; Job 13: 7 (And your memory will be compared to ashes, and your necks will be reduced to clay ".

(Verses 16, 17). Here fourthly, from this kind of equality discovered, he concludes vanity in his pursuit and his own reproof; whence he repeats the equality, saying: The learned one dies likewise as the unlearned, and thus they are equal. — And from this he concludes: Therefore I was weary of my life, as though he refused to live out of weariness, as Job 10: ("My soul is weary of my life"); on account of occasions of sin: Genesis 27, Rebecca said: "I am weary of my life because of the daughters of Heth." On account of labors: Numbers 21: "The people began to grow weary of the journey and the labor, and spoke against the Lord," etc. On account of tribulations: 2 Corinthians 1: "We were burdened beyond measure, so that we were weary even of living." On account of the delay of future glory: Job 10: "My soul is weary of my life." On account of impending temptation: Psalm: ("My soul has slumbered from weariness; strengthen," etc. On account of the horror of impending death: Mark 14: "Jesus began to be in dread and to grow weary."

Seeing that all things under the sun are evil, and all is vanity and affliction of spirit: Wisdom nine: "The body that is corrupted weighs down the soul, and the earthly dwelling presses down the mind that thinks upon many things"; therefore frequent meditation on the flesh is affliction.

Questions.


I. The question is raised here first concerning what he says: What is man, that he should be able to follow God?

On the contrary: 1. Sirach twenty-three: It is a great glory to follow the Lord."

2. Likewise, Job twenty-three: (My foot has followed his steps".

I respond: it must be said that there is a following by equality, and this has been given to no creature; and because Satan desired this, he fell. There is again a following by subjection and obedience; and this indeed man can do, but not just anyone, rather only those to whom it has been given by God through grace, and whom he himself draws; and therefore no one can do this by himself without the help of God.

II. The question is raised concerning what he says: What does it profit me, that I devoted greater effort to wisdom?

Because according to this it seems that he would discourage study, the contrary of which he often urges: and Proverbs twenty-seven: "Study wisdom, my son" etc.

I respond: it must be said that to devote oneself to wisdom can be done in two ways: for the advantage of the present life, or for the reward of eternal glory. For the advantage of the present life, wisdom is profitable before death, because it directs a person in his works; but in death it profits nothing, since the learned person dies just as the unlearned, nor is life prolonged for him. But if it is for the advantage of eternal life, then one does not die, but rather lives.

It is useful for the working of good: Sirach twenty-one: A golden ornament to the prudent is teaching, etc.

It is useful for the avoidance of evil: Sirach 3: A wise and understanding heart will abstain from sins.

It is useful for the increase of grace: Proverbs four: She shall give to your head increases of graces.

It is useful for the increase of glory: Proverbs 3: The wise shall possess glory.

Thus therefore he discourages, that one should not devote oneself to wisdom for present advantage and curiosity, but for eternal salvation: and therefore he reproves himself, because he himself had labored for curiosity and worldly vanity.

III. It is asked concerning what he says: There will be no remembrance of the wise as of the fool.

Contrary arguments: 1. Proverbs 10: The memory of the just is with praises, and the name of the wicked shall rot. 2. Likewise, Sirach 39: His memory shall not depart, and his name shall be sought out etc. I respond: to this some say that he speaks in the person of the carnal man, whence what he says is false absolutely, but true according to the estimation of carnal men.

But this does not fully resolve the question, because he ought not to assert the position of the carnal-minded, but rather to refute it. Therefore it must be said that speaking of the memory of the wise man and the fool is either with respect to God or with respect to men. If we speak with respect to God, the memory certainly is of the good wise man, who pursues wisdom for the glory of God; but with respect to men, the memory of both good and evil passes away, for the most part, because men remember few things.

But if concerning the wicked wise man and the vain one, there is no memory, neither with regard to God nor with regard to men, indeed his name passes away like the name of a fool: and therefore it is foolish to labor in wisdom for the sake of the renown of one's name. But Ecclesiastes speaks of the vain wise man, who has his eye on earthly things, and he speaks of human memory, not divine.

IV. Likewise, it is asked about what he says: I was weary of my life, seeing all the evils under the sun.

Against this: Genesis 1: God saw all things that he had made, and they were very good: therefore they are not evil; indeed, this is heretical.

I respond: it must be said that, as the Apostle says in Romans 7: The law is holy, and the commandment is holy, yet by occasion the commandment was deadly to those not having grace and worked death; just as creatures are very good to the just and those who love God and work together unto good, and are good in themselves, yet for the impious, sinners, and the foolish, all things are turned into evils by occasion; whence Wisdom 14: The creatures of God are for a temptation and a snare to the feet of the foolish; and Sirach 39: All these things shall be turned into evils for the impious and sinners.

Secondly, he specifically reproves his own curiosity with regard to the pursuit directed toward wealth, again with a fourfold argument.

Again I hated etc. He reproved above his own curiosity concerning the works of wisdom; here secondly he reproves his own curiosity concerning the works of human industry directed toward wealth, that is, the artificial works of the mechanical arts. And indeed he does this in the following order. First, he reproves his own curiosity on account of the uncertainty of a good succession; second, on account of toilsome anxiety; third, he prefers present pleasure to this; fourth, he condemns that anxiety itself through the equity of divine judgment.

(Vers. 18. 19. 20. 21.). He reproves himself therefore first as overly concerned about acquiring earthly things on account of the uncertainty of good offspring, because he himself could not always possess those things and feared having a useless successor. Therefore he says: Again I detested all my industry, that is, my anxious diligence, with which I most zealously labored under the sun, namely to increase these goods: Jeremiah 6: "From the least even to the greatest, all are given to covetousness."

And the reason for the loathing is introduced: I am going to have an heir after me, whom I do not know whether he will be wise or foolish. Literally, Solomon had as heir Rehoboam, a foolish son, who, following the counsel of the young men, caused the kingdom to be divided, 3 Kings 12. And he will have dominion over my labors, in which I toiled, as regards the body; and I was anxious, as regards the soul. Concerning this sweat, Genesis 3: "In the sweat of your face you shall eat your bread"; concerning anxiety, Matthew 6: "Be not anxious for your soul, what you shall eat, or for your body, what you shall put on." And that is exceedingly vain; whence he says: And is there anything so vain? Supply: as to labor anxiously for a fool and an idler, as if to say: no.

From the consideration of this vanity, therefore, he ceased from curiosity, whence he says: Wherefore I ceased, and my heart renounced laboring under the sun, that is, for temporal goods. I ceased, in deed; and my heart renounced, in will. But the greedy man does not cease on account of this, below in chapter four: "There is one alone, and he has not a second, and yet he does not cease to labor."

And he repeats the reason why he ceased: For when another labors in wisdom, in heart: and in doctrine, in mouth: and in care, in work: he leaves what was acquired to an idle man. And from this he concludes vanity: and this therefore is vanity and a great evil: vanity indeed, because riches are left to a useless man, but a great evil: whence Hugh: "It is a great evil that what one is worn down by in labor, another abuses idly and unlawfully": evil, because to a squanderer: Jeremiah 51: "Their works are vain" etc.: Deuteronomy 28: "Let there be no strength in your hand: let a people whom you do not know eat the fruit of your land and all your labors".

(Vers. 22. 23.). What indeed will it profit etc. Here secondly he rebukes the curiosity of acquiring because of laborious anxiety: on account of which he says: What will it profit a man from all his labor, in body, and affliction of spirit, in mind, by which he is tormented under the sun, as if to say: nothing: whence Proverbs twenty-three: "Do not labor to become rich, but set a limit to your prudence." It does not profit, namely him who labors in order to possess these visible things. Whence Bernard: "The fruits of riches are with others, but to the rich only the name remains and anxiety"; and therefore it does not profit: whence below in chapter five: "What does it profit the owner, except that he beholds his riches with his eyes"?

Not only does it not benefit, but it actually harms; whence he adds: All his days are full of labors and sorrows, outwardly; nor does his mind rest at night, inwardly. Therefore it is said in Job 7: "I have numbered for myself empty months and wearisome nights"; and Proverbs 15: "All the days of the poor are evil. A secure mind is like a continual feast." And from this he concludes that to labor thus is vain, because it is useless; therefore he says: And is this not vanity? As if to say: indeed; below in chapter 5: "He who loves riches will reap no fruit from them; and this therefore is vanity," because they are harmful. Whence in the same place: "There is another infirmity which I have seen under the sun: riches gathered up," etc.

(Vers. 24. 23.). Is it not better etc. Here, thirdly, he prefers the pleasure of the flesh to the aforementioned solicitude of curiosity. And therefore he says: Is it not better to eat and drink and show his soul good things from his labors? supply: than to labor always continuously; as if to say: it is better, that is, less evil, because the former is an evil of guilt and punishment, while this is an evil of guilt, but has pleasantness: Isaiah 22: "Behold, joy and gladness, slaying rams" etc. And that it is better, he makes clear: because this is a gift of God; whence he says: And this is from the hand of God: Jeremiah 5: "I filled them to the full, and they committed adultery" etc.

And he puts himself forward as an example of this to confirm it: Who will so devour, as regards desire; and abound in delights, as regards abundance; as I? as if to say: no one; 3 Kings 4: "Now Solomon's provision for each day was thirty cors of fine flour and sixty cors of meal, ten fattened oxen and twenty pasture-fed oxen," etc.

(Vers. 26.). To the good man etc. Here fourthly he condemns anxiety through the equity of divine judgment, because such anxiety is not given to the good, but to the wicked, and this by divine judgment. Therefore he says: To the good man in his sight, namely, who pleases him, not men, like the hypocrite: Psalm: "Let the just feast" etc. God has given wisdom, with regard to eternal things: and knowledge, with regard to managing temporal things, and understanding, with regard to things to be understood or considered: Job 38: "Who has placed wisdom in the inward parts of man, or who has given the rooster understanding"? This according to the generosity of mercy. But according to the equity of justice he adds: But to the sinner he has given affliction and superfluous care, that is, he permitted it to be given by just judgment: that he may add and gather and deliver to him who has pleased God: Proverbs 13: "The substance of the sinner is kept for the just": Proverbs 28: "He who gathers riches by usury and interest gathers them for him who is generous to the poor." And from this he infers vanity: But this too is vanity and a vain anxiety of the mind, because Proverbs 11: "The expectation of anxiety shall perish."

Note that there is study for the acquisition of wisdom: Proverbs twenty-seven: Apply yourself to wisdom, my son, so that you may be able to answer a word to him who reproaches you. For the exercise of justice: Jeremiah seven: Now make your ways and your pursuits good. For the fulfillment of desire: Proverbs twenty-three: To whom is woe? To whose father is woe? Is it not to those who linger over wine and study to drain their cups? For the rule of greed: Jeremiah six: From the least even to the greatest, all are devoted to greed.

The first two pursuits are good, the two following are evil, and in these the goodness of a person is discerned: whence Proverbs 20: A child is known by his pursuits, whether his works are clean and right. Because therefore Ecclesiastes had not yet devoted himself to these, but to wantonness and gluttony, therefore he was condemning his own pursuits.
