QUESTION I. On the Purpose of the Book of Ecclesiastes.


First concerning the end. For it is said that the end of the book is contempt of the world. That it is so determined,

I. It is proved by that which is said in James 4: The friendship of this world is enmity with God: but everything that is hostile to God, it is good to despise.

2. Likewise, 1 John 2: Do not love the world, nor the things that are in the world.

But against this: 1. Praise of a work redounds to the craftsman, therefore contempt of the work also redounds to the same, therefore whoever despises the world despises God: either therefore this world was not made by God, or it is not to be despised.

2. Likewise, Proverbs 16: The Lord has made all things for himself, therefore all things are ordered toward God; but what is ordered toward an end is not to be despised, but to be received and loved: therefore this world, and the things that are in it, are to be loved.

I respond: it must be said that, as Augustine and Hugo maintain, this world is like a certain ring given by the bridegroom to the soul itself: now the bride can love the ring bestowed upon her by the bridegroom in two ways, namely with a chaste love and an adulterous one. Chaste love is that by which she loves the ring in memory of the bridegroom and for the love of the bridegroom: adulterous love is that by which she loves the ring more than the bridegroom: and this the bridegroom cannot but regard as an evil. For just as love is twofold, so too is hatred or contempt twofold, because "as often as one of two opposites is spoken of, so too is the other." Contempt for the ring, as if it were a small and worthless gift, redounds against the bridegroom: but contempt for the ring, as if it were considered nothing in comparison with the love of the bridegroom, this is to the glory of the bridegroom: and of this contempt it is said in the last chapter of Song of Songs: If a man should give all the substance of his house for love, he would despise it as nothing. This passage speaks of this contempt: and thus the objections are resolved.

Question II. On the subject matter of the same.


Afterwards, the question is asked about the subject matter, and it is said that here the topic treated is vanity.

But on the contrary: 1. Every science is about the good and the true: but vanity removes goodness and truth: therefore there is no science about it.

2. Furthermore, it seems that there is no vanity in things, because it is said in Genesis 1: God saw all things that he had made, and they were very good: therefore if all things are very good, nothing is vain.

3. Furthermore, that which is not ordered toward an end is vain and futile, just as if someone were to bathe in order that the sun might be eclipsed, he would be working in vain, because this is not orderable toward that end: but all creatures tend toward the supreme good in their own way, as Dionysius says,

and Boethius, that all things desire the good, and also the Philosopher: therefore nothing vain is found among all things.

4. Furthermore, if vanity is found there, and this science is founded upon it as upon a subject, and what is founded upon vanity is vain, therefore this science is vain.

I respond: for the understanding of the foregoing, it can be stated in one way as follows, that vain is said in two ways: in one way absolutely, and in this way it deprives of truth and goodness, and such a thing does not exist in the universe. In another way vain is said by comparison to the initial starting point, because it is from nothing: and in this way it is not opposed to the good, indeed one and the same thing is both good and vain: but good, because it is from the good and directed toward the good: vain, because it is from nothing and left to itself tends toward nothing; and such vanity possesses a goodness that falls under knowledge, and therefore concerning such vanity there can be knowledge and doctrine.

And by this, all objections can be resolved, because they proceed as if they were about vanity in an absolute sense.

But this solution does not resolve the issue, because Ecclesiastes does not treat of things insofar as they are from nothing, but rather insofar as they are desired by men; and furthermore, he treats of the vanity of fault, which is a privation: therefore it is necessary to speak otherwise.

Therefore it must be answered differently and said that just as true is said in three ways, so also vain, its opposite, according to the rule stated above. In one way true is said insofar as it is convertible with being, and thus it is said that every being is true; and thus vain, opposed to true, is called non-being, just as we say that a chimera is non-being.

In another way true, is said insofar as beyond entity it adds an ordering toward operation and end, just as true wine, is said to be that which has the true operation of wine, so a true man, is one who has the operation proper to man, and this is the operation of virtue; and thus vain is said through the privation of ordering toward an end, and in this way all sins and abuses are vain.

In another way, something is called true, insofar as it adds above being a freedom from admixture with non-being, such that it in no way has potency toward it: and thus that is said to have true being, which has immutable being: and in this way, the vain opposed to the true is that which is mutable and changeable. And thus every creature is vain, because subject to vanity, that is, to mutability.

Thus therefore, vanity having been spoken of in three ways: in one way through the privation of being absolutely, in another way through the privation of ordered being, in a third way through the privation of unchangeable being: concerning the first there is in no way knowledge or teaching, unless perhaps it has being in a cause and according to that is known and taught: and thus it is not vain: but insofar as it is vain, neither per se nor per accidens is there knowledge of it

Concerning the second mode, there is not knowledge per se, but per accidens. For just as medicine is the science of the healthy and the sick—of the healthy indeed per se, but of the sick per accidens, as the Philosopher holds; so there is theoretical knowledge of fault or sin, but by reason of virtue.

Concerning the third mode, namely concerning the mutability of creation, knowledge is per se, as is evident regarding the motion of the sun and the spheres and other things.

In this book, therefore, the subject treated is the vanity of mutability or of nature, because it is beautiful and fitting, and the vanity of fault: the other vanity, namely that of complete privation, is not treated, because of that there is neither knowledge nor doctrine.

1. To the first objection, then, that all knowledge is about the good and the true: this is true in itself, but knowledge can also be about the vain by reason of the true; and he who knows true principles also knows false ones; and it is similar with the sick and the healthy, and likewise in the matter at hand.

2. 3. 4. To the objection that creatures are not vain, because they are very good and ordered toward an end: the answer is that they are not called vain on account of a defect of goodness or order, but on account of a defect of unchangeable being; and in this way every creature is vain, yet not so vain that it lacks truth and goodness; and therefore knowledge and teaching about it is true.

Question III. On the manner of acting in the same.


Item, it is asked about the method of proceeding: for it has been said that he acts and speaks in the persons of different characters, namely of the fool and of the wise man.

But: 1. As it is said in Sirach 20, a parable from the mouth of a fool will be rejected: therefore a statement spoken in the person of a fool is also not to be heeded: therefore if the statement of the sacred books is to be heeded, one should not speak in the person of a fool.

2. Likewise, what he says in the person of the fool and the carnal man is to be rejected; but it is not known when he speaks,

whether he says this in his own person or in another's: therefore it is not known what in this book is to be held and what is to be rejected: therefore this knowledge is a path to error. But canonical books ought to purge error: therefore this book should be removed from the canon.

I respond: it must be said that to speak something in the person of a fool or a carnal man is twofold: either to approve it, or to reprove it and show it to be vain. The first way does not befit the knowledge of truth; the second way does befit it, just as if someone wished to reprove an error and first explained it, then afterward destroyed it. And he speaks in this way, not the first; and therefore he is not to be reproved, but rather commended, because he reproves what is reprovable.

2. As to the objection that it is not known when he speaks in his own person: it must be said that the other side of a disputation cannot be known until one arrives at the judgment and the determination, because in the resolution it is known what is chosen and what is rejected. Thus I say that Ecclesiastes proceeds as if disputing up to the end of the book, and at the end gives his judgment, when he says: Let us all hear together the end of the discourse: Fear God, and know that for every error God will bring you into judgment. In which word he condemns all the opinions of the foolish, the carnal, and the worldly. Hence what agrees with that judgment he says in his own person, but what disagrees, in the person of others: and therefore this book cannot be understood unless it is heard in its entirety.

Question IV. On the efficient cause of the same.


It can also be asked lastly concerning the efficient cause, and it has been said that the efficient cause was Solomon. That such a person is not a fitting author, however, it seems:

I. Because he himself was a sinner and carnal; but when a carnal man preaches spirituality, he scandalizes rather than edifies; therefore this book serves more to generate scandal than edification.

2. Likewise, in the Psalm it is said: But God said to the sinner: Why do you declare my judgments? Therefore, if Solomon was a sinner, then he was sinning by declaring divine justice.

3. Furthermore, a good author produces faith, whence the argument from authority has its strength; therefore a bad author produces no faith. But the books of Sacred Scripture are for the purpose of generating faith; therefore, etc.

To this it must be answered that, as Jerome says, according to the tradition of the Hebrews, this book was written by Solomon while he was doing penance; and because God does not reject the penitent but rather accepts them, accordingly Solomon was not in a blameworthy state when he wrote this book.

It can be answered differently, that the Holy Spirit speaks truth and goodness not only through the good, but even through the wicked: hence the Lord himself says in the Gospel: Do what they say, but do not do what they do: hence also through Balaam he prophesied most clearly, and so too through Solomon, even in his carnal state, he said many good things.

2. To the objection, then, that he himself was sinning: it must be answered that the gift of wisdom had been entrusted to him most especially among all others; and because he was bound not to hide the talent of the Lord, he ought to teach the people of the Lord both by word and by writing, especially since he had been appointed to govern them. Hence he did not sin in the fact that he taught, but in the fact that he did not properly dispose himself.

3. To the objection that the book should not be believed, since the author is not worthy of trust: I respond: just as we believe the Prophets, because they spoke not by themselves but by the Holy Spirit, so also in all the books of Scripture, because they were produced by the dictation of the Holy Spirit. Hence the goodness of the person does not contribute to greater or lesser faith, supposing that he spoke by the Spirit. And because it is established for us that Solomon was filled with the spirit of wisdom, as is clear from 3 Kings 3, therefore the Church without doubt receives his books.

Prologue of the Commentary of St. Jerome on Ecclesiastes.

I remember that about five years ago, when I was still in Rome and was reading Ecclesiastes to holy Blesilla, so that I might provoke her to contempt of this world, and that she might consider everything she saw in the world to be as nothing, I was asked by her to explain the obscure passages in the manner of a short commentary, so that without me she might be able to understand what she was reading. And so, since she was taken away by sudden death at the very outset of our work, and we did not deserve, O Paula and Eustochium, to have such a companion in our life, struck dumb by so great a wound I fell silent. Now, stationed in Bethlehem, that is, in a more venerable city, I pay what I owe both to her memory and to you, briefly noting this: that I have followed no one's authority, but translating from the Hebrew, I have rather conformed myself to the usage of the Seventy Interpreters, at least in those places which did not differ greatly from the Hebrew. At times I have also made reference to Aquila, Symmachus, and Theodotion, so that I might neither deter the reader's interest by excessive novelty, nor again, against my conscience, having abandoned the fountain of truth, pursue the rivulets of opinions.

Exposition of this Prologue.

To this book Saint Jerome prefixes a Preface, in which he first reveals the motive for translating. Second, he determines the method of translation,

There: Briefly advising this.

The first part has three parts. First he says what moved him to begin, namely the intercession of Saint Blesilla. - Second he touches on what hindered him so as to delay, namely her death, at the words: Itaque, quoniam in procinctu etc. - Third, what moved him to complete the work, namely both her memory and the intercession of Eustochium and Paula, at the words: Nunc in Bethlehem positus etc

Briefly advising this. Here the manner of transferring is touched upon, and first the manner is set forth, namely that it neither entirely agrees with others nor entirely differs,

Indeed, where he could do so in a good way, he imitated them. Second, the reason is added, namely there: So that neither by excessive novelty. And the reason is that he followed them in some things, lest he seem to introduce excessive novelty; yet he did not follow them entirely, lest he abandon the truth.
