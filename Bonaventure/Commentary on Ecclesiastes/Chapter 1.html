Chapter I.


The words of Ecclesiastes etc. This entire book is divided into three parts: the title or prologue and the treatise, which begins there: Vanity of vanities etc.; and the epilogue, which begins there: And since Ecclesiastes was most wise.

In the title the efficient cause is made manifest, in the treatise the matter and form, in the epilogue the end.

On the title.

(Vers. 1.). In the title, therefore, the author is made known with respect to wisdom, with respect to goodness, with respect to royal power.

By reason of wisdom he says: The words of Ecclesiastes, supply: what follows are the words of Ecclesiastes: because Ecclesiastes means preacher, and one cannot be a preacher unless he is wise: because it is said in Proverbs twenty-six: "As if a thorn were to grow in the hand of a drunkard, so a parable is unfitting in the mouth of fools": whence Proverbs seventeen: "Eloquent words do not befit a fool".

As for goodness, it is manifested when it says: Son of David. For David was holy, and by imitation the children of a holy man are holy; whence in Scripture the name is one of kindness and gentleness: Matthew fifteen: (Have mercy on me, Son of David".

As for royal power, when it is said: King of Jerusalem, because Jerusalem was the most noble city and the capital of the whole kingdom, below: I, Ecclesiastes, was king of Israel in Jerusalem, because he dwelt there above all.

QUESTIONS.


I. Here a question arises: since Solomon wrote three books, why is it that he places a title before the book of Proverbs and before this one, in which his name is expressed, but does not place one before the Song of Songs?

I respond: it must be said that in the book of Proverbs, Solomon himself is introduced speaking as a wise man to a disciple: hence he calls the hearer son; similarly here he is introduced as a man who has experienced diverse things; but in the Song of Songs the bride and bridegroom speak. Lest

Therefore it would seem that these were the words of Solomon to his wife

but are of Christ to the Church

he does not prefix his name there. But here, because he was speaking in his own person for the most part, and in the book of Proverbs 1, therefore he prefixes a title in which his name is specified and disclosed.

II. Likewise it is asked why in the book of Proverbs he says parables, but here he says words.

If you say that there he speaks parabolically and figuratively, but here openly: then I ask, why here and there he varies the manner of proceeding.

I respond: it must be said that there is a certain mode of speaking that is proper and open, and another that is parabolic: and this latter is twofold: one through parables and open similitudes, another through hidden and profound ones. Since therefore Solomon in the book of Proverbs speaks to the little ones and the unlearned, he therefore speaks parabolically and openly: but since in Ecclesiastes he speaks to those who are advancing, therefore openly and properly: but in the Song of Songs, since he speaks to the perfect, whose food is solid food, therefore parabolically and secretly.

III. The question is, why in the title of Proverbs does he call himself by his own name, but here he calls himself the Preacher?

I respond: it must be said that, as Jerome says, Solomon had three names, which indeed suited him according to the threefold scripture which he produced. For he was called Solomon, called Ecclesiastes and Jedidiah. Solomon, the peaceful one, because he possessed the whole kingdom in peace: whence he also handed down to all the teaching of the Proverbs. Ecclesiastes, the preacher, because he speaks in the persons of various people. Jedidiah is the same as beloved of the Lord.

Therefore he calls himself Ecclesiastes, because in this book he brings forward the opinions of various people and accordingly persuades in diverse ways. He was called Jedidiah in the title of the Psalm Eructavit, which is about him in the literal sense.

IV. Likewise it is asked why here he calls himself son of David and king of Jerusalem, but in Proverbs he calls himself king of Israel?

I respond: one reason is taken from the part of the author, because, according to what the Hebrews believe, this book was written by Solomon while doing penance and humbling himself. And therefore in Proverbs he does not name himself from another,

rather, he manifests the power of his entire kingdom; but here he humbles himself. Therefore, naming himself from another, he indicates not his entire power, but a part, and therefore, etc.

Another reason was on the part of the work, because in Proverbs he leads to the knowledge of things to be done, while here to the contempt of present things. And because there are very many who wish more to be wise than to despise present things: therefore there he calls himself king of Israel, but here of Jerusalem, because despisers of the world are citizens of the heavenly Jerusalem, who do not have here a lasting city, but seek the future one.

V. And from this the solution to that question which can be raised is clear: why he does not express the purpose here, as in Proverbs. Because the purpose of Proverbs is wisdom , which attracts one to listen: the purpose of this book is contempt of the world, about which few wish to hear.

From these things it is clear why he said words, why he said Ecclesiastes, why son of David, why king of Jerusalem, and why he did not also add concerning the usefulness of the teaching of this book, as in Proverbs.

The treatment of this book follows.

Vanity of vanities, said Ecclesiastes, vanity of vanities, and all is vanity.

Here the treatise begins: and because "the end imposes necessity on those things which are ordered to the end"; since the end of this book is the contempt of all things and the fear of God alone: Therefore in all things he intends to show vanity. And because he proceeds as a preacher by persuading and proving,

Therefore this treatise has three parts: because first he sets forth what he intends to prove, namely that there is vanity in all things; second, he proves it, there: "What more does a man have from his labor," etc.; third, he concludes it as proven near the end: Vanity of vanities, etc.

Statement of the matter to be proved.

He therefore sets forth a threefold vanity in things, namely of punishment, of iniquity, of mutability. As for the vanity of punishment: Vanity of vanities, said Ecclesiastes, that is, he set forth. Concerning this, below in the second chapter: All his days are full of labors, nor does his mind rest at night: and is this not vanity? - As for the vanity of iniquity, he says again: Vanity of vanities: whence Jeremiah 10: Their works are vain and worthy of laughter: in the day of their visitation they shall perish.

As for the vanity of mutability, he says: and all is vanity, because all things are subject to mutability; Romans 8: The creature was made subject to vanity, not willingly; and below in chapter 3: All things are subject to vanity, and all things go to one place. Therefore it is not a repetition of words when he says vanity three times, but a wonderful distinction of intelligible realities.

And it should be noted that this threefold vanity follows in sequence, and one arises from another: for the vanity of iniquity arises from the vanity of mutability, although the latter is not its entire cause; while the vanity of penalty arises from the vanity of iniquity. He therefore arranges these vanities in a reverse order of resolution, and in what follows he pursues them in the opposite direction. And because that twofold vanity has its origin from another vanity, but the third does not: therefore he says vanity of vanities twice, and afterward only vanity.

It should also be noted that this threefold vanity is found in man, because he is the one upon whom punishment and fault fall: and on account of this it is said in the Psalm: Altogether vanity is every man living. For in him there is vanity of nature, vanity of fault, and vanity of punishment. Concerning the vanity of nature, in the Psalm: Man is made like unto vanity, his days pass away like a shadow. Concerning the vanity of fault, likewise in the Psalm: The Lord knows the thoughts of men, that they are vain. Concerning the vanity of punishment, Job chapter fourteen: Man born of woman, living for a short time, is filled with many miseries.

It should also be noted that Solomon himself speaks of himself as if of another, as John does in the twenty-first chapter: This is that disciple who bears witness concerning these things; and Moses does likewise. Now a threefold reason can be assigned for this: one is on the part of the first efficient cause, because the Holy Spirit himself is the author of Scripture, not Solomon or another; therefore, when he is introduced in sacred Scripture, Scripture ought to speak of him as if of another, as Balaam said in Numbers twenty-four: The hearer of the words of God said, etc.

Another reason is taken from the part of the immediate efficient cause: because Scripture rejects all pride and holds forth humility: and therefore no teacher in it ought to speak of himself as of himself, but for the sake of humility as if of another. - The third reason: when Scripture suggests great and wondrous things, it ought to show them as if from afar: whence Hugh says: "This manner of speaking, whenever it is employed in Scripture, is done either for the sake of humility or of wonder. For we ought both to remove great things from ourselves on account of humility, and to show wondrous things as if from a distance on account of wonder." An example is found in Paul and John.

Division of the entire proof.

What more has man etc. Above he proposed what he intends to prove; here he begins to prove it: and just as a threefold vanity was proposed to exist in things, so also a threefold vanity is proven. Hence this part has three parts. First, in things he shows there to be the vanity of mutability: second, the vanity of iniquity, at: I saw under the sun, in the place of judgment, wickedness: third, the vanity of punishment, below in chapter seven: I have tried all things in wisdom. "The first is natural and fitting, as Hugh says; the second is culpable, because perverse; the third is penal and wretched."

Part I. The vanity of mutability is proven with regard to two things.

Mutability is considered in things in two ways: in one way by reason of change, in another way in comparison to the measure of a fixed time. First, therefore, he treats the vanity of mutable nature with respect to change: second, with respect to time, below in chapter three: All things have their season.

First part, member I. The vanity of mutable nature is shown with respect to change. The first part has two sections, because first he shows that there is mutability in things, and through this, vanity; second, from knowledge of this he reproves his own curiosity, at the passage: I, Ecclesiastes, was king, etc.

Art. 1. The mutability in the existence of creatures is proved with respect to a twofold being.

Therefore, the mutability in the existence of creatures is shown first. And because creatures have a threefold being, namely in the Word, by reason of exemplarity; in the universe, by way of materiality; in the human mind, by way of abstraction—the first being is unfailing and immutable, and therefore has no vanity; but in the others there is vanity—and so this part has two sections: because first the vanity of mutability in things is noted according to the being they have in the universe; second, with regard to the being they have in the human mind, at the words: All things are difficult, etc.

The vanity of mutability is proven according to the existence of things in the universe, and this in three ways.

The first part has three divisions according to the three kinds of creatures, in which he shows mutability: because first he shows it in the rational creature; second, in the celestial, there: The sun rises; third, in the elemental, there: Traversing all things, etc.

(Vers. 3. 4.). As for the rational creature, namely man, he shows that there is mutability, from which he can be delivered by no labor; on account of which he says: What more does man have from all his labor, with which he labors under the sun? Supply: except vanity and mutability, as if to say: nothing. Concerning this labor it is said in Job 5: "A bird is born to fly, and man to labor"; and Genesis 3: "Cursed is the earth in your work: in labor you shall eat from it all the days of your life".

And that it has nothing more than vanity, he shows when he adds: A generation passes away, and a generation comes. Therefore there is change and succession both in man and in his works: Sirach fourteen: "Some are brought forth, and others are cast down; so is the generation of flesh and blood: one ends, and another is born," according to what is said in James four: "What is your life but a vapor appearing for a little while?" But when they are corrupted, they do not pass into nothing, but into something, because into earth: whence it was said to Adam: "You are dust, and to dust you shall return." Therefore he adds: But the earth stands forever, as the matter into which dissolution occurs; Psalm: "Who founded the earth upon its stability"; and Proverbs eight: "When he was weighing the foundations of the earth."

(Vers. 8. 6.). The sun rises and sets etc. Here he secondly shows mutability in the celestial nature, as established in the sun, which never rests, but when it rises, it tends toward setting, whence it does not remain in its rising. Therefore he says: The sun rises and sets, so that it does not remain in the place of rising nor even in the place of setting: then he adds: And it returns to its place. Nor does it remain there, whence he also adds: And there being born again it revolves

through the south. Nor does it even remain in the middle place; therefore he adds: And it turns to the north.

This can be explained with respect to the diurnal motion, or also the annual. With respect to the diurnal, Hugh says: " The sun rises daily, when it is presented to human sight. It sets when, descending to the lower parts, it withdraws itself from our eyes. It returns to its place, because, driven by the rotation beneath, it returns again to its place. But rising again, it circles through the south, because by an oblique course it ascends from the east to the meridian line; then it turns toward the north, because, descending toward the west, it again inclines by an oblique progression toward the north."

As for the annual motion, it is thus: "The sun rises, when through the vernal equinox it ascends to our pole. It sets, when passing through the autumnal equinox it descends to the lower regions. It goes about through the south, when it lingers in the winter signs. It turns to the north, when it is carried around in the summer signs, which are closer to the north pole." And thus the sun does not stand still by nature, but only by miracle, as is read in Joshua 10: "Sun, do not move toward Gabaon, and moon, toward the valley of Hailon," etc.

(Vers. 6.). Going about all things. Here is touched upon thirdly the mutability in elemental nature, and first with regard to air, but secondly with regard to water, at the words: All the rivers enter into the sea.

As for the air, when he touches on mutability in the spirit, that is, in the wind, which is either the motion of the air according to some philosophers, or according to Aristotle, the motion of vapor in the air, by whose motion the air is also moved. On account of which he says: The wind goes on in a circuit, surveying all things, that is, the wind goes around surveying all things. Spirit is said to mean wind: Exodus 15: "Your spirit blew, O Lord, and the sea covered them"; and in the Psalm: "Fire, hail, snow, ice, stormy winds".

Here it goes about in a circuit, because it rises from the four regions of the earth: Zechariah 2: "Flee from the land of the north, for I have scattered you to the four winds of heaven"; and Ezekiel 37: "Come from the four winds, O spirit," etc. For there are four principal winds and eight collateral ones.

And returns to its circuits, because the wind ceases when that vapor settles and returns to the earth from which it rises. And because that cause is hidden, therefore it is said in the Psalm: "Who brings forth the winds from his treasures," that is, from hidden places.

Alternatively, as the Gloss explains, this can be understood of the sun with respect to its annual motion, which traverses all things and returns through the circles of the zodiac: and the sun is called a spirit, because it is the principle and cause of all life: whence it causes all other things to breathe.

(Vers. 7.). All the rivers etc. Here mutability is touched upon with regard to visible elemental nature, namely water, because there is no permanence there: for all waters move toward the sea. Whence he says: All rivers enter the sea, namely the great ocean, about which it is said in Job 38: "Who shut up the sea with doors, when it burst forth, as if proceeding from the womb?" And this motion does not end, because the sea is not filled. Therefore he adds: And the sea does not overflow. And because this seems wonderful, he gives the reason for this: To the place from which they go out, the rivers return, not so that they might rest there, but so that they might flow again. Rivers go out secretly and return openly, because they go out through subterranean passages and certain filtrations. And so all things are carried about in a circuit and are subject to vanity. Hugh says: "Behold how all things are carried about in a circuit and are subject to vanity; and we know that a circle has no end. Therefore those things which run in a circuit run indeed, but do not arrive at an end. What rest, therefore, can be hoped for, where there can be no permanence? And therefore it was said of the wicked: 'The head of their circuit'; and again: 'The wicked walk round about'; and again: 'My God, make them like a wheel.'"

Spiritually it should be noted who this sun is, who this traversing spirit is, and what the rivers going around and returning.

This Sun is Christ, of whom Malachi 4: "The Sun of Justice shall rise upon you who fear my name." This sun rises in the nativity to illuminate: Isaiah 60: "Arise, be enlightened, O Jerusalem": and John 1: "He was the true light, which enlightens every man." - This sun sets, in the passion to redeem: Amos 8: "The sun shall set at midday," that is, in the vehement love of charity. - This sun returns to its place, in the ascension: Luke 19: "A certain man went into a far country," etc. - This sun circles through the south, that is, in the fervent love of the Saints: Song of Songs 1: "Show me, O you whom my soul loves, where you feed, where you lie down at midday": and John 14: "If anyone loves me," etc., and further: "We will come to him and make our abode with him." - This one turns to the north in the final judgment: Jeremiah 1: "From the north shall all evil spread forth," etc.

This Spirit traversing is the Holy Spirit, who

Therefore it is said to traverse, because it makes us search out all things: 1 Corinthians 2: "The Spirit searches all things, even the deep things of God".

This one is said to Go

in a circuit, because it makes us go around: whence Wisdom 7: "The spirit of wisdom is mobile"; just as also going around, because it makes us consider the circuit of misery: Job 1: "Naked I came forth from my mother's womb, and naked shall I return there." Likewise the circuit of grace, concerning which John 16: "I came forth from the Father and came into the world, and again I leave the world and go to the Father." Likewise of glory, Ezekiel 1: "This was the appearance of the splendor round about, and this was the likeness of the glory of the Lord." The Spirit of God makes us consider this: 2 Corinthians 3: "We with unveiled face shall behold the glory of the Lord," etc.

This circuit is in the contemplation that all things are from God and unto God. And Dionysius says that the Angels move in a circular motion around the throne.

These rivers are the rivers of graces, about which John 7 says: "He who believes in me, as the Scripture says, rivers shall flow from his belly."

The sea, from which the rivers have their origin, is the Immensity of divine generosity, which is neither increased nor diminished: "For great is our Lord and great is his power, and of his wisdom there is no number."

To this sea return the rivers of thanksgivings, so that they might flow again: John 4: "Whoever drinks of this water that I shall give him, it shall become in him a fountain of water springing up into eternal life." From this they go out: James 1:

"Every best gift and every perfect gift is from above." And just as rivers cease to exist and are dried up when they cease to flow and become still, so also the gifts of graces: Psalm: "You broke open springs and torrents," by bounty; "you dried up the rivers of Ethan," by the severity of judgment.

Questions.


1. It is asked here first concerning what he says: What more does man have etc.?
1. This seems to be a heretical opinion and to agree with that saying, that vain is everyone who serves God

2. Likewise, in the Psalm it is said: The patience of the poor shall not perish forever: therefore something, etc.

3. Likewise, Proverbs 12: He who tills his land shall be satisfied with bread: therefore it is good to work and to labor.

I respond: as Hugh says, emphasis must be placed

in this, that he says: In which he labors under the sun: "for it is the same as if he were to say: under time."

For this reason it should be noted that certain things come into being with time, such as those whose origin was not preceded by time, like the angelic nature and prime matter. Certain things come into being in time, which began after the origin of time but in time have no end, like the soul of Peter. Certain things come into being in time and under time, which begin after the origin of time and are terminated in time, of which kind are mutable things. And such things are twofold: for some come into being for time and under time, and such things pass away in both act and fruit; some for eternity, and such things pass away in act but remain in root and in fruit, because the good will remains, to be rewarded for eternity.

When therefore he says, that man has nothing but vanity from all the labor with which he labors under the sun:

it is understood insofar as labor is under time with respect to both act and intention; and then it has no place among good things.

II. Likewise, it is asked about what he says: The earth stands forever.

On the contrary: 1. Matthew twenty-four: Heaven and earth will pass away: therefore the earth does not remain.

2. Likewise, Revelation twenty-one: I saw a new heaven and a new earth, therefore the earth grows old: therefore it does not stand still, etc.

I respond: it must be said that the earth standing is twofold: either with respect to substance, or with respect to figure or disposition; I say that the earth stands forever with respect to substance, but passes away with respect to figure: 1 Corinthians 7: The figure of this world passes away. But men are generated and corrupted with respect to substance, so that they are rightly called not men, but the dead.

Or eternal is said in multiple ways: in one way, that which has no end prescribed in Scripture: thus the promised land was given to the children of Israel as an eternal possession. In another way, that which has an end, but not in time: thus time is said to be eternal. In the third way, that whose signified reality never ends. In the fourth way, that whose substance never ends. In the fifth way, that whose substance neither ends nor began.

This last mode belongs to God alone; the fourth mode applies to the earth with respect to substance, the third mode with respect to signification, and the first two modes with respect to figure.

And thus the response to that is clear in two ways,

III. Likewise, it is asked concerning what he says: The sun rises and sets.

For since other luminaries likewise rise, the question is asked: why does he speak more about the sun than about the others?

2. Likewise, it seems to proceed in a disordered manner, because it first says that it sets, before it says that it circles through the south: for it circles before it sets.

I respond: to this it must be said that, having determined the mutability in the sun, which is the noblest and most luminous among the celestial bodies, it is sufficiently given to be understood in the others.

2. To that second objection it must be said that he does not arrange them according to progression, but according to dimensions, or the endpoints of dimensions. Therefore, because east and west are opposites, they are placed immediately together; likewise, because south and north are opposites, they are similarly placed immediately together in the form of a cross.

IV. But 1. there is a difficulty about what he says: It returns to its place, because in the firmament no one place of the sun is more its own than another.

If you should say that to the place from which it began its course: This is manifestly false, because the sun is always moved by its own motion, either ascending or descending; therefore it never returns to the same point.

2. Likewise, what does he mean when he says: Turning toward the north. For if the sun is moved by the diurnal motion of the firmament, and the firmament moves uniformly in the same way, then it does not turn more toward the arctic pole than toward the antarctic, or conversely.

If you say that it is understood of its proper motion: but the sun, according to its proper motion, ascends and descends in the zodiac, and when it ascends, it approaches the north; when it descends, it draws near to the south; therefore it does not turn more toward the north than toward the south.

I respond: it must be said that one can speak of the motion of the sun as daily and annual. If we speak of the annual motion, it is true that the sun returns to its place, because it began to move from there. But if we understand this of the daily motion, it does not return to its place from which it began to move, according to the truth, but according to our estimation, since it does not make a sphere but a spiral.

2. To the second objection, it must similarly be said that if we speak of the annual motion, it holds true according to the four seasons, as was stated above, because it rises in spring, sets in autumn, circles through the south in winter, and turns toward the north in summer. - But if you understand this of the diurnal motion, it does not hold true except according to our estimation. For we see the firmament moving, and we imagine its poles to be on the right and on the left; but it is not so. Rather, one pole is as if above our heads, namely the northern pole, and the other as if beneath our feet. Therefore, when the sun revolves according to the poles of the firmament, it does not go straight over our heads and under our feet, but as if obliquely; and because we see along a level plane, it seems to us that by day it inclines toward the south and by night toward the north.

V. Likewise, it is asked about what he says: The rivers go out from the sea.

Contra: The sea is bitter, and rivers are sweet: but contraries do not have their origin from one principle, nor one from another: therefore neither the sea from the rivers, nor the rivers from the sea.

I respond: it must be said that all water by nature is fresh or tasteless, as the Philosopher says; but it becomes bitter by accident, either through the evaporation of its subtle parts, with the earthy parts remaining, or through the admixture of earthy parts. Conversely, that water, just as it was accidentally made bitter, can return to freshness through refinement, as is shown by the experiment of an earthen vessel placed in the sea. Since therefore rivers, even though they return to the sea in great abundance, nevertheless flow out secretly and underground and through a certain filtration, the water is refined and turned to freshness.

As for the objection that one of two contraries does not have its origin from the other: it must be said that water is not contrary to water; and if it has a contrary disposition, it does not have it by nature but by accident, as is evident in hot and cold water. Nor is the origin of rivers from the sea by reason of property, because sweetness in water is prior by nature, but with respect to outflow, just as a drink of wine comes from a cask: because that containing place holds an abundance of waters, whence emanation occurs unceasingly.

Secondly, the vanity of the mutability of things is considered according to the being which they have in the human intellect, and this is proven by a threefold argument.

All things are difficult etc. Above, vanity was determined in things according to the being which they have in the universe; here vanity is determined according to the being which they have in the human intellect; and vanity is shown in them in three ways: first, because they are not evident; second, because they do not satisfy, at the passage: The eye is not satisfied with seeing; third, because they do not endure, at the passage: There is no remembrance of former things. They are not evident for interpreting or teaching; they do not satisfy for refreshing and quieting the learner; they do not endure for remembering. And these three aspects correspond to a threefold power in us: the interpretive, the cognitive, and the memorative.

(Vers. 8.). First, therefore, it is noted that they are not open to explanation: therefore he says: All things are difficult, namely to discover their reason: whence Wisdom 9: "We judge with difficulty the things that are on earth, and what is before us we find with labor"; below in chapter 8: "I understood that man can find no reason for the works of God," etc.: and therefore, because they are difficult, man cannot explain them in speech: Job 38: "Who shall declare the order of the heavens"? Sirach 43: ("We shall say much, and shall fall short in words: the sum of our words: He is all."

(Vers. 8. 9.10.). Is not satisfied, etc. Here in the second point it is noted that they do not fill so as to refresh the learner; and because we learn most through two senses, namely sight and hearing, he shows that in neither is there satisfaction; and therefore he says: The eye is not satisfied with seeing: Proverbs 27: "For the eyes of men are insatiable." Nor is the ear filled with hearing, because there is an itching to hear new and curious things: 2 Timothy 4: "They will heap up to themselves teachers according to their own desires, having itching ears." And the reason for this is that that in which there is true satisfaction, neither has the eye seen nor the ear perceived: 1 Corinthians 2: "Neither has the eye seen, nor the ear heard," etc.

And he shows that one cannot be satisfied in these earthly things, because the eye and the ear want to perceive new things: but nothing remains in newness: therefore the ear and the eye are filled by nothing. He assumes the major premise, and it is made clear in Acts seventeen: "The Athenians spent their time in nothing else but either to tell or to hear some new thing"; but here he sets forth the minor, because nothing has newness, neither with respect to being nor with respect to becoming. Therefore he says: What is it that has been? He asks and he himself answers: The same that shall be: therefore by simple conversion: what shall be has already been. Likewise it follows: What is it that has been done? He answers: The same that shall be done: therefore by simple conversion: what is to be done has already been done. - And from this he concludes: There is nothing new under the sun, nor can anyone say: Behold, this is recent: therefore neither to tell nor to hear anything new. And he repeats the proof: For they have already gone before in the ages that were before us: Second Peter three: "Since our fathers fell asleep, all things continue as they were".

(Vers. 11.). There is no remembrance of former things. Third, here it is touched upon that things do not remain to be remembered, because all things are handed over to oblivion; whence he says: There is no remembrance of former things, namely among us who come after: Wisdom chapter two: "Our name will be forgotten in time, and no one will have remembrance of our works." And so it is with things past and things future; whence he adds: Neither indeed shall there be any remembrance of things that are yet to come, among those who shall exist in the last times, that is: those who come later will not be mindful of those who came before, and this on account of time, which brings on oblivion; below in chapter two: "Future times will cover all things with oblivion."

Questions.


I. The question is raised about what he says: All things are difficult.

On the contrary: 1. There are many things whose knowledge is common to us and to brute animals: therefore it is not difficult for man to know what a beast knows.

2. Likewise, there are certain things in which it is not possible to err, as in the knowledge of sensible things

because the sense is not deceived regarding its proper object

and of intelligible principles, such as axioms, and such things are easy to know.

3. Likewise, what does he mean when he says: Man cannot explain them in speech? It is easier for man to speak than for any creature to work easily; therefore, if it is easy for any creature to work, then it is easy for man to explain its operation.

I respond: it must be said that there is knowing incompletely, as when something is known according to its accidents or according to some operation. There is also knowing perfectly, and he knows perfectly who fully knows the substance, power, and operation, and the causes and reasons of these. And because power is hidden and substance is remote from the senses, therefore all things are difficult for us.

1. To the objection, then, that we know some things in common with brute animals, this is true in a certain sense, as for example that this thing is bitter: but in the full sense, a brute animal knows nothing.

2. Similarly, the second point is clear: because, insofar as it is perceptible to the senses, it is manifest.

3. Through this the following is also clear: because it is different to say something and to explain it: for to explain is to bring forth hidden reasons that are inwardly wrapped up; and this only one who understands can do; and this is difficult and belongs to few.
II. Likewise, there is an inquiry concerning what he says: The eye is not satisfied with seeing, etc.

1. It is asked why he does not bring in the other senses, since it is likewise possible to acquire knowledge through them?

2. Likewise, what is it that he says: Is not satisfied? For it is said to be satisfied when one no longer wants more of what one had desired. But it sometimes happens that a man does not want to see or hear more, but rather to rest. Therefore, etc.

3. Likewise, if it is not satisfied, then their desire never rests; therefore, since a desire that has no rest is frustrated of its end, and every such thing is futile, therefore the desire of sight is futile.

I respond: it must be said that he mentions only these two senses because he is speaking of the senses insofar as they are a pathway to knowledge and are directed toward sensory delight. And there is a twofold word by which we recognize all things: namely the divine word and the human word. The divine word is every creature, because it speaks God: this word the eye perceives. The human word is the uttered voice, and this the ear perceives.

Or because knowledge is twofold: through discovery and teaching: for the first regards sight, but the second regards hearing.

1. 2. As to what is objected concerning satiety: I say that he speaks of the satiety of these things, insofar as they are instruments of the heart and the will or appetite: and because the heart is not satisfied, neither are these two senses. Whence Hugh: "All beauty, all delight, all sweetness of created things can affect the human heart, but cannot satisfy it — only that sweetness alone, for which it was made."

3. As for what he therefore objects, namely that it is frustrated, this is false, because it finds its end elsewhere.

2. To the objection that he does not want more: this is because he grows weary and becomes disgusted, not because he is satisfied.

It is asked concerning what he says: Nothing new under the sun.

The contrary seems to be the case: 1. Jeremiah thirty-one: The Lord will make a new thing upon the earth.

2. Likewise, Revelation twenty-one: I saw a new heaven and a new earth.

3. Likewise, from this passage the error seems to be confirmed that there is a circulation in things according to their going forth, so that the same things exist which formerly were, and this through the following text: it has already gone before in the ages that were before us; and thus that error seems to be verified which posits that, after the great year, which contains fifteen thousand years, all things are renewed.

I answer: it must be said that there is the operation of creation, of conservation, of restoration, and of glorification: each of these operations is above nature, and therefore it will not be under the sun or under time, but above, apart from propagation: hence he understands this only concerning that.

And so the other points are clear.

Concerning this it should be noted that he calls something new whose like has not preceded it; and thus there is nothing new according to propagation, because there like always comes from like. But he raises an objection, as if speaking of the same thing; and that is impossible to understand in this way, because if every form that has been corrupted and generated again differs numerically, it cannot be understood as the same in number.

IV. Likewise, there is a question about what he says: There is no remembrance of former things.

Against this: It is said in the Psalm: I remembered your works, O Lord.

I reply: to this, some say that it is understood concerning the wicked, not concerning the good.

But this is still false, because there are also many evil things in memory.

And therefore it must be said that this proposition is not to be understood as universally true, but as true in most cases, because there is more forgetting than remembering; yet very many of the things that have been done remain in our memory.

Art. 2. From the consideration of the twofold vanity of things, he reveals and reproves his own curiosity.

I Ecclesiastes was king etc. Above he showed vanity in things: here from the consideration of the mutability of things he reproves his own curiosity. And this part has two sections, because first he reveals his curiosity; second, he reproves his curiosity on account of vanity, in the second chapter: And when I turned myself to all the works.

First he reveals his curiosity, first indeed in the works of prudence.

The first has two parts, because first it treats of curiosity in the works of prudence or philosophy; second, of the curiosity of wealth or mechanical art, at the words: I magnified my works.

The first has two parts: because first he treats curiosity with regard to the consideration of natural things, that is, speculative science; second, with regard to the consideration of moral things, that is, practical science, at the passage: I spoke in my heart, etc.

The curiosity of philosophy is described, first indeed with regard to the consideration of natural things, and this for a fourfold reason.

He therefore describes curiosity in the works of nature, or in the consideration of divine works, in this order. First, the suitability of the one considering is indicated; second, the curiosity itself; third, the severity of divine judgment; fourth, vanity is concluded.

(Vers. 12.). First, therefore, the suitability of the one considering is noted, when he says: I, Ecclesiastes: because in him there was wisdom, therefore he calls himself Ecclesiastes, that is, a preacher: there was also power, whence he says: I was king: there was also peace, whence he adds: in Jerusalem, which is interpreted as "vision of peace": Sirach forty-seven: "Solomon reigned in days of peace": 1 Chronicles twenty-two: "The son who will be born to you will be peaceful"; and because he had this, therefore nothing held him back from consideration.

(Vers. 13.). And I proposed in my mind etc. Here the second point is touched upon, namely his curiosity, because he wanted to know all things and to investigate them subtly. Therefore he says: And I proposed in my mind to seek, namely from another, and to investigate wisely, by myself; and this is curiosity; Romans 12: "Not to be wise more than it behooves to be wise, but to be wise unto sobriety"; whence Proverbs 25: "You have found honey; eat what is sufficient for you, lest perhaps being satiated you vomit it up". Concerning all things that are done under the sun. Behold, a greater curiosity, because concerning all things: Sirach 3: "Do not scrutinize superfluous things in manifold ways, and do not be curious about many of his works: for many things beyond the understanding of men have been shown to you".

This most grievous occupation etc. Here the third point is touched upon, namely the severity of the divine judgment: because by divine judgment, on account of the sin of the first parent, it came about that our rational faculty so readily indulges in the knowledge of earthly things. On account of which he says: This most grievous occupation, because it is not only blameworthy but also penal; God gave to the sons of men, that is, he permitted it to be given: or by reason of its penal nature, he gave it in a just manner; so that they might be occupied in it, and through this be forgetful of their salvation.

Hence it should be noted that there is an occupation that is evil, which is from infirmity: concerning this, Sirach forty: "A great occupation has been created for all men, and a heavy yoke upon the sons of Adam from the day of their going forth from their mother's womb until the day of burial in the mother of all".

There is a worse occupation, which is from ignorance, about which Job 3: Let darkness and the shadow of death obscure it: let gloom seize it and let it be wrapped in bitterness ".

Third is from curiosity, and this is the worst, and of this he speaks here.

And it should be noted that "preoccupation, as Hugh says, is a distraction of the mind, which turns aside and scatters and ensnares the soul, so that it cannot think on the things that pertain to salvation." Curiosity, however, is the lustful prostitution of the human intellect, indiscriminately embracing any truth whatsoever and committing adultery with it, because the first truth alone is the bride.

(Vers. 14.15.). I have seen all things that are done etc. Here is touched upon fourthly the discovered vanity, because he profited nothing more, except that in his consideration he found vanity. And therefore he says: I have seen all things that are done under the sun, that is, I have considered all things. And behold, all is vanity, that is, vanity openly appears in them, because, as the Apostle says in Romans 8, "the creature was made subject to vanity, not willingly"; and not only vanity, but also affliction of spirit.

But because not all see this, and because they labor in vain and do not feel their affliction: he responds to this unspoken question saying: The perverse etc. Because more are found wicked than good, more foolish than wise; therefore he says: The perverse are corrected with difficulty: whence Proverbs 18: "The fool does not receive words of prudence, unless you speak those things which revolve in his heart." And the number of fools is infinite, namely on account of their multitude: and to consider this is not only vanity, but even affliction: whence it is said in the Psalm: "I beheld the transgressors, and I was grieved"; and again: "My zeal has made me waste away." A person is perverted through evil thought: Wisdom 1: "Perverse thoughts separate from God: but proven virtue corrects the foolish." Through evil affection: Proverbs 17: "He who is of a perverse heart shall not find good, and he who turns his tongue shall fall into evil." Through evil speech: Proverbs 10: "The lips of the just consider what is pleasing, and the mouth of the wicked perverse things"; Proverbs 2: "That you may be delivered from the man who speaks perverse things." Through wicked action: Proverbs 2: "They rejoice when they have done evil, and exult in the worst things"; Isaiah 1: "Cease to act perversely."

QUESTIONS.


I. But it is asked here first concerning what he says: This worst occupation etc.

On the contrary: This is sin: therefore God gave sin.

If you say: he gave, that is, he permitted it to be given;

but he similarly permitted stealing: therefore it would rightly be said that God steals.

I respond: "sins that are midway between the first apostasy and the final punishment are both punishment and fault"; but some partake more of the nature of fault, and some more of the nature of punishment: those which partake more of the nature of punishment are attributed to the just judgment of God, and such is this kind of preoccupation and blindness.

II. Likewise, a question is raised about what he says: The perverse are corrected with difficulty: because it is easy for someone to be perverted; but perversion is against nature; therefore, if it is very easy to return to nature, since vice is against nature, it is very easy to be corrected.

I answer: the reason is taken both from the side of the work

of evil, because "good exists in one way, but evil in every way"; and on the part of the operating power, which is rendered weak through fault, and therefore cannot produce vigorous work; and on the part of the opposition of good to evil according to privation, because it is very easy to fall from a habit to privation, but difficult, or entirely impossible, to return.

The curiosity of philosophy is described secondly with regard to the consideration of moral matters, and this for a fourfold reason.

I spoke etc. He dealt above with the consideration of natural things; here he similarly shows his curiosity in the consideration of moral things: and indeed he does this in the following order. For first is noted his transition: from natural things to moral things; second, the discovery: of affliction; third, the turning to the enjoyment of delightful things; fourth, the return to the consideration of evils.

(Vers. 16. 17.). First, therefore, his turning to the consideration of moral matters is indicated, and this after he had considered the natures of things. For this reason he says: I spoke in my heart, namely through thought, saying: Behold, I have become great, in fame: and I have surpassed all in wisdom who were before me in Jerusalem: Third Kings chapter four: "And the wisdom of Solomon surpassed the wisdom of all the Orientals and Egyptians, and he was wiser than all men"; Sirach chapter forty-seven: "He was instructed in his youth, and was filled with wisdom like a river." And he had this knowledge partly by revelation: whence he says: My mind has contemplated many things wisely, namely being divinely illuminated: and partly also by his own diligence: whence he also adds: And I have learned: Proverbs chapter four: "I was a tender son of my father and an only child before my mother, and he taught me,"—Proverbs last chapter: "The vision by which his mother instructed him."

Considering therefore that he had this wisdom in the investigation of natural things, because "he discoursed from the cedar of Lebanon to the hyssop," according to what is said in 3 Kings 4, he wished to transfer himself to moral matters; whence he says: And I gave my heart to know prudence, as regards things to be done; and doctrine, as regards things to be known; and errors, against prudence; and folly, against doctrine; because nothing is known perfectly unless its opposite is known. And it is useful to know these things when they are investigated in the proper manner; whence Proverbs 8: "Receive instruction and not money; choose doctrine rather than gold."

(Vers. 17. 18.). And I knew etc. Here the second point is touched upon, namely, in the consideration of this, the discovery of spiritual affliction: because the better a person knows good and evil, the more he is afflicted when he errs. On account of which he says: And I knew that in these things also there was labor and affliction of spirit, that is, in the knowledge of useful things: labor, namely in acquiring; Psalm: "I thought to know this, it is a labor before me"; affliction of spirit, after acquisition, either because of the detestation of fault, or because it is an occasion of pride and impatience, since "knowledge puffs up."

Both expositions agree with the following text, when he adds: Because in much wisdom there is much indignation, either of grief, against fault, or of impatience from pride, because great scholars, when they do not have what they want, are most easily angered: whence it is said (Job thirty-two) that Elihu was indignant, and this because he considered himself wise. And therefore it follows: He who adds knowledge adds also sorrow, because an impatient man endures many sorrows, since there is no one for whom all things turn out as desired. And with this exposition agrees another translation of Jerome: "I recognized that in these things there was a feeding on wind," and therefore rightly there is labor and affliction of spirit. Whence Hugh: "Curiosity drove it to inquiry, pride to display: and therefore it was fitting that labor should press down the swollen, and occupation should scatter the curious."

It can also be understood of the affliction of contrition, because even where there is much wisdom, there is much indignation against sin: Jeremiah thirty-one: After you showed me, I struck my thigh. And whoever adds knowledge adds also sorrow, namely of penance: Job, last chapter: "Now my eye sees you: therefore I reproach myself and do penance in dust and ashes".
