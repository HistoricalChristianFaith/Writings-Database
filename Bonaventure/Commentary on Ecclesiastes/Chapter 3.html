Chapter III.


Part one, section II. The vanity of things is shown with regard to their variety in time, and this in three articles.

All things have their time etc. Above he dealt with the vanity of things, showing that in them there is changeableness by reason of motion: here secondly he wishes to show vanity with respect to determination and variety in time. And because he shows the mutability and variety of things not for contempt of them insofar as they are the works of God, but insofar as they are loved by us: therefore in this part he shows first the variety of times: second he reproves our curiosity, at: What more does man have: third he shows the perpetuity of the divine works, at: And I learned that the works of God.

Art. 1. On the variety and mutability of things in time, both generally and specifically.

The first part has two sections, because first it treats of the variety and mutability of things in time in general, second in particular, by bringing forward and proving, at the words: A time to be born.

The variety and mutability of things in time is proposed and proved in general.

(Vers. 1.). He proposes therefore in general, saying: All things have their time, that is, the variation of time, because Romans 8: "The creature was made subject to vanity, not willingly." And all things pass away in their appointed spans under the sun, with respect to the determination of a preset time; Wisdom 11: "You have disposed all things in number, weight, and measure"; 1 Corinthians 7: "The figure of this world passes away"; and especially by this span of time appointed for it; Job 14: "You have appointed his bounds, which cannot be passed."

Questions.


I. Here there is a question concerning what he says: All things have their time.

i. The objection is: that spiritual substances, as Jerome says, are contained neither by place nor by time.

2. Likewise, everything that is in time and has time is corporeal; but spiritual substances are incorporeal: therefore they are not in time nor do they have time.

That they exist in time, however, seems clear: 1. Because Augustine says to Dardanus: "The uncreated spiritual substance is not moved through time nor through place; the created spiritual substance is moved through time, but not through place; the created corporeal substance, through time and place."

2. Likewise, what is in a place is in time: but "God alone is uncircumscribable," as Damascene says, therefore he alone is atemporal: therefore all other things have a time.

I reply: it must be said to this that this distribution is accommodated, just as when it is said: heaven covers all things, it is understood of those things which are under heaven; and therefore no objection should be raised regarding spiritual substance.

If, however, we wish to extend the distribution, we will say that time is spoken of in three ways: commonly and properly and more properly. Commonly, insofar as it denotes the measure of the passage from non-being to being: and in this way it applies to all things, because all things had a beginning. And the text seems to suggest this, because he says that all things have their time, but not that all things pass away except those under the sun.

In another way properly, insofar as it implies variation either in substance or in affection; and in this way a spiritual substance still has time with respect to the change of affections.

In the third way, more properly, as it is a surpassing measure: and thus it belongs to corruptible things, and not to all.

Therefore, to the question of whether spiritual substances have time: it must be resolved that in the first way they have it with respect to substance: in the second way, with respect to certain affections: in the third way they do not have it. - And by this the contradictions on both sides are resolved.

II. It is asked concerning the second part of the proposition: And all things pass away in their appointed times under the sun, whether the elements pass away.

It seems so.

1. Revelation twenty-one: I saw a new heaven and a new earth: therefore the old had passed away.

2. Likewise, 1 John 2: The world passes away and its concupiscence: and Matthew 24: Heaven and earth shall pass away.

ON THE CONTRARY: They are part of the constitution of the universe: therefore since the universe remains, it is clear that the elements do as well.

I respond: it must be said that for something to pass away in its time is threefold: either with respect to corruption according to its whole substance, as brute animals are corrupted; or with respect to the corruption of itself according to a part, as elements are generated and corrupted at their boundaries, where they touch one another; or to pass away, that is, to be changed from the state of corruptibility to another state, and in this way this sensible world will be renewed.

What is said here, therefore, that they pass in their own times, is understood in one of these three ways.

2. To what is further objected: The world passes away and its concupiscence, the qualification resolves this, namely, the world insofar as it is an occasion of pleasure.

1. As to what is said: I saw a new heaven: the Gloss says: "that is, made new".

2. What is said in Matthew: Heaven and earth will pass away: the Gloss explains: they will pass away, from a changeable form to an unchangeable one.

The same is proved in particular with regard to rising and setting, with regard to state, use, and manner of conduct.

A time to be born. Above he set forth the general proposition; here he proves it by induction in particular cases, showing the diverse varieties of times in things. And this part has four parts. First, the variety of times is touched upon which regards rising and setting. Second, that which regards state, there: A time to weep and a time to laugh. Third, that which regards use, there: A time to embrace. Fourth, that which regards the manner of due conduct, there: A time to keep silence.

On the variety of times with regard to rising and setting in four ways.

First, therefore, that variety is introduced which regards the origin of things and their decline under a fourfold distinction: first, of living things, second, of plants, third, of sentient things, fourth, of artificial things.

(Vers. 2.) As for the birth and death of living things, he says there is variety, when he says: A time to be born and a time to die, and this in all living things, because from birth one comes to death: Job 14: "Man, born of a woman, living for a short time, is filled with many miseries"; Sirach 40: "A great occupation has been created for all men, and a heavy yoke is upon the sons of Adam from the day of their coming forth from their mother's womb until the day of burial in the mother of all." And therefore Wisdom 5: "We, having been born, immediately ceased to exist"; Sirach 14: "Some things are generated and others are cast down; so the generation of flesh and blood: one ends and another is born."

As for the growth of plants, he says: A time to plant: as for their decline, a time to uproot what has been planted: Genesis 2: " The Lord God had planted a paradise of pleasure from the beginning "; but a time to uproot, the unfruitful: whence Luke 13: (Behold, it is three years since I have come seeking fruit on this fig tree, and I find none: cut it down therefore".

(Vers. 3.). As for the rise and fall of sentient things, he adds: A time to kill, and a time to kill is placed first, and a time to heal follows, on account of the state of corrupted nature, which has taken deeper root, so that health has no place. Concerning this variation, Deuteronomy 32: "I will kill, and I will make alive"; and again, 2 Samuel 8: "He measured two lines, one to put to death and one to keep alive".

As for the rise and fall of artificial things, he adds fourthly: A time to destroy, behold the decline: and a time to build, behold the restoration of what was destroyed. Things falling into ruin must be destroyed: Luke 19: "There shall not be left in you a stone upon a stone." A time to build, when an abundance of resources is at hand: Luke 12: "I will pull down my barns, and again build greater ones." Haggai 1: "Bring wood, build the house, and it shall be acceptable to me." And thus there is no permanence in the passing of things.

On the variety of times with regard to state, according to a threefold distinction.

A time to weep, etc. Having set forth the variety of times that regards the rise and fall of things, here is set forth the variety of times that regards their state, namely of prosperity and adversity. And this is considered according to a threefold difference, namely of prosperity, joy, and security, and their opposites.

(Vers. 4.). Therefore, as to the time of prosperity, in which one should laugh, and of adversity, in which one should weep, he says: A time to weep, in adversity: 1 Maccabees 1: "Every husband took up lamentation, and those who sat upon the marriage bed mourned"; and Lamentations 1: "Weeping, she wept in the night, and her tears were on her cheeks." And a time to laugh, in prosperity: Esther 8: "A new light seemed to rise for the Jews, and joy and gladness." And he places weeping before laughter, either because it comes first according to fallen nature; Wisdom 7: "I uttered my first cry, like all others, weeping"; or because one must first grieve, because Luke 6: "Blessed are you who weep now, for you shall laugh"; conversely, concerning the wicked: Proverbs 14: "Laughter shall be mingled with sorrow."

As for the state of joyfulness and its opposite, he says: A time to mourn and a time to dance. Mourning and weeping, laughing and dancing differ, because weeping is in tears, mourning in the voice, laughing in outward expression, dancing in the elevation of the body. Concerning mourning, in the last chapter of Genesis: "This is a great mourning to the Egyptians," in that place, that is, at funerals; but dancing is for splendid festivities: in Mark 6 it is said that on Herod's birthday the daughter of Herodias came in to dance.

(Vers. 5.). As for the time of security and its opposite, he says: A time to scatter stones, namely when there is security: Micah 4: "No man shall take up a sword against another man." A time to gather, for building fortifications, namely when there is fear of enemies: 2 Chronicles 32: "He built up all the wall that had been broken down, and raised up towers."

On the variety of times with respect to their use, according to a fourfold distinction.

A time to embrace etc. The variety of times has been set forth which regards the rise and fall of things and also their state: here the third is set forth, which regards use, and this under a fourfold distinction, namely with respect to the use of wives, possessions, treasures, and garments.

(Vers. 3.). As for the use of wives, which consists in carnal union for the generation of offspring, he says: A time to embrace and a time to be far from embraces: 1 Corinthians 7: "It remains, that those who have wives be as though they had none"; and again: "Do not defraud one another, except perhaps by consent for a time, that you may devote yourselves to prayer, and return together again".

(Vers. 6.). As for the use of possessions, he says: A time to gain and a time to lose. We gain through diligence, but we lose through laziness; Proverbs 14: "The wise woman builds her house, but the foolish one tears down even what has been built with her hands."

Or of acquiring in life, of losing in death: Job 20: "The riches which he has gathered, he shall vomit up" etc.

Or of acquiring, when we are united with God; of losing, when we are separated: Psalm: "He blessed them, and they were multiplied"; and after: "And they were made few".

As for the use of treasures, he adds: A time to keep, when there is abundance; and a time to cast away, when there is the need of the poor. The example is of Joseph, Genesis forty-one, who stored up in time of fertility and distributed in time of famine. For riches are to be kept for this purpose, that they may be distributed, whence in the Psalm: "He has distributed, he has given to the poor."

(Vers. 7.). As for the use of clothing, he adds: A time to rend, when they are worn out; and a time to sew, because our garments are worn out daily and are not renewed, except perhaps by a divine miracle, as in Deuteronomy 8: "Your garment, with which you were covered, was in no way consumed by age"; but ours are quickly consumed: therefore little care should be given to them: 1 Timothy 6: "Having food and covering, let us be content with these."

On the variety of times with regard to the manner of living, according to the threefold alternation of actions.

A time to be silent, etc. Here is set forth the fourth variety of time, which regards the proper manner of conduct, which is considered according to a threefold alternation of acts, namely with respect to the act of the rational power, which is to speak; of the concupiscible, which is to love; of the irascible, whose act is to wage war, and with respect to their opposites.

(Vers. 7.). As regards the alternation of speech, he says: A time to be silent and a time to speak, because one should not always speak nor always be silent; Sirach 20: "A wise man will be silent until the right time; but the babbler and the fool will not observe the time." For the wise man is silent and speaks at the proper time; whence Proverbs 25: "Golden apples in beds of silver is he who speaks a word in its proper time." And he places silence before speech, because one should speak deliberately: Proverbs 13: "He who guards his mouth guards his soul; but he who is heedless in speaking will experience evils."

(Vers. 8.). As for the alternation of the act of the concupiscible appetite, he says: A time of love, when a thing possesses goodness; and a time of hatred, when it turns to wickedness; the Psalm: "I have hated and abhorred iniquity"; and Romans 12: "Hating evil, clinging to good, loving one another with brotherly love."

As for the alternation of the act of the irascible appetite, he says: A time of war, against attacking adversaries: 2 Samuel 11: "And it came to pass, at the return of the year, at the time when kings go forth to war, David sent Joab," etc. And a time of peace, when those who rise up cease: 1 Maccabees 14: "The land of Judah was silent in the days of Simon," etc.; and Micah 4: "Nation shall not lift up sword against nation, neither shall they learn war anymore."

Spiritually, this fourfold variation is referred to the four states of men existing in the Church: the first with regard to the reborn, the second with regard to penitents;

the third regarding the just; the fourth regarding prelates. —First, therefore, in the baptized there is a time to be born and to die, who are born to God: John 3: "Unless one is born again of water and the Holy Spirit," etc., just as they die to the world: Romans 6: "We were buried together with him through baptism into death."

Second is a time for planting and a time for uprooting, because virtues are planted in them: Psalm: "Your children like young olive trees"; but vices are uprooted; Wisdom 4: "Adulterous plantings will not put down deep roots," because, Matthew 15, "every planting that my heavenly Father has not planted will be uprooted." - Third, in the baptized there is a time for killing the movement of concupiscence: Colossians 3: "Put to death your members that are upon the earth"; and there is a time for healing the power of nature: Jeremiah 30: "I will form a scar over your wound and I will heal you of your injuries."

Fourth, there is a time to destroy and to build up in the baptized, because the dwelling place of the devil is destroyed, and it becomes a dwelling place of Christ: First Peter 2: "Coming to him, a living stone, you yourselves are built up as spiritual houses"; Jeremiah 1: "I have set you over nations and kingdoms, to uproot and to destroy and to scatter and to build and to plant".

The second variety of times pertains to the penitent with regard to three things: namely contrition, confession and satisfaction. A time to weep and a time to laugh, in contrition, in which there is joy and sorrow: Proverbs 14: "The heart that knows the bitterness of its own soul, in its joy no stranger shall intermingle." - A time to mourn and a time to dance, in confession, in which one ought to confess with lamentation: Job 10: "Release me, that I may lament my sorrow a little." One ought to confess nakedly and openly: 2 Samuel 6: "David danced girded before the ark."

There is a time to scatter stones and a time to gather them, in satisfaction. Stones of stumbling are occasions of sin: Sirach 21: "The way of the wicked is paved with stones, and at their end are hell and darkness and punishments." These are gathered in consideration and scattered in cautious avoidance and mortification of the flesh. From these occasions few are freed, except those who are guarded by the Lord: Psalm: "In their hands they shall bear you up, lest you strike your foot against a stone."

The third variety regards the just with respect to their twofold state, namely that of actives and contemplatives. As for the contemplatives: A time to embrace and a time to be far from embraces, because they ought to attend now to action, now to contemplation. Concerning which embrace, Proverbs 4: "You will be glorified by her, when you have embraced her." They ought to be far from the embraces of the flesh, concerning which Proverbs 7: "Come, let us be inebriated with desired embraces." - As for the actives, who ought to be exercised in works of piety, he adds: A time to acquire and a time to lose, that is, to distribute what has been acquired through almsgiving: Sirach 29: "Lose your money for the sake of a brother and friend, and do not hide it under a stone." - This is said of perfect justice; but it is of supererogation to give everything, and with respect to this it is said: A time to keep and a time to cast away, as if to relinquish everything: Matthew 19: "If you wish to be perfect, go, sell what you have, and give to the poor."

Again, as regards the active: A time to rend and a time to sew, and this as regards clothing: Job thirty-one: "If I have let the poor go without covering, and if his sides have not blessed me."

The fourth variety is considered with regard to prelates, first in teaching, when he says: A time to be silent and a time to teach, because one must first learn and afterward teach; Sirach eighteenth: "Before you speak, learn"; Sirach thirty-second: "Listen in silence".

A time of love and a time of hate, in the correction of the wicked, which consists in hating the vice and in loving the nature: Second Thessalonians, last chapter: "Do not regard him as an enemy, but correct him as a brother"; and the Psalm: "I hated them with a perfect hatred."

A time of war and a time of peace: of war, against the incorrigible, who are to be struck with the sword of excommunication: Sirach 7: "Do not seek to become a judge, unless you have the strength to break through iniquities." Of peace, toward those who return: Luke 10: "If a son of peace be in that house, your peace shall rest upon him; but if not, it shall return to you."

Question.


The question concerns the number of these times. For it seems that he set forth the diversities of the variations of times insufficiently, because there are many that are not reduced to these, such as a time for eating and fasting and similar things. - If you say that these are given by way of example, then they are superfluous, because far fewer would have sufficed.

I respond: it must be said that, as Augustine says, even though any change in the universe considered in itself is a mark of imperfection, nevertheless in the whole of the universe a perfect song resounds. Because therefore Solomon wished to narrate variety in such a way as to show imperfection in individual things and perfection in the whole: he therefore used such a number as comprehends both universality and perfection. And so he enumerates twenty-eight times and fourteen variations: because twenty-eight is a perfect number and comprehends the sevenfold, which is the universality of time. Although the sixfold is perfect, it does not however comprehend the sevenfold: therefore he set down this many, not fewer, lest universality be lacking: not more, because one must stand at perfection and universality.

Art. 2. Our curiosity is reproved in two ways.

What more has man, etc. He showed above the variety of things according to diverse times; here secondly, from this he rebukes human curiosity, and he does this indeed in this order. First he suggests the uselessness of curiosity; second, in detestation of it, he commends present pleasure.

The uselessness of curiosity is suggested, and present pleasure is commended.

(Vers. 9. 10. 11.). He rebukes our curiosity on account of its uselessness, whence he says: What has man more? supply: nothing but mutability and variety and through this affliction.

And he shows that there is affliction: I have seen the affliction which God has given to the sons of men, to be exercised in it: he gave, that is, he permitted it to be given, according to that passage in Romans 1: "God gave them over to a reprobate mind"; to the sons of men, that is, to sinners: above in chapter two: "But to the sinner God has given affliction and superfluous care." To be exercised, namely by various cares: wherefore Sirach 11: "Son, let not your activities be in many things." And he explains what that affliction is, namely of consideration: whence he says: He made all things good in their time; whence Sirach 39: "All the works of the Lord are good, and in its hour he will supply every work. It is not to be said, this is worse than that: for all things will be proved in their time." And although he made them good, yet he did not give knowledge of them, but gave the capacity for inquiry: whence he says: And he delivered the world to their disputation, namely for seeking: Sirach 17: "He gave them counsel and a tongue and eyes and ears and a heart for thinking," not for attaining it: whence he adds: So that man may not find out the work which God has wrought, that is, so that he may not arrive at perfect knowledge: and this because of doubt. From the beginning to the end, namely of his life: Job 36: "Remember that you are ignorant of his work, about which men have sung"; and below in chapter eight: "I understood that I could find no reason for the works of God."

(Vers. 12. 13.). And I recognized etc. Here secondly

, he commends present pleasure, and this to rebuke curiosity: whence he says: And I knew that there was nothing better, supply: among these transitory goods, than to rejoice and to do good in one's life, namely to rejoice, in heart, to do good, in deed.

Or: to enjoy oneself, in taking pleasure: Wisdom 2: "Let us everywhere leave signs of our joy": to do good things, in preparing delightful things; Luke 12: "My soul, you have many good things, laid up for many years: rest, eat, drink, feast".

And he adds the reason: whence he says: For every man who eats and drinks sees good from his labor, and there is superfluity there, so that the sense is: he who eats and drinks sees good from his labor, because he immediately has his reward: whence he says: And this is the gift of God: below in the fifth chapter: "Every man to whom God has given riches and has granted him the power to eat from them, this is the gift of God." The Gloss explains spiritually: And I knew that there was nothing better, that is, more useful, than to rejoice, namely with the joy of the mind; Sirach thirty: "Gladness of heart, this is the life of man and an unfailing treasure of holiness"; and to do good in his life, namely meritorious works: Galatians six: "Therefore, while we have time, let us do good to all."

For every man who eats and drinks: supply: from Scripture, he eats the difficult things and drinks the easy things: in the ninth chapter of Proverbs, Wisdom says: "Come and eat my bread and drink the wine which I have mixed for you." And this is the gift of God, because the understanding of the Scriptures is given by God: Second Corinthians 3: "Not that we are sufficient to think anything of ourselves as of ourselves, but our sufficiency is from God."

Art. 3. The immutability of the divine works is shown.

I learned that all the works etc. He showed above the mutability of works, and from this reproved the curiosity of men. Here thirdly he shows the immutability of divine works, because above he had shown their variability.

(Vers. 14. 15.). He says therefore: I have learned that all the works which God has made endure forever. Behold the perpetuity, on account of which our consideration can change nothing: whence he says: We cannot add anything to them or take away from them, which God has made, that he may be feared, and thus they are not in our power: Proverbs 30: "Do not add anything to his words, lest you be reproved and found a liar".

And he demonstrates this, because not even God himself diminishes or adds; whence he says: What has been made, itself remains. Nor does he even add; whence he concludes: What things are to come have already been, because similar things have preceded; whence he also says: And God restores what has passed away, because "the corruption of one is the generation of another"; above in chapter one: "There is nothing new under the sun, nor can anyone say: this is new"; Genesis chapter two: "God rested on the seventh day."

Question.


Here a question arises: since he intends to treat of the vanity of mutability, why does he treat here of perpetuity?

i. It seems outside his subject.

2. Likewise, a question is raised about this very perpetuity, because he says: The works that God has made endure forever; but no corporeal things endure forever; therefore God made no corporeal things; therefore this leads back to the heresy of the Manicheans, who said that corruptible things were not created by the good God.

3. Likewise, it seems that he contradicts himself, because

. immediately it follows: God restores what has passed away: therefore something of the works of God is corrupted and restored:

Therefore he speaks falsely when he says that all the works of God

persevere forever.

But that all the works of God are incorruptible, as he says in the text, it seems:

1. Because cause and effect are proportional, therefore the effect of an incorruptible cause must be incorruptible: but God remains forever and is incorruptible: therefore the works of God are also.

2. Likewise, since all the works of God are very good, as is said in Genesis 1; and since incorruptible things are better than corruptible things: it seems that these corruptible things were not created by the good God.

I respond: to the first point, why he here treats perpetuity, it must be said that it is not for its own sake, but on the occasion of mutability; and indeed he does this for a threefold reason. The first is to remove error; for since he had shown above the mutability in creatures, lest it be believed that the works of God were entirely defective, he adds that in some way they are perpetual. Again, another reason is to restrain curiosity, which he had restrained, because we cannot investigate the mutability and cause and origin of things; he also wishes to restrain it because we can neither change nor increase the very substance of things. The third reason is to repress pride: for since he seemed to have diminished the works of the Lord through mutability, lest anyone on this account condemn God their author, he returns again to insinuating perpetuity in them.

As to what is asked about permanence, the answer is that there is a threefold being of things: namely, in their own genus, in the soul, and in the divine foreknowledge; and above he showed that things are mutable in their own genus and insofar as they are in the soul: here he touches upon their perpetuity in the divine foreknowledge.

Alternatively, it can be answered that to speak of things according to their proper being is twofold: either according to common being, or individual. According to individual being, many things are corruptible; according to common being, they are perpetual, because God continually preserves their being through generation; "for because they are far distant from the principle, God completed their being in the remaining way, making generation continuous in them."

1. To the objection that they are incorruptible because they are proportional to their cause: it must be said that this is understood where there is univocal generation, where a cause produces something similar to itself. But this is not the case with the Creator and the creature; rather, by infinite power He makes great and small things, corruptible and incorruptible.

2. As to what is objected concerning goodness, it must be said that all things are good in themselves, but exceedingly good in their order: and because where there is order, there is a greater and lesser good: therefore some things are corruptible, some incorruptible. For this reason goodness exists in things not only according to more and less, but according to opposition and likeness: therefore the world is compared to a most beautiful song: therefore it is fitting that some things are corruptible, and some the contrary.

Part II The vanity of fault is proven, then the remedy against it is set forth.

I saw under the sun etc. He dealt above with the vanity of nature; here with the vanity of fault; and because this is dangerous, therefore it needs a remedy. First, then, he sets forth the vanity; second, the remedy against it, below in chapter four: Keep thy foot.

Part two, section I. The vanity of fault is shown under a threefold distinction, both with regard to rulers and with regard to subjects. Now vanity falls under a threefold distinction: first he treats of the vanity of malice: second, of avarice, at the passage: Considering, I found: third, of imprudence, at the passage: Better is a child. Now vanity is found in rulers and subjects. First, therefore, he treats of the vanity of malice in rulers and judges; second, in subjects, at the passage: I turned.

Art. I. On the vanity of wickedness, first described in rulers in a fourfold order.

First, therefore, the vanity of malice, which is in rulers, is described in this order: first, he introduces the consideration of this vanity; second, that from this one arrives at the premeditation of future judgment; third, at the consideration of the present state; fourth, how one falls into erroneous opinion.

(Vers. 16.). First, therefore, he indicates that he has considered the wickedness of judges, saying: I saw under the sun in the place of judgment, ungodliness, with respect to the examination of the case; and in the place of justice, iniquity, with respect to the rendering of the sentence. Isaiah 1: "How has the faithful city, full of judgment, become a harlot! Justice once dwelt in her, but now murderers. Your princes are faithless, companions of thieves; they all love bribes, they pursue rewards. They do not judge for the orphan, and the cause of the widow does not come before them"; because it is said in the Psalm: "In whose hands are iniquities, their right hand is filled with bribes."

(Vers. 17.). And I said in my heart: God will judge the just and the wicked. Here he shows secondly how from this he arrived at the premeditation of the future judgment. For which reason he adds: And I said in my heart: He will judge the just, unto reward; the wicked, unto punishment: Job thirty-four: "Far be wickedness from God, and iniquity from the Almighty. For he will render to a man his work, and according to the ways of each one he will restore to them. For truly God will not condemn in vain, nor will the Almighty subvert judgment." And therefore God will judge them according to their works, which will then be manifest; whence he adds: And the time of every thing will then be, that is, the time of judgment of every person, who seems to be every thing, 2 Corinthians 5: "For we must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ, that each one may receive the things proper to the body, whether it be good or evil."

Or: a time for every matter, that is, of the manifestation of every thing: 1 Corinthians 4: "Do not judge before the time, until the Lord comes, who will both bring to light the hidden things of darkness and make manifest the counsels of hearts."

Or: a time for everything, that is, of the retribution of everything: Romans 8: "Every creature groans," etc.

Ecclesiastes expected this time to come from the sight of present iniquity: Isaiah 59: " Judgment is turned backward, and justice stood far off, because truth has fallen in the streets, and equity could not enter; and the Lord saw, and it appeared evil in his eyes, because it is not manifest; and the just one put on a breastplate and the helmet of salvation.

I said in my heart concerning the sons of men etc. Here thirdly is addressed how from this he arrived at a consideration of the present condition, namely why the Lord willed men to be miserable in the present: for this purpose, namely that he might test them, and therefore he made the life of man similar to that of beasts.

(Vers. 18. 19. 20. 21.). For this reason he says: I said in my heart, that is, I knew: concerning the sons of men, as regards their nature: that God might test them and show that they are like the beasts, that is, by likening them to beasts he might test who they were, whether truly good or evil: because the evil, seeing their likeness to beasts in life, wish to be made like them in conduct and live like beasts; but the good live spiritually: therefore it is said in the Psalm: "Man, when he was in honor, did not understand; he was compared to senseless beasts and was made like them." And he shows the likeness of men to beasts in death and life and in matter: the dissimilarity in spiritual substance is not manifest. - As regards death he says: And therefore the destruction of man and of beasts is one, and the condition of both is equal, namely, for the testing of men, because, just as a beast does not always live, but rather sometimes dies, so also does man: below in chapter nine: "There is no one who lives forever and who has confidence in this matter"; and therefore he makes clear that the likeness is in death, whence: As man dies, so also do they die.

Nor is the likeness only in the end of life, but also in the act of living: therefore he adds: All things breathe alike, namely those that breathe: nor has man more than the beast, namely as regards the act of life: Wisdom 7: "The going-forth of men into life is one, and the like departure." Thus the likeness as regards life is clear.

And he adds the likeness with respect to matter: and therefore he says: All things are subject to vanity, namely, because they were made from nothing, therefore they are vain; Romans 8: "The creature was made subject to vanity." He does not say that nothing is the matter, but he means to say that they do not have uncreated matter nor essence, but they have created matter, which he understands by the name of earth.

Therefore he says: And all things go to one place, namely to the matter from which they were made. They were made from earth and equally return to earth, equally, that is, in like manner. And thus a likeness in matter is noted: Sirach 40: "All things that were made from earth shall return to earth: likewise all waters shall return to the sea."

But only in these things is there an open similarity, but the difference in the soul does not appear. Who knows if the spirit of the children of Adam ascends upward? to heaven, namely to reward, or downward through corruption: just as conversely, the spirit of beasts descends downward? through corruption, or even upward, like that of man, to the retribution of judgment. Few have known this: many have erred and held that the spirits of man and beast are uniform. The faithful have known this, in whose person it is said below in the twelfth chapter: "The body shall return to its earth, whence it was, and the spirit shall return to God, who gave it." But the carnal do not know this, in whose person it is said below in the ninth chapter: "The dead know nothing more, nor do they have any further reward."

And I found etc. In the fourth place, on account of this resemblance, he adds how he fell into the erroneous commendation of pleasure, as if nothing were better than it.

(Vers. 22.). For this reason he says: I found that nothing is better than for a man to rejoice in his work, that is, to take pleasure in the present, as carnal men do, of whom Isaiah fifty-six says: "Come, let us take wine and be filled with drunkenness, and it shall be as today, so also tomorrow and much more." And this commendation is erroneous, because it posits no other recompense nor another life; whence he adds: And that this is his portion, as if he expects nothing else; whence in Isaiah twenty-two it is said in the person of such men: "Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we shall die"; and Wisdom two: "Let us everywhere leave signs of joy, for this is our portion, and this is our lot." And this erroneous opinion has its origin from uncertainty of judgment; for which reason he says: For who shall bring him to know what shall be after him? That is, who will show him that there are any future goods which he ought to expect? As if to say: no one. And certainly no one will persuade him, unless he is raised to contemplate spiritual things: "while we look not at the things which are seen, but at the things which are not seen; for the things which are seen are temporal, but the things which are not seen are eternal," Second Corinthians four. But because the carnal man regards only visible things, therefore he does not expect eternal things; therefore First Corinthians two: "The natural man does not perceive the things that are of God." Therefore if he wishes to know future goods, it is necessary to set aside carnality.

Questions.


I. The question is raised concerning what he says: Man has nothing more than the beast, and that the condition of both is equal. That this is false, it seems:

I. In Genesis chapter two it is said: For Adam there was not found a helper like unto him, therefore no beast is like man: therefore he has something more than the beasts.

2. Likewise, it is certain that man deliberates about future things and discerns past things and expresses his conceptions; but a beast does not do this; therefore, etc.

3. Furthermore, man tames beasts, but no beast tames man: therefore man has something more than a beast.

But that he does not have it, seems apparent: because he is born, lives, and dies and goes into the earth, just as a beast does.

I respond: some say that he says this in the person of the carnal man, who believes man has nothing more than a beast. - It can be said otherwise, that he speaks in his own person: and he speaks truly according to his own meaning. For he intends to show the uncertainty of the soul's recompense through a comparison to a beast. But he sets forth and understands that comparison with respect to visible things, which can be discerned by the senses: and through this he does not conclude a likeness regarding spiritual things, but rather a doubt: whence he says: Who knows, etc.

It must be said, therefore, that in truth man possesses reason beyond the beasts, but shares with them in corruptibility, life, and respiration; and because reason is not something sensible, but intelligible, and he is speaking about sensible things, therefore there is no objection whatsoever in this matter.

II. There is a doubt about what he says: Who knows whether the spirit of the sons of Adam ascends upward? That it does, however, is shown as follows:

1. First, through the disposition of the body, whose disposition and organization is directed according to a fitness toward what is above. If therefore "he gave man an uplifted face and bade him gaze upon the heavens," and he looks upward: therefore the spirit is borne upward.

2. Likewise, all naturally desire happiness; but it is established that no one can be happy amid miseries, especially one to whom death comes unwillingly; therefore, if one cannot have happiness in this life, one is destined to have a separated spirit after this life; therefore, etc. 3. Furthermore, our intellect is unmixed, whence it understands all things; therefore it does not depend on the body with respect to its act, therefore much less with respect to its being; therefore, when the body descends, it is possible for the spirit to ascend.

4. Likewise, by the reasoning of Ecclesiastes himself: because, if some people have good things and some have bad things, and we see that in the present life bad things happen to the good and good things happen to the bad, if therefore it is necessary to posit one ruler, it is necessary to posit another time and recompense, in which God may judge the wicked and the just. Since therefore our faith says this, Ecclesiastes seems to have been a heretic, who doubted this.

That it goes downward, however, seems clear:

1. Through Scripture: because in Wisdom 9 it is said: The body that is corrupted weighs down the soul, therefore if the soul is corporally corrupted, it is totally corrupted.

2. Likewise, that which suffers with the one suffering dies with the one dying; but the soul suffers with the body; therefore, etc.

3. Likewise, no substance is idle; therefore, that which takes away from something all operation takes away from it being and duration. But the soul loses all operation when the body is injured, which is evident because it loses understanding, concerning which this seems less likely, as in the case of the mad. Therefore, when the body is corrupted, it loses its substance.

I respond: it must be said that the souls of the good ascend upward to reward, and the souls of beasts descend downward into corruption — this our faith both states and presupposes; and reason and philosophy agree with this. Nevertheless, that reasoning and philosophy is hidden and has many grounds for doubt; therefore almost no one, or very few, were able to be truly certified concerning the immortality of the soul and its blessedness, unless faith had assisted them. Hence even Plato, who held the soul to be immortal, erred in turn regarding blessedness, since he held that souls cycle through the bodies of brute animals. — Ecclesiastes therefore does not deny or doubt this, but says that it is difficult to demonstrate; and this is indeed true for the unbeliever.

2. 3. To the objection raised concerning operation and co-suffering, it must be said that for something to be united to another, this happens in two ways: either as a mover only, and then it can be separated with no change made in itself; or as a mover and perfection; and this in two ways: either it does not have its own proper operation, as the soul of a brute animal, or it has its own proper operation, as the soul of a human being. Since therefore the soul of a human being is united to the body as perfection and mover, it suffers together with the suffering body; and therefore it is also impeded when the body is impeded. But since it is joined separably, it is not necessary that it itself be corrupted: when every harmony is dissolved, the soul departs.
