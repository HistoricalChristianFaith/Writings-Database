INTRODUCTION

This work originally began as a catena on the deuterocanonical Old Testament book called the Wisdom of Solomon, which quickly developed into a complete translation of the commentary on Wisdom of Solomon by Rabanus Maurus, with added excerpts by the Early Church Fathers. I did make some modifications to the text of the commentary by using the Greek Septuagint version of the Scripture text of the Wisdom of Solomon instead of the Latin version used by Rabanus. Rabanus used the Latin Vulgate for the Scripture text in his commentary, which has some minor differences from the Greek Septuagint, such as in readings and in verse numbering on some occasions.

This translation of the commentary by Rabanus on the Wisdom of Solomon is the first ever English translation as far as I am aware of. Rabanus is the first person to write a commentary on the Wisdom of Solomon. Many of the commentaries by Rabanus have not been translated into English at this point. I also translated most of the other excerpts from the Early Church Fathers in this commentary. I found most of those excerpts in the 1601 Venice edition of the Glossa Ordinaria. That edition has the glosses of the original Glossa Ordinaria with the addition of added excerpts from a variety of Church Fathers. I adopted nearly all of those added excerpts from the Venice edition Gloss, which many of them have not been translated until now. Here are those names of the twelve sources found and adopted into this commentary: Augustine of Hippo (354-430AD), John Chrysostom (died 407AD), Ambrose of Milan (died 397AD), Anastasius of Sanai (620-700AD), Vigilius of Thapsus (5th century),  Jerome of Stridon (died 420AD), Cerealis of Castellum Ripense (480AD), Gregory the Great  (died 604 AD), Victor Vitensis (430-486AD), Didymus of Alexandria (313-398AD), Eusebius of Caesarea (died 339AD), and Ambrosiaster (around 370AD).

Below is an overview on the life and works of Rabanus Maurus that I gleaned from the prologue of volume 107 of the Patrologia Latina, pages 1-40.

Early life

RABANUS, so called by his native name, received another name, MAURUS, from his teacher Alcuin, as he himself testifies in the preface to the commentaries on the Kings, where he says that in those places which he explains himself, he marked the letter M in the margin, expressing the name Maurus: "which my master, Albinus of blessed memory, gave to me." Indeed, in that age, it had become customary among learned men living in these parts, that besides their family name, which was often somewhat harsh in sound, they would adopt another name from Latin or Greek, rarely from Hebrew, seeking the smoothness of a more refined language…

It is clear that he was a Frank, that is, born in eastern France, not indeed at Fulda, but at Mainz. Listen to Rabanus himself, offering his books on the Cross to the pope in the person of Alcuin, and speaking therefore of himself: "He himself is of Frankish descent, and an inhabitant of the forest of Bochonia," etc. For he was a monk at Fulda, which is a town situated in Bochonia; but he was not born there, but in the city of Mainz, as he himself sings in the caption: "Indeed, I was born in this city, and baptized at the holy font: After this, I learned sacred doctrine at Fulda."… Nevertheless, it is certain that Rabanus was born of illustrious lineage, as he had a brother Tutin, a man of the highest nobility,… Rabanus was born around the year 776. Certainly, he began to write two books on the praises of the Holy Cross before the year 810, when he had completed six lustra, that is, thirty years… He was a monk of Fulda from boyhood… While Rabanus was teaching literature, the Fulda academy enjoyed great renown, abounding then in books of all disciplines and producing illustrious teachers for the Christian world…

So great was his love for literature that neither the labor of teaching nor the duties of regular life, nor various occupations, nor even illness could draw him away from his devotion to writing. Indeed, he was easily inclined to obey the wishes of his friends; his sole concern was to be able to benefit the Church of Christ and the salvation of others…

His love of the Church Fathers

Appointed head of the Fulda academy, although he was occupied with regular trainings and instructing the youth, he nevertheless did not cease from composing commentaries from the opinions of the Fathers, as his letter to Haistulf, Bishop of Mainz, attests, regarding his exposition of Matthew: “Therefore, intent on reading these, as much as the countless bonds of monastic service allowed me, and for the nourishment of the young—which brings us no small annoyance and does injury to reading—I myself, being both author and scribe, took care to record on a slip of paper what they had expounded, either in their own words or, for brevity’s sake, in mine.” He imitated the Venerable Bede, whose words are almost the same, as related in his Eulogy from the previous century. Having become abbot, he cultivated the same study, although living in deserted places and detained by great occupations, by which he was compelled daily to serve and provide necessities for the servants of Christ living under the Rule of St. Benedict. He had no leisure to fully examine the sayings of the Fathers, as he writes in his letter to Freculf, Bishop of Lisieux in Gaul, to whom he sent his exposition of Leviticus; he had said the same in a letter to him when he sent his explanation of Genesis… The extent of his reverence for the holy Fathers is shown by all his works, which he composed from their opinions, with their names placed in the margin: “So that I may not be accused of stealing the sayings of the elders and composing them as if they were my own,” he says in the preface to the Gospel of Matthew. He did this, as he himself says, for the benefit of others: “So that the poor reader who does not have an abundance of books, or who cannot examine the deep meanings of the Fathers in many works, may at least find sufficiency for his need here.” No one can deny that this was done with the best intention, who considers the scarcity of books as it was at that time. For outside the monasteries, there were hardly any libraries, not even in most cathedral churches, as Freculf testifies about his own church of Lisieux in his letter to Abbot, from whom he requested Commentaries on the Pentateuch composed from the sayings of the Fathers: “Therefore, if you attempt to offer any excuses to avoid undertaking a work of such size and immense labor, and try to answer why I do not examine by my own effort those books from which I command these things to be made, and gather whatever I please by picking them out, let the vigilance of your charity consider that we have no abundance of books to do this, even if the smallness of our dull mind were vigorous: for in the bishopric entrusted to our insignificance, I do not even find the canonical books of the Old and New Testament, much less their expositions.”… 

Death

In the year 856, it is recorded in the Annals of Fulda, in the month of February, on the fourth day of the month, Archbishop Rabanus of the Church of Mainz died, having served as bishop for 9 years, one month, and four days… 

The commentaries by Rabanus

Made abbot in the year 822, Rabanus dedicated Commentaries on Matthew and certain Homilies to Haistulf, who died in the year 826. Around the year 830, he submitted an Explanation of the Octateuch to Freculf, bishop of Lisieux in Gaul, who obtained it from him through letters and requests… At about the same time, he expounded the Book of Joshua at the request of Frederick, Bishop of Utrecht on the Rhine, who was killed in the year 834, preceded by a memorial letter, a fragment of which was related by Henschenius, received from Peter Franscisco Chiffletius… along with the Commentary itself divided into three books, which does not exist in the published editions. Shortly after, at the request of Humbert, Bishop of Würzburg, he explained the Books of Judges and Ruth; then the Books of Kings at the request of Hilduin, abbot: which exposition he offered to Louis the Pious at Fulda; and not long after, he sent commentaries on the books of Chronicles to King Louis. Around the year 836, he dedicated his writing on the books of Judith and Esther to Monarch Judith, after she had defeated her enemies to a considerable extent, whose preface, not yet published in print, which we received through the kindness of the same learned man Peter Franscisco Chiffletius, is to be rendered here from the Benignian and Fontan manuscripts. Afterwards, he dedicated his explanation of the Books of Maccabees to Gerold, Archdeacon of the palace of Emperor Louis, before the year 840, then the Exposition on Wisdom and Ecclesiasticus (Sirach) to Olgar, Archbishop of Mainz; and he wrote a letter to Noting, Bishop of Verona, before the death of Emperor Louis. After the year 840, with Louis the Pious dead, he offered the Books on Jeremiah to Emperor Lothair: which Hartmot, dean of Saint Gall, took care to have written, as Ratpert reports in chapter 9. At the beginning of the year 842, he sent the Collectarium on the Epistles of Paul to Lupus, then abbot of Ferrara, who had recently returned from the Fulda school to Gaul, as stated: about the same time, he inscribed the Commentary on Ezekiel to Lothair the Emperor; and the penitential book to Otgar, Archbishop, in whose chapter 15 he mentions the Battle of Fontanet, which was fought between the sons of Louis the Pious in the year 841. To the same period must also be referred the Letter on Chorepiscopi to Drogo, Bishop of Metz, as well as the letter to Regimbold, Chorepiscopus of Mainz, concerning certain questions of penance. At the same time, he also explained the Book of Daniel the Prophet, at the request of friends, and later inscribed his work on this subject to Louis, King of Germany; to whom he also dedicated the Exposition on the Canticles, which are read at matins throughout the week.

PREFACES

RABANUS. To the most reverend lord and to the holy man, to be observed with the sincerest devotion of love, and most worthy of all honor, Otgar, Archbishop, Rabanus sends greetings in Christ. Whenever I think of your presence, most blessed Father, I am always refreshed by sweet delight, recalling the gentleness and humility, modesty and temperance, which especially flourish in your holiness, so that from this you are truly recognized as an imitator of Him who commanded His disciples, saying: Learn from me, for I am gentle and humble of heart. Therefore, many, inspired by sacred love, have rushed to the service of your honor, and wish to offer whatever duty they can with devoted minds. Therefore, although I do not possess the riches of earthly things, because I have only those which pertain to relieving the needs of Christ’s holy poor, yet that which I have received by divine grace, I have added as a voluntary gift to the sacred riches which you abundantly possess in wisdom and knowledge of the divine law, wishing greatly that whenever it is presented to your sacred sight, you may remember me, and if you find any usefulness in it, you may agree to adapt it both for your own use and for the use of your people. For indeed the gift of which I speak is the present volume, which I recently published as an explanation of the Book of Wisdom, which by many is entitled under the name of Solomon; yet blessed Jerome maintains that it was not written by Solomon, as is commonly believed, but by the most learned Jew Philo, whom he designated as a falsely attributed work, because he claims for himself the use of another’s name. For this volume expounded by the presbyter Bellator, he testified that he had undertaken it in eight books. About which Father Augustine and Saint Ambrose spoke some things under the name of homilies. The wording of this book also is most sweet and truly magnificent with the dignity of its name, and most useful for instructing the Church of God, although the above-mentioned interpreter of the divine law Jerome says that the same book is nowhere found among the Hebrews, but rather scents of Greek eloquence, nor is it received among the canonical Scriptures by the ancients, as are the books of Judith, Tobit, and Maccabees, which even in modern times the holy Church enumerates among the sacred Scriptures, and reads them publicly like the other canonical Scriptures; and frequently also orthodox teachers use the same passages in their doctrines. The true reason why I undertook the labor of expounding this book was this: because I found no previous exposition on the whole book, although the aforesaid priest Bellator mentions that he made eight books on it, as I said above; these have not yet reached us. However, I have summarized this work of mine in three little books, so that I might provide the eager reader with some understanding of the mysteries of the aforementioned book; but I reserve more perfect things for the more perfect. But you, most religious bishop of Christ, make use of the gift bestowed upon you by your devoted servant, and if you find anything pleasing in it, attribute it solely to the gift of God; but if you find anything blameworthy, ascribe it more to my ignorance and weakness than to spite, and do not delay to let me know in what way it should be corrected; also, support my weakness with your prayers before the divine mercy, so that since He has already given the will and in some way the beginning, He Himself may grant all things perfectly according to His will. Nor do I think it should be concealed from your reverence that I have another work in hand, that is, an explanation of the book of Ecclesiasticus, which they call the Wisdom of Jesus, son of Sirach, and which contains many rituals of Christ and the Church. This also, when, God willing, it has been gratefully received by your holiness, I will soon try to send to you with a generous mind. May the Holy Trinity and undivided Unity consent to preserve your love unharmed and unstained forever, holy Father, be mindful of us. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

JEROME There is also the all-virtuous [πανάρετος] book of Jesus son of Sirach, and another falsely attributed work [ψευδεπίγραφος] which is inscribed Wisdom of Solomon. Of these, I found the former in Hebrew, not Ecclesiasticus (Sirach), as among the Latins; but marked Parables, to which Ecclesiastes and Song of Songs were joined, so that it matched the likeness of Solomon not only in the number of books, but also in the kind of subjects. The second is nowhere found among the Hebrews, since even its style breathes Greek eloquence; and some of the ancient writers affirm that this is the book of the Jew Philo. Therefore, just as the Church indeed reads the books of Judith, Tobit, and the Maccabees, but does not receive them among the canonical Scriptures: so let it read these two volumes for the edification of the people, not for confirming the authority of ecclesiastical doctrines. [Jerome of Stridon, Preface to the Books of Solomon, PL028 1241A] died 420AD

Chapter 1

Wisdom, which is Christ, commands the inhabitants of the earth to love righteousness, indicating to them that perverse thoughts separate from God, and that wisdom will not enter into a wicked soul.

1:1 Love righteousness, you who judge the earth, think about the Lord with goodness, and seek him with simplicity of heart;

RABANUS. Love righteousness, you who judge the earth. Divine wisdom admonishes the rulers of God's people, to whom the power of judging has been given, that they should love righteousness and preserve equity in judgment. Otherwise, this statement may generally pertain to all who are endowed with reason and have an understanding mind, so that, judging the earth, that is, their own flesh, they may love righteousness; namely, that they do not make provision for the flesh in its lusts, but, subjecting it to the command of the spirit, devote themselves especially to the virtues by which the soul is nourished; then let them care for their body according to necessity, and not according to pleasure, always recalling that saying of the law: For man does not live by bread alone, but by every word that proceeds from the mouth of God (Matt. 4:4). think about the Lord with goodness, and seek him with simplicity of heart. But he thinks about the Lord with goodness who reads and understands Sacred Scripture according to piety, and he seeks Him in simplicity of heart who humbly submits himself to His will, namely to keep His commandments. For he who devoutly seeks the Lord honors Sacred Scripture, and does not criticize what he does not yet understand, and therefore does not resist: this is to be meek, of whom in the Gospel Truth itself says: Blessed are the meek, for they shall possess the earth (Matt. 5:5). Therefore, philosophers of the world and their heretical followers are excluded, who think that by human reason they can grasp divine secrets, since it is written: Who shall investigate the wisdom of God that precedes all things? (Rom. 1:33) And the Apostle clearly says: The judgments of God are incomprehensible, and His ways unsearchable; He is to be worshipped in this present life with right faith, rather than with reckless conflict. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

 

1:2 for he is found by those who do not test him, and he appears to those who do not distrust him. 1:3 For crooked thoughts separate from God, and when power is tested it exposes the foolish.

RABANUS. Because He is found by those who do not test Him, but He appears to those who have faith in Him. For it is dangerous to tempt God by human assumption, since it is written: You shall not tempt the Lord your God. (Deut. 6:16) But it is beneficial to honor Him with true faith, because the righteous shall live by faith. For true wisdom by no means enters a wicked mind, nor can it dwell in a wicked man; therefore the philosophers of the Gentiles flatter themselves in vain, and all heretics, as well as false Christians, who think that with a defiled mind and criminal conduct they can receive the wisdom of God, which only those of a pure heart deserve to receive by the gift of the Holy Spirit. For perverse thoughts separate from God; but proven virtue corrects the foolish. It is clear that every wicked thought and depraved will can in no way be joined to the Lord, nor be in harmony with the highest good; for there is no fellowship between light and darkness, nor agreement between Christ and Belial. But where the intention and pursuit of sacred virtues are present in a devout heart, they always reject the vices of the foolish and refute their errors; therefore, those who truly love wisdom must cleanse their hearts and direct the whole intention of their mind to the love of their Creator. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

1:4 Because wisdom will not enter a deceitful soul, nor dwell in a body enslaved to sin; 1:5 for the holy spirit of discipline will flee deceit and remove from thoughts without understanding and will be put to the test when unrighteousness comes in.

RABANUS. For wisdom will not enter into a malicious soul, nor dwell in a body subject to sins. In agreement with this testimony, the apostle James wrote similarly in his Epistle, saying: Can a fig tree, my brethren, bear olives, or a vine figs? So neither can salt water make sweet. Who is wise and learned among you? Let him show by good conduct his works in the meekness of wisdom. But if you have bitter jealousy and strife in your hearts, do not glory and be liars against the truth. For this wisdom does not descend from above, from the Father of lights; but is earthly, sensual, devilish. For where there is jealousy and dispute, there is instability and every evil work. But the wisdom that is from above is first indeed pure, then peaceable, gentle, compliant, full of mercy and good fruits, judging without hypocrisy. And the fruit of righteousness is sown in peace by those who make peace (James 3:12-18). For true wisdom does not enter into a hateful mind, nor can it dwell in a wicked man; therefore, in vain do the philosophers of the Gentiles flatter themselves, and all heretics, as well as false Christians, who think that with a base mind and wicked conduct they can receive the wisdom of God, which only those with a pure heart are worthy to receive as a gift of the Holy Spirit. Because wisdom will not enter a deceitful soul .Therefore, it is most certain that Mani, Arius, and Eunomius, and the other heretics, composed their doctrines, most full of errors, without the grace of the Holy Spirit, because the good Spirit of the Lord always leads into the right way; but the evil spirit deceives, and drags his followers with him into the snare of eternal perdition, concerning whom it is also added: and will be put to the test when unrighteousness comes in. For when the time of retribution comes, all the wicked and sinners will receive perpetual punishment for their iniquities. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

VIGILIUS. Where the entire Trinity is called holy. Holy Father, holy Son, holy Holy Spirit. Of the Father in Ezra: For holy is God; truth. And in Leviticus: Be holy, for I also am the holy Lord your God (Lev. 11:44). Of the Son in the Gospel: I know you who you are, the holy one of God (Mark 1:24). Likewise there: That which shall be born of you shall be called holy, the Son of God (Luke 1:35). Of the Holy Spirit in Solomon: For the Holy Spirit of discipline will flee from deceit, and remove himself from thoughts that are without understanding (Wis. 1:5). [Vigilius of Thapsus, Against Marivadus the Arian, PL 62] 5th century

VIGILIUS. On common discipline. The discipline of the Father, the discipline of the Son, the discipline of the Holy Spirit. Of the Father in Solomon: My son, do not neglect the discipline of God, nor grow weary when you are rebuked by Him (Prov. 3:11). Of the Son in Psalm seventeen (Vers. 36): Your right hand has received me, and Your discipline itself will teach me, and Your doctrine will guide me to the end. Of the Holy Spirit in Solomon: For the Spirit of discipline will flee deceit, and will remove itself from thoughts that are without understanding (Wis. 1:5). [Vigilius of Thapsus, Against Marivadus the Arian, PL 62] 5th century

1:6 For wisdom is a kindly spirit, and will not acquit a blasphemer from what his lips have spoken; because God is witness of his inmost being, and a true observer of his heart, and a hearer of his tongue.

RABANUS. This statement refutes the error of those who, just as good thoughts are known to be inspired in us by God, also believe that evil thoughts are generated in our minds by His instigation. In no way does God, who is supremely good and wills all men to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth, impose the necessity of an evil word or wicked deed on anyone, through which he might perish, as the apostle James shows when he says: "Let no one, when he is tempted, say: I am tempted by God; for God is not the tempter of evils, and He Himself tempts no one" (James 1:13); as if he were saying: Let no one, when he has committed robbery, theft, false testimony, murder, adultery, or other such things, say that he was compelled by God to do these things, and therefore could in no way have avoided their outcome. “For God is not a tempter of evils,” it is understood, of temptations; for He Himself tempts no one, namely with that kind of temptation which deceives the wretched so that they sin. For there are two kinds of temptation: one which deceives, the other which tests; according to that which deceives, God tempts no one; according to that which tests, God tempted Abraham. Concerning this, the Prophet also asks: Prove me, O Lord, and try me (Ps. 26:2). But each one is tempted by his own desire, drawn away and enticed, namely, drawn away from the right path and enticed into evil, into which, however, he is permitted to fall by the just judgment of God, who did not wish to obey the Spirit of truth. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

1:7 For the spirit of the Lord has filled the world, and that which holds all things together has knowledge of what is spoken.

RABANUS. For the spirit of the Lord has filled the world. Therefore, the Holy Spirit, who in the beginning of creation was moving over the waters with invisible power, fills and contains every creature with His majesty, or by the gift of His power fills the whole world of the Church, in which He daily shows the abundance of His gift. Therefore it is added: and that which holds all things together has knowledge of what is spoken, because, as Paul says: To one is given through the Spirit the word of wisdom, to another the word of knowledge according to the same Spirit; to another faith in the same Spirit, to another the grace of healing in one Spirit, to another the working of miracles, to another prophecy, to another the discernment of spirits, to another various kinds of tongues, to another the interpretation of tongues: for all these are worked by one and the same Spirit, distributing to each one individually as He wills (1 Cor. 12:8-11). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

VIGILIUS. But in order to teach that the Holy Spirit is spread everywhere, we shall examine the testimony of David, from whose prophecy the Holy Spirit is taught to be diffused not by places, as you, held fast by a heavy sleep, suppose, but by His power, like the Father and the Son, everywhere. For he himself sings and says in Psalm (138:7): Where shall I go from your spirit? and where shall I flee from your face? If I ascend into heaven, you are there; and if I descend into hell, you are present. If I take my wings in the morning, and dwell in the uttermost parts of the sea, even there your hand shall lead me, and your right hand shall hold me. And in Solomon: The Spirit of the Lord has filled the whole world (Wis. 1:7). [Vigilius of Thapsus, Against Marivadus the Arian, PL 62] 395-469AD

1:8 Therefore no one who speaks unjust things will go unnoticed, nor will convicting justice pass him by.

RABANUS. For just as the elect receive the gift of knowledge and virtue through the grace of the Holy Spirit, so heretics and all the depraved, puffed up by their own pride and incited by the evil spirit, cannot hide before the Spirit of God the wickedness of their error, which they shamelessly proclaim, and therefore they cannot escape the just punishment of vengeance; for just as the eyes of the Lord are upon the righteous, and His ears are open to their prayers, so the face of the Lord is against those who do evil, to cut off the remembrance of them from the earth. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

1:9 For a searching inquiry will be made into the plans of the ungodly, and the report of his words will come before the Lord to expose his lawless deeds. 1:10 For the ear of jealousy hears all things, and the sound of murmuring does not escape notice.

RABANUS. The outcry of Sodom and Gomorrah has multiplied and come to the Lord, and He descended to see whether they had completed by deed the outcry that had come to Him. Therefore, the Lord rained upon the sinners sulfur and fire from the Lord, and overthrew these cities and the entire surrounding region. Therefore, also the murmuring of the children of Israel went up to the Lord, and behold, the wrath of the Lord was stirred up against the people, and He struck them with a very great plague. For the Lord God is a consuming fire, a jealous God, and He will render to each according to his works. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Wisdom commands its listeners to guard themselves against murmuring, detraction, and lying, for justice is immortal, nor will the wicked grasp it.

1:11 Therefore guard yourselves against useless grumbling, and refrain your tongue from slander; for no secret utterance will go unheard, and a lying mouth destroys the soul.  

RABANUS. How wicked grumbling and detraction are can be proven by many statements of the Holy Scriptures and by examples of the judgments of the Lord; especially since the Lord Himself is often found to have sent vengeance upon those who grumble and complain. And Scripture says that every scoffer is an abomination to the Lord (Prov. 24:9). The Apostle also says: Whisperers and detractors are hateful to God (Rom. 1:30). Therefore, the psalmist also says: Whoever slanders his neighbor in secret, him I will destroy. And elsewhere it is read: Whoever slanders anything binds himself for the future, and just as a serpent bites in silence, so does he who secretly slanders his brother. (Ps. 101:5) Death and life are in the power of the tongue. He who is of a perverse heart finds no good, and he who twists his tongue falls into evil. And it is well said that an obscure answer will not go in vain, because it will be reserved for the judgment of the Lord. Therefore, that saying of the Lord: Every idle word that men shall speak, they shall give account thereof in the day of judgment. (Matt. 12:36) For by your words you shall be justified, and by your words you shall be condemned. and a lying mouth destroys the soul. Therefore, a lying mouth kills the soul of the liar himself, because it pierces it with the sword of sin; therefore, every lie must be most carefully avoided, although sometimes there is a kind of lie that is a lesser fault, if someone lies in order to do a good deed. This is clearly expressed by Father Augustine in his book On Lying, where he mentions that there are eight kinds of lies; anyone who wishes can find out how he discusses this there. But whoever thinks that any kind of lie is not a sin deceives himself disgracefully, while he considers himself to be an honest deceiver of others. Therefore, when examples of lying are presented to us from the Holy Scriptures, either they are not lies, but are thought to be so because they are not understood; or if they are lies, they are not to be imitated, because they cannot be just, but as it is written: God did well to the Hebrew midwives and to Rahab the prostitute of Jericho, not because they lied, but because they were merciful to the people of God. Therefore, it was not their deceit that was rewarded, but their goodwill; the kindness of their mind, not the wickedness of their lying. For just as it would not be strange or absurd if God wished to forgive evil deeds previously committed by them because of their later good deeds, so it is not surprising that, at one time and in one case, God, seeing both aspects of the same act, mercifully rewarded the act as good and, because of this good, forgave the evil in it. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

1:12 Do not seek death in the error of your life, nor draw destruction upon yourselves by the works of your hands; 1:13 for God did not make death, nor does he take pleasure in the destruction of the living.

RABANUS. Above, he has listed those things which are harmful to man, that is, grumbling, detraction, and lying, from which he has commanded us to guard ourselves. Now he repeats the same in other words, namely, that we should not do those things which are subject to death, because our destruction does not please God, who wills all men to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the Truth. He who speaks through the prophet: Why will you die, house of Israel? For I do not will the death of the one who dies, says the Lord God (Ezek. 18:31-32). Turn back and live. For God is the author of our salvation, not of our destruction, who saw all that He had made, and it was very good. But according to the Apostle, the wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life (Rom. 6:23). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

AMBROSIASTER. How then does the same Solomon say, “God did not make death,” when in another place he says, “Good and evil, life and death, poverty and honor are from God”? (Wis. 1:13; Eccles. 11:14) There is no doubt that all good things are from God, but the evils which He has said are only evils, since vengeance is taken upon us. For punishment, although justly inflicted, still seems evil to the one who suffers it. For God also speaks to sinners through the prophet, saying, “I will bring evils upon you,” (Jer. 11:11) that is, “I will subject you to punishments.” But life and death are therefore from God, because He gave the law, which promises life to those who keep it, but death to those who despise, though that which is called the second death. For in judging each one what he deserves, from Him is either absolution unto life or guilt unto death. Therefore, He is not the inventor of death, but the judge, because the author of death is sin. How then could God, who knows not how to sin, make death? But when He repays sins, death is said to be from Him, though it is not from Him, but from the one who sinned. By the same reasoning, poverty and riches are from God. For some, having the fear of God, despise luxury, flee wasteful living, and do not turn away their eyes from the needy and the poor: such persons increase good things. For it is written in Proverbs, “Discipline takes away poverty and dishonor” (Prov. 13:18). But those who do not have the fear of God, living more carelessly and despising the needy, as it is written, “in need He shall deliver”; (Ps. 72:12) are not only negligent and foolish toward themselves, but also despise of the law given them. In this way God made the poor and the rich: because while the violation of the law produces want, He is said to do it as the author of the law; and when some, through fear, keep the discipline of the law, they are made rich by God, the author of the law. [Ambrosiaster, Q&A on the Old and New Testaments, PL 35, 1st cat. Q.34] around 370AD

ANASTASIUS. For our salvation and destruction are indeed in our own power and free will, as it is written: He made man from the beginning, and left him in the hand of his own counsel. (Eccles. 15:14) And: Before men are life and death, and whichever he chooses shall be given to him. (Eccles. 15:18) Therefore the Wise Man also warns: Do not be zealous for death in the error of your life, nor acquire destruction by the works of your hands. For God did not make death, nor does He rejoice in the destruction of the living. (Wis. 1:12-13) But showing that our downfall is chosen by us of our own accord, he says again: But the wicked have called it to themselves by their hands and words. (Wis. 1:16) A witness to this matter, to speak theologically, is he who was called Lucifer because of his brightness, and became darkness because of his pride. [Anastasius of Sanai, PL 89, Questions 27] 620-700AD

1:14 For he created all things that they might exist, and the generations of the world are wholesome, and there is no destructive poison in them, nor has Hades’ dominion on earth.

RABANUS. From this statement, as in the previous one, it is evident that the Lord is the author of good works, not of evil ones; therefore, He is a just judge, who punishes wicked deeds and sins. Nor do we sin out of necessity when we transgress, but by a stubborn will, which proceeds from the quality of free will. We therefore acknowledge God as just and good, the creator of human substance and of both inner and outer senses, so that absolutely everything pertaining to nature is from Him, and absolutely nothing that is against nature is from Him. But sin is against nature, from which death and all things that arise from death originate. Man then clothed himself with these evils when, deprived of faith and obedience, the Devil led him away from the law of God into trust in his own promises, and bound all the offspring of his successors to himself by the condition of the appointed lineage. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

1:15 For righteousness is immortal. 1:16 But the ungodly summoned him with hands and words, considering him a friend; they wasted away and made a covenant with him, because they are worthy to be of his portion.

RABANUS. Therefore, righteousness is rightly said to be perpetual and immortal, because it frees those who obey it from eternal death. Unrighteousness, on the other hand, is the purchase of death, because the torments of Hades are the certain reward of sinners. The immoral have summoned unrighteousness with their hands and words, because they follow it in their deeds and speech, and, deceived by error, confidently believe they can sin with liberty, in no way consenting to be separated from its company, since, already condemned by the just judgment of God, they do not strive to escape it. Therefore the saying from the Gospel: But he who does not believe is already judged, because he has not believed in the name of the only-begotten Son of God (Jn. 3:18). And again: He who believes in the Son of God has eternal life. But he who does not believe in the Son will not see life, but the wrath of God remains upon him (Jn. 3:36). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Chapter 2

Here the wicked, despairing of their salvation, declare that they will perish, and that they will live only in the present age.

2:1 For they said among themselves, reasoning not rightly: our life is short and sorrowful, and there is no healing in the end of a man, and no one has known one who has been released from Hades. 2:2 For we were born by chance, and afterward we shall be as though we had never been; for the breath in our nostrils is smoke, and thought is a spark in the movement of our heart, 2:3 which being extinguished, the body will turn to ashes, and the spirit will be dispersed like thin air. 2:4 And our name will be forgotten in time, and no one will remember our deeds; and our life will pass away like traces of a cloud, and like mist it will be scattered, driven away by the rays of the sun and weighed down by its heat. 2:5 For our life is the passing of a shadow, and there is no turning back of our end, because it is sealed, and no one returns. 2:6 Come, then, let us enjoy the good things that exist and make use of creation eagerly, as in youth. 2:7 Let us fill ourselves with costly wine and perfumes, and let no flower of spring pass us by. 2:8 Let us crown ourselves with rosebuds before they wither. 2:9 Let none of us be without a share in our revelry; everywhere let us leave tokens of joy, for this is our portion and this our lot.

RABANUS. This is the voice of the lost, who, like brute animals, look only at the present and do not think about the future, nor do they have any hope of eternal rest after the end of this life, about whom it is said by the psalmist: Man, when he was in honor, did not understand; he was compared to the senseless beasts, and became like them (Ps. 49:20). The prophet Isaiah rebukes their voice, saying: For you have said: We have made a covenant with death, and with hell we will make an agreement. When the overwhelming scourge passes through, it will not come upon us, for we have made lies our hope, and we are protected by falsehood (Isa. 28:15). Therefore says the Lord: I will set judgment as a measuring line, and righteousness as a plumb line; hail will sweep away the hope of falsehood, and waters will overflow the shelter, and your covenant with death will be annulled, and your agreement with Hades will not stand. When the overwhelming scourge passes through, you will be trampled by it; whenever it passes through, it will take you away (Isa. 28:17–18). To which the prophet Amos added similar words, saying: Woe to you who are wealthy, and separated for the day of evil, and who draw near to the throne of iniquity; who sleep on beds of ivory, and indulge on your couches; who eat lambs from the flock, and calves from the midst of the herd; who sing to the sound of the harp, and, like David, think themselves to have instruments of song. Who drink wine in bowls, and are anointed with the best ointment, and do not grieve over the ruin of Joseph. Therefore now you will go into exile at the head of the exiles, and the revelry of those who indulge will be taken away (Amos 6:4-7). And again: Because you plundered the poor, and took the best spoil from him, you build houses of hewn stone, but you shall not live in them; you plant pleasant vineyards, but you shall not drink their wine: for I know your many crimes, and your mighty sins. (Amos 5:11–12) [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

AMBROSE. [Verse 7] But the luxurious say: Let us fill ourselves with costly wine and perfumes. For they anoint themselves with perfumes, who seek the pleasure of bodily fragrance. Yet those perfumes are accustomed to stir up the allure of lust. There is a different perfume of sobriety, about which the Church says to her spouse: Your name is poured out like perfume (Songs 1:2); and another oil by which the limbs and certain members of the soul are made fat. Therefore, David also says: You have made my head fat with perfume (Ps. 22:5). And this is the oil of gladness, with which Christ was anointed by God the Father, so that He might stand out above all His companions. This He commands to anoint our head, so that all insincere sadness may be covered with the oil of gladness; lest you appear to sell your fasting to men, lest you seem to grieve over the salvation of your soul. For no one who is sad is crowned, no one who is sorrowful triumphs. Therefore anoint your head, where the senses of the wise are; for the eyes of the wise are in his head. You are called to mysteries, and you do not know: you learn, when you come. And remember this: Like ointment on the head, which descends upon the beard of Aaron (Ps. 132:2), then you know what it means: Anoint your head; how you have pleased God, so that He might reveal His mysteries to you, and grant you spiritual grace. [Ambrose of Milan, On Elijah and Fasting, PL 14.10.36] died 397AD

The council of the ungodly Jews against Christ, discussing his death, and accusing him because he reproached them for sins against the law.

2:10 Let us oppress the righteous poor man, let us not spare the widow, nor reverence the gray hair of the aged. 2:11 But let our strength be the law of justice, for what is weak is shown to be useless.

RABANUS. Although these things may generally pertain to those who, despising God, spare neither sex nor age, but through injustice and greed wish to oppress all, nevertheless, in a deeper sense, they can be applied to the treacherous persecutors of Christians and to heretics, who burden the poor people of the Church with their wickedness and persecutions, and that widow of whom it is written: “Blessing, I will bless his widow, I will satisfy his poor with bread,” (Ps. 132:15) they intend to crush with the worst will, and strive to find fault with the prophets and apostles, whom he here calls elders, in their words; by no means do they endeavor to keep the statutes of the divine law, but strive to proceed in the unorthodoxies of their own error, nor do they themselves obey our Head, but presume to utter such words of wickedness against Him. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

2:12 Let us lie in wait for the righteous man, because he is troublesome to us and opposes our deeds, and reproaches us for sins against the law and charges us with sins against our upbringing; 2:13 he professes to have knowledge of God and calls himself a child of the Lord; 2:14 he has become to us a reproof of our thoughts; he is grievous to us even to look at, 2:15 because his life is unlike others, and his ways are different; 2:16 we were counted by him as counterfeit, and he avoids our ways as from impurities; he blesses the final end of the righteous and boasts that God is his father.

RABANUS. The dishonest Jews were then planning to bypass righteousness, when, confronted by the words of the Gospel, they were admonished through Christ to abandon their former filth of vices and to follow the truth of God's commandments. But where they ought to have hoped for the cause of salvation, taking grave offense, they considered killing him as if he were a blasphemer. As it is written in the Gospel: The Jews took up stones to stone him. Jesus answered them: I have shown you many good works from my Father; for which of these do you stone me? The Jews answered him: We do not stone you for a good work, but for blasphemy, and because you, being a man, make yourself God, etc. (Jn. 10:31-33). For, instigated by envy, they did not consider what he was doing, but what they themselves were unwilling to do; in the rays of light they became darkened. Therefore such words suited them, so that they said: He is even a burden to us to look upon, because his life is different from others, and he keeps himself away from our ways as from filth. And because they did not think about the future life, they therefore grieved that the hope of future life was preferred to their own pleasure; and therefore, by wicked counsels, they aimed at his death, as the following words demonstrate. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

2:17 Let us see whether his words are true, and let us test what will happen to him; 2:18 for if the righteous man is a son of God, He will take care of him and deliver him from the hand of his adversaries. 2:19 Let us test him with insult and torment, that we may know his gentleness and prove his patience; 2:20 let us condemn him to a shameful death, for there will be an examination of him according to his words.

RABANUS. They are clearly described in the Gospel, where the chief priests and Pharisees took counsel on how they might deliver Jesus to death, and when he was placed on the cross, the chief priests with the scribes and elders, mocking, said: He saved others; himself he cannot save. If he is the King of Israel, let him come down from the cross, and we will believe him. He trusts in the Lord; let him deliver him now if he will have him; for he said, I am the Son of God (Matt. 27:42-43). He calls the most shameful death the cross, because until the passion of Christ it was the punishment of criminals, which now is the trophy of victory and the glory of believers. For when he says, “for there will be an examination of him according to his words,” this refers to his resurrection; for the unbelieving Jews wanted to test whether he could raise his flesh after death, because they remembered that he had said: Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up (Jn. 2:19). And elsewhere: Behold, he said, we go up to Jerusalem, and all things that are written by the prophets concerning the Son of Man shall be accomplished. For he shall be delivered to the Gentiles, and shall be mocked, and spitefully entreated, and spitted on; and after they have scourged him, they will kill him, and the third day he shall rise again (Luke 18:31-34). Wherefore, even during His Passion, they said to Pilate: Lord, we remember that this deceiver said while still alive: After three days I will rise again. Therefore, command that the tomb be guarded until the third day, lest perhaps His disciples come and steal Him away, and say to the people: He has risen from the dead; and the last error will be worse than the first (Matthew 27:64). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

2:21 These things they reasoned, and they went astray; for their wickedness blinded them, 2:22 and they did not know the mysteries of God, nor hoped for the reward of holiness, nor judged the prize for blameless souls.

RABANUS. Therefore, they were not able to grasp the mystery of God's counsel, envious and deceitful, “for wisdom will not enter a deceitful soul” (Wis. 1:4); nor could they understand that the death of Christ is the honor and salvation of holy souls, because through it they hope for the reward of justice, and receive entrance into the heavenly kingdom. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

2:23 For God created man for incorruption and made him an image of His own proper nature; 2:24 but by the envy of the Devil death entered into the world, 2:25 and those who belong to his side put him to the test.

RABANUS. By this statement, the error of the Manichaeans is refuted, who introduced two natures and substances, that is, good and evil: because our Creator is not the author of our downfall, and as far as the truth of nature is concerned, we were created immortal by Him. However, by assenting to the serpent’s suggestion, we were subjected to the sentence of death; yet the first robe of immortality, which the human race, created in the image of God from the beginning but lost in Adam through the Devil’s envy and seduction, was received again through the death of Christ: for our death was destroyed in His death, and our life was restored in His resurrection, although those who choose to be imitators of the Devil’s envy rather than of Christ’s humility and gentleness do not belong to that destiny. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

EUSEBIUS. What is it to apostatize, if not to depart from God? And indeed, because one is separated from God, one is joined to the Devil. But can anyone who is envious be without a demon, as Scripture says, “but by the envy of the Devil death entered into the world, and those who belong to his side put him to the test, Catholic Scripture likewise testifies. For if the prophet says, “You have been deceived by the spirit of fornication.” [Eusebius of Caesarea, quote found in Glossa Ordinaria, Venice edition] died 339AD

GREGORY THE GREAT. For it is written of him: But by the envy of the Devil, death entered into the world (Wis. 2:24). Because he himself lost heaven, he envied the man who was created, and, ruined, he increased his own damnation by ruining others. The envious must be admonished to recognize the many falls to which the growing ruin subjects them, because, while they do not cast envy out of their hearts, they are led into open wickedness of deeds. For if Cain had not envied his brother’s accepted offering, he would not have come to extinguish his life. Therefore it is written: And the Lord looked upon Abel and his gifts; but to Cain and his gifts He did not look. And Cain was very angry, and his face fell (Gen. 4:4). [Gregory the Great, Pastoral Rule, PL 77.3.10] died 604 AD

Chapter 3

Of the holy martyrs, that their glory through present tribulation will be eternal.

3:1 But the souls of the righteous are in the hand of God, and no torment shall touch them.

RABANUS. In the previous chapter, the opinion of the wicked was expressed, which they put forth against our head with perverse understanding and nefarious words. Now, however, their error is condemned, which they believed occurred in the destruction of his members, when they slaughtered them with various punishments for the confession of his name. But the souls of the righteous are in the hand of God, that is, under his protection, and the torment of eternal death cannot harm them in any way. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

3:2 In the eyes of the foolish they seemed to have died, and their departure was accounted as misery 3:3 and their going from us as destruction; but they are in peace.

RABANUS. They were truly foolish when they considered the suffering and extermination of the martyrs to be a defeat, since these were for them the cause of victory and perpetual peace. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

3:4 For even if in the sight of men they be punished, their hope is full of immortality; 3:5 and having been a little chastened, they shall be greatly benefited, because God tested them and found them worthy of himself;  3:6 as gold in the furnace he proved them, and as a whole burnt offering he accepted them.

RABANUS. Temporal death and the affliction of this present life have brought perpetual prosperity as a reward to the saints of God, because their hope was unfailing and their solid faith endured to the end. For God chastised them with various temptations, and through the endurance of patience proved them worthy of the eternal crown. The two examples he set forth, that is, gold tested in the furnace and holocausts burned in fire, show their most thorough examination and perfect purity, because just as gold is not burned by fire but tested, so the holy substance of the martyrs is not brought to destruction, but prepared for immortality and future glory; and just as the holocaust, which is wholly offered to God through fire, is a most acceptable sacrifice, so also the passion of the martyrs, offered to the Lord in the fragrance of sweetness through the heat of faith and the fervor of love, stands as a most pleasing gift and a most precious offering to Him. Therefore it is said by the Psalmist: Precious in the sight of the Lord is the death of His saints. (Ps. 116:15) And what is added: as gold in the furnace he proved them, [Verse 6] signifies that the death of the saints is not perpetual according to the estimation of the reprobate; but rather, on the day of judgment, when Christ judges, their reward will be most abundant. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

VIGILIUS. [Verse 6] On common testing. The Father tests, the Son tests, the Holy Spirit tests. Of the Father, to the Thessalonians: So we speak, not as pleasing men, but God, who tests our hearts (I Thess. 2:4). Of the Son, in Solomon: As gold in the furnace he tested them, and found them worthy of himself (Wis. 3:6). Of the Holy Spirit, in Solomon: The Spirit of the Lord tests hearts (Prov. 17:3): but he will destroy the wicked from the earth (Prov. 2:22). [Vigilius of Thapsus, Against Marivadus the Arian, PL 62] 5th century

3:7 And in the time of their visitation they shall shine forth, and run to and fro like sparks among stubble;

RABANUS. By presenting another example, he demonstrates that at the final judgment there will be a sudden reward for the saints, and a swift punishment for the depraved. For when the elect are changed from corruption to incorruption, from mortality to immortality, from disgrace to glory, then they will rise to judge their adversaries, and just as a spark of fire suddenly kindles a flame in a field of reeds, so the sentence of the just Judge of the saints brings strong and swift vengeance of punishments upon the reprobate. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

GREGORY THE GREAT. Again, through the pen or reed, the pursuit of temporal glory is expressed, as it is said of the just through Wisdom: The just will shine, and run to and fro like sparks among stubble (Wis. 3:7). For indeed, he calls the life of worldly people stubble, who, like stubble, advance outwardly through temporal glory as if to lofty heights, but inwardly become empty of the solidity of truth. Therefore, the kingdom of the Jews is also compared to stubble, when, through the prophet, it is said of the Lord appearing in the truth of the flesh: A bruised reed he will not break, and a smoking flax he will not quench (Isa. 42:3). [Gregory the Great, Morals on Job, PL 75-76. Sixth and last part.4.7] died 604 AD

3:8 they shall judge nations and rule over peoples, and the Lord shall reign over them forever.

RABANUS. The saints will judge the nations, but through them God judges, who presides over their minds. They will rule over peoples, for no adversity of opposition resists them. Their Lord will reign forever, because the kingdom of Christ will endure without any end. Concerning this, the angel said to Mary: The Lord God will give him the throne of David his father, and he will reign over the house of Jacob forever, and of his kingdom there will be no end (Luke 133). Therefore, it is also said through Isaiah: His dominion will be multiplied, and there will be no end of peace (Isa. 9:7). The saints will also reign with their king, that is, the members with their head, as the words of the angel testify to Daniel in prophetic vision, saying: But the kingdom and power and greatness of the kingdom under all heaven will be given to the people of the saints of the Most High, whose kingdom is everlasting, and all kings will serve him and obey him. (Dan. 7:27) [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

3:9 Those who trust in him shall understand truth, and the faithful shall abide with him in love, because grace and mercy are with his holy ones, and visitation with his chosen ones.

RABANUS. It is clear that whoever perfectly trusts in the Lord in this present life, according to the distribution of the Holy Spirit, perceives here an understanding of the truth in a manner appropriate to himself; because now we see through a glass, darkly, but then face to face; now we know in part, but then we shall know even as we are known. But now remain faith, hope, love, these three; but the greatest of these is love. (1 Cor. 13:12-13) Because when the others pass away, that alone remains, therefore it is said: The faithful shall abide with him in love, for the elect of God, after the end of the world, delighting in the good which they formerly sought with faith and hope, will in no way be able to be separated from His company, since, rewarded with the gift of eternal satisfaction, they will enjoy perpetual peace. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

The wicked will receive fitting punishments according to what they have done, because they have turned away from the Lord.

3:10 But the ungodly shall have punishment as they devised, those who neglected the righteous and departed from the Lord.

RABANUS. The sentence of the universal judgment will be completed when the saints, united with their King, joyfully enter the kingdom of their heavenly homeland; then the wicked and sinners who were negligent regarding their salvation, according to the wickedness of their hearts and actions, will be plunged with the Devil into the abyss of Gehenna, where they will be tormented without end, for their worm does not die, and the fire is not extinguished. (Mark 9:48) [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

3:11 For whoever despises wisdom and discipline is miserable, and their hope is vain, and their labors unprofitable, and their works useless;

RABANUS. Now he shows what this is, which he previously said had departed from the Lord, that is, to cast away wisdom and discipline, of which hope is empty, and labors are without fruit, because they vainly hope that they can use temporal things perpetually, or that by committing sins they can escape unpunished. The labors of these people are without the fruit of future reward, and their works are rundown: for the workers themselves cannot make anyone a participant in the heavenly dwelling. Against this, when the Psalmist foretold the crimes of the chief of all the wicked by reproaching him, he said: Therefore God will destroy you forever, He will pluck you out and remove you from your tent, and uproot your root from the land of the living (Ps. 52:5). Heretics also cannot inappropriately be understood in this statement, who, rejecting divine wisdom and the rule of right faith, spend the labors of their doctrine and the sects of their errors in vain and without the fruit of eternal life, whose intention is foolish and whose works are most wicked. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

3:12 Their women are senseless, and their children are most wicked.

RABANUS. He calls women either carnal desires or interpretations of wicked sense: from which the most wicked children, that is, nefarious acts or perverse doctrines, proceed, who are liable not to the reward of the heavenly kingdom, but to the torment of Hades. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

3:13 Their cursed creature, for the barren woman is happy; and the undefiled one, who has not known the bed in sin, will have fruit in the consideration of holy souls;

RABANUS. The cursed creature of those, that is, their creation. For divine wisdom does not impose the mark of a curse on the creatures of God, whom their Creator blessed at the beginning when He created them, but shows that the work of the depraved is worthy of eternal curse. for the barren woman is happy; and the undefiled one, who has not known the bed in sin, will have fruit in the consideration of holy souls. He praises the barren, that is, the chaste and disciplined soul, which is not defiled by the filth of heretics, nor contaminated by the impurities of idolatry; for this soul will have fruit in the regard of holy souls, indeed on the day of judgment, when the saints will be taken up to contemplate the glory of God, then she too will receive the reward of her labor in heavenly blessedness. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

3:14 and the eunuch who has not worked iniquity with his hand, nor thought wicked things against the Lord, for a chosen grace of faith shall be given to him and a portion in the temple of the Lord more pleasing.

RABANUS. According to the rule of the figurative interpretation, in the name of this blessed barren one, or eunuch, virgins can be understood, about whom it is written accordingly in Isaiah: Let not the eunuch say, “Behold, I am a dry tree,” for thus says the Lord to the eunuchs: “Who have kept my sabbaths, and have chosen what I desired, and have held fast my covenant, I will give them in my house and within my walls a place and a name better than sons and daughters; I will give them an everlasting name that shall not perish” (Isa. 56:3-5). This barren one is fruitful in virginity. This eunuch forces his way into the kingdom of heaven and aggressively seizes it. He is the keeper of the sabbaths, and will never perform the works of marriage. He has chosen what the Lord wished, so as to offer more than is commanded, so that he considers not the apostle’s indulgence but the Lord’s will. He holds fast the Lord’s eternal covenant, so that he does not devote himself to prayer only for a time and then return again to it, but knows that he will receive in the house of the Lord, which is His Church, the best place: for there are many dwelling places with the Father. (Jn. 14:2) And whoever is a eunuch and does all that is written, will have the best place within its walls, so that he may indeed become a tower of the Lord, and stand in the priestly rank, so that for sons of the flesh he may have many spiritual sons. Such a eunuch, whom Jesus loved most dearly, was the evangelist John, as ecclesiastical histories relate; he who reclined on the breast of Jesus (Jn. 13:23); who, while Peter walked more slowly, ran to the Lord, lifted on the wings of virginity. Therefore, let all the mockery of the Jews be put away, and let them cease to make themselves a laughingstock by claiming that the kingdoms of heaven are closed to eunuchs, since it is by chastity, not by bodily weakness, but by the will of the spirit; nor will sloth have the merit of a good reward, but virtuous striving. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

3:15 For the fruit of good labors is glorious, and the root of understanding does not fail.

RABANUS. The fruit of good labors is indeed glorious; for eternal joy and certain rest follow them, nor do those seeds of doctrines or actions which have proceeded from the root of true wisdom ever fail, but they will bring forth the fruit of everlasting salvation. But on the contrary, the dogmas of heretics and the disciples and followers of their wickedness will be exterminated; nor will they have hope of salvation, but condemned to a bitter death, they will be given to perpetual oblivion. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

That the sons of adulterers, that is, heretics, will be in the end, and the generation of the chaste will remain in perpetual brightness.

3:16 But the children of adulterers shall be without perfection, and the seed of an unlawful bed shall be destroyed. 3:17 For even if they live long, they shall be counted as nothing, and their old age shall be dishonorable at last; 3:18 and if they die quickly, they shall have no hope, nor comfort in the day of reckoning; 3:19 for the end of an unrighteous generation is grievous.

RABANUS. Indeed, heretics are adulterers, who, adulterating the word of God, attempt to transfer it into another meaning. Their children, that is, their disciples, will be at the end, because they tend toward eternal perdition. These, having been brought up from a wicked bed, nourished by the poisonous nourishment of a polluted conscience, even if they are long-lived upon the earth, yet this will profit them nothing, when they are to be destined for the burning of eternal fire. and their old age shall be dishonorable, because their memory will forever lack blessing, nor on the day of recognition, namely at the hour of the universal judgment, when the elect, recognized by their judge, will be invited to the possession of the heavenly kingdom, will they be consoled by any hope of their restoration, but will be expelled to be tormented forever from the presence of the human king. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Chapter 4

4:1 Better than childlessness with virtue; for immortality is in her memory, because she is known both by God and by men; 4:2 and when present, they imitate her, and when gone, they long for her; and in the age she, crowned, proceeds in triumph, having conquered the contest of undefiled rewards.

RABANUS. Just as he previously criticized the lead heretics and their schismatic followers, so now he praises the Catholics and their disciples, whose faith is pure, and whose doctrine is simple and eternal; therefore, they are worthy of remembrance, because their generation is approved by the Lord and honorably accepted among men. In this present life, this produces fruit through sacred examples of good works, and when transferred to the heavenly kingdom, it leaves a worthy remembrance of itself to generations to come, nor will there be any sorrow for the completed struggle, when the greatness of persecutions is surpassed by the reward. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:3 But a numerous offspring of the ungodly will be of no use, and from bastard shoots it will not strike root deep, nor establish a secure foundation;

RABANUS. For he alternates, speaking of Catholics and heretics, so that he may show the praiseworthy wisdom of the former and demonstrate the blameworthy cunning of the latter. Indeed, the diverse and various doctrine of the heretics will not be useful, but harmful; nor do their redundant arguments, which indicate spurious offspring, have the root of truth, therefore they do not have the foundation of strength, but when touched by the wind of persecutions or the test of strict judgment, they quickly fall from their firmness. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:4 for even if for a time it sprout in the branches, standing insecurely it will be shaken by the wind and uprooted by the violence of winds. 4:5 Its branches will be broken off before their time, and their fruit useless, unripe for eating and fit for nothing;

RABANUS. He names branches as the sayings of perverse scriptures, and the fruits as the meanings of those sayings; by winds, he also signifies either the troubles of persecutions or the debates of judgment: because just as fragile branches are broken by the wind, so their imperfect sayings, when struck by any hardship or debate, are easily broken, and their fruits are useless and bitter, because the meanings of their words are depraved and poisonous, and therefore suited to no usefulness. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:6 for children born from unlawful unions are witnesses of wickedness against their parents at their examination.

RABANUS. He calls the sons of the wicked either disciples or works, because just as the followers of heretics are, on the day of judgment, an increase of sorrow for those who have been led astray by false doctrines, so evil works, at the time of the final examination, are proof of their wickedness, for which reason they are deservedly condemned. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

That the souls of the righteous after death will be in refreshment, and their passing should not be mourned.

4:7 But the righteous, if he is overtaken by death, will be at rest;

RABANUS. Having previously spoken about the destruction of the wicked, he now afterward explains the rest of the good, because after the end of the present life, which is common to both good and bad, the just will then begin to live better, when they seem to be utterly destroyed by the wicked, since eternal rest will then be given to them, when perpetual punishment follows for these. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:8 for honorable old age is not the length of time, nor is it measured by number of years; 4:9 but gray hair is wisdom for men, and the age of old age is a blameless life.

RABANUS. He shows what should be sought in old age, because it is not the age of the body, but the maturity of the mind and the uprightness of character that are praised in it. Therefore, in the Book of Numbers, the Lord commanded Moses to choose seventy elders, whom He Himself knew to be elders, so that He might appoint them as teachers and judges of the people. (Num. 11:16-17) And Paul, writing to Timothy, says: Let no one despise your youth; but be an example to the faithful in word, in conduct, in love, in faith, in purity. (1 Tim. 4:12) [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:10 Having become pleasing to God, he was loved and, while living among sinners, was taken away; 4:11 he was snatched away, lest evil change his understanding or deceit deceive his soul;

RABANUS. God does not therefore take a man whom He loves out of this world because He cannot keep him, living among sinners, free from sin; He who, sending His disciples as lambs among wolves, praised them to the Father, saying: I do not pray that You take them out of the world, but that You keep them from evil (Jn. 17:15); but rather, so that He may observe the order of His judgment: those whom He foreknew and predestined to be conformed to the image of His Son, He calls, justifies, magnifies, and glorifies; but the rest, on account of their iniquity, He condemns by His just judgment. Concerning this, the Apostle Peter says: God knows how to deliver the godly out of temptation, but to reserve the unjust under punishment for the day of judgment (2 Peter 2:9). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

ANASTASIUS. But it also benefits many of the righteous that they live for only a short time, as Solomon once said: He who was pleasing to God was made beloved, and living among sinners, he was taken away; he was snatched away so that wickedness might not alter his understanding, or deceit might not beguile his soul. For the fascination of frivolity obscures good things, and the instability of desire perverts the mind without malice. Perfected in a short time, he fulfilled many years; for his soul was pleasing to God, and therefore He hastened to remove him from the midst of iniquity. (Wis. 4:10-14) [Anastasius of Sanai, PL 89, Questions 18] 620-700AD

4:12 for the bewitching power of wickedness obscures what is good, and the wandering of desire corrupts an innocent mind.

RABANUS. By this statement, although generally the immorality of the wicked is denoted, more clearly, however, the cunning of heretics is reproved in it, which, by depraved doctrines and harmful desire, turns the mind of man away from the path of truth; for it fascinates, that is, it burdens, whoever hinders by flattering or deceives by praising. We read in the Apostle: You were running well; who has fascinated you? (Gal. 3:1). And in Proverbs: The gift of the envious torments the eyes. (Sir. 18:18) He who is envious among us is more significantly called a fascinator among the Greeks. In these examples, we are taught that either the envious person is tormented by another’s happiness, or he in whom there are some good things is harmed by another who fascinates or envies. For the envy of heretics, which is accustomed to offer mere plays, not the foundation of truth, often obscures the good things of mortality in the simple, as in that saying: Evil communications corrupt good manners (1 Cor. 15:33). Therefore Wisdom forbids having fellowship with such people, saying in Proverbs: Do not eat with an envious man, nor desire his foods, for he estimates what he does not know as the likeness of a diviner and a soothsayer. Eat and drink, he will say to you, but his heart is not with you. The foods which you have eaten you will vomit up, and you will lose your beautiful words (Prov. 23:6-8). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:13 Having been made perfect in a short time, he fulfilled many years, 4:14 for his soul was pleasing to the Lord; therefore he rushed away from the midst of wickedness. But the peoples, seeing and not understanding, nor laying such a thing to heart,

RABANUS. Just as in the previous verse he exposed the deceit of the heretics, so now in the following he expresses the glory of the martyrs, who, although they spent their admirable lives in a short time through the tortures of their persecutors, nevertheless that very death is reckoned to them as a long life, because it brought them eternal life. Indeed, their Creator, loving them, separated them from the company of the wicked, and led them out from the oppression of this present life, so that in the heavenly kingdom he might hide them in the secret of his face, from the disturbance of men, and protect them in his tabernacle from the contradiction of tongues. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:15 that grace and mercy are with his elect and visitation with his holy ones.

RABANUS. Therefore, the persecutors of the martyrs could by no means recognize the future glory that followed them, but thought that they would perish together with the torments of their bodies, and that they could not receive the blessedness of eternal life after death; but everything happens differently for each side, because just as life and victory will follow the good after death, so also death and perpetual damnation will follow the wicked after temporal life. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:16 But the righteous who has died will condemn the living ungodly, and youth brought quickly to completion the long old age of an unrighteous man.

RABANUS. It is clear indeed that the martyrs of Christ will be judges of their persecutors after death, just as in the Apocalypse, in the condemnation of Babylon, the divine voice shows, saying: Come out of her, my people, so that you do not partake in her sins, and so that you do not receive of her plagues, for her sins have reached up to heaven, and the Lord has remembered her iniquities. Render to her as she herself rendered, and double the double according to her works. In the cup which she mixed, mix for her double; as much as she glorified herself and lived in luxury, give her that much torment and grief, because in her heart she says: I sit as a queen, and am no widow, and will never see mourning. Therefore in one day her plagues will come, death and mourning and famine, and she will be burned with fire (Rev. 18:4-8). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:17 For they will see the end of the wise man and will not understand what the Lord purposed concerning him and to what end the Lord had safeguarded him. 4:18 They will see and scorn, but the Lord will laugh them to scorn 4:19 and after this they will become an object of dishonor and a reproach among the dead forever, because he will dash them down speechless, face down, and shake them from their foundations, and they will be utterly laid waste and be in pain, and their memory will perish.

RABANUS. For the persecutors of the Church of Christ, as we have said, believed that the end of the martyrs was destruction, and therefore they despised the steadfastness of their faith. But the Lord will mock them, when He shows them that what they hoped would benefit them has completely harmed them; for when they wished to defend the false deities of their gods against the followers of Christ, they delivered themselves into perpetual damnation. [Verse 19] For the Lord will overthrow them, swollen with pride, without a word of excuse, so that they will then be in all things inexcusable, because although they knew God, they did not glorify Him as God or give thanks; but they became fruitless in their thoughts, and their foolish heart was darkened. Claiming to be wise, they became fools; and they exchanged the glory of the incorruptible God for the likeness of an image of corruptible man, and of birds and serpents and four-footed animals. Therefore God gave them up to shameful passions, and to a debased mind, to do things that are not fitting, filled with all unrighteousness, wickedness, fornication, greed, malice, full of envy, murder, strife, deceit, malevolence, whisperers, slanderers, hateful to God, rude, proud, arrogant, inventors of evil, disobedient to parents, senseless, untrustworthy, without affection, without covenant, without mercy; who, although they knew the justice of God, did not understand that those who do such things are worthy of death (Rom. 1:22-32); and He will shake them from their foundations, because He will utterly overthrow the state of their life, and they will be desolate even to the utmost; for never after this will they be relieved by any comfort, but they will be groaning in torments, and their memory will perish forever. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

4:20 They will come in the reckoning of their sins as cowards, and their lawless deeds will convict them to their face.

RABANUS. Therefore, the wicked will come into judgment, timid because of the conscience of their sins, for their thoughts accuse them for the evil deeds they have previously committed, nor is any place given for defense, where there is no fruit of good works in the conscience. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Chapter 5

That at the coming of the Lord the righteous will stand with great devotion against their persecutors, and that these persecutors will finally experience late repentance in Hades for their error and sins.

5:1 THEN the righteous man shall stand with great confidence before the face of those who afflicted him and those who set at nought his labors. 5:2 seeing this, they shall be troubled with terrible fear and shall be amazed at the unexpectedness of salvation. 5:3 They shall speak within themselves, repenting, and groaning for anguish of spirit they shall say: 5:4 This was he whom we once held in contempt and as a good example of reproach, the foolish ones; we counted his life madness and his end without honor. 5:5 How has he been reckoned among the sons of God and his lot among the saints?

RABANUS. Then indeed the righteous will stand with great determination against their persecutors, who afflicted them here with undeserved punishments and reproached their labors as foolish; for, being raised by heavenly brightness, they will judge according to truth those who had previously condemned them unjustly. Therefore, when these persecutors see themselves suddenly changed from glory to humiliation, and equally see their adversaries transferred from temporal affliction to heavenly honor, they will then seek with late and fruitless repentance what they could previously have obtained fruitfully, if they had wished, while it was permitted. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

5:6 Therefore have we strayed from the way of truth, and the light of righteousness did not shine for us, and the sun did not rise for us;

RABANUS. The way of truth is the Gospel of Christ, and the divine law, from which the sinful have strayed, because they were unwilling to believe and obey. For them, the light of righteousness did not shine, and the sun of understanding did not rise, because for them Christ is not the true light, which enlightens every man coming into this world (Jn. 1:9), and the sun of righteousness who rises for those who fear Him. (Mal. 4:2) In whose punishments, too, there is true healing, since in His protection the abundance of eternal life is assured. He did not shine for the faithless philosophers of the Gentiles, nor for the heretics, because, by despising the path of truth, they preferred to follow vain traditions and wicked sects. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

5:7 we were filled with paths of lawlessness and destruction, and we crossed roadless deserts, but the way of the Lord we did not know.

RABANUS. The philosophers were left on the path of evil and destruction, because by reasoning they wished to elevate the machinery of the world to divine honor, and chose rather to serve the creature than the Creator: in their very inquiry they failed, and found not salvation but destruction. Similarly, the heretics, by walking difficult paths in pride, composed wicked sects, nor did they recognize the way of the Lord, which is truth and life; therefore, they delivered themselves to perpetual destruction, nor did their pursuit of the present life bring them any benefit, for after death not reward but punishment awaited them. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

5:8 What profit has our pride brought us? And what wealth with boasting has accumulated to us? 5:9 All those things have passed away like a shadow and like a fleeting report; 5:10 like a ship passing through the turbulent water, of which, once it has passed, there is no trace to be found, nor path of its capsize in the waves; 5:11 or like a bird flying through the air, no evidence of its route is found, but the light breeze, struck by the beating of wings and cut through by the force of rushing sound, passes along with the moving wings, and afterward no sign of its passage is found in it; 5:12 or like an arrow shot at a target, the air cut straightway reverts into itself so as to ignore its passage. 5:13 Therefore we also, born, came to an end, and we had no sign of virtue to show, but in our wickedness we were consumed.

RABANUS. Through the five types of likenesses, which, by presenting, he laments that the fruitless repentance of the wicked causes their efforts to flow away, the fleeting nature of life and the deficiency of earthly things are shown to us. For just as a shadow has no firmness in its state, nor does a messenger running about endure delay, and just as a bird in the sky, or a ship in the sea, or an arrow in the air, proceed on an uncertain path, so too is the course of brief life always unstable and uncertain, nor does anything from it remain fixed, except what is expended with virtuous effort for the attainment of eternal life. According to the allegory, however, the shadow can be understood as forgetfulness of the mind or death of the flesh, to which human life is subject. In the messenger running, we can see the passions of the soul or illnesses of the body, which indicate and proclaim the end of corruptible life. By the bird, however, is signified the arrogance of worldly boasting, or heresy. But by the ship is signified the lightness of the human mind, which is tossed by the bitter waves of this age and the blasts of unclean spirits. By the arrow are signified the deceits and snares of the wicked. For all these things are involved in the tribulations of the present life, but after the end is accomplished, they leave fruitless repentance to their possessors in the underworld, for whom they will always groan and will be uplifted by no consolation. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

5:14 For the hope of the ungodly is like chaff carried by the wind and like thin frost driven away by a storm, and like smoke dispersed by the wind, and like the memory of a guest of one day it passed by. 5:15 But the righteous live forever, and their reward is in the Lord, and their care is with the Most High.

RABANUS. He has further introduced four other examples to demonstrate the weakness of the hope of the wicked, by which they either believed that present things were stable and would endure, or thought that they could live in sin and escape with impunity. For the fuzz that arises from the withering flower of herbs expresses the frailty of the flesh, which is taken away by the wind of sickness or bodily distress, nor is it permitted to remain long, because all flesh is grass, and all its glory like the flower of grass. The grass has withered, and its flower has fallen; but the word of the Lord endures forever (Isa. 40:8). But the foam which is produced from the waters and floats on the waters can be understood as the desire for human favor and common boasting: which is scattered by the storm of temptations or the scrutiny of future judgment, because their vanity is proven by such contact. Likewise, smoke demonstrates the vanity of a fleeting life; for just as smoke is dispersed by the wind, so human life is dissolved by the change of times. Therefore, by analogy, it is compared to a passing guest; for we are all guests and strangers here, of which Ecclesiastes says: One generation passes away, and another generation comes, but the earth stands forever; therefore, all is vanity (Eccles. 1:4). For what is more vain than this vanity, that the earth remains, which was made for the sake of men, and man himself, the master of the earth, is suddenly dissolved into dust? Therefore it follows: The sun rises and sets, and returns to its place (Eccles. 1:5). For the sun itself, which is given to mortals for light, daily indicates the destruction of the world by its rising and setting; and all this is to teach that by the changes of times and the rising and setting of the stars, human life slips away and perishes while it is unaware. Therefore, it is just that the wicked, placed in Hades, grieve that they put their hope in that which has no stability, and turned their minds away from Him who, before all things and after all things, remains the same now and forever. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Of the holy martyrs, that they live forever, and that the Lord avenges them against their adversaries.

5:16 Therefore they shall receive the kingdom of majesty and the crown of beauty from the hand of the Lord, because with his right hand he shall shelter them and with his arm he shall defend them. 5:17 He shall take zeal as his armor and arm creation for the defense of enemies;

RABANUS. But when he described the punishments of sinners and their late repentance in Hades, he consequently recounted the glory and reward of the just with the Lord in heaven, who, having been received into the heavenly kingdom, enjoy a blessed life in the contemplation of God; and this is because they placed all their hope and the intention of their mind in the goodness of their Creator, by whose hand they are also crowned and protected by the right hand of His strength. For the hand of God, and the right hand, and the arm, because of their effective power, signify the only-begotten Son of God: through whom all things were made, in whom we have redemption through His blood, the forgiveness of sins, whom the Father raised from the dead and set at His right hand in the heavenly places, above every principality and power and virtue and dominion, and every name that is named, not only in this age but also in the one to come, and He subjected all things under His feet and gave Him as head over all things to the Church, which is His body and the fullness of Him who fills all in all. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

5:18 he shall put on righteousness as a breastplate and place impartial judgment as a helmet; 5:19 he shall take holiness as an invincible shield, 5:20 and he shall sharpen stern wrath into a sword, and creation shall fight with him against the foolish. 5:21 Well-aimed bolts of lightning shall go forth, and from the well-arched bow of the clouds they shall fly to the target,

RABANUS. According to history, therefore, the Creator of all, whose weapons are truth, justice, and judgment, through the creature subject to Him, rebukes and strikes those who do wrong. According to the allegory, however, the same Lord arms the creature for the vengeance of His enemies, when through the preachers of the Gospel He reproves the treachery of the Jews, the foolishness of the Gentiles, and the deceit of heretics, concerning sin and righteousness, and judgment, and pierces their hearts with the darts of words, so that, terrified by wholesome fear, they may yield to the hand of Almighty God and learn to be subject to Him, whose admonition they ought by no means to resist, since they are in no way able to withstand His power. But when he says that the whole world fights with Him against the senseless, he shows that the entire Church of Christ, together with its King, that is, the members with the head, fights against the errors and wickedness of the unjust, through the weapons of truth and justice. And which, founded by the authority of the Lord upon the most solid rock, in no way fears ruin: Because the gates of hell shall not prevail against it (Matt. 16:18); but fixed and unshaken, it remains through all ages. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

GREGORY THE GREAT. In all these things, it is clearly seen what a certain wise man testifies about the Lord, saying: and creation shall fight with him against the foolish (Wis. 5:20). Indeed, the world fights with the Lord against the senseless when, in the punishment of sinners, the adversity of the elements takes part. Yet pain does not come forth from the earth, because every insensible thing is stirred to our affliction by the impulse of our own actions. [Gregory the Great, Morals on Job, PL 75-76. 2.14] died 604 AD

5:22 and hailstones full of wrath shall be hurled from a stone-throwing catapult. The waters of the sea shall rage against them, and rivers shall overwhelm them violently.

RABANUS. Lightning emissions are the thunder of proclamations, or the flashes of miracles, which will go directly: because, according to the privilege of their author, they occur for the benefit of the elect or for the damnation of the reprobate. They will be discharged like from a well-bent bow of clouds, when, by the authority of the divine Scriptures, through the virtuous intention of the holy doctors, they are brought forth to terrify adversaries; and they will leap to a certain place, because they proceed in a straight path against those whom it is necessary, nor can it be confused, what divine wisdom is known to be ordered. And hailstones are sent from the stony wrath: because, according to the equality of merits, harsh rebukes are issued. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

5:23 A spirit of power shall stand against them, and like a whirlwind shall winnow them. And lawlessness shall lay waste all the earth, and wrongdoing shall overturn the thrones of rulers.

RABANUS. And the water of the sea will burn upon them, and the rivers will rush together violently. He calls the disturbances of the sea the troubles of the world, the rivers the agitations of persecutions; because often the hearts of the wicked, which the teachings of preaching do not soften, are broken by the scourges of various corrections through fear. The spirit of virtue is the vengeance of the final judgment, which will separate the wicked and ultimately bring all the land to the end of their iniquity, when it will imitate earthly substance and carnal works; by its sentence, the just judge will assign punishment in Gehenna and eternal desolation. For wickedness will overthrow the seats of the mighty, because the evil of the reprobate is the cause of their damnation. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

VIGILIUS. The Lord taught in the Gospel that the Holy Spirit is judge, and is able to convict the whole world concerning sin, righteousness, and judgment, saying: But when the Holy Spirit has come, he will convict the world concerning sin, and concerning righteousness, and concerning judgment (Jn. 16:8). And Isaiah: The Lord, he says, will wash away the filth of the sons and daughters of Zion by the spirit of judgment (Isa. 4:4). And in Solomon: The Spirit of the Lord will stand against them, and like a whirlwind of wind will divide them (Wis. 5:23). Likewise Isaiah: Behold, I make judgment in my spirit, says the Lord (Isa. 26:9). [Vigilius of Thapsus, Against Marivadus the Arian, PL 62] 5th century

Chapter 6

6:1 Hear therefore, O kings, and understand; learn, judges of the ends of the earth.

RABANUS. Wisdom, therefore, wherever it dwells, always governs the mind well. For the powers of the body, being mostly foolish, often cast themselves into the abyss of sins: therefore the wise is preferred to the strong, because wisdom directs the steps of man; but bodily strength, although sometimes, when it has overcome someone through power, may imitate strength, yet without the guidance of carefulness it will appear weak, when it is exhausted by foolish labor. Therefore it is written in the psalm: He will not take pleasure in the strength of the horse; nor is there delight in the legs of a man. The Lord’s delight is in those who fear Him, and in those who hope in His mercy (Ps. 147:10–11). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Wisdom rebukes the judges of the earth, who have received authority from the Lord, because they did not keep the law of righteousness, foretelling to them the punishments which they will suffer.

6:2 Give ear, you who rule a multitude and boast over crowds of nations; 6:3 for authority has been given to you from the Lord, and sovereignty from the Most High, who will examine your works and inquire into your plans. 6:4 Because, as servants of His kingdom, you did not judge rightly, nor keep the law, nor walk according to the counsel of God. 6:5 Terribly and swiftly He will come upon you, because a severe judgment comes upon those in high place. 6:6 For the lowliest may be pardoned in mercy, but the mighty will be mightily tested;  

RABANUS. They admonish the rulers of the Church to keep the justice of God's commandments, and to hurry to learn and understand the truth of the divine testimonies: for just as they themselves, having been appointed by the Lord with given authority, are made teachers and judges of their brethren so that they may judge them according to the principle of impartiality, and teach them to keep the rule of faith with good works, so they themselves also await divine judgment, which neither deceives nor can be deceived, who judges not only their deeds but also their thoughts and wills, and will judge with strict examination those who did not live according to the will of God and the law of the commandments, nor even taught or judged others. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

VIGILIUS. Of the common kingdom. The Father reigns, the Son reigns, the Holy Spirit reigns. Of the Father in the Gospel: Unless a man be born again of water and the Holy Spirit, he cannot see the kingdom of God (Jn. 3:5). Of the Son in the ninety-fifth psalm (Verse 10): Say among the nations, the Lord has reigned from the tree. Of the Holy Spirit in Solomon: Power has been given to you by the Spirit of the Lord (Wis. 6:3). And below: And when you were ministers of that kingdom, you judged not rightly (verse 5). [Vigilius of Thapsus, Against Marivadus the Arian, PL 62] 5th century

GREGORY THE GREAT. [Verse 6] In Holy Scripture, when the sun is presented figuratively, sometimes the Lord is signified by it, as is stated in the book of Wisdom, where it is said that all the wicked, upon recognizing their own condemnation at the final judgment, will say: We have strayed from the path of truth, and the light of justice did not shine for us; and the sun did not rise for us (Wis. 5:6). As if they were openly saying: The ray of inner light did not shine upon us. Therefore, John also says: A woman clothed with the sun, and the moon under her feet. For in the sun is understood the illumination of truth, but in the moon, which wanes with its monthly cycles, is understood the mutability of temporality. [Gregory the Great, Exposition of the New Testament, 24.38] died 604 AD

ANASTASIUS. Through this, God teaches us that from those who are perfect in virtue, God demands an exact account of life, and towards other men, who commit great crimes, He is lenient and merciful; He does not grant this pardon to the saints. A certain Wise Man also said this: "For mercy is indeed to be granted to the least; but the mighty will be examined mightily." The Lord: "For to whom, He says, little has been given, little will be required from him; but to whom much, much will also be required from him." [Anastasius of Sanai, PL 89, Questions 37] 620-700AD

6:7 for the Lord of all will not show partiality, nor will He stand in awe of greatness, because He made the small and the great alike and cares for all equally;

RABANUS. To those who, through ignorance or weakness or even necessity, have committed certain crimes, and yet have not ceased through humility and repentance to erase those sins, the most merciful Judge grants the grace of forgiveness. But for those who, through stubbornness and contempt for the commandments of God, have committed many and grave crimes, and have neglected to repent of them, more severe torments are assigned in Hades: for just as they were more powerful in the act of wickedness, so they endure stronger punishments in the fire of Gehenna. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Therefore, after wisdom has spoken to the powerful and rebuked the hardness of their hearts with words, it now turns the speech of exhortation to the rulers themselves, and wonderfully explains the praise of wisdom. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Therefore, rulers should consider how fitting it is that they themselves be constant in meditation on the divine law. Chapter 7

7:1 I too am a mortal man, equal to all, and a descendant of the earthborn first-formed man; and in my mother’s womb I was molded into flesh, ten months old, solid in blood from the seed of a man and the pleasure of intercourse. 7:3 and when I was born I drew in the common air and fell upon the earth, akin to all, crying with the same first voice as all. 7:4 I was nursed in swaddling clothes and with cares; 7:5 for no king had any other beginning of birth, 7:6 but one entry into life for all, and an equal departure.

RABANUS. Therefore, wisdom displays its virtue through a vessel suited to itself; first, it makes its own preacher humbly recount the origin of human condition, so that everyone may know what they possess from the origin of the flesh and what from divine gift. For when he says that he was formed in blood and the seed of man during a period of ten months, and by the agreeable pleasure of sleep, he demonstrates the difference of the two sexes. For, as the natural philosophers say, the seed of man is white, and the seed of woman is bloody. Their mixture, within certain periods of time, causes the body of the infant to be formed, until it becomes animated and reaches the time of birth in the tenth month, although not all reach the tenth month, since some are born in the seventh, others in the eighth, others in the ninth; but they assert that the birth which occurs in the tenth month is more perfect, more vital, and healthier than that which occurs in the eighth or ninth month. For this, he says, is the beginning of birth common to all men, both noble and immoral, and he shows that there is one entrance for all into life and a similar exit for all: because every earthly man, sprung from the seed of man and woman, receives the beginning of temporal life, and passes from this world through the death of the body. However, the heavenly Adam, who was born without male seed from a virgin mother, has his own manner of birth, and he also arranged the manner of his death by his sovereign power. Nevertheless, the seriousness of his birth is celebrated by Christians in the tenth month after his conception, when from the eighth day before the Kalends of April, when he is believed to have been conceived, to the eighth day before the Kalends of January, when he is said to have been born, two hundred and seventy-six days are counted, which number of days joins nine months and six days, and therefore that evangelical saying, by which the same Savior foretold to the Jews that the temple of his body, built in forty-six years, would be destroyed, shows the perfection of his incarnation by the multiplication of the number six: because two hundred and seventy-six contains forty-six sixes. From this it is clearly evident that just as he is perfect God, so also is Jesus Christ our Lord perfect man. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

On the fact that he confesses to having received the spirit of wisdom, in which he recounts several gifts of its charisms.

The lover of wisdom said that he had chosen, invoked, and sought after wisdom, and for this reason, through the grace of the Holy Spirit, he obtained it, because, as the apostle James says: Whoever lacks this, let him ask of God, who gives to all abundantly and does not reproach (James 1:5); which is rightly preferred to kingdoms, thrones, and all riches, since all precious things of the world become worthless in comparison to it. Therefore, Truth itself in the Gospel likens the kingdom of heaven to a merchant seeking fine pearls; who, having found one precious pearl, went and sold all he had and bought it (Matt. 13:45-46); because whoever truly finds heavenly wisdom, that is, the word of God in the highest, despises all advantages of this present life, all human philosophy, and all eloquence of lecturers, and will rejoice to devote himself solely to divine contemplation. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

RABANUS. The salvation of the soul, which is in the possession of divine wisdom, together with works of justice, must absolutely be preferred to all bodily health and to every kind of corporeal things, because, as will be shown later, this wisdom is more beautiful than the sun and surpasses every arrangement of the stars; compared to light, it is found to be purer, since night and darkness follow them, but its light is inextinguishable. Moreover, that all good things came to him together with it was experienced by Solomon himself, who, asking God for wisdom alone, also obtained with it the greatness of the kingdom, riches, and glory. RABANUS. Whoever is guided in all things by wisdom, which leads and governs him in all his counsels and actions, is truly happy in everything; but it is remarkable how he says that he was ignorant that she is the mother of all these things, when previously he said that she anticipates those who desire her and shows herself first to them. Unless perhaps, in the course of his meditation, he realized that he had advanced so much that, by equating his former knowledge with the latter, when he saw that the latter excelled the former, he considered the former as ignorance in comparison to the latter. It is like that passage in the Gospel: for John the Baptist, proclaiming the coming Lord and pointing to him present with his finger, after he had baptized him, said that he did not know him before, but that it was revealed to him when the Holy Spirit descended upon him, saying therefore: "And I did not know him, but he who sent me to baptize with water said to me: 'He upon whom you see the Spirit descending and remaining upon him, he it is who baptizes with the Holy Spirit.' So also among the lights of the world, although the other stars are bright in themselves, in comparison to the sun they are found to be by no means bright. Therefore, the sun itself, because it alone shines with daylight among the other stars, is so named. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

 

7:13 I learned without cunning and share without envy; I do not hide her wealth.

RABANUS. Earlier it was said that the Holy Spirit, the giver of divine wisdom, would flee from a artificial discipline, and that wisdom would not enter into a wicked soul. For where there is zeal and contention, there will be instability and every evil work. But the wisdom that is from above is first indeed pure, then peaceable, gentle, open to persuasion, consenting to good, full of mercy and good fruits, judging without hypocrisy (James 3:13–17). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

The infinite therefore calls wisdom a treasure, because the more diligently one learns it, the deeper one realizes it to be; and the more one advances by meditating on it, the more one understands that it is sought at greater depth; and therefore those who meditate on it become participants in the friendship of God, because they become true knowers of the commandments and will of God. Concerning this, Truth itself says to the disciples in the Gospel: "I no longer call you servants, because a servant does not know what his master is doing. GREGORY THE GREAT. And it often happens that they speak boldly, but nevertheless do not know how to live according to what they say. And he who desires not only to speak from knowledge, but also from understanding, wishes to experience what he says by feeling, not merely by knowing. But the mind of the arrogant does not penetrate the sense of their own words, because it is repelled from inward taste and right judgment toward those outward favors which it loves. 7:17 For he himself gave me unerring knowledge of what exists, to know the structure of the world and the operation of the elements, 7:18 the beginning and end and middle of times, the rotations of the turning seasons and the changes of the times, 7:19 the cycles of years and the positions of stars, 7:20 the natures of living creatures and the tempers of wild beasts, the violence of spirits and the thoughts of men, the varieties of plants and the powers of roots, 7:21 all that is hidden and all that is visible I learned; for wisdom, the craftsman of all, taught me.

7:22 For in her is the spirit of understanding, holy, unique, diverse, subtle, mobile, clear, undefiled, lucid, unharmed, loving the good, keen, unconstrained, beneficent, 7:23 humane, steadfast, secure, free from care, all-powerful, all-seeing, and passing through all spirits that are intelligent, pure, and most subtle.

RABANUS. Therefore, when the writer of history wished to recount the power of this wisdom, he said that in it is the Spirit of understanding, because the recognition of truth is discussed through it. Holy, because it sanctifies all things. Unique, because it creates unity and concord among the elect. Diverse, because it bestows variety of knowledge and virtue. Subtle and mobile, because the word of God is living and effective, and sharper than any two-edged sword, reaching even to the division of soul and spirit, of joints and marrow, and discerning the thoughts. Eloquent, because it opens the mouth of the mute, and makes the tongues of infants eloquent. Unstained, because no spot of sin adheres to it. And it is called steadfast, because by its nature it contains all things. But it is called mobile, because it meets even those who do not know it everywhere. Therefore, holding all things, being steadfast, and presenting itself to all, it is called mobile. But in these words a great question arises for us: why is this Spirit, who fills all things at once, called both mobile and steadfast? But if we return to the usage of human custom, we find the sense more quickly. For a man who moves everywhere in the region where he is, without doubt comes to meet everyone, and is suddenly found where he is not expected. Therefore, the omnipotent Spirit, so that it might be signified as present everywhere, is at once mobile, possessing all power, foreseeing all things, because it gives strength to the weary and light to the blind, for he alone will give strength and fortitude to his people. He comprehends all intelligible, pure, and subtle spirits, because in his hand are all the ends of the earth, and he beholds the heights of the mountains (Ps.95:4). RABANUS. The Apostle says of the same Son: Who, being the brightness of glory, and the express image of his substance, and upholding all things by the word of his power (Heb. 1:3). 7:27 And though she is one, she can do all things, and remaining in herself she renews all things, and from generation to generation, passing into holy souls, she makes friends of God and prophets.

RABANUS. Therefore, when something is pronounced or narrated in the Scriptures about wisdom, whether by wisdom itself speaking, or when something is said about it, the Son is especially indicated to us, by the example of whose image we also may not depart from God, because we too are the image of God, not indeed equal, for we are made by the Father through the Son, not born of the Father as that wisdom is; and we, because we are illuminated by the light, but that wisdom truly because it is the light illuminating, and therefore that wisdom is without an example, but for us it is an example. 7:28 For God loves nothing except the one who dwells with wisdom.

For every temporary thing, when compared to what is everlasting, seems as if it no longer exists, since it is consumed by eternity. Therefore, in a mystical sense, the brightness of the wisdom of God, which is Christ, when compared to the splendor of any of the saints, whom He here calls by the name of the sun and stars, far surpasses them, because they are changeable, but He always remains the same. The Psalmist, contemplating this, says to the Lord Himself: In the beginning, Lord, you founded the earth, and the heavens are the work of your hands. Chapter 8

That same Wisdom reaches from end to end mightily, and arranges all things sweetly; she teaches the four principal virtues, that is, temperance and wisdom, justice and virtue, and makes her participants to be glorious.

8:1 And it reaches mightily from end to end and orders all things sweetly.

RABANUS. Therefore, the only-begotten Son of God is co-eternal and co-equal in all things to the Father and to the Holy Spirit. From end to end, that is, from eternity to eternity, he reaches mightily, because he is the power of God; and he arranges all things sweetly, because he himself is wisdom, who in the Revelation of John therefore says: I am the Alpha and the Omega, the beginning and the end, says the Lord God (Rev. 22:13). The beginning, whom no one precedes. The end, to whom no one succeeds in the kingdom, who is, who was, and who is to come, the Almighty. Therefore, the one and the same creator is of both Testaments, namely the mediator of God and men, who in the law commended the fear of himself to his elect, and in the Gospel the love of himself. Therefore, elegantly, wisdom is said to reach from end to end mightily: because everywhere it is proclaimed to be of flawless and perfect virtue; for we often find in the Scriptures that the end is put for perfection, as is that saying of the Apostle: The end of the commandment is charity, from a pure heart and a good conscience, and faith sincere (1 Tim. 1:5). 8:2 I loved her and sought her out from my youth, and I sought to take her as my bride, and I became enamored of her beauty.

RABANUS. I have loved her, I have sought her from my youth. The lover of wisdom says that he has sought wisdom itself from his youth, that is, from the cradle of human life, and therefore he has truly found it, because he was a diligent seeker of it, according to that promise which she elsewhere made to her lovers, saying: Blessed is the man who listens to me, and who watches at my doors daily, and observes the posts of my gates (Prov. 8:34). Whoever finds me finds life, and will draw salvation from the Lord (Prov. 8:35). And again: I, she says, love those who love me, and those who watch for me in the morning will find me (Prov. 8:17). And I sought to take wisdom as my bride, and I became a lover of her beauty. I sought, he says, to take wisdom as my bride, that is, to join her to myself in a perpetual covenant, from whose companionship I know that I never wish to be separated, and whose beauty I have chosen to desire above all; for the beauty of wisdom is truth, which all who are truly religious especially long to know: because it is by this alone that all the elect of God hope to be made blessed in eternal rest. Therefore, Truth itself says to the Father in the Gospel: This is eternal life, that they may know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom you have sent (Jn. 17:3). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

8:3 She glorifies noble birth by living with God, and the Lord of all loved her;

RABANUS. Generosity is glorified by having fellowship with God. Therefore, generosity and nobility belong to the only-begotten Son, to have an eternal and omnipotent Father, with whom he himself remains coequal and coeternal from the beginning: because in the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us, and we saw his glory, the glory as of the only-begotten from the Father, full of grace and truth (Jn. 1:1-2). The Lord of all sits and loved her. Because the Father loves the Son, and has given all things into his hand: For she is the teacher of God's discipline, and the chooser of his works. John clearly proves this in the Gospel, saying: He who is to come after me has been made before me, because he was before me, and from his fullness we have all received, and grace for grace: because the law was given through Moses, grace and truth came through Jesus Christ. No one has seen God at any time. The only begotten Son, who is in the bosom of the Father, he has declared him (Jn. 1:15-18). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

8:4 for she is an initiate in the knowledge of God and an associate in his works. 8:5 And if wealth is a desirable possession in life, what is richer than wisdom, which works all things?  

RABANUS. And if riches are sought in life, what is richer than wisdom, which accomplishes all things? Therefore, the wisdom and power of God is the Lord Christ, through whom all things were made, and who, together with the Father, arranges and governs all things by his own will, according to the saying of the Gospel: My Father, he says, works even now, and I work (Jn. 5:17). He contains within himself the riches of all virtues, and distributes them to his lovers according to his will, as is equally declared in both the New and the Old Testament. Therefore, as wisdom itself speaks in Proverbs, saying: With me are riches and glory, lofty sheep and righteousness. My fruit is better than gold and precious stone, and my produce than chosen silver. I walk in the ways of righteousness, in the midst of the paths of judgment, that I may enrich those who love me, and fill their treasures (Prov. 8:18–21). So also in the Gospel, the same cries out and says: I am the light of the world. Whoever follows me does not walk in darkness, but will have the light of life (Jn. 8:12). Come to me, all you who labor and are burdened, and I will refresh you. Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light (Matt. 11:28-30). And elsewhere: Everyone, he says, who has left house or brothers, or sisters, or father, or mother, or wife, or children, or fields for my name's sake, will receive a hundredfold, and will possess eternal life (Matt. 19:29). This saying can also especially pertain to those who meditate on the divine law, who meditate on it day and night properly, and are constant in contemplation, because whoever seeks it with attentive heart and good determination will be filled with the indescribable sweetness of its inner truth, and will enjoy the abundance of all good things. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

8:6 And if prudence brings things about, who among the things that are is a more skillful craftsman than she?

RABANUS. But if sense is at work, which of these is more an artificer than that? If, therefore, wisdom is the creator of sound sense and profound understanding, how is it that she herself is not the producer of all good works, of sacred virtues, which offer lasting salvation to their lovers? [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

8:7 And if anyone loves righteousness, the labors of this are virtues; for she teaches soberness and caution, righteousness and courage, than which nothing is more useful in life for human beings.

RABANUS. And if anyone loves justice, the labors of it possess great virtues. No one can attain the prize of righteousness unless he follows the rule of wisdom, because it preserves the standard of equity and leads by the way of truth to eternal life. For indeed, the labors of it possess great virtues, because there is nothing idle, nothing combative in its works, but whatever things are true, whatever are pure, whatever are righteous, whatever are holy, whatever are lovable, whatever are of good reputation, these it performs; and it entrusts these things to its lovers to be done, so that by the practice of this good they may obtain eternal life; for the fruit of light is in all goodness, and righteousness, and truth. for she teaches soberness and caution, righteousness and courage, than which nothing is more useful in life for human beings. Not only do philosophers believe that there are four principal virtues, but our own authorities also agree, which here are called restraint, wisdom, righteousness, and virtue. Elsewhere, however, these same virtues are called by different names: wisdom, righteousness, courage, and restraint. But what these four are, or what the works of each are, we ought briefly to demonstrate with the Lord enlightening us. Hardly anyone is ignorant that the number four is sacred to perfection, since the whole world is found to be bounded by four parts or corners: east, west, north, and south; and Adam himself, the father of the human race, or the general name which is called “man,” is spelled with four letters. The body also, constructed from four elements, contains within itself the mystery of the number four. Even the soul itself is said to have four affections, by which we are led either to good or to evil; and the ancients discovered this subtly, and their discoveries have been approved and received by their successors. Moreover, the four rivers which flow from the source of paradise, or the four Gospels as the divine course of the wheel, and the four living creatures, their four wings and faces, all abundantly commend the dignity of this number. Therefore, we must carefully consider how much perfection these virtues, which are so numerous, contain in their number, how much sanctity they grant upon the Christian soul, and how there is nothing perfect anywhere which is not found in these virtues. For if restraint makes one moderate, wisdom wise, righteousness righteous, and courage brave, I do not know what could be more perfect than one who acts with restraint, righteously, wisely, and bravely. Therefore, it is indeed difficult for us to show the names of the individual virtues from which perhaps other virtues arise, which originate from those four we have called principal; but when their properties and actions begin to be revealed, it may appear from the effects what virtues arise from each. Nevertheless, above all, we must know and hold that these four virtues, or all those which arise from them, are gifts of God, and that no one has, had, or will have them except to whom God, who is the source and origin of all virtues, has granted them. For whoever at any time, in any nation, believed in God and lived by faith, could certainly become moderate, wise, righteous, or brave by His gift. But those who, not knowing God or even blaspheming, lived without faith, are to be believed to have been unable to receive or possess any of those virtues from God. For all have turned aside, together they have become worthless; there is none who does good, not even one (Ps. 14:3). And although they seemed to have a certain appearance of holiness, they were entirely ignorant of its power, because they were unwilling to submit to their Creator. Therefore, searching various things and striving by different inquiries and debates, they did not know the way of wisdom, nor did they find the way of discipline, and therefore they perished. And because they did not have the wisdom of God, they perished by their own wisdom. For God, our God, who is the source of life and the true light, the Word and power and wisdom of God, to whom no other can be compared nor is any like found, He discovered every way of discipline, and gave it to Jacob His servant, and to Israel His beloved. After these things, He appeared on earth and lived among men (Baruch 3:36–38). Therefore, all usefulness of discipline and understanding, of wisdom and prudence, must be referred to Him, because every good gift and every perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of lights (James 1:17). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

8:8 And if anyone desires great experience, she knows the things of old and can foretell things to come; she understands the turning of words and the solutions of riddles, she foreknows signs and wonders and the outcomes of seasons and times.

RABANUS. It is clear that to the eternal power and wisdom of God, namely our Lord Jesus Christ, all things are open, and everything is possible for Him; there is nothing hidden from Him, nor can anything resist His will. But whatever He wishes, He does in heaven and on earth, in the sea and in all the depths; He Himself knows the past and judges the future, as He says through the prophet: “Before Me no god was formed, and after Me there will be none” (Isa. 43:10). And in Proverbs: “The Lord,” he says, “possessed me at the beginning of His ways, before He made anything from the beginning; I was ordained from eternity, and from ancient times, before He made the earth” (Prov. 8:22-23). And again: “When He prepared the heavens, I was there; when He set a law and circle around the depths, when He established the sky above, and balanced the fountains of waters, when He surrounded the sea with its limit, and set a law for the waters so that they would not pass their boundaries, when He hung the foundations of the earth, I was with Him arranging all things, and I delighted every day, playing before Him” (Prov. 8:27-30). He Himself knows the subtleties of words and the endings of arguments, who in the Gospel said to His disciples: “I will give you a mouth and wisdom, which all your adversaries will not be able to resist or contradict” (Luke 21:15). He Himself knows signs and wonders before they happen, and the outcomes of times and ages, who, instructing His chosen ones about the future, foretold to them the signs and wonders which will come before the day of judgment and His coming at the end, therefore saying: “There will be signs in the sun and moon and stars, and on earth distress of nations in perplexity because of the roaring of the sea and the waves, men fainting with fear and expectation of what is coming upon the whole world. For the powers of the heavens will be shaken. And then they will see the Son of Man coming in the clouds of heaven with great power and majesty,” and the rest (Luke 21:25-27). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

DIDYMUS. Because there is no ignorance in Him, Daniel makes this clear, saying about Him: “He who knows what is in the darkness, and light is with Him,” (Dan. 2:22) and also: “Blessed is the knower of secrets, knowing all things before they happen.” (Dan. 13:42) For if He has knowledge of secrets and things hidden in darkness and of all things that are to be done, of what could He still be ignorant? Therefore, this light has no communion with ignorance of intelligible things. Therefore it is written: “What communion has light with darkness?” (2 Cor. 6:14) If the heretics wish to say, in order to detract from the divinity, that the presence disagrees with what was said: “He made darkness His hiding place” (Ps. 18:12), this is God; and also: “Moses entered the cloud where the Lord was” (Ex. 24:18), let them know that in these passages, darkness signifies the obscurity of His incomprehensibility. For it is possible that this obscurity arises for us through our ignorance of the reasons, not in God, who is light. Attention must also be paid to the very words, because what is meant by saying that God is in darkness and in a cloud or gloom, and that He made darkness His hiding place, is signified by these words: “And in Him there is no darkness at all.” (1 Jn. 1:5) Therefore, in Him there is no darkness, because it is not in His nature to have any; but He seems to us to be in darkness and gloom, since we do not have comprehension of Him, whose substance is known to exist. [Didymus of Alexandria, Commentary on the first epistle of John 2:4, PG 39] 313-398AD

8:9 I therefore judged this one to be taken for companionship, knowing that she will be a counselor of good things and an encouragement in cares and sorrow.

RABANUS. The wise man, recounting many things about the virtues of wisdom, finally added that he desires to be joined to its company, from which he would have comfort and relief from his weariness; so also the people of the Church, who can truly be said to be king and genuinely wise, because they are members of the heavenly King and Lord, as the apostle Peter says: But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for his own possession (1 Peter 2:9), rejoice in having the communication of divine wisdom, namely of the Lord Jesus Christ, and delight in remaining continually in his embrace and spiritual banquet, whom they know to be most merciful and kind, and the most generous dispenser of heavenly gifts. Therefore, the same speaks in the Apocalypse of Saint John, saying: Behold, I stand at the door and knock; if anyone hears my voice and opens the door, I will come in to him and dine with him, and he with me. This passage can also be referred to the Virgin Mother of God, who, having the purpose of most chaste virginity within herself, deserved to be made fruitful by the divine word of power, and by giving birth to the Son of God for the salvation of the human race, is to be venerated by all with the proclamation of most celebrated praise. Therefore, she herself says in the song of the Gospel: Because he has regarded the humility of his handmaid, for behold, from now on all generations will call me blessed (Luke 1:48). To which voice also agree those things which are added in this place. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

8:10 Through her I shall have glory among the multitudes and honor before the elders, though young; 8:11 I shall be found sharp in judgment and shall be admired in the sight of rulers; 8:12 when I am silent they will wait for me, and when I speak they will pay attention, and when I talk they will lay their hands on their mouths.

RABANUS. For the birth from the Virgin has been a wonder for all ages, who came, according to the word of blessed Simeon, for the fall and resurrection of many, and as a sign which would be contradicted. Of whom it is also said through Isaiah: The root of Jesse, who stands as a sign for the peoples, before whom kings will shut their mouths, him the nations will seek, and his tomb shall be glorious (Isa. 11:10). Otherwise, the assembly of holy teachers, filled with the doctrine of the Savior and illuminated by the grace of the Holy Spirit, has brightness before the crowds, when by preaching the Gospel and by recommending the word of God through miracles, it calls together a multitude of the elect from all nations to the faith of God. It has honor among the elders when it subjects the principalities and powers of this world to the Christian religion; or even, according to the mystical sense, it has honor among the elders when it is joined with the patriarchs and prophets in the heavenly kingdom. It is found young and sharp in judgment, when it discerns and judges all things according to the law of God. And it is admirable in the sight of the mighty, when the kings of the earth and princes are amazed at the preaching of the Gospel, and, marveling, revere it, and look upon the speaker, and are unable to resist his argument, as has happened very often, as the book of Acts of the Apostles and the passions of the holy martyrs under the New Testament testify. Therefore, it is read in the aforementioned book that, when Peter was preaching, fear came upon every soul. Many wonders and signs were also done by the apostles in Jerusalem, and great fear was upon all (Acts 1). Similarly, in the same book it is narrated that those who disputed with Stephen could not resist the wisdom and the Spirit by which he spoke (Acts 6). Likewise, Saul, after he received the faith of God and was baptized in Damascus through the preaching of Ananias, grew stronger and confounded the Jews who lived in Damascus, affirming that this is the Christ, acting confidently in the name of God (Acts 9). The same apostle, when he was preaching the word of God on the island of Cyprus, and there found a false prophet of the Jews named Bar-Jesus, who was with the proconsul Sergius Paulus, a wise man, when he found that this man was resisting him, he rebuked him and struck him with blindness; immediately the proconsul, when he saw what had happened, believed, being astonished at the doctrine of the Lord (Acts 13). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

8:13 Through her I shall have immortality and leave behind an everlasting memory to those after me.

RABANUS. The saints possess immortality, because, instructed by the truth of faith and rooted and grounded in divine love, they are already partakers of the hope of future immortality, and by the teachings of sound doctrine, as well as by the examples of good works with the fruits of virtues, they will leave a blessed memory to the generations that come after them, and deservedly so, because they will have a great multitude of faithful disciples from all nations as preservers of their discipline. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

8:14 I shall govern peoples, and nations will be subject to me; 8:15 dreaded tyrants will fear me when they hear of me; in the multitude I shall appear good, and in war brave.

RABANUS. Holy teachers arrange the peoples and the nations subject to them to live with discipline, when they open up and explain to them the testimonies of the Law and the Prophets, and make them obey the sacred words of the Gospel. They no longer allow them to wander, wavering through various sects, but compel them to preserve the truth of faith and the purity of doctrine in all things. Even the most savage persecutors and dreadful kings are forced to yield to their patience, because they are in no way able to resist the power of Christ, who works wonders in them. He said to his disciples in the Gospel: "In the world you will have tribulation, but take courage, for I have overcome the world" (Jn. 16:33). And again: "He who is in you is greater than he who is in the world." (1 Jn. 4:4) Therefore, among the multitude of the faithful, they appear sincere and kind, but in battle against their adversaries, they are loyal and most courageous. But while these things are done outwardly, let us hear what wisdom grants them inwardly. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

8:16 Entering into my house, I shall rest with her; for her companionship has no bitterness, nor her fellowship any pain, but cheerfulness and joy.

RABANUS. After the preaching of the divine word, after the labors of good works by which holy men outwardly offer comfort to their listeners, returning to the homes of their hearts, they devote themselves in meditation to the contemplation of divine wisdom, and therefore, from the troublesome annoyances of secular affairs, they inwardly attain the peace of heavenly sweetness. For, as it is said, “her company has no bitterness, nor does her companionship bring weariness,” because bodily pleasures, when taken greedily, burden the body; but spiritual delights, the more they are received, the more they uplift the mind. For this is the usual difference between the delights of the heart and those of the body: bodily pleasures, when not possessed, kindle a grievous desire; but when greedily consumed, they immediately turn the eater to disgust through filling. On the other hand, spiritual delights, when not possessed, are a source of weariness; but when possessed, they are desired; and the more they are consumed by the one who hungers, the more they are hungered for by the one who consumes. In the former, desire pleases, experience displeases; in the latter, desire is of little value, experience pleases more; in the former, desire produces filling, filling produces disgust; but in the latter, desire produces filling, filling produces desire. Chapter 13

On the error of idolatry. That the idolaters themselves, seeking the Creator in created things, if they were willing, could find Him with His help.

13:1 For all men by nature were vain, in whom there was ignorance of God, and who were unable from the visible good things to know him who is, nor by attending to the works did they recognize the craftsman;

RABANUS. For all men by nature were vain, in whom there was ignorance of God. And if every living man is altogether vain, how much more vain are those who do not acknowledge their Creator. But all men are vain in whom there is no knowledge of God, that is, the fear and reverence of God. Therefore it is said through Job: Behold, the fear of the Lord, that is wisdom, and to depart from evil is understanding (Job 28:28). For he departs from evil who withdraws from the worship of idols; he fears God who keeps His commandments. For the beginning of wisdom is the fear of the Lord; a good understanding have all they that do His commandments (Ps. 111:10). and who were unable from the visible good things to know him who is. For from good works they could have known the good craftsman, who truly is, because He is always unchallengeable, who alone has immortality, and dwells in inaccessible light; who is always the same, and whose years never fail: who is eternal and omnipotent, without beginning and without end, always remaining in Himself, according to His will governs and directs all things; therefore, the vanity of men is most vain, who worship creatures instead of the Creator. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

13:2 but either fire or wind or swift air or the circle of stars or violent water or the luminaries of heaven, the rulers of the world, they supposed to be gods. 13:3 If, delighted by their beauty, they thought these to be gods, let them know how much better than these is their Lord, for the author of beauty created them;

RABANUS. For some worshipped fire as a divine power, which they called Vulcan; others worshipped the ether or air, which they named Jupiter or Juno; others worshipped excessive water, which they called Neptune; others the circle of stars, who distributed the positions and courses of the stars according to the various shapes of animals; others venerated the sun as God, which they called Phoebus or Apollo; others the moon, which they used to call Diana. For, deceived by such error, they attributed the power of the almighty God to their false inventions, as a certain pagan poet concluded in three verses, saying: “So many and such images did God place in the heavens, and through the dark shadows scattered, He commanded the bright lights to give clarity to the devastating night.” But if it is not possible for the stars to be gods, then neither can the sun nor the moon be gods, since they differ from the lights of the stars not in kind, but in size. But if these are not gods, then neither is the sky, in which all these are contained. In the same way, if the earth which we tread, which we cultivate and till for sustenance, is not a god, then neither will the fields nor the mountains be gods; if these are not gods, then the whole earth cannot be considered a god. Likewise, if the water which serves living beings for drinking or washing is not a god, then neither are the springs from which water flows. If the springs are not gods, then neither are the rivers which are gathered from the springs. If the rivers also are not gods, then the sea, which is made up of rivers, cannot be considered a god. But if neither the sky, nor the earth, nor the sea, which are parts of the world, can be gods, then neither is the whole world a god, which the same Stoics claim to be both living and wise, and therefore God, in which they have been so inconsistent that nothing has been said by them which has not also been overturned by themselves. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

13:4 but if, astonished at their power and activity, let them understand from them how much more powerful is he who made them. 13:5 For from the greatness and beauty of created things their maker is correspondingly perceived.  

RABANUS. It would have been fitting that the most beautiful and splendid Creator be recognized through beautiful creatures, but they became vain in their thoughts and chose to serve the creature rather than the Creator, who is blessed forever. Therefore, they are refuted not only by the truth but also by their own words; for just as a house, made for the purpose of dwelling, feels nothing of itself and is subject to the master who made or inhabits it, so the world, feeling nothing of itself, is subject to God the Maker, who made it for His own use. Therefore, the sinner is at fault before the foolish for two reasons: first, because they prefer the elements, that is, the works of God, to God Himself; then, because they worship images of the elements themselves, fashioned in human form, for they make images of the sun and moon in the manner of humans, likewise of fire and earth and sea, which they call Vulcan, Vesta, and Neptune. Such is the desire of men for images that they are even more attracted to those which are rare; they delight, of course, in gold and gems and ivory, the beauty and brilliance of these dazzle their eyes, nor do they consider any place sacred unless these things have shone there. Therefore, under the hoax of gods, greed and desire are worshipped; for they believe that the gods love whatever they themselves desire, whatever it is for which thefts, murders, and robberies are daily committed, and for which wars overturn peoples and cities throughout the world. Therefore, they consecrate to the gods their spoils and plunder, whom, indeed, they must admit to be weak and devoid of the highest virtue, if they are subject to desires: for why should we think them heavenly, if they desire something from the earth? Or blessed, if they need anything? Or incorruptible, if they take pleasure in those things in the desire for which human greed is rightly condemned? [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

AUGUSTINE. That God is a certain eternal life, unchangeable, intelligible, understanding, wise, making others wise, even some philosophers of this age have seen. They have indeed seen a fixed, stable, unchanging truth, where all the reasons for all created things are found, but they saw it from afar; they saw it, but while in error; and therefore, to that great, indescribable, and beatifying possession, they did not find the way by which one might arrive. For since they too saw (as much as can be seen by a human) the Creator through the creation, the Maker through the thing made, the Fashioner of the world through the world, the Apostle Paul is a witness, whom Christians ought surely to believe. For he says, when speaking of such people: The wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness. These, as you recognize, are the words of the Apostle Paul. The wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who hold the truth in unrighteousness. Did he say they do not hold the truth? Rather: they hold the truth in unrighteousness. What they hold is good; but where they hold it is evil. They hold the truth in unrighteousness. But it might be asked: How do those ungodly ones hold the truth? Did God speak to any of them? Did they receive the law, as the people of Israel did through Moses? Whence then do they hold the truth, even in unrighteousness? Listen to what follows, and it is shown. Because what is known of God, he says, is manifest in them; for God has shown it to them. Did he show it to those to whom he did not give the law? Hear how he showed it. For his invisible things, being understood by the things that are made, are clearly seen. Question the world, the beauty of the sky, the brightness and arrangement of the stars, the sun sufficient for the day, the moon the comfort of the night; question the earth, fruitful with herbs and trees, full of animals, adorned with humans; question the sea, full of so many and such kinds of creatures that swim; question the air, enlivened by so many birds; question all things, and see if by their own sense they do not answer you: God made us. These things even noble philosophers sought, and from the art they recognized the artist. What then? Why is the wrath of God revealed against that ungodliness? Because they hold the truth in unrighteousness? Let him come, let him show how. For he has already said how they knew. His invisible things, that is, of God, are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made; even his eternal power and divinity; so that they are without excuse. Because, when they knew God, they did not glorify him as God, nor gave thanks; but became vain in their thoughts, and their foolish heart was darkened. These are the words of the Apostle, not mine: And their foolish heart was darkened; for, professing themselves to be wise, they became fools. What they found by curiosity, they lost by pride. Professing themselves to be wise, that is, attributing to themselves the gift of God, they became fools. The words, I say, are the Apostle's: Professing themselves to be wise, they became fools. Show, prove their foolishness. Show, Apostle, and just as you showed us how they could come to the knowledge of God, because his invisible things, being understood by the things that are made, are clearly seen; so now show how, professing themselves to be wise, they became fools. Listen: Because, he says, they changed the glory of the incorruptible God into the likeness of the image of corruptible man, and of birds, and of four-footed beasts, and of creeping things. For the pagans made for themselves gods in the forms of these animals. You found God, and you worship an idol. You found the truth, and you hold that very truth in unrighteousness. And what you knew through the works of God, you lose through the works of man. You considered everything; you gathered the order of heaven, earth, sea, and all the elements; but you do not wish to consider this, that the world is the work of God, the idol is the work of the craftsman. If the craftsman, as he gave the idol its shape, also gave it a heart, the craftsman would be worshipped by the idol itself. [Augustine of Hippo, Sermon 141.1-3] 354-430AD

RABANUS. Paul the Apostle, writing to the Romans in his Epistle, as we have already shown above, expressed the likeness of this place, saying: For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who hold the truth of God in unrighteousness, because that which is known of God is revealed in them, for God has revealed it to them. For the invisible things of him, from the creation of the world, are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made, even his eternal power and divinity, so that they are without excuse (Rom. 1:18-20). The knowledge of God is revealed from the structure of the world. For God, who is by nature invisible, can also be known as visible, for a work was made by him which, by its visibility, reveals the maker, so that through the certain, the uncertain can be known; so that by what is impossible for others, he who made this work might be believed to be the God of all; so that they are without excuse: because they could not be ignorant of his existence and his working for their benefit, for it is clear to all that he has ordained those things which are produced for human use in the course of the year; but when they had known him, they did not glorify him as God or give thanks, but became vain in their imaginations, claiming to be wise, they became fools (Rom. 1:21-22). For they consider themselves wise because they think they are investigating physical principles, examining the course of the stars and the qualities of the elements, but despising the Lord of these things, they are therefore fools. Since these things are praiseworthy, how much more so is their creator? Yet, having suffered the shame of neglecting God, they often use a miserable excuse, saying that through these things one can go to God, just as one reaches the king through companions. Come now, is anyone so senseless or so forgetful of his own salvation as to give the honor of the king to a companion, when, if anyone is found even to discuss such a thing, he is rightly condemned for treason? And these people do not consider themselves guilty who prefer the honor of God's name to the creature, and, leaving the Lord, worship fellow servants, as if there were something more that could be preserved for God! For indeed, it is through the king that one goes to tribunes or companions, because the king is, after all, a man, and does not know to whom he should entrust the state; but to God, whom nothing escapes, for he knows the merits of all, there is no need for an intercessor to obtain favor, but only a devoted mind. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

RABANUS. They therefore come to the gods, not so much for the sake of religion, which can have no place in things ill-prepared and perishable, as to feast their eyes on gold, to gaze at the polish of refined marble or ivory, to handle with greedy contemplation the garments distinguished by little stones and colors, or cups adorned with shining gems; and the more decorative and beautiful the temples and statues are, the more majesty they are believed to possess. RABANUS. This wisdom was brought forth to insult idols and idolaters, so that such makers and worshippers might understand how wicked and how close to eternal death it is to abandon the one omnipotent God and Creator of all things, and to worship various creations as divinity in contempt of God. Therefore, through the prophet, the Lord spoke long ago to His people about His own veneration and the falsity of idols: I am the first and I am the last, and besides me there is no other God. Who is like me? Let him call and declare, and set in order for me since I established the ancient people, and let him announce to them the things to come. Do not fear, nor be troubled; from then on I have made you hear, and I have announced the future: you are my witnesses. Is there any God besides me, or a maker whom I do not know? All makers of idols are nothing, and their most beloved things will not profit them: they themselves are their witnesses, because they do not see nor understand, so that they may be confused. Who has formed a god, or cast a graven image that is of no use? Behold, all his associates shall be confused; for they are craftsmen, and they gather from all, all shall stand and tremble and be confounded together. The ironworker works with a file in the coals, and with hammers he shapes it, and works with the strength of his arm; he will hunger and grow weak, he will not drink water and will faint. The woodworker stretches a line and shapes it with a plane; he makes it with a square, and turns it with a compass: and he makes the image of a man, as a beautiful person dwelling in a house. He cuts down cedars, takes holm oak and oak which stood among the trees of the forest; he plants a pine which the rain nourished, and it becomes fuel for men; he takes some of it and is warmed, and kindles it and bakes bread; but from the rest he makes a god, and worships it; he makes a graven image, and bows down before it. He burns half of it in the fire, and with the half he eats meat; he cooks stew and is satisfied, and is warmed, and says: Aha, I am warmed, I have seen the fire. But the rest of it he makes into a god, and a graven image for himself; he bows down before it, and worships it, and prays saying: Deliver me, for you are my god. Nor do they consider in their mind, nor know, nor feel, so as to say: I burned half of it in the fire, and baked bread on its coals, cooked meat and ate, and from the rest shall I make an idol? Therefore, according to the history, the power of the Lord was demonstrated when He made the people of Israel cross the Red Sea on dry foot; likewise, when they left the desert, He led them through the bed of the Jordan, with the waters divided on either side, so as to instill terror in the nations of the Canaanites to be exterminated. 14:5 And because you do not wish the works of your wisdom to be idle, for this reason people trust their lives even to the smallest piece of wood, and, having crossed the surge on a vessel, were saved. 14:6 For even at the beginning, when the proud giants were perishing, the hope of the world, taking refuge on a vessel, left to an age a seed of birth, guided by your hand.

RABANUS. The wondrous Creator gave knowledge to His creature, so that in the present age he might be able to attend to his own necessity, and serve the will of his Lord. Therefore also from the beginning, when the human race began to serve idolatry, and all evils were fashioned upon the earth by proud giants, the almighty Judge commanded His faithful servant Noah to build an ark, through which he and his offspring might be saved, and the species of all living creatures might be preserved for the future restoration of the world. The mystery of this, indeed, the Apostle Peter shows in his Epistle, saying: Christ once died for our sins, the just for the unjust, that He might offer us to God; put to death in the flesh, but made alive in the spirit. In which also He went and preached to the spirits in prison, who had once been disobedient, when the patience of God waited in the days of Noah, while the ark was being built, in which a few, that is, eight souls, were saved through water; which now saves you also, baptism of a like figure (1 Peter 3:18-21). They say that the form of baptism is likened to the ark and the waters of the flood, and rightly so, because the very construction of the ark from smoothed wood signifies the building of the Church, which is made from the gathering of the souls of the faithful by the architects of the Word. And that, when the whole world perished, only a few, that is, eight souls, were saved through water, signifies that, in comparison to the perishing Gentiles, Jews, heretics, and false believers, the number of the elect is much smaller. Therefore, concerning the narrow gate and the strait way which leads to life, it is said: And few there are who find it (Matt. 7:13-14). And again: Fear not, little flock, for it has pleased your Father to give you the kingdom (Luke 12:32). Therefore, that the water of the flood did not save those outside the ark, but destroyed them, undoubtedly prefigured that every heretic, although having the sacrament of baptism, is not raised by the waters but is drowned by them into hell, while the ark is lifted up to heaven. Also, the very number eight of the souls who were saved through water signifies that the holy Church receives the washing of baptism as a sacrament of the Lord’s resurrection, so that just as He rose from the dead by the glory of the Father, so we, cleansed from sins by the water of regeneration, may walk in newness of life. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

14:7 For blessed is the wood through which righteousness comes; 14:8 but the handmade thing, accursed is it and the one who made it, because the one worked, but the perishable thing was called a god.

RABANUS. This comparison of the two trees is different, because one deserves blessing, while the other deserves a curse. For blessed is the wood of the cross, on which, through the blood of Christ, the world was restored, and the redemption of the human race was perfected. On the contrary, the idol is cursed, that is, the image and worship of false gods, whose destruction is certain, and eternal torment awaits its maker. For the Devil will rightly be punished for having persuaded and agreed to have divine honor offered to himself; and men who worshipped him as God will deservedly suffer torments, because they tried to venerate falsehood instead of truth, and the creature instead of the Creator. But since we often find in the Holy Scriptures that wood is set for man, according to the rule of figurative speech, blessed is the man who speaks words of truth and does works of righteousness. But the heretic who worships the inventions of his own error is cursed, and assigned to eternal destruction, he himself, indeed, and his work, that is, his doctrine, which he presumed to teach falsely as truth. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

14:9 For equally hateful to God are both the ungodly person and his ungodliness; 14:10 and indeed the thing done together with the doer will be punished.

RABANUS. Therefore, it is also said through Jeremiah: Every man has become foolish by his own knowledge, every maker of idols is confounded by his carved image, because his molten image is false, and there is no spirit in them; for their works are vain and worthy of ridicule; in the time of their visitation, they shall perish (Jer. 10:14-15). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

JEROME. But perhaps you say: If God is my lover, why then do the lashes of my lover so grievously sadden me with severe wounds, and disturb me by hurting me? If He who loves me torments me with the embraces of scourges, then false love now exercises the parts of hatred: now it would be better for Him to hate, so that He would not torment. Rather, be careful that He does not hate; ask that He agrees to love. For love corrects, but hatred threatens punishment. Love is accustomed to bestow discipline, but hatred, when judgment dictates, recites the guilt. This hatred is propagated from God to man by the birth of the human race. And therefore, it is of man when God hates or is angry: He hates when He is offended, He is angry when, being offended, He is not entreated. For he who by hateful deeds makes himself hateful provokes the Lord to hatred of himself, and scorns to entreat the one he has offended. But God loves man as an artisan loves his work, but He hates evil deeds, by which the work is driven to ruin and forced into destruction. And He who hates evil deeds cannot love the doer of evil deeds: as the most holy Solomon declared: "The impious and his impiety are hateful to God" (Wis. 14:9). [Jerome of Stridon, Epistle 30, PL 211 30.0063C-30.0064A] died 420AD

14:11 Therefore there will also be an examination of the idols of the nations, because among God’s creatures they became an abomination, and a stumbling block to the souls of men and a snare for the feet of the foolish.

RABANUS. For the idols of the nations are not gods, and their inhabitants are, evidently, evil spirits, deserving of pardon, since men who were created in the image and honor of God were being led away from their Creator, and by casting them into the snare of their own error, were being overthrown. Likewise, if heretics persist in their wickedness, by which they lead men from truth into falsehood, and do not correct themselves and do penance, they will not escape the judgment of eternal fire. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

14:12 For the beginning of fornication is the invention of idols, and the discovery of them is the corruption of life. 14:13 For they were not from the beginning, nor will they be forever;

RABANUS. Although there are many kinds of fornication, the worst is that by which the soul fornicates, departing from God and worshipping idols. Similarly, that is also destructive which abandons the truth of divine doctrine and follows the falsehood of heretical opinion. For the Apostle Paul calls greed the service of idols (Eph. 5:5), from which it can be assumed that every evil desire may rightly be called fornication, when the soul, neglecting the higher law by which the lower is governed, is corrupted as if prostituted for a price to the base will of the natures. But all these things, just as they were not created from the beginning, so also they will not remain forever. For the Lord will destroy all deceitful lips, and will cut off all who speak lies (Ps. 12:3). Therefore, it is also said through Jeremiah: The gods who did not make the heavens and the earth will perish from the earth and from under these heavens. But the Lord is the true God, he is the living God and the eternal King (Jer. 10:10-11). Therefore idols first arose, the following sentence shows. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

14:14 for they entered the world through men’s vanity, and therefore their end was devised to be brief. 14:15 For a father, grieved by untimely mourning, made an image of the child quickly taken away, and then honored as a god the man who had died then, and handed down mysteries and rites to those under his control. 14:16 Then in time the godless custom, having gained strength, was kept as law, and carved images were worshiped at the commands of tyrants, 14:17 whom men, unable to honor in person because they lived far away, having reproduced the distant appearance, made a visible image of the honored king, so that by their zeal they might flatter the absent as though present. 14:18 And the ambition of the craftsman also urged on those who did not know, to intensify the religion; 14:19 for he, perhaps wishing to please the ruler, forced by his art the likeness into greater beauty; 14:20 and the crowd, drawn on by the attractiveness of the work, regarded as worship the man who had recently been honored. 14:21 And this became a snare for life, because men, enslaved either by misfortune or by tyranny, bestowed on stones and wood the incommunicable Name.

RABANUS. However, Firmianus Lactantius, in the first book he wrote on the false religion of the gods, presents similar things regarding this passage, saying: It is not obscure by what reasoning men began to be called gods, for it was because of the depravity of men who lived a rural life without any ruler. There is no doubt that in those times they began to praise a man who was king, and his whole people, with the highest praises, so that they even called them gods, whether they truly believed this because of the miracle of virtue, being still rude and simple, or, as often happens, out of flattery for present power, or because of the benefits by which they were brought to civilization. Then, when these kings were dear to them, they also arranged their lives accordingly, and after their death left behind a great longing for themselves. Therefore, men fashioned images of them, so that they might have some consolation from the contemplation of their likenesses, and, going further out of love, they began to honor the memory of the deceased, so that they might seem both to repay gratitude to those who had deserved well, and to attract their successors to the desire of ruling well. Cicero teaches this about the nature of the gods, saying: But the common custom of men has taken up the practice that those who excel in benefits, men, are raised to heaven by fame and will. Therefore Hercules, later Castor, therefore Pollux, later Aesculapius, therefore Liber. And in another place: And in most cities it can be understood that, for the sake of sharpening virtue, or because they would more willingly face danger for the sake of the republic, the memory of the best and bravest men was consecrated in honor of the immortal gods. By this reasoning, of course, the Romans consecrated their Caesars, and the Moors their kings; therefore, little by little, religions began to exist, while those first who knew them in that rite filled their children and grandchildren, and then all their descendants; and yet these greatest kings, because of the fame of their name, were worshipped in all provinces. But privately, each people venerated with the highest reverence the founders of their race or city, or men notable for their bravery, or women remarkable for their chastity, as the Egyptians did Isis, the Carthaginians Urania, the Romans Quirinus, Samos Juno, Naxos Liber, the Moors Juba, the Latins Faunus; likewise, Paphos Venus, Delos Apollo, the Macedonians Scabrius, the Athenians Minerva, Lemnos Vulcan; therefore, throughout peoples and regions, various sacred rites were undertaken, while men desired to show gratitude to their rulers. And why they should bestow other honors on those who lack life, I cannot find: therefore, the devotion of those who succeeded contributed greatly to the error, who, so that they might seem born of divine stock, bestowed divine honors on their parents, and ordered them to be bestowed. Therefore, he says, the incommunicable name was placed upon stones and wood: because the incomprehensible name of the living and omnipotent God, who alone is, they wickedly ascribed to images treacherously composed of insensible and fragile matter, and, together with false religion, shamelessly destroyed themselves with all vices. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

14:22 Then it was not enough to stray concerning the knowledge of God, but, living also in a great war of ignorance, they call so many evils peace. 14:23 For either they hold murders of children as rites, or secret mysteries, or mad revels from alien customs, 14:24 and no longer keep lives or marriages pure; one destroys another by ambush or grieves him by adulterating him. 14:25 And blood and murder, theft and deceit, corruption, unfaithfulness, turmoil, perjury, confusion over good things hold all men in common, 14:26 forgetfulness of grace, defilement of souls, change of birth, disorder of marriages, adultery and wantonness.  

RABANUS. For they exchanged the glory of the incorruptible God for the likeness of the image of corruptible man, and of birds and quadrupeds and serpents, and they exchanged the truth of God for a lie and served the creature rather than the Creator, who is blessed forever; although they called themselves wise, they became fools: for they were given over to a reprobate mind, so that they dishonored their bodies among themselves, and those who refused to enter into a covenant with God through right faith and good works, being placed under the worship of the Devil, could in no way have true peace among themselves. Therefore they sacrificed their own children, as the pagans themselves did in the rites of Saturn, who sacrificed to Saturn out of hatred for Jupiter, and extinguished tender and innocent souls, which is the age most sweet to parents, without any regard for virtue. Others, indeed, offered human blood to the virtue they called Bellona; for the priests themselves sacrificed not with another’s blood, but with their own, striking themselves with drawn swords. About this, Quintilian rightly says: “If God requires this, He is angry.” Others, performing obscure rites, held vigils full of madness, as they were accustomed to do in the rites of Egyptian Isis and Eleusinian Ceres; for just as Osiris was sought there with the lamentation of his mother, so here Proserpina, abducted for the incestuous marriage of her uncle, was sought. Because Ceres is said to have searched for her in Sicily with torches lit from the summit of Etna, therefore her rites are celebrated with the tossing of burning torches. Likewise, in the rites of Father Liber, vigils full of madness were held; also in the festival of the Great Mother, where, following the example of their gods, who spent the night in feasting and games during her festival, these people consumed the whole night in useless play. Neither life nor marriage was kept pure by the pagans themselves, because they thought they could please their gods through lust and impurities of life; for all things were confused where reason and true religion held no place, but the more wicked someone was, the more he thought he pleased his god. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

14:27 For the worship of nameless idols is the beginning and cause and end of every evil;

RABANUS. There is no greater madness than to abandon the living God and serve dead idols, and therefore the cause and beginning of all evil is the worship of idols, because after the human mind turns away from its creator, it becomes entangled in the errors of all crimes. For the beginning of all sin is pride (Eccles. 10:13). Likewise, the end, that is, the completion or fullness of all evil, is the same idolatry; for nothing is lacking in any wickedness to him who worships the Devil as divine power; therefore, everything is reckoned to him as evil and sin. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

14:28 for either they party madly, or prophesy lies, or live unjustly, or swear falsely without delay; 14:29 for trusting in soulless idols, and having sworn wickedly, they do not expect to be wronged.

RABANUS. Their whole life is established in errors; if they rejoice, either at festivals or in their games, they act madly, while they either commit murders or defile themselves obscenely with lust; while they prophesy, they deceive and lead their listeners astray; while they live unjustly, they are dead in soul; if they swear falsely, they are quickly led to hell; when they swear by idols, they pollute the name of God, who commands in the law, saying: You shall not take the name of the Lord your God in vain (Ex. 20:7). And again: By the name, he says, of foreign gods you shall not swear, but by the name of the Lord your God (Ex. 23:13). But when they think they can mock God, they cast themselves into eternal death. Allegorically, however, heretics, worshipping the images of their errors, are themselves the cause of their own destruction, whose doctrine is false, life unjust, joy insane, and death abominable. For when they swear unjustly by an idol and do not hope in the true God, despising justice, they will receive the just punishment of their deeds. This is also consequently implied. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

14:30 But both righteous judgments will overtake them, because they thought wrongly about God by paying heed to idols, and swore unjustly, despising holiness in deceit; 14:31 for it is not the power of those by whom they swear, but the punishment of sinners that always pursues the transgression of the unjust.

RABANUS. By the just judgment of God, those who are condemned and repent too late recognize what, while living before, they refused to believe; therefore also the Psalmist bears witness, saying: The Lord will be known by executing judgments; the sinner is caught in the works of his own hands. The sinners shall be turned into Hades, all the nations that forget God (Ps. 9:16-18). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Chapter 15

He confesses to God that His wisdom governs all things.

15:1 But You, our God, are kind and true, long-suffering and governing all things in mercy. 15:2 For even if we sin, we are yours, knowing your power; but we shall not sin, knowing that we have been accounted to you. 15:3 For to know you is complete righteousness, and to know your power is the root of immortality.

RABANUS. Our God is gentle, for His love is indescribable. He is true, because He deceives no one and is deceived by no one. Patient and in mercy arranging all things; for because of His goodness He patiently endures us, waiting for us to turn for the better. Not wishing anyone to perish, but that all may be saved and come to the knowledge of the truth. (1 Tim. 2:4) As He says through the prophet: I do not desire the death of the sinner, but that he should turn and live (Ezek. 33:11). If we have sinned, we cannot escape His hand, because we are His creation. But if we cease to sin and persist in good works, we will receive a sure reward from Him, with whom the full number of all good deeds is kept. For He knows all, and no secret is hidden from Him. What His knowledge brings us is made clear by the following statement. For to know you is perfect justice, and to know your justice and virtue is the root of immortality. Whoever truly desires to know God strives to abstain from all sin, as the apostle John confirms, saying: Everyone who has this hope in him purifies himself, just as he is pure (1 Jn. 3:3). And again: Everyone who abides in him does not sin; everyone who sins has not seen him nor known him (1 Jn. 3:6). But to know God is perfect justice, that is, the highest perfection of blessedness; and to know his justice and virtue is the root of immortality. But what is the justice and virtue of God except him of whom the Apostle says: I mean Christ, the power of God and the wisdom of God, who has become for us from God wisdom and justice, sanctification and redemption. For to know God and his justice and virtue is the root of immortality (1 Cor. 1:30), that is, the perception of incorruption and the contemplation of truth in eternal life, as Truth itself says to the Father in the Gospel: This is eternal life, that they may know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom you have sent; whom now we know in part by faith, but then we shall know in truth, just as we are known, for we shall see him as he is; for now we see through a mirror and in a riddle, but then face to face (Jn. 17). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

VIGILIUS. On common truth. True Father, true Son, true Holy Spirit. Of the Father in Solomon: For our God, are kind and true: For to know you is complete righteousness (Wis. 15:1, 3). Of the Son in John: That we may be in his true Son Jesus Christ; this is the true God, and eternal life (1 Jn. 5:20). Of the Holy Spirit in the Gospel: When that Paraclete, the Spirit of truth, shall come, he will teach you all truth (Jn. 16:13). [Vigilius of Thapsus, Against Marivadus the Arian, PL 62] 5th century

AUGUSTINE. [Verse 2] Do we not see how the Israelites got to their own hurt what their guilty lusting craved? For while it was raining manna on them from heaven, they desired to have flesh to eat. (Num. 11:32) They disdained what they had, and shamelessly sought what they had not: as if it were not better for them to have asked not to have their unbecoming desires gratified with the food that was wanting, but to have their own dislike removed, and be made themselves to receive aright the food that was provided. For when evil becomes our delight, and what is good the reverse, we ought to be entreating God rather to win us back to the love of the good, than to grant us the evil. Not that it is wrong to eat flesh, for the apostle, speaking of this very thing, says, Every creature of God is good, and nothing to be refused which is received with thanksgiving; (1 Tim. 4:4) but because, as he also says, It is evil for that man who eats with offense; (Rom. 14:20) and if so, with offense to man, how much more so if to God. [Augustine of Hippo, Tractate on the Gospel of John, 73.1] 354-430AD

On the various errors of idols, how they have been introduced into the human race; and that the worship of idols has always been a deceiver of human life.

15:4 For neither did the evil trick of men mislead us, nor the barren labor of a shadow-caster, an image stained with varied colors, 15:5 the sight of which brings disgrace to the foolish, and which longs for the lifeless form of a dead image without breath.

RABANUS. He speaks from the perspective of the faithful, who, fortified by the grace of God, in no way wish to involve themselves in the wickedness of the pagans, nor do they deem it worthy to imitate the errors they have in idolatry and various crimes, because such labor is without the fruit of righteousness; indeed, it deserves eternal death. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

15:6 Lovers of evil and worthy of such hopes, both those who do them and those who desire them and those who honor them.

RABANUS. Not only therefore are those who make idols guilty, but also those who love and worship them, placing their hope in them, are guilty and transgressors, because not only those who do evil, but also those who consent to those who do evil are worthy of death. The kind of people who place their hope in their idols is shown by the Psalmist, saying: The idols of the nations are silver and gold, the work of human hands. For the Lord will destroy the man who has done this, both the master and his disciple (Mal. 2:12). Woe to their souls, for evil has been repaid to them (Isa. 3:11). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

15:7 For a potter, kneading soft earth with toil, fashions each vessel for our service; but from the same clay he molded both vessels fit for pure works and those that are opposed, all alike; but as to which use of each belongs to each, the potter is judge. 15:8 And with weary labor he fashions from the same clay a vain god, who, having been born a little while ago from earth, after a little while goes away to that from which he was taken, when the debt of the soul is demanded. 15:9 But he is concerned not that he is about to labor, but that he has a brief life; and yet he struggles with goldsmiths and silversmiths, imitates bronze-workers, and considers it glory that he makes counterfeit things. 15:10 His heart is ashes, and his hope more worthless than earth, and his life more disgraceful than clay, 15:11 because he did not know the one who formed him and who breathed into him an active soul and breathed into him the breath of life; 15:12 but they thought our life a game, and our existence a profitable festival; for he says that one must somehow, even from evil, make a gain.

RABANUS. Previously, he had spoken about those who had made idols from wood or stones or certain metals; now he also speaks about those who, following the same vanity in the craft of the potter, are shown to be equally guilty through a similar error. For the pagans themselves have fallen into such foolishness that not only did they make idols from gold or silver, or even from precious stones, but they also fashioned images in the same way from cheap clay, calling a god that which was formed from the clay of the earth by the work of a potter. Therefore, it was justly repaid to them that, since they were made from the earth and did not wish to follow the better part of themselves, that is, the rational soul which is created in the image of God, to worship the living God, by imitating the baser part of themselves, that is, the earthly body, they worshipped a clay god. Therefore, afterwards, without the fruit of good works, they themselves, senseless and useless, were rendered to the insensible earth from which they were taken. Mystically, all heretics and schismatics who hold the truth of God in unrighteousness and worship a lie instead of the truth in the images of their doctrines, even if they sometimes possess attractive speech or seem to have reasonable virtues, will nevertheless be proven to be altogether fragile, as if made of clay, because they invent useless things. Therefore, their heart is ashes, that is, their thought is foul, and their life is more sleazy than any mud, because they are ignorant of Him who formed them and gave them an intelligent mind. Instead, they vanish in their own thoughts, despising the understanding of the holy Fathers, which those Fathers had in the divine Scriptures through the gift of the Holy Spirit. They shamelessly establish new errors, as if vain amusements, for human favor and earthly gain, wandering themselves and leading into error whomever they can, so that they may be inexcusable on the day of judgment, when the Judge of all shall come to judge the secrets of hearts and render to each according to his works. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

15:13 For he knows better than all that he sins, making brittle vessels and carved images from earthy material.

RABANUS. Heretics have no excuse, for they sin knowingly, when they refuse to follow the holy and catholic teachers in right faith and sound doctrine, but according to their own perverse inventions, they choose rather an earthly and fragile understanding than to strive for a spiritual and heavenly one in their dogmas. For all fools are excessively proud in their souls. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

That all wicked things were invented by the pagans because of idolatry, and that on account of it many have suffered torments.

15:14 And all the enemies of your people, who oppressed them, are most foolish and miserable beyond the soul of a child, 15:15 because they also considered all the idols of the nations to be gods, to whom there is neither the use of eyes for seeing, nor nostrils for drawing in air, nor ears for hearing, nor fingers of hands for feeling, and their feet are useless for walking. 15:16 For a human being made them, and one who borrowed breath molded them; for no human being like himself is able to fashion a god.

RABANUS. Indeed, the proud enemies of the people of God and all enemies of justice, although they may seem to rule on earth for a time, yet because they are foolish, they consider the senseless idols of the nations to be gods, which have neither sight, nor hearing, nor touch, nor any sense of life; they are most unhappy, awaiting the destruction of their souls in Gehenna, where there will be perpetual punishment of torment; for their worm does not die, and the fire is not quenched, because they did not give honor to the living and true God, and served carved images which they themselves wickedly made (Mark 9:44-48). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

15:17 But though mortal, he makes dead things with lawless hands; for he is better than the objects of his reverence, since he himself lived, but they never did.

RABANUS. For man is better than idols according to the reason of his creation, because he himself lives and understands, but they never do. 15:18 And they even worship the most hateful animals; for, compared in folly, they are worse than the others; 15:19 nor, as things that happen in the sight of animals, are they even worth desiring as beautiful, but they have escaped both the praise of God and his blessing.

RABANUS. Miserable paganism not only worshipped the human form shaped in insensible things as a deity, but also perversely adored the forms of other animals, nor did it have a just discernment in any matter. For according to the consideration of truth, living things are set before the dead, and sensible things before insensible, and rational animals before other animals; but it was fitting that those who ignored their Creator also did not know how to make proper distinctions among His creatures; for whoever neglects to have the praise and blessing of God within himself will prove himself to err in the observation and discernment of animals. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

Chapter 16

16:1 For this reason they were rightly punished by similar creatures and tormented by a multitude of beasts.

Chapter 17

Wisdom praises God, because His judgments are great and His words are inexpressible, He who wondrously punished enemies.

17:1 For great are Your judgments, and hard to describe; for this cause uninstructed souls went astray.

RABANUS. Great are the judgments of the almighty God, and inexpressible are the words by which, with wondrous command, He governs all creation. AUGUSTINE. Just as in the instrument of the organ, different sounds differ from each other, but musicians agree to temper them for the harmony of the melody, so also, although the various elements differ from each other in quality, they are nevertheless bent to the will of their Creator, preserving the law of their nature; for our Maker did not create His creation contrary to Himself, but made it in all things conformable to His will. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

19:18 For the elements, changing among themselves, as in a psaltery the notes of the rhythm alter the name, always remaining in sound, which one may accurately infer from the sight of what has happened. 19:19 For earthly things were changed into watery, and those that swim passed over onto land; 19:20 fire maintained its strength in water beyond its own power, and water forgot its power to quench; 19:21 flames, on the other hand, did not consume the flesh of perishable living creatures walking among them, nor did the melting, crystal-like, easily melted race of ambrosial food.

RABANUS. For men and beasts crossed the depths of the Red Sea on dry ground, and frogs coming out of the rivers filled the houses of the Egyptians. Fire mixed with hail rained down from heaven, and neither did fire shun water, nor water shun fire, but falling together, they struck the land of the Egyptians. But where the children of Israel were, they harmed no one, so as to show that all the elements of the world not only strive for their own nature, but are subject and serve the will of their Creator. [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD

19:22 For in all things, O Lord, you magnified your people and glorified them, and you did not overlook them, standing by at every time and place.

RABANUS. This closing passage of the book makes mention not only of the faithful of former Fathers, but also of those present and future at the same time, because our virtuous and merciful Creator, almighty God, never abandons nor will abandon those who hope in Him; rather, He cherishes, protects, and governs them with caring help. Nor should anyone object to us that the bodies of the holy martyrs are handed over into the hands of their persecutors, because this is permitted not for their condemnation, but for their testing, so that just as gold in the furnace of fire is not destroyed but tested, so too the souls of the elect, examined by the fire of tribulation, may be rendered more precious in the sight of their Creator. For it is written: The furnace tests the potter’s vessels, and the trial of tribulation tests just men (Sirach 27:5). For many are the tribulations of the righteous, and from all these the Lord will deliver them. The Lord guards all their bones; not one of them shall be broken. He will redeem the souls of His servants, and none of those who hope in Him will be forsaken (Ps. 34:22). [Rabanus Maurus, Commentary on Wisdom, PL 109] 780-856AD
